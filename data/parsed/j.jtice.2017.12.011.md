Synergistic peroxidase-like activity of CeO<sub>2</sub>-coated hollow Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanocomposites as an enzymatic mimic for low detection limit of glucose

Fei Huang<sup>1</sup>, Jianzhi Wang<sup>1</sup>, Weimin Chen, Yinjia Wan, Xianming Wang, Ning Cai, Jie Liu, Faquan Yu*

Key Laboratory for Green Chemical Process of Ministry of Education, Hubei Key Laboratory for Novel Reactor and Green Chemistry Technology, School of Chemical Engineering and Pharmacy, Wuhan Institute of Technology, Wuhan 430073, China

**A B S T R A C T**

Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> yolk–shell nanocomposites (NCs) were synthesized by growing CeO<sub>2</sub> onto Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> hollow nanoparticles (NPs) as an artificial peroxidase for the purpose of elevated activity. The resultant NCs exhibited strong magnetic response with narrow size distribution and high surface area. Owing to the individual peroxidase-like catalytic activity of CeO<sub>2</sub> onto Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles (NPs), Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was investigated to achieve synergistic catalytic activity. As a result, the as-prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs exhibited impressive peroxidase-like catalysis activity for 3, 3', 5, 5'-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB). Moreover, comparing with natural enzymes, hollow Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs and CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs, the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs displayed a lower Michaelis constant ($K_m$) value or stronger affinity to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB. A limit of detection of 8.5 μmol/L for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and 21 μmol/L for glucose was attained with acceptable stability and reproducibility. The activation mechanism was also studies using competitive radical tests and Electron spin resonance (ESR) analysis.

**1. Introduction**

Enzymes as an extremely efficient biological catalyst have attracted great interests because of these advantages, such as high substrate specificity, activities, and yields under mild reaction conditions so that broadened their application fields in pharmaceutical processes, agrochemical production, food industry and biosensing [1]. However, the disadvantages of natural enzymes lie in easy loss of activity, low stability and high cost, thus seriously restrict their practical applications [2]. Recently, artificial enzyme mimics based on inorganic nanoparticles (NPs) have been arising intense interest due to their impressive advantages, including easy preparation, low cost, high stability and tunable catalytic activities [3]. The typical examples include MFe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (M=Co, Zn, Mn) [4–6], Ceria NPs [7], graphene oxide (GO) [8], Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> magnetic NPs [9,10], gold NPs [11], CuO [12] and so on. Ceria has been comprehensively explored and applied in the field of catalysis, water treatment, fuel cells, and gas sensor due to its single crystal structure, good electrical and optical properties [13]. In addition, ceria NPs are a good candidate for catalysis materials owing to its oxygen storage capacity (OSC) associated with the ability to undergo a facile conversion between Ce (III) and Ce (IV) [14]. However, these inorganic NPs may somewhat achieve limited success in terms of catalysis efficiency, aqueous dispersion, separation and recovery. Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> magnetic NPs possess this kind of enzyme activity [15] and offer the advantages of convenient enrichment and subsequent easy separation or recovery under external magnetic field. The intrinsic instability or being easily oxidized in air might still restrain applications [16].

Yolk–shell structured materials with discrete functions are able to extend their application [17,18]. This kind of structured materials represent an unique class of complex hybrids, preventing the undesirable coalescence, sintering, aggregation or corrosion/dissolution of entrapped catalytic species [19]. Up to now, the well-defined structures have been usually prepared in complicated procedures and severe reaction conditions. Therefore, a simple and reliable synthesis research is desired to extend its practical applications.

Herein, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> nanocomposites (NCs) were successfully synthesized via a simple hydrothermal chemical reaction route. The as-prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs were then used to catalyze the oxidation of a peroxidase substrate 3,3',5,5'-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to the oxidized colored product, which provided a colorimetric detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration. Additionally, the detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> can be readily extended to figure out the concentration of glucose in terms of the fact that H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was evolved in the catalytic reaction of glucose with glucose oxidase (GOx). An extremely low detection of glucose was thus achieved benefiting from the elevated catalytic activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs in contrast to Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs and CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs alone. The result will allow practical and wide applications in clinics.

**2. Experimental section**

*2.1. Materials and reagents*

Cerium (III) nitrate hexahydrate (Ce(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O, 99.99%, Sigma-Aldrich), iron(III) chloride hexahydrate (FeCl<sub>3</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O), hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, 30%), ethylene glycol (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub>O<sub>2</sub>), 5,5-dimethyl-pyrroline-oxide (DMPO), polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP, (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>9</sub>NO)<sub>n</sub>), N,N- dimethylformamide (DMF, C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>7</sub>NO), ammonium acetate (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>7</sub>NO<sub>2</sub>, 98%), urea (CH<sub>4</sub>N<sub>2</sub>O, 99%), N,N,N,N-tetramethylethylenediamine (TMEDA, C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>16</sub>N<sub>2</sub>, 99%), glucose (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O), glucose oxidase (GOx from Aspergillus niger, Type VII, 100–250 units/kg) and 3,3',5,5'-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). All reagents in this study were in analytic grade and used without any further treatment. Deionized water was used throughout this experiment.

*2.2. Instruments and characterization*

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM, FEI, Tecnai G20) were used to observe the morphology of the samples. The crystal structure was examined by X-ray diffraction (XRD, Germany Bruker D8 advance X-Ray diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation). Fourier transfer infrared (FT-IR) spectra of the samples were obtained by a PerkinElmer 580B using the KBr pellet technique. The magnetic properties were measured by a vibrating sample magnetometer (VSM, Quantum design, USA physical properties measurement system (PPMS-9T) with VSM option) at room temperature. Electron spin resonance (ESR) spectra were acquired using a Bruker e-scan EPR spectrometer (Burker, Karlsruhe, Germany). The surface area was estimated using the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) equation and N<sub>2</sub> adsorption-desorption experiments were performed on a Micromeritics ASAP 2460 analyzer.

*2.3. Synthesis of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> hollow NPs*

The Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> hollow NPs were prepared through a modified solvothermal reaction [20]. Typically, 0.8 g of FeCl<sub>3</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O and 2.0 g of NH<sub>4</sub>Ac were dissolved in 40 mL of ethylene glycol with vigorous stirring, and then 1.0 mL of TMEDA was added. The mixture was stirred vigorously for 2 h to form homogeneous yellow solution, and then transferred into a Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave (50 mL capacity). The autoclave was heated to 200 °C and maintained for 10 h. After cooling to room temperature, the dark product was collected and rinsed with ethanol several times, and dried in a vacuum oven at 60 °C overnight for further experiments.

*2.4. Synthesis of CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs*

0.12 g of Ce(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O and 0.18 g PVP were stirred in 19 mL of deionized water until colorless transparent solution was obtained. Then 1 mL of DMF and 0.1 mL H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were added. The obtained solution was transferred to a stainless-steel sealed autoclave (50 mL capacity) and maintained at 180 °C for 20 h. The grey product was collected by centrifuge and dried in a vacuum oven at 60 °C overnight.

*2.5. Synthesis of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> yolk–shell NCs*

50 mg of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> hollow NPs was dispersed in 80 mL of 0.4 mol/L glucose solution under ultrasonication for 30 min. Then 0.4 g of Ce(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O and 0.6 g of urea were added to the above mixture. After 30 min sonication, the homogeneous mixture was transferred into a 100 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and heated at 120 °C for 8 h. After cooled to room temperature, the dark precipitate was collected and washed with ethanol several times with magnetic separation. Finally, the as-prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> yolk–shell NCs dried at 60 °C for 20 h in a vacuum oven.

*2.6. Peroxidase-like catalytic activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs*

To investigate the peroxidase-like catalytic activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> yolk–shell NCs, the catalytic oxidation of TMB was measured in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. In a typical procedure, 0.72 mg of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was dispersed in 4 mL of 0.02 mol/L acetate buffer and sonicated for 10 min. Then 300 μL of 50 mmol/L DMF solution of TMB and 31 μL of 100 mmol/L H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were added to the above solution. The absorbance of oxidized TMB evolved with time was monitored at 652 nm unless otherwise stated.

Effect of pH on the catalytic activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was investigated by catalyzing the substrate at 40 °C in the pH range of 1.0–7.0. Effect of temperature was also studied at different temperatures (20–60 °C). The relative activity (%) was adopted and defined as the ratio of the activity over the maximum activity in each group of experiments.

The kinetic parameters of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs were evaluated at varied concentrations of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0.5–4.0 mmol/L) or TMB (0.05–1.0 mmol/L) in buffer solution (pH 3.5). The Michaelis–Menten constant was calculated from the Lineweaver–Burk plot: $1/v = (1/v_{max}) (1 + K_m /[S])$, where $v$ is the catalytic velocity, $v_{max}$ is the maximal catalytic velocity, $[S]$ is the concentration of the substrate, and $K_m$ demonstrates the Michaelis constant.

*2.7. Lower limit of detection of glucose by Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs*

First, 5 μL of GOx (1 mg/mL) was added into 240 μL of phosphate buffer solution (10 mmol/L, pH 7.0) containing the varying concentrations of glucose (0–2.0 mmol/L) and incubated at 37 °C for 30 min. Then the mixture was transferred to 2.7 mL of acetate buffer (0.2 mol/L, pH 4.0) containing 0.36 mg of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs and 8 mmol/L of TMB and then incubated at 45 °C for 10 min. The supernatant’s absorbance was measured. The limit of detection (LOD) was calculated from the typical definition: $S/N \ge 3$, where $N$ is the standard deviation of blank sample and $S$ is the signal of sample.

**3. Results and discussion**

*3.1. Characteristics of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> hollow NPs, CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs*

The fabrication process of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs is displayed in Scheme 1. At first, the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> hollow NPs were prepared by a solvothermal process. Then the yolk–shell Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs were acquired using a one-pot coating process. CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs were prepared for the comparative study. Their morphologies were observed with TEM. The as-prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs showed regular spherical shape with a average diameter of 590 nm (Fig. 1a). Specifically, an obvious hollow structure was observed in Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs, and this hollow structure was further identified by high magnified TEM images (Fig. 1d). CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs were prepared for the comparative study and shown in Fig. 1b and e. These images revealed that the mean size was about 54 nm. Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs had very narrow diameter distributions with a mean diameter of 630 nm. After the coating by CeO<sub>2</sub>, the hollow structure was still observed clearly (Fig. 1c). A coarse surface was further revealed in the magnified TEM image (Fig. 1f). The higher magnified TEM even unveiled numerous small particles incrusted in the outer layer. These small particles were estimated in size in around 17 nm and believed to be CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs, which was validated in the following characterization. This observation verified the yolk–shell structure.

The chemical elemental composition of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was characterized by EDX spectra, elemental area mapping and line scan analysis. Fig. 2a–d analysis disclosed the coexistence of iron and cerium in the composite. Iron and cerium were distributed through the whole particles (Fig. 2a and c). EDX measurements revealed the signal of Fe and Ce elements (Fig. 2e). A line-scan experiment of an individual nanoparticle was performed to further confirm the yolk–shell structure. Fig. 2f showed the Fe, O and Ce L-line data across an individual Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NC. There Ce element was observed to be approximately even distributed along the scanning line while Fe mainly located at the center and was at nearly zero concentration at the edge of the particle. These observations verify the yolk–shell composite structure, being just in good accordance with the TEM observation.

*3.2. Evaluation of mimic peroxidase activity*

The peroxidase activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was demonstrated with TMB, ABTS and OPD as the respective substrate. As seen in Fig. 6A, all three substrates in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> showed color variation within 10 min, suggesting the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs possess catalytic activity. And we can found that the typical absorbance peak of oxidized TMB was observed at 652 nm, so in the further studies, we choose 652 nm to detection the signal of UV experiment.

The synergistic enhanced peroxidase activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was verified in comparison of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs and CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs. Both Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs and CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs possess peroxidase-like activity as for the previous reports [7,9,10]. In the process of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs catalysis, Eqs. (1)–(3) describe the catalytic process of the oxidation of the substrate TMB [27]. Fe<sup>2+</sup> was oxidized into Fe<sup>3+</sup>, releasing ·OH (Eq. (1)). Fe<sup>3+</sup> was reversely reduced into Fe<sup>2+</sup>, releasing HO<sub>2</sub>· species (Eq. (2)). The back-and-forth process keeps the catalytic species balance. The released ·OH species in Eq. (1) will further oxidize the substrate TMB (Eq. (3)), so that the colorless solution will become blue [28]. On the other hand, Ce<sup>3+</sup> was oxidized into Ce<sup>4+</sup>, releasing ·OH (Eq. (4)), similar with the process of Eq. (3). The released ·OH will oxidize TMB, the substrate in terms of Eq. (3). However, the Ce<sup>4+</sup> cannot go the similar route with Eq. (4) back to Ce<sup>3+</sup>. The back route needs HO<sub>2</sub>· species (Eq. (6)). Thus Eq. (5) is just needed to provide HO<sub>2</sub>· species to keep the dynamic equilibrium of Ce<sup>3+</sup> and Ce<sup>4+</sup>. Eq. (5), however, consumes ·OH species, which will compete with the oxidization of substrate (Eq. (3)). Obviously, the consumption of ·OH species turns out to slow down the oxidation of substrate. As a matter of fact, the process with Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs releases the HO<sub>2</sub>· species (Eq. (2)). If we coupled both the processes, the released the HO<sub>2</sub>· species could just cater for the requirement of Eq. (6). In this way, the process of Eq. (5) seems be omitted. The benefit lies in the fact that more ·OH species will participate in the process of substrate oxidation, enhancing the efficiency of peroxidase. Other than that, the consumption of HO<sub>2</sub>· in Eq. (2) further accelerates the rate of Eq. (2). To this knowledge, the coupling of both Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs and CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs will exhibit higher catalytic activity than any of both, or exhibit the synergistic enhancement in the activity. A close-loop process for the synergistic effect is illustrated in Fig. 7. Theoretically, the integrity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs and CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs into one system would elevate the catalytic oxidation process. Moreover, the highly dispersed CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs and large surface area of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs increase the contact of Ce<sup>3+</sup> active sites with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.

The peroxidase-like activity was investigated in different temperature and pH range, as presented in Fig. 8A and B. It was observed that the optimum pH and temperature were 3.5 and 40 °C, respectively. Mild acid environment does in favor of the catalytic process, which can be derived in light of the Eqs. (3) and (6) and the scheme in Fig. 7. Proton is necessary to complete the process of TMB oxidation. The reusability of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was evaluated, as shown in Fig. 8C. After seven cycles, the catalytic activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs still remained 90%, which is beneficial for the practical applications. In order to exclude the possibility that the enzymatic activity resulted from the leaching of free Fe<sup>2+</sup> ions or Ce<sup>3+</sup> in buffer, the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs were incubated in the acetate buffer solution (50 mmol/L, pH 3.5) for 24 h. The supernatant was recovered by the removal of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs by magnetic separation. As shown in Fig. 8D, The synergistic effect of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was highlighted in comparison with Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs, CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> under the same conditions. All the three types of nanoparticles exhibited peroxidase-like activity for TMB except H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. As expected, the absorbance corresponding to Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was highest among them, suggesting that the activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was remarkably enhanced. These results were assumed to be attributed to the synergistic effects as shown in Eqs. (1)–(6).

ESR is the most powerful and direct analytical method for quantification of short-lived radicals [29]. From the above mechanism, it is evident that ·OH is also formed owing to the typical chain reaction (Eq. (1)), which was further confirmed by ESR experiments with DMPO as the spin trapping agent. As shown in Fig. 9A, there was no signal from a radical identified in the ESR spectrum when H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> catalyst was added alone. We speculated that H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or catalyst can hardly generate radicals alone, which is consistent with the observation that H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or catalyst alone does not decolorize TMB. On the contrary, obvious signals were observed when Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were added together (in Fig. 9B). Only characteristic signals with an intensity ratio of 1:2:2:1 belong to DMPO–·OH product ($\alpha_H = \alpha_N = 14.9$ G) were existence, suggesting that ·OH were produced with the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> [30]. The evolution of the peak intensities of DMPO–·OH adducts depends on the time period. No was this kind of signal observed before adding the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0 min), which was similar to that result of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> reacting with TMB solution without the presence of catalysts. After 1 min, ·OH was identified apparently and this signal was intensified when reaction time was further extended to 2 min, indicating more ·OH had been produced compared with the reaction at 1 min. However, after 5 min, little or no DMPO–·OH adducts could be detected, suggesting the depletion of ·OH by the rapid reaction. These results indicate that Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> possesses the catalyticability to decompose H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to generate ·OH, which endorsed the peroxidase-like activity of the nanohybrids.

The steady-state kinetic parameters of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs, CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs were experimented by the initial rate method by choosing TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as respective substrate. The $K_m$ and $V_{max}$ were obtained using Lineweaver–Burk double reciprocal plot (Fig. 10A–F). The smaller the value of $K_m$, the stronger is the affinity between the enzyme and the substrate. The $K_m$ value was compared with the reported data [31–39] (Table 1). It was found that the $K_m$’s values of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB were lower than that of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs, CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs. This further verified the synergistic peroxidase-like activity. In addition, previously study has been shown the $K_m$ of the iron based materials such as FeS (7.2 mmol/L) [31], FeSe (13.2 mmol/L) [32], ZnFe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (1.66 mmol/L) [33], etc. More importantly, the previously study of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs [34] and CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs [35] indicated that their $K_m$ is 154 and 3.182 mmol/L, respectively. By comparing the apparent kinetic parameters, the $K_m$ value of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrate was 2 orders of magnitude lower than those of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs [34], and about 3 times lower than those of CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs [35] and HRP [36]. Indicating that Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs have stronger affinity toward H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB as the respective substrate. The low $K_m$ value corresponds to strong affinity toward substrates. The increased affinity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs may be attributed to their synergistic and coupling function effect [39]. The high affinity would allow useful application in the detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration [40]. This result is in agreement with the fact that a lower H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration is required for the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs when maximum activity is obtained. In addition, the double reciprocal plots of velocity against the concentration of one substrate were also acquired as shown in Fig. 10. The double-reciprocal plots revealed the characteristic parallel lines of a ping–pong mechanism.

On the basis of the elevated peroxidase-like activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs, a colorimetric method for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection was designed. Fig. 11(A) provided the direct observation of color change with the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in 10 min. It is clear that the color intensity changed from dark blue to colorless with decreasing H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration. After the plot of the absorbance at 652 nm with the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration, a good linear relationship was set up in the range from 8.5 μmol/L to 0.5 mmol/L of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration with Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs as the peroxidase-like catalyst, shown in Fig. 11(B). Obviously, the linear relationship can be used as the method of quantitative detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration. Therefore, the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs exhibited extremely strong peroxidase-like activity at low concentration and held great potential for serving as color reagent. Triple fluctuation of the baseline ($S/N \ge 3$) was determined to be the limit of detection (LOD) as shown in Fig. 11(B). When H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration was under 8.5 μmol/L, the absorbance was located in the triple fluctuation range of the baseline. Thus, the detection limit of the method was determined to be 8.5 μmol/L. Hence, our methodology provides a visual detection method without any instrumentation complicated design.

Glucose content could be readily detected by utilizing the same chromogenic substrates studied above. In principle, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is the main product of GOx oxidation of glucose. Meanwhile, it can oxidize TMB in the presence of a peroxidase. Therefore, the color change of TMB can be employed to indirectly measure glucose concentration. Fig. 12A showed the absorbance spectrum of oxidized TMB in accordance with different concentrations of glucose. The glucose-induced changes in color can be observed by naked eyes (the inset). In Fig. 12B, the absorbance of oxidized TMB at 652 nm increased as glucose concentration. Clearly, the absorbance was linearly correlated to glucose concentration from 21 μmol/L to 1.0 mmol/L. In terms of the $S/N \ge 3$, the limit of detection was determined at the glucose concentration of 21 μmol/L. In comparison with other reported inorganic nanomaterials [41–45] (Table 2), the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs is superior to those reported in some other works such as Au [44] and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> [45]. Furthermore, our catalyst exhibits a satisfactory integrative performance that has the characteristics of high sensitivity, low detection limit, and large liner range. These results showed that the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs colorimetric method is applicable to efficient determining glucose in practical applications.

**4. Conclusion**

Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs have been successfully prepared to present highly synergistic effect in peroxidase-like activity. First, the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> hollow NPs were synthesized by a solvothermal process. Subsequently, the yolk–shell Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs were acquired using Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs as cores by a one-pot coating process. The inherent synergic effect of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> NCs was demonstrated intensively. The synergistic effect was elucidated in terms of the coupling of both the catalytic elemental reactions of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs and CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs. The steady-state kinetic parameters of the peroxidase-like catalysis were figured out in comparison with the both Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs and CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs. The catalysis activity was successfully applied in the detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and glucose. Specially, this present structure endows the magnetic mobility with the ability of easy-separation after the application. These advantages would open a wide range of practical applications not only in H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and glucose detection but also in other assays and catalytic processes.
