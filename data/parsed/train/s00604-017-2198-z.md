ORIGINAL PAPER

# Visual determination of hydrogen peroxide and glucose by exploiting the peroxidase-like activity of magnetic nanoparticles functionalized with a poly(ethylene glycol) derivative

Ho Yun Shin<sup>1</sup> & Bong-Geun Kim<sup>2</sup> & Seongyeon Cho<sup>1</sup> & Jongwon Lee<sup>2</sup> & Hyon Bin Na<sup>2</sup> & Moon Il Kim<sup>1</sup>

Received: 21 November 2016 / Accepted: 18 March 2017
© Springer-Verlag Wien 2017

**Abstract** A colorimetric assay is described for the determination of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and glucose based on the peroxidase-mimicking activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> magnetic nanoparticles functionalized with a poly(ethylene glycol) derivative (PEG-MNPs). PEG ligands conjugated to a phosphine oxide and a phospholipid were synthesized and deposited on the surface of magnetic nanoparticles (MNPs). This leads to enhanced peroxidase-like activity of the MNPs by providing more monodispersed MNPs to be present in solution and by reducing self-aggregation. The PEG-MNPs were then used as enzyme mimetics that catalyze the oxidation of 2,2′-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzo-thiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) to form a green product having an absorption maximum at 417 nm which enables a visual detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. These findings are exploited in a highly sensitive and selective glucose bioassay based on the formation of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> by glucose oxidase (GOx). The calibration plot covers the glucose concentration range from 5 μM to 1 mM, and the limit of detection is as low as 3 μM. The diagnostic capability of the method is demonstrated by analyzing glucose in human blood serum. It is perceived that the PEG-MNPs presented here also may act as potent peroxidase mimetics in other methods for the determination of clinically important analytes.

**Keywords** Colorimetric biosensor . Peroxidase mimetics . Glucose detection . Monodispersed magnetic nanoparticles . Surface coating

### Introduction

Magnetic nanoparticles (MNPs) of the type Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> have attracted significant attention as potent peroxidase mimetics [1]. Compared to natural peroxidase enzymes, they exhibited highly stable catalytic activity over a broad range of pH and temperature values and over a long incubation or storage period. They were also relatively amenable to a conjugation reaction with other biological receptor molecules, such as DNA, antibodies, or enzymes, and they can be produced at a significantly low cost with a mass yield. Since the pioneering study for the first investigation of the intrinsic peroxidase activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> MNPs, a significant amount of research has been devoted to demonstrating the possible applications of MNPs [2]. For example, the peroxidase activity of MNPs has been utilized to detect various molecules, such as H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, glucose and other carbohydrates, small molecules, DNA, proteins, and cells, by employing colorimetric substrates, such as 2,2′-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzo-thiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) diammonium salt (ABTS) or 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB), or *N,N*-diethyl-*p*-phenylenediamine sulfate (DPD) [3–10]. Electrochemical detection strategies using MNPs, MNPs assembled on a carbon nanotube, or a conductive nanocomposite consisting of MNPs in the pores of mesoporous carbon have also been reported [11–13]. Furthermore, MNPs have been used for immunochemical staining, pollutant removal, and the promotion of stem cell growth by replacing the role of conventional horseradish peroxidase (HRP) [14–16].

Although the above-mentioned examples demonstrate the potential of MNPs to replace natural peroxidase, numerous limitations should still be overcome for their widespread utilization, such as low specificity and activity, monotonous catalysis based on a redox-type reaction, potential toxicity, and decreased activity after surface modifications. Among them, surface modifications of MNPs should be performed for the applications in biosensors and immunoassays to obtain the required functionalities. For example, Yan’s group modified MNPs with SiO<sub>2</sub>, 3-aminopropyl triethoxysilane (APTES), dextran, or PEG as chemical linkers to conjugate antibody molecules; however, the surface coating shields the available surface and catalytic site from the substrate, consequently resulting in lowered catalytic activity [2]. Thus, there is a significant incentive to develop effectively modified MNPs that exhibit higher peroxidase activity with abundant chemical functionalities.

Along this line, we present a colorimetric biosensor for the determination of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and glucose by using the peroxidase activity of poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) derivative-functionalized Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> magnetic nanoparticles (PEG-MNPs). Although PEGylation on MNPs with free PEG molecules has been reported to provide solubility in aqueous buffer solution [1, 2], PEG-derivative modified MNPs have not been widely investigated as peroxidase mimetics. Phosphorous-based ligands such as phosphine oxide or phospholipid were additionally derivatized with PEG to provide further steric stabilization of MNPs and active functional groups that can be used to conjugate biological receptors [17]. The PEG-MNPs yielded a significant enhancement on the catalytic activity of MNPs by preventing self-aggregation and facilitating transfer of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and ABTS substrate toward MNPs via hydrophilic interaction. The catalytic performance of the newly synthesized PEG-MNPs and their analytical application for visual detection of glucose were systematically demonstrated (e.g., specificity, linearity, and sensitivity).

### Materials and methods

#### Materials

2,2′-Azino-bis(3-ethylbenzo-thiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) diammonium salt (ABTS), 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB), β-D-glucose, arabinose, fructose, lactose, Glucose oxidase (GOx) from *Aspergillus niger*, oleic acid (90%), poly(ethylene glycol) methyl ether (Mn: 2000), phosphorus oxychloride (99%), APTES, 1-octadecene (90%), human serum, and glucose assay kit were all purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Milwaukee, WI, http://www.sigmaaldrich.com), and 35% H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was purchased from Junsei Chemical Co. (Japan, http://www.junsei.co.jp). Iron(III) chloride hexa-hydrate (97%) was purchased from Alfa Aesar (Ward Hill, MA, http://www.alfa.co.kr). Sodium oleate (97%) was purchased from Tokyo Chemical Industry (Japan, http://www.tcichemicals.com). 1,2-distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamine-N-[methoxy(polyethylene glycol)-2000] (mPEG-2000 PE) was purchased from Avanti Polar Lipids (Alabaster, AL, https://avantilipids.com). Chloroform (99.5%), tetrahydrofuran (THF, 99.5%), n-hexane (95%), and acetone were purchased from Samchun chemicals (South Korea, http://www.samchun.com). All other chemicals were of analytical grade or higher.

#### Synthesis of water-dispersible PEG-MNPs, bare MNPs, and APTES-modified MNPs

Water-dispersible MNPs were prepared by two different water-dispersing methods, ligand exchange with poly(ethylene glycol)-derivatized phosphine oxide (POPEG) ligands and encapsulation by PEG-phospholipid shell (Lipid PEG) [17, 18]. First, oleic acid-stabilized MNPs were synthesized using thermal decomposition of Fe-oleate in high-boiling point solvents using procedures developed by Hyeon and co-workers [19].

Two different approaches were applied to disperse oleic acid-stabilized MNPs in aqueous media. The first is cap exchange by POPEG, which has a PEG segment and a surface-coordinating phosphine oxide head group, for displacing the oleic acid on the nanoparticles. We denoted the MNPs as POPEG-MNPs. Briefly, 10 mg of nanoparticles were dispersed in 3 mL of THF solution containing 50 mg of POPEG. After removing THF by evacuation, the sticky mixture was incubated at 80 °C under a vacuum for 3 h. DI water was added, and the dispersed nanoparticles were purified by syringe and centrifugal filters. The other approach was the preparation by encapsulation via a hydrophobic interaction between alkyl chains in Lipid PEG and oleic acid on the nanoparticles. We also denoted the MNPs as Lipid PEG-MNPs, where 10 mg of oleic acid-stabilized MNPs were dispersed in 2 mL of chloroform. Then, it was mixed with 1 mL of chloroform solution containing 10 mg of mPEG-2000 PE. After evaporating the solvent, it was incubated at 80 °C in a vacuum for 1 h. After the addition of DI water, Lipid PEG-stabilized MNPs were purified by filtration and ultra-speed centrifugation. After synthesis, the resulting POPEG-MNPs and Lipid PEG-MNPs were re-dispersed in water to create a stock solution with a total Fe concentration of 2.67 mg·mL<sup>−1</sup> and 1.91 mg·mL<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, and stored at 4 °C for use.

Bare MNPs with the hydroxyl group were synthesized using the reported procedure [20]. Briefly, a 1 M sodium hydroxide solution was added to a 0.25 M Fe<sup>3+</sup> and Fe<sup>2+</sup> aqueous salt solutions (2:1 M ratio) while stirring at 80 °C for 40 min (pH 10). The MNPs obtained in this manner were washed several times with water and ethanol and dried at 70 °C under a vacuum. MNPs modified with APTES (APTES-MNPs) were synthesized using the procedures reported by Gao et al. [21]. The MNPs were dispersed in water to make a 10 mg·mL<sup>−1</sup> stock solution and stored at 4 °C until use.

The synthesized MNPs were analyzed by transmission electron microscopic (TEM) images on a Jeol EM-2010 microscope (Jeol Co.). The hydrodynamic diameters (HDs) of MNPs were determined by light-scattering analysis using a Zetasizer Nano-ZS (Malvern Co.). Iron concentrations in MNPs were also measured by inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP-AES) (model: Polyscan 60E, Thermo Jarrell).

#### H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection using PEG-MNPs (POPEG- & Lipid PEG-MNPs), bare MNPs, and APTES-MNPs

To investigate the peroxidase-like activity of PEG-MNPs, the catalytic oxidation of ABTS in the presence of pre-determined concentrations of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was performed in a 96-well plate as follows: A solution containing 140 μL of sodium acetate buffer (0.2 M, pH 4.0), 20 μL of 1 mg·mL<sup>−1</sup> PEG-MNPs, 20 μL of 100 mM H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, and 20 μL of 60 mM ABTS was added into a well and incubated at room temperature (RT) for 5 min. After the reaction, the MNPs were separated from the mixtures in each well by the application of a strong external magnetic field for 30 s. The supernatant was subsequently transferred to a well of new 96-well plate and used to capture color images. Spectrophotometric measurements were conducted in a scanning mode or at 417 nm using a microplate reader (Synergy H1, BioTek, VT).

The effects of reaction buffer pH on the activity of PEG-MNPs were investigated following the same procedures but using 0.2 M acetate buffer solutions prepared from pH 2 to 10. The effects of incubation temperature on the activity of POPEG-MNPs and Lipid PEG-MNPs were also investigated following the same procedures but incubated in different temperatures from 4 to 60 °C. The relative activity (%) was calculated by using the ratio of the residual activity to the original activity before the incubation of each sample.

#### Kinetic analysis

Steady-state kinetic assays were carried out at RT in a 1.5 mL cuvette. For the kinetic assay of TMB, a solution was prepared containing 0.02 mg·mL<sup>−1</sup> of POPEG-MNPs or Lipid PEG-MNPs and 1 M H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in 1 mL of sodium acetate buffer (0.2 M, pH 4.0), and TMB concentration varied. For the kinetic assay of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, TMB (final 800 μM) was added in 1 mL of reaction buffer with varied concentrations of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. After the substrates were mixed, color changes were observed immediately. All reactions were monitored using a kinetic mode at 652 nm. The kinetic parameters were calculated based on the equation $\nu = V_{max} \times [S]/(K_m + [S])$, where $\nu$ is the initial velocity, $V_{max}$ is the maximal velocity, $[S]$ is the concentration of substrate, and $K_m$ is the Michaelis constant. The catalytic constant $k_{cat}$ was derived from $k_{cat} = V_{max}/[E]$, where $[E]$ is the iron concentration measured by ICP-AES.

#### Determination of glucose using PEG-MNPs (POPEG- & Lipid PEG-MNPs) and GOx

The detection of glucose concentration was performed in a 96-well plate as follows: 24 μL of 10 mg·mL<sup>−1</sup> GOx and 120 μL of glucose at different concentrations (1 μM – 5 mM) were first incubated in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) (10 mM, pH 7.0) at 37 °C for 20 min. Then, 24 μL of 60 mM ABTS, 24 μL of 10 mg·mL<sup>−1</sup> PEG-MNPs, and 48 μL of sodium acetate buffer (0.5 M, pH 4.0) were additionally added into each well and incubated at RT for 10 min. After the reaction, the PEG-MNPs were separated and the supernatant was further analyzed by measuring the absorbance (417 nm), as described above. Other carbohydrates such as arabinose, fructose, and lactose were used instead of glucose for selectivity test.

#### Glucose detection in human serum

Initial glucose concentration of human serum was first determined by glucose assay kit (Sigma-Aldrich) and then predetermined amount of glucose was further added into human serum to make spiked samples, which represent normal, boundary, and high level of blood glucose. Finally, glucose concentration of each spiked sample (120 μL, 10-fold dilution) was determined by the same procedures described above.

### Results and discussion

#### Construction of PEG-MNPs for colorimetric quantification of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and glucose

As most of the peroxidase activity of MNPs stems from surface atoms, a large surface area available for the catalytic event is necessary for obtaining high catalytic activity. Thus, we designed MNPs having PEG-derivatives that can be monodispersed in aqueous buffer without self-aggregation, thereby providing highly enhanced peroxidase activity. The highly active MNPs can also be applied to detect glucose by coupling with GOx catalysis (Fig. 1).

To this goal, we prepared two different PEG-derivatized ligands, POPEG and Lipid PEG, and modified the surface of MNPs with each ligand. POPEG and Lipid PEG modified MNPs were synthesized via extremely simple strategies of cap exchange and encapsulation by hydrophobic interaction, respectively, compared to the conventional surface modification methods generally achieved by passivating the MNPs with silica or polymer shells [22]. TEM analysis clearly reveals the monodispersed state of PEG-MNPs, whereas the conventionally synthesized MNPs are prone to aggregation (Fig. 2). In order to study the stability of aqueous dispersion of MNPs, the hydrodynamic diameters (HDs) of MNPs were measured by dynamic light scattering (DLS) (Fig. S1). The average HDs of POPEG-MNPs and Lipid PEG-MNPs were 29.6 nm and 34.7 nm, respectively, where the increase of size was resulted from PEGylated shells by POPEG and Lipid PEG. On the other hand, bare MNPs synthesized by co-precipitation had much larger average HD (72.7 nm) than both PEG-MNPs. In particular, bare MNPs had two distributions of HD resulted from interparticular aggregations, demonstrating that bare MNPs were not stable in aqueous media.

#### Investigation for peroxidase-like activity of PEG-MNPs

The peroxidase activities of PEG-MNPs, bare MNPs, and APTES-MNPs were examined by performing the representative peroxidase-catalyzed ABTS oxidation reactions in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, and the responses were monitored using absorption spectroscopy (Fig. 3). The results of this experiment show that the PEG-MNPs have highly enhanced ABTS oxidation rates and, consequently, much more intense colorimetric responses take place in contrast to those systems containing bare MNPs or APTES-MNPs. There is no obvious difference in colorimetric responses between POPEG-MNPs and Lipid PEG-MNPs, confirming their similar peroxidase activity to oxidize ABTS. Observations made in study on the effects of pH and temperature on the catalytic activity of POPEG-MNPs and Lipid PEG-MNPs, showed that pH 4.0 and 45 °C were ideal assay conditions (Fig. S2 and S3). Although the incubation at 45 °C resulted in maximal peroxidase activity, the incubation at RT was carried out rather than 45 °C because of the practical convenience and sufficiently acceptable catalytic performance of MNPs at RT (about 75% of maximum activity). PEG-MNPs were also proved to be stable over a broad range of pH from 2 to 9 and temperature from 4 to 90 °C, whereas HRP lost most of its activity at an extreme pH or temperature conditions (Fig. S4), demonstrating their practical applicability.

To elucidate further the peroxidase activity of both POPEG- and Lipid PEG-MNPs, steady-state kinetic constants were determined and compared with those of bare MNPs (Fig. S5, Table S1). Both the apparent $K_m$ values of the POPEG- and Lipid PEG-MNPs for TMB were about 7–8% higher than that of bare MNPs, indicating the mass transfer limit after the modification of PEG-ligands is not significantly increased. Moreover, the $K_m$ values of the POPEG- and Lipid PEG-MNPs for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were even significantly lower than that of bare MNPs and determined to be only ~20% of the value for bare MNPs. This indicates that the modification of PEG-derivatized ligands on the surface of MNPs resulted in a significantly enhanced affinity toward H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, possibly due to the excellent dispersion of both POPEG- and Lipid PEG-MNPs in aqueous buffer and hydrophilic PEG segments of the ligands that facilitate substrate transfer [4]. Furthermore, $k_{cat}$ values of POPEG- and Lipid PEG-MNPs for both TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were found to be generally higher up to 2-fold, indicating that PEG-MNPs leads to an enhanced activity compared with that of bare MNPs, which are prone to aggregation. Overall, both POPEG- and Lipid PEG-MNPs have an approximately 6-fold higher catalytic efficiency ($k_{cat}/K_m$) for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> compared to that of bare MNPs.

#### Analytical capabilities of PEG-MNPs for the detection of glucose

The feasibility of the method was demonstrated by coupling GOx with either POPEG- or Lipid PEG-MNPs. In the presence of target glucose, GOx catalyzed the oxidation of glucose to generate H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which should activate PEG-MNPs to convert selected substrates into colored product. Through the typical colorimetric reaction, glucose was successfully detected by the generation of a visibly or spectroscopically detected color change (Figs. 4). On the other hand, in either case, no colorimetric signal or absorbance peak was observed in the negative control samples, where other carbohydrates (arabinose, fructose, and lactose) similar to the target glucose are used even at 10-fold higher concentrations. This result clearly demonstrates that the present PEG-MNP-based biosensor shows excellent specificity to the corresponding glucose. From an analysis of dose-response curves using both POPEG- and Lipid PEG-MNP systems, the limit of detection (LOD) for glucose was determined to be as low as 3 μM in the linear range from 5 to 1000 μM (Fig. 5). Although the previous reports using GOx/HRP bienzyme system showed a lower detection limit down to 0.3 μM, the LOD and linear range values of our system are among the best results describing the various peroxidase mimics-based colorimetric detection of glucose (Table 1) [2, 23–32]. Considering the excellent selectivity and sensitivity along with the merits of MNPs as peroxidase mimetics, it is evident that our method can serve as a promising alternative to existing methods for the detection of glucose.

#### Diagnosis of hyperglycemia using clinical blood samples

Finally, we evaluated the diagnostic capability of the selected PEG-MNP system by using real human blood samples that contained representative levels of glucose corresponding to the normal, boundary, and high stage of hyperglycemia (normal; ≤ 5.6 mM, boundary; 5.6 ~ 7 mM, and high; >7 mM) [33]. Original amount of glucose in serum sample was first determined by glucose assay kit (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) and then predetermined amount of glucose was further added to make the representative levels. As a result, the serum glucose levels were quantified with excellent precisions yielding CVs in a range of 4.3–7% and recovery rates of 97.9–103.8% (Table 2), verifying the excellent reproducibility and reliability of the method. These results show that the POPEG-MNP-based assay system should serve as a promising analytical platform to diagnose high level of glucose in clinical settings.

### Conclusion

We herein developed an efficient colorimetric biosensor for detecting and determining the concentrations of glucose based on the enhanced peroxidase activity of POPEG- or Lipid PEG-modified MNPs. The results of the investigations demonstrate that the PEG-MNP-based system incorporating GOx catalysis has high selectivities and sensitivities for the detection of the corresponding glucose. As the current system enabled the rapid visual detection of target molecules with the peroxidase mimicking activity of PEG-MNPs, it should find practical applications in facility-limited or point-of-care testing (POCT) environments without the addition of natural peroxidase enzyme. Because the MNPs can rival natural peroxidase enzymes due to their high stability, robustness, and low cost by facile scale-up, the analytical platform based on effectively modified MNPs can be further extended to many potential applications in biosensors in the future.
