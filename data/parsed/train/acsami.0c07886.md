# An Ultrasmall RuO<sub>2</sub> Nanozyme Exhibiting Multienzyme-like Activity for the Prevention of Acute Kidney Injury

Zhou Liu, Lina Xie, Kangqiang Qiu, Xinxing Liao, Thomas W. Rees, Zizhuo Zhao,* Liangnian Ji, and Hui Chao*

Cite This: https://dx.doi.org/10.1021/acsami.0c07886

**ABSTRACT:** Oxidative stress induced by reactive oxygen species (ROS) is one of the major pathological mechanisms of acute kidney injury (AKI). Inorganic nanomaterial-mediated antioxidant therapy is considered a promising method for the prevention of AKI; however, currently available antioxidants for AKI exhibit limited clinical efficacy due to the glomerular filtration threshold (~6 nm). To address this issue, we developed ultrasmall RuO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles (RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs) (average size ≈ 2 nm). The NPs show excellent antioxidant activity and low biological toxicity. In addition, they can pass through the glomerulus to be excreted. These properties in combination make the ultrasmall RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs promising as a nanozyme for the prevention of AKI. The NP catalytic properties mimic the activity of catalase, peroxidase, superoxide dismutase, and glutathione peroxidase. The nanozyme can be efficiently and rapidly absorbed by human embryonic kidney cells while significantly reducing ROS-induced apoptosis by eliminating excess ROS. After intravenous injection, the ultrasmall RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs significantly inhibit the development of AKI in mice. In vivo toxicity experiments demonstrate the biosafety of the NPs after long-term preventing. The multienzyme-like activity and biocompatibility of the ultrasmall RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs makes them of great interest for applications in the fields of biomedicine and biocatalysis.

**KEYWORDS:** RuO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles, multienzyme-like activity, ultrasmall, acute kidney injury, in vivo clearance

## 1. INTRODUCTION

Reactive oxygen species (ROS), such as singlet oxygen (<sup>1</sup>O<sub>2</sub>), superoxide anions (O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup>), hydroxyl radicals (•OH), and hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>), are the metabolites of normal cells in living organisms.<sup>1−3</sup> Moderate levels of ROS are important for maintaining cellular signal transduction and other intracellular functions.<sup>4−6</sup> Excess ROS may, however, induce oxidative stress in normal cells, which can, in turn, trigger a series of harmful processes in normal cells, including cell death and inflammation.<sup>7,8</sup> Acute kidney injury (AKI) is a clinical syndrome with a high lethality that can result from one or more etiologies. It is also one of the more common disorders considered “critical illnesses”.<sup>9,10</sup> AKI usually manifests as kidney damage leading to renal insufficiency, which results in the buildup of metabolic waste and perturbation of water, electrolyte, and acid−base homeostasis.<sup>11</sup> Numerous studies have confirmed that oxidative stress, apoptosis, and inflammatory responses play important roles in the pathogenesis of AKI. Oxidative stress contributes toward AKI and typically involves the overproduction of ROS.<sup>12−15</sup> Patients with AKI have excessive oxidative stress due to increased oxide activity and decreased antioxidant activity in their serum, which subsequently leads to renal dysfunction.<sup>16</sup>

Eliminating excess ROS can protect tissues from oxidative stress and effectively scavenging excess ROS can, therefore, have a significant therapeutic effect on AKI.<sup>17,18</sup> Under pathological conditions, natural enzymes, such as peroxidase [POD], superoxide dismutase [SOD], catalase [CAT], and glutathione peroxidase [GPx]), which help maintain cellular redox balance, are sensitive to the physiological environment and prone to inactivation.<sup>19−21</sup> NPs with enzyme-like activities have recently become the focus of research to solve this problem, because of their excellent physiochemical properties, biological safety, biostability, and ROS scavenging ability.<sup>22−25</sup> A large number of enzyme-like nanomaterials, including metal oxides,<sup>26−29</sup> metal nanomaterials,<sup>30,31</sup> and polymers,<sup>32</sup> have been used innovatively to treat diseases caused by ROS. However, because of the glomerular filtration threshold (~6 nm),<sup>33−35</sup> most large-sized nanozymes are poorly absorbed by the kidneys, leading to potential organ toxicity.<sup>36−38</sup> This limits the use of large-sized nanozymes for the treatment of AKI diseases. Ultrasmall NPs, whose hydration diameter is lower than the kidney filtration threshold, can be rapidly excreted through the glomeruli.<sup>39−42</sup> Ultrasmall nanozymes that have excellent antioxidant activity and low biological toxicity and can be excreted by the kidney are highly desirable as potential therapeutic agents for the prevention of AKI. Their development, however, remains a challenge.

We herein report the synthesis of an ultrasmall **RuO<sub>2</sub>** nanozyme with good biocompatibility and enzyme-like activity for the prevention of AKI. As illustrated in Scheme 1, the ultrasmall **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** can be used as an antioxidant because of their multienzyme-like (CAT, GPx, and SOD) properties. In vitro results demonstrate that the ultrasmall NPs have a remarkable cytoprotective effect on oxidative stress-induced nephrotoxicity. An AKI mouse model was used to elucidate the in vivo therapeutic properties of the ultrasmall **RuO<sub>2</sub>** nanozyme. Because of their ultrasmall size, effective renal accumulation, renal clearance, and long-term biosafety of the NPs were demonstrated in vivo. The excellent properties of these ultrasmall **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** highlight their promise as a multienzyme-like nanozyme for the prevention of AKI.

## 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS

Ruthenium(III) chloride trihydrate (RuCl<sub>3</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O), salicylic acid (SA), bovine serum albumin (BSA), FeSO<sub>4</sub>, and peroxy orange 1 (PO) were purchased from Aladdin. Chitosan (CS), thiazolyl blue tetrazolium bromide (MTT), and hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) were obtained from Energy Chemical. Glycerinum was purchased from Macklin. Acetic acid, 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB), 2′,7′-dichlorofluorescin diacetate (DCFH-DA), ethanol, 5,5-dimethyl-1-pyrroline-N-oxide (DMPO), and an Annexin V-FITC apoptosis detection kit were obtained from Sigma-Aldrich. Calcein-AM and propidium iodide (PI) were obtained from Thermo Fisher Scientific. Antigamma H2A.X antibodies were acquired from Abcam Co. Ltd. A mitochondrial membrane potential assay kit was purchased from Beyotime Biotechnology. Malondialdehyde (MDA) assay, creatine kinase assay, urea assay, and creatinine (Cr) assay kits were obtained from Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute. Superoxide dismutase (SOD) assay and glutathione peroxidase (GSH-PX) assay kits were purchased from Suzhou Comin Biotechnology.

### 2.1. Characterization.
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was obtained on a JEOL JEM-ARM200P microscope. The elemental concentration of Ru was measured by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS, Thermo Fisher Scientific iCAP RQ). A Malvern Zetasizer Nanoseries was applied to record the hydrodynamic particle size. A LSM 810 NLO, (Carl Zeiss AG) confocal laser scanning microscope was used for cell imaging. UV−vis absorption spectroscopy was determined on a RF-5301 spectrometer. The imaging of H&E-stained sections was performed on a positive fluorescence microscope (Carl Zeiss AG, AX10). A Milli-Q system by Millipore was used to obtain ultrapure water. A Bruker ELEXSYS E580 X-band pulse electron paramagnetic resonance spectrometer was utilized to detect free radicals. Cytotoxicity was determined by an MTT assay using a light absorption microplate reader (Molecular Devices, Spectra Max). Quantitative cell analysis was obtained by flow cytometer (FACS Canto II, BD Biosciences).

### 2.2. Synthesis of RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs.
**RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** were synthesized by the solvothermal method. Three milligrams per milliliter CS was dissolved in 3% acetic acid. RuCl<sub>3</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O (25 mg) was added to the CS solution (3 mg/mL, 20 mL), and stirred for 30 min before adding to a round-bottomed flask. The mixed solution was then heated to 120 °C for 6 h. A dark green solution was obtained and purified by dialysis with distilled water at room temperature for 48 h (MWCO: 3500).

### 2.3. Antioxidative Enzyme-like Properties of RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs.
The CAT-like activity of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was measured by recording the changes in the absorption peak of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at 240 nm on a UV−vis spectrophotometer. In the assay, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (20 μg/mL) was added to the solution of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 mM), and the elimination rate (decrease of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) was measured over 5 min at room temperature.

The POD-like activity of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was investigated by using TMB as the substrate. TMB (1 mM), H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 mM), and **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (20 μg/mL) were combined at room temperature for 5 min in an acetate buffer solution. The absorbance of TMB (650 nm) was recorded after 30 min of reaction time.

The ability of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** to quench the superoxide anion (O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup>) was determined by measuring the formation of formazan using a SOD assay kit. O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> was produced through the oxidation of xanthine by xanthine oxidase. O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> reacts with azetidine to produce the water soluble dye formazan. The formazan concentration was determined by measuring absorbance at 560 nm.

The GSH-Px-like activity of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was evaluated using a GSH-Px assay kit. GSH-Px catalyzes the oxidation of glutathione (GSH) by hydrogen peroxide which produces oxidized glutathione (GSSG). Glutathione reductase (GR) catalyzes the reaction of GSSG with NADPH, regenerating GSH and oxidizing NADPH to NADP+. NADPH has a characteristic absorption peak at 340 nm, whereas NADP+ does not. GSH-Px activity was therefore calculated by recording the rate of decrease in light absorption at 340 nm.

### 2.4. Steady-State Kinetic Assays.
The catalytic parameters were determined by converting the absorbance to the concentration of substrate and then the Michaelis−Menten equation was fitted,<sup>43</sup> which described the relationship between the conversion rate of the enzyme (**RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**) to the substrate and the concentration of substrate. The formula was shown in the following:

$$V = \frac{V_{max}[S]}{K_m + [S]}$$

In this formula, $V$ represented the conversion rate, $V_{max}$ indicated the maximum conversion rate, $K_m$ was the Michaelis constant, and $[S]$ was the concentration of substrate.

### 2.5. Hydroxyl Radical (•OH) Scavenging Activity of the RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs.
EPR spectroscopy was used to evaluate the ability of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** to scavenge (•OH). The trapping agent DMPO was utilized to monitor the reaction of the NPs with •OH. DMPO/•OH yields a characteristic EPR signal with a peak ratio of 1:2:2:1. The •OH-scavenging ability of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was recorded by incubating H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 mM), in the presence of FeSO<sub>4</sub> (10 μM), and following by the addition of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (20 μg/mL) and DMPO (50 mM). The EPR signal was measured after 5 min of reaction.

The •OH-scavenging activity of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was also assessed by fluorescence spectroscopy. •OH was produced by the reaction between FeSO<sub>4</sub> (1 mM) and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (2 mM) for 5 min. The •OH scavenging activity of the NPs was determined by recording the characteristic absorbance at 510 nm, which was generated by the reaction of SA (1.5 mM) with •OH.

### 2.6. Cell Culture.
All cells were cultured in a humidified atmosphere at 37 °C, 5% CO<sub>2</sub>, and 95% air. Human embryonic kidney cells (HEK 293) were incubated in DMEM with 10% fetal calf serum and 1% penicillin streptomycin.

### 2.7. Cytotoxity Assay.
The cell viability was calculated by MTT assay. HEK293 cells were seeded into a 96-well plate and grown overnight. H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, or **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**+H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>: 500 μM, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**: 20 μg/mL) were added to the wells, and the cells then cultured for 24 h. At same time, 1 μM Calcein-AM and 2 μM propidium iodide (PI) were also applied to investigate the viability of cells (Calcein AM for live cells; PI for dead cells). The fluorescence images were taken on an inverted fluorescence microscope.

### 2.8. H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> Production in HEK293 Cells.
HEK293 cells seeded in a 96-well plate were incubated with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, or **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**+H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>: 500 μM, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**: 20 μg/mL). After 24 h of incubation, the cells were washed with PBS for three times before 2 μM peroxy orange I was added into the cells, which then were cultured for a further 30 min. After a further wash by PBS, the fluorescence intensity was recorded using a multiplate reader ($\lambda_{ex}$ = 548 nm, $\lambda_{em}$ = 610 nm).

### 2.9. Detection of Intracellular ROS.
Intracellular ROS measurement was assayed by 2′,7′-dichlorofluorescin diacetate (DCFH-DA) staining. HEK293 cells were incubated in confocal microscope dishes for 24 h, and the cell density was 1 × 10<sup>5</sup> cells/well. After incubation, the cells were incubated with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, or **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**+H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>: 500 μM, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**: 20 μg/mL) for a further 24 h. The cells were then washed three times with PBS and cultured with DCFH-DA (5 μM) at 37 °C for a further 30 min. The intracellular ROS levels were determined by confocal laser scanning microscopy (Carl Zeiss AG) ($\lambda_{ex}$ = 480 nm, $\lambda_{em}$ = 525 nm).

### 2.10. Mitochondrial Membrane Potential (MMP) Assay.
Mitochondrial membrane potential perturbation was measured with a JC-1 mitochondrial membrane potential assay kit. HEK293 cells were incubated in a 6-well plate for 24 h, the cells density was 2 × 10<sup>5</sup> cells/well and cultured with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, or **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**+H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>: 500 μM, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**: 20 μg/mL) for a further 24 h. And then, the cells were trypsinized and collected, then stained with JC-1 following the manufacture’s protocol. The cells obtained were then investigated by flow cytometry (BD FACS Canto II).

### 2.11. Detection of Cell Apoptosis.
The apoptosis of HEK293 cells was assessed by the Annexin V Apoptosis Detection Kit (Sigma Aldrich). HEK293 cells were incubated in a 6-well plate for 24 h, the cells density was 2 × 10<sup>5</sup> cells/well and cultured with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, or **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**+H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>: 500 μM, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**: 20 μg/mL) After treatment, the cells were collected and then washed three times with ice-cold PBS before staining with Annexin V and PI. The collected cells were then assessed by flow cytometry.

### 2.12. DNA Double-Strand Breaks.
HEK293 cells were incubated in confocal microscope dishes for 24 h, the cells density was 1 × 10<sup>5</sup> cells/well. After incubation, the cells were incubated with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, or **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**+H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>: 500 μM, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**: 20 μg/mL) for a further 24 h. The cells were washed three times with PBS and fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 15 min, and permeabilized in 0.2% Triton-100 for 10 min. After blocked (1% bovine serum albumin) for 1 h, anti-$\gamma$H2AX antibodies were incubated with fixed cells overnight at 4 °C. Cells were washed by PBS and then cultured with secondary antibodies for 1 h at room temperature. All cells were counterstained with Hoechst. Immunofluorescence was recorded by confocal laser scanning microscopy.

### 2.13. Establishment of an Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) Model.
All female, ICR mice (4−6 weeks old, Beijing Vital River Laboratory Technology Co., Ltd.) were randomly divided into five groups: (i) injected with PBS only; (ii) injected with **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** only; (iii) injected with glycerol only; (iv) injected with glycerol and PBS; and (v) injected with glycerol and **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**. Groups iii, iv, and v were then provided with food but no water for 12 h. After the water restriction period ended, group iv was intravenously injected with PBS and group v with **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (5 mg/kg). Two hours postinjection, both hind limbs of groups iii, iv, and v received intramuscular injection of 50% glycerol (8 mL/kg) to trigger AKI, and all mice were then free to obtain food and water. The AKI model was established successfully and used for the following studies.

### 2.14. Renal Clearance Analysis.
**RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (5 mg/kg) were intravenously injected into healthy mice. Subsequently, urine and feces were collected at various time (4, 8, and 12 h and 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, and 7 days). The obtained samples were dissolved in 80% nitric acid and the amount of Ru was analyzed by ICP-MS. The urine samples from 0, 4, 12, and 24 h time points were also examined by TEM imaging to further evaluate the renal clearance of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**.

### 2.15. Biodistribution Analysis.
**RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (5 mg/kg) were intravenously injected into healthy mice. The mice were sacrificed at various time (2, 4, 8, 12, 24, 48, and 72 h and 7 days) postinjection. The organs were collected and weighed for the quantitative analysis of the biodistribution of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**. The obtained organs were dissolved in 80% nitric acid, and Ru accumulation in the organs was evaluated by ICP-MS.

### 2.16. Therapeutic Effect in AKI Mice.
To analyze the therapeutic effect of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, we divided the mice into five treatment groups: (i) healthy mice treated with PBS; (ii) healthy mice treated with **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (5 mg/kg); (iii) AKI mice without further treatment; (iv) AKI mice treated with PBS; (v) AKI mice treated with **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (5 mg/kg). After treatment, kidney function and body weight were monitored over time.

### 2.17. Evaluation of the Effect of Treatment on AKI Status.
Biomarkers of kidney function in AKI mice were evaluated by hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining in order to investigate the prevention effect of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** on AKI. All the groups were euthanized at different time’s postinjection, (1, 2, 3, and 7 days). Their blood samples were collected and centrifuged at 1500 rpm for 20 min at 4 °C to obtain plasma. The levels of blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatine (CK), malondialdehyde (MDA), and creatinine (CRE) were determined using an ELSA Kit.

The glomeruli, collecting ducts, tubules, and urethrae from the second group of mice intravenously injected with **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** were also collected and stained with H&E to evaluate the renal toxicity of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**.

## 3. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### 3.1. Characterization of the RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs.
The **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** were prepared via our previously reported method,<sup>44</sup> which is both convenient and reproducible. Analysis of the as-obtained **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was performed by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (Figure S1A, B) which revealed that the mean diameter of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was ~2 nm, and the hydrodynamic size of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was about 5.4 nm (Figure S1C). And the Brunauer−Emmett−Teller surface area was calculated to be 14.46 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S1D). As shown in Figure S1A inset, the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** in PBS exhibit a uniform green color, demonstrating the excellent dispersion of the NPs.

### 3.2. Multienzyme-like Activity of the RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs.
The multienzyme-like activity of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was evaluated. The CAT-like activity of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was investigated by recording the changes in absorbance of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. As shown in Figure 1A, the CAT-like activity of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** showed good results within 5 min. CAT can catalyze the decomposition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> into H<sub>2</sub>O and O<sub>2</sub>, thereby protecting cells and tissues from oxidative damage.<sup>45</sup> A rapid decomposition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was observed within 5 min. The decomposition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> can also be observed qualitatively as the formation of bubbles (Figure S1E). A steady-state kinetic analysis was applied to measure the CAT-like activity of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (Figure S2). And the Michaelis−Menten equation was introduced to obtain typical curve within a suitable concentration range of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. This reaction indicates typical Michaelis−Menten kinetic. $K_m$ represents the affinity of the enzyme for the substrate, and a lower $K_m$ value indicates higher affinity.<sup>43</sup> The $K_m$ value of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrate was 3.59 mM, and the $V_{max}$ value was 2.29 mM/min. Overall, these results illustrate that **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** act as an efficient CAT-like agent.

Using 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) as the chromogenic substrate, the peroxidase (POD)-like activity of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** was detected (Figure 1B). TMB can acts as a hydrogen donor for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> reduction, and the resultant diamine has a characteristic absorption peak at 650 nm. After 10 min reaction, the experimental group (TMB + **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) exhibited a clear absorption characteristic peak at 650 nm, and the solution turned blue. As shown in Figure S3, a steady-state kinetic analysis, with TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrates, was further used to evaluate the POD-like activity of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**. The $K_m$ value of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** with TMB as the substrate was 0.0990 mM, and the $V_{max}$ value was $12.9 \times 10^{−6}$ M/min. The $K_m$ value of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrate was 0.146 mM, and the $V_{max}$ value was $12.4 \times 10^{−6}$ M/min, indicating that **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** exhibited excellent POD-like catalytic activity.

SOD-like enzymes can be used to treat a variety of oxidative stress-related diseases.<sup>46,47</sup> Expect for their CAT and POD-like activity, the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** also showed SOD-like property. The SOD-like ability of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** to eliminate O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> was measured using formazan, produced by the reaction of O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> and nitrogen blue tetrazole. The specific absorption of formazan was measured at 560 nm. At different concentrations of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, the absorption of formazan was obviously reduced, indicating the SOD-like activity of the NPs (Figure 1C).

GSH-Px catalyzes the oxidation of GSH to GSSG to promote the elimination of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<sup>48</sup> GSH react with dinitrobenzoic acid to form the 5-thio-dinitrobenzoic acid anion with a characteristic absorption at 412 nm. The concentration of the anion was determined by measuring the absorbance at 412 nm to evaluate the GSH-Px-like activity of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, and therefore indirectly estimate GSH concentration. As shown in Figure 1D, an increased GSH-Px-like activity was observed with increased concentration. By changing the various concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and GSH, steady-state kinetic measurements were used to confirm the enzyme catalytic mechanism. This reaction fitted well with the typical Michaelis−Menten kinetic (Figure S4), and the $K_m$ value of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** with GSH as the substrate was 0.870 mM, and the $V_{max}$ value was 65.1 μM/min. The $K_m$ value of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrate was 17.2 μM, and the $V_{max}$ value was 47.0 μM/min. These results demonstrated good GSH-Px-like activity and efficiency of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**.

The ability of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** to scavenge •OH was determined by electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy and UV−visible absorption spectroscopy.<sup>49</sup> The signal intensity of the trapping agent 5,5-dimethyl-1-pyrroline-N-oxide (DMPO)/•OH was diminished in the presence of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (Figure S5A). In addition, The •OH-scavenging activity<sup>45</sup> was assessed by measuring the absorbance of 2,3-dihydroxybenzoic acid (the product of reaction between salicylic acid (SA) and •OH) at 510 nm. As shown in Figure S5B, in the presence of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, the absorption peak at 510 nm was significantly reduced, clearly demonstrating the •OH-scavenging activity. Overall, these results show that not only do **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** have multienzyme-like activity but they can also effectively scavenge •OH. The above results display the excellent potential of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** as therapeutic agents that can restrain oxidative stress and reduce ROS-induced damage.

### 3.3. Preventing Cell Death by Efficient ROS Scavenging.
H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> reacts to generate free radicals responsible for the cause of cell damage.<sup>50</sup> The ability of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** to reduce the levels of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and ROS production was therefore investigated in HEK293 cells. As shown in Figure 2A, peroxy orange I (PO) was utilized as a H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-sensing fluorescent probe to evaluate the behavior of the NPs to reduce H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. HEK293 cells treated with **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** showed lower intracellular H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> production than H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-treated cells. The cell viability of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-treated and **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**/H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-treated cells was subsequently studied. As shown in Figure 2B, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** treatment can significantly improve the cell viability caused by excessive ROS generated by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The live−dead cell staining result further verified the admirable biological safety and prophylactive properties of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (Figure 2C).

DCFH-DA was used to detect intracellular ROS to further verify the ability of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** to limit H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> induced ROS production in HEK293 cells. As shown in Figure 3, it demonstrate that H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> treatment of HEK293 cells stimulates ROS production, while in **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**/H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-treated cells reduced ROS production was observed. These results indicate that **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** can effectively reduce the generation of free radicals mediated by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Moreover, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** are biosafe for in vitro and in vivo applications.

### 3.4. Inhibition of Apoptosis by RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs in Cells under Oxidative Stress.
Excessive ROS can induce cell apoptosis. Because renal tubules are vulnerable to oxidative stress, HEK293 cells were used to evaluate the ability of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** to protect against excessive ROS. An Annexin V/PI apoptosis asssay was conducted to investigate HEK293 cell death in cells treated with **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Figure S6). Apoptotic cells accounted for 6.04% in the control group. In the presence of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** alone, 14.6% of cells underwent apoptosis, whereas exposure to 500 μM H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> induced apoptosis in 42.9% of cells. In the group treated with both H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, the rate of apoptosis was significantly reduced (19.1%). This experiment provides convincing evidence that **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** can protect cells against oxidative stress.

### 3.5. Prevention of Mitochondrial Injury and DNA Damage by RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs.
Excessive ROS is known to cause mitochondrial membrane potential (MMP) loss, which is an important sign of mitochondrial dysfunction.<sup>51</sup> Flow cytometry was used to assess HEK293 cells stained with JC-1 to characterize whether **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** protect against oxidative stress induced by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The results show that the MMP of the untreated control cells was normal. After addition of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, no significant change in MMP was observed. The MMP decreased after exposure to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which is regarded as an early and irreversible step of apoptosis, whereas cells treated with both H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** gave very similar results to cells treated with the NPs alone (Figure 4). These results indicate that **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** inhibit H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-induced MMP dissipation.

![Figure 4](https://example.com/figure4.png)
**Figure 4. Flow cytometry of JC-1-stained HEK293 cells untreated (control) or treated with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (500 μM), RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs (20 μg/mL), or RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs (20 μg/mL) and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (500 μM).**

Excessive production of intracellular ROS can induce DNA damage, and oxidative byproducts of DNA damage are important biomarkers of oxidative stress. DNA damage induced by ROS includes both nucleotide modifications and DNA strand breaks. $\gamma$-H2AX is an indicator of DNA double-strand breaks, and the immunofluorescence of $\gamma$-H2AX can be an important marker of DNA damage.<sup>52</sup> As shown in Figure 5, intense $\gamma$-H2AX fluorescence was observed in immunostained HEK293 cells treated with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The addition of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** significantly decreased the fluorescence intensity of $\gamma$-H2AX, indicating that the NPs effectively reduce oxidative damage to DNA. The above results clearly show that **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** can effectively reduce the production of ROS and protect DNA from the damage of oxidative stress.

### 3.6. Renal Clearance, Pharmacokinetics, and Biodistribution of RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs.
Encouraged by the above results in vitro and the 6 nm size threshold for efficient renal filtration,<sup>30</sup> the pharmacokinetics, biodistribution, and clearance of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (~2 nm) was evaluated in AKI mice. First, 5 mg/kg **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** were intravenously injected into normal mice. After 2 h postinjection, the AKI was induced by intramuscular injection of 50% glycerol into dehydrated mice. Second, as shown in Figure 6A, the blood circulation half-life of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** in AKI mice was determined to be 1.58 h by ICP-MS. This indicates that the ultrasmall **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** have a relatively short blood circulation time. The biodistribution and clearance of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** in AKI mice is shown in Figure 6B and Figure S7. **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** gradually accumulated in kidneys within 24 h of intravenous injection, which may be due to the ultrasmall hydrodynamic diameter of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**. High non-specific accumulation in the lung, spleen, and liver was also detected, however, which could be caused by the serum protein adsorption of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** in biological fluid in vivo.<sup>53−55</sup> The concentration of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** in the organs of the mice was assessed for 24 h after intravenous injection and found to decrease gradually. Most of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** were cleared via the renal system and were excreted by liver metabolization within 7 days. To further prove the efficient renal clearance of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, we collected and analyzed samples of urine and feces. As shown in Figure 6C, time-dependent ICP-MS analysis of the Ru concentration exhibited that the majority of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** were detected in the urine within 1 week. Some of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** were detected in the feces, suggesting that the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** were cleared mainly via the renal pathway. TEM images of the urine samples were taken to further verify the clearance pathway. The presence of the NPs in the TEM images further demonstrates their excretion via urine (Figure 6D).

### 3.7. Evaluation of AKI Preventing Efficacy.
**RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** exhibit effective ROS scavenging in vitro and excellent renal accumulation. In vivo AKI preventing was, therefore, conducted by the intravenous injection of AKI mice with **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**. AKI mice injected intravenously with PBS served as controls. Changes in body weight were recorded over 7 days. It was found that PBS-treated AKI mice showed severe weight loss, whereas **RuO<sub>2</sub>NP** (5 mg/kg)-treated AKI mice gained weight normally (Figure 7A). The PBS-treated and **RuO<sub>2</sub>NP**-treated AKI mice were sacrificed 24 h after injection, and results indicate that the kidney weight ratio of PBS-treated AKI mice increased significantly (Figure 7B) and the kidneys were significantly enlarged compared with **RuO<sub>2</sub>NP**-treated AKI mice. The pictures of the corresponding kidneys are shown in Figure 7C. In addition, hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining of kidney tissue revealed that PBS-treated AKI mice had severe renal tubular injury (a marker of kidney damage), whereas renal tubular injury was found to be reduced in **RuO<sub>2</sub>NP**-treated AKI mice (Figure 7D).

### 3.8. Analysis of Kidney Function after Treatment.
To further evaluate the therapeutic effect of the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, we analyzed the blood urea nitrogen (BUN), serum creatinine, creatine kinase (CK), and malondialdehyde (MDA) leve, which are clinical indicators of renal excretory function (Figure 8A−D). These results showed that PBS-treated AKI mice showed higher levels of BUN, serum creatinine, CK, and MDA, which are signs of renal failure. In addition, these indicators were found to be significantly reduced after the injection of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, indicating that **RuO<sub>2</sub>NP** treatment alleviates renal damage in AKI mice.

To evaluate whether **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** can inhibit the excessive production of ROS in the kidney, we stained frozen sections of kidney from the different experimental groups of AKI mice with DCFH-DA (a fluorescent probe of ROS) and then them analyzed by laser scanning confocal microscopy. As shown in Figure 9, **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** exhibit a significant inhibitory effect on ROS generation in the kidneys of AKI mice.

### 3.10. Evaluation of the Toxicity of RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs In Vivo.
To assess the renal toxicity of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, kidney sections from renal tubules, collecting ducts, glomeruli, and urethra from AKI mice were collected 7 days after **RuO<sub>2</sub>NP** treatment. The samples were then fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde and stained with H&E. As shown in Figure S8, no obvious tissue damage or inflammatory lesions were detected in the **RuO<sub>2</sub>NP** treatment group samples compared to the control group, indicating no obvious side effects. In addition, to verify the biosafety of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, normal mice were injected intravenously with **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** (5 mg/kg), and 7 days later, the main organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung, intestine, and brain) were collected for H&E staining (Figure S9). The primary organs of normal mice treated with the NPs displayed no significant damage relative to the control group, indicating that **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** do not induce adverse side effects in healthy mice. The above results demonstrate the biocompatibility and low toxicity of **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** in vivo, making them excellent candidates a as therapeutic agents for the clinical prevention of AKI.

## 4. CONCLUSION

In this study, we demonstrate the novel use of ultrasmall **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** as an antioxidant. The NPs not only mimic the activities of several enzymes, but also can effectively scavenge free radicals. Although **RuO<sub>2</sub>** is a simple and widely used catalyst, the antioxidative activity, biological stability and biocompatibility of ultrasmall **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs**, were demonstrated in HEK293 cells for the first time. The ROS scavenging and effective inhibition of ROS-induced apoptosis by the NPs reveal the potential of this nanozyme as an effective therapeutic antioxidant in vivo, further proving the outstanding biocompatibility and protective effects of the ultrasmall NPs in AKI mice. Ultrasmall **RuO<sub>2</sub>NPs** are a promising agent for the prevention of AKI. In addition, these findings indicate this novel nanozyme may be further applied to the prevention of other diseases induced by ROS such as atherosclerosis and neurodegenerative disorders.
