# Iron selenide thin film: Peroxidase-like behavior, glucose detection and amperometric sensing of hydrogen peroxide

## ABSTRACT

Iron selenide (FeSe) thin film was deposited on glass or indium doped tin oxide (ITO) coated glass substrates through chemical bath deposition process from a precursor complex, [Fe<sub>3</sub>(μ<sub>3</sub>-O)(μ<sub>2</sub>-O<sub>2</sub>CCH<sub>2</sub>Cl)<sub>6</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>3</sub>]NO<sub>3</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O and was characterized by X-ray diffraction, field emission scanning electron microscopy and UV–vis spectroscopic techniques. Structural analyses revealed that the FeSe film had spherical morphology with average diameter of 30 nm. The film was shown to possess peroxidase-like catalytic activity toward oxidation of 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and followed Michaelis–Menten kinetics. It was exploited as a simple and cheap material in selective colorimetric method for detection and estimation of glucose in buffer solution. It gave a linear response to glucose over the concentration range from 2 × 10<sup>−6</sup> to 3 × 10<sup>−5</sup> M with a detection limit of 5 × 10<sup>−7</sup> M. Furthermore, the FeSe film, deposited on ITO, performed as good amperometric sensors for detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. To compare the catalytic and sensing efficiency of the FeSe film, we have also prepared FeS film under similar conditions.

## 1. Introduction

Convenient detection and quantitative determination of hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) are of practical importance because H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> plays important roles in several fields including food, pharmaceutical, plastic industries, biological analysis, clinical diagnostics and environmental chemistry [1–3], and it is also a by product of several highly selective oxidases [4–7]. Numerous analytical techniques are developed for determination of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, such as titrimetry [8], spectrophotometric [9,10], fluorimetric [11,12], chemiluminescence [13–16], electrochemical [17–21], etc. Among all these analytical techniques, spectrophotometric and electrochemical methods have received considerable attention owing to their simplicity, improved sensitivity and high selectivity. Several peroxidase mimetics such as hematin, porphyrin, hemoglobin, cyclodextrin, noble metals, macrocyclic complex of transition metals, carbon nanotubes, horseradish peroxidase (HRP) enzyme and metallic nanoparticles combined to HRP [22–26] have been applied in these two above analytical methods. Naturally occurring peroxidase enzyme, HRP, is emerged as the most commonly used enzyme for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensing [27,28] but it suffers some disadvantages due to lack of stability, difficult to produce in large quantities and are easily denatured in conditions like strong acidic, basic medium and high temperature.

Not with standing the developments made on the application of peroxidase-like activity, there is growing interest on the detection and estimation of glucose concentration by a simple, cheap, and sensitive colorimetric method. The chemical reactions involved in this process are the combination of the catalytic reaction of glucose with glucose oxidase (GOx) (Eq. (1)), followed by the reaction of the liberated H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with a peroxidase substrate in the presence of nanomaterials (Eq. (2)):

$$O_{2} + Glucose \xrightarrow{GOx} H_{2}O_{2} + Gluconic acid \text{ (1)}$$
$$H_{2}O_{2} + TMB \xrightarrow{FeSe} 2H_{2}O + oxidised TMB \text{ (2)}$$

Till now, few reports have been published on glucose detection using Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles [29], copper oxide [30,31], carboxyl modified graphene Oxide [32], graphene oxide-Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> [33], ZnFe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> [34], gold nanoparticles [35], etc.; however, a good deal remains to be done to develop the catalytic activity using other nanomaterials.

In recent years, there has been considerable interest in the use of nanoparticles for a wide range of biomedical and biosensing applications. Especially, magnetic nanoparticles (MNPs), which are in general considered to be chemically and biologically inert, have attracted much interest because of their interesting biomedical applications such as protein immobilization and separation [36,37], magnetic targeting, biosensor, and drug delivery [38,39], tissue engineering [40], mediator of cancer therapy [41–43], magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) [44–46], and so on. Apart from biomedical application, the MNPs have also been attracted in biosensor community. In particular, magnetite (Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) has been widely used as an artificial peroxidase [47] and can effectively catalyzes the reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> [48–51] and therefore, MNPs have been investigated more and more as peroxidase enzyme mimetics [52–56].

In this work, we describe deposition of FeSe thin film from a trinuclear Fe(III) cluster [Fe<sub>3</sub>(μ<sub>3</sub>-O)(μ<sub>2</sub>-O<sub>2</sub>CCH<sub>2</sub>Cl)<sub>6</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>3</sub>]NO<sub>3</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O as a precursor through simple chemical bath deposition process [57–59]. Although some efforts have been made to synthesize FeS, FeS<sub>2</sub> by chemical bath deposition process [60], developing a simple, reproducible and eco-friendly method to synthesize FeSe still remains as a challenging task. Till date, many reports regarding various applications of FeSe, FeS nano-powders have been furnished [52,61,62]; however, very few attempts have been made for the investigation on biomedical and biosensing applications, specially peroxidase-like activity and electrochemical biosensing activity. It occurred to us that FeSe nano-film could provide the enzymatic active center of Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> for electron transfer and could act as artificial peroxidase to perform biosensing of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Herein, we report for the first time, the peroxidase-like activity of FeSe film and a simple analytical approach that allows visual detection and estimation of glucose in aqueous solutions. The FeSe film-based new enzyme-mimic has also been used successfully for detection and quantification of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> by amperometric technique. We have also deposited FeS thin film on glass or ITO coated glass substrates and the catalytic and sensing efficiency has been compared with that of FeSe film.

## 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. Chemicals and materials

All chemicals were of reagent grade and used without further purification. Fe(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·9H<sub>2</sub>O, sodium selenite (Na<sub>2</sub>SeO<sub>3</sub>), sodium thiosulfate (Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>·5H<sub>2</sub>O), Na<sub>2</sub>HPO<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O, anhydrous sodium acetate, acetic acid, orthophosphoric acid were purchased from commercial sources. Sodium chloroacetate, polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP), hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, 30%), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), and 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB), glucose oxidase (Aspergillus niger, GOx) and β-d-glucose were purchased from Sigma–Aldrich. Solvents were used as received. In our previous report, we have already synthesized the precursor complex [Fe<sub>3</sub>O(ClCH<sub>2</sub>COO)<sub>6</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>3</sub>]NO<sub>3</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O [63].

### 2.2. Deposition of FeSe thin film

A properly cleaned glass or ITO substrate was dipped into a stirred 20 mL aqueous solution containing 440 mg (0.5 mmol) precursor complex in 50 mL beaker. To this solution, 20 mL of an aqueous solution of Na<sub>2</sub>SeO<sub>3</sub> (260 mg, 1.5 mmol) was added dropwise. The red colored solution slowly changed to black. Then the mixture was stirred at 85 °C. After 10 h a gray film was obtained on the glass or ITO substrate having a film thickness of ca. 250 nm, as determined by gravimetric analysis.

### 2.3. Deposition of FeS thin film

FeS thin film was deposited in the same way as FeSe film, except that Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>·5H<sub>2</sub>O was used instead of Na<sub>2</sub>SeO<sub>3</sub>.

### 2.4. Physical measurements

XRD patterns were obtained on a Philips PW 1140 parallel beam X-ray diffractometer with Bragg–Bretano focusing geometry and monochromatic CuKα radiation (λ = 1.540598 Å). Size and surface morphologies were studied with Gemini Zeiss Supra 35VP field emission scanning electron microanalyzer using an acceleration voltage of 4.9 kV. Absorption spectra were recorded on a JASCO V-530 UV–vis spectrophotometer. Kinetics studies were performed spectrophotometrically using Agilent-8453 diode-array spectrophotometer. Electrochemical measurements were carried out on CHI620D electrochemical analyzer.

### 2.5. Catalytic oxidation of TMB

A 2.4 μL aliquot of TMB (0.125 M, dissolved in DMSO) was diluted by 3 mL of sodium acetate buffer (0.1 M and pH 4.0). Then, it was treated separately with (i) as-deposited FeSe or FeS film (0.5 cm × 1 cm), (ii) 4 μL of 30% H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, (iii) 4 μL of 30% H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with the FeSe or FeS film (0.5 cm × 1 cm). Absorption spectra were recorded immediately after the addition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at an interval of 30 sec. The reaction temperature was kept at 30 °C. The kinetic analysis with TMB as the substrate was performed using FeSe or FeS film (0.5 cm × 1 cm) with fixed concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (13 mM) and varying concentration of TMB (0, 8.3, 16.6, 29.1, 35.4, 41.6, 54.1, 62.5, 75 and 91 μM). Similarly, the kinetic analysis with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrate was performed using the same FeSe or FeS film with fixed concentration of TMB (100 μM) and varying concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0, 6.5, 9.8, 13, 19, 26, 32, 39, 45 mM). Apparent kinetic parameters were calculated based on the Michaelis–Menten equation [47].

### 2.6. Glucose detection

This measurement has been carried out by following steps: (a) 20 μL of 20 mg/mL GOx and 200 μL of glucose of different concentrations in 10 mM phosphate buffer solution (PBS, pH 7.0) were incubated at 40 °C for 1 h; (b) 24 μL of 60 mM TMB, FeSe film (0.5 cm × 1 cm), and 800 μL of 0.2 M acetate buffer (pH 4.0) were added into the above 220 μL GOx-glucose reaction system; (c) the mixture solution was incubated at 45 °C for 10 min, then it was used to perform the absorption spectroscopy measurement.

### 2.7. Electrochemical H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> measurements

All electrochemical experiments were carried out in a cell containing 20 mL of 0.1 M phosphate buffer solution (PBS) with FeSe or FeS film on ITO substrate (1 cm × 2 cm) as working electrode, platinum wire as counter electrode and Ag/AgCl reference electrode at 30 °C at scan rate of 0.1 V s<sup>−1</sup>. The current responses after successive addition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to the buffer solution were monitored by cyclic voltammogram and amperometric technique. Amperometric measurements were carried out by applying fixed potential of −0.5 V for FeSe/ITO and −0.4 V for FeS/ITO (vs. Ag/AgCl). The Michaelis–Menten constant ($K_{m}^{app}$) and maximum current ($I_{max}$) were determined from the electrochemical version of Lineweaver–Burk equation [64].

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. Preparation of the FeSe and FeS thin-films

The concentrations of iron and selenium/sulfide ions have to control very carefully during the deposition of film. This can be easily achieved by using a complex with high stability and moderately stable selenium/sulfide source. Here, a water soluble trinuclear carboxylate-bridged [Fe<sub>3</sub>(μ<sub>3</sub>-O)(μ<sub>2</sub>-O<sub>2</sub>CCH<sub>2</sub>Cl)<sub>6</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>3</sub>]<sup>+</sup> core has been employed as a precursor complex, sodium selenite and sodium hyposulfite as selenium and sulfur source, respectively. In reaction bath, water-soluble trinuclear iron core facilitated the slow release of iron ion and thus its concentration was controlled. In this context, it can be mentioned that, according to Qu et al. [65], metal salts with an anion of a weak acid are all presumably excellent precursor for metal ion source. The existence of any anion of a strong acid often made it difficult to form metal chalcogenides. So, using this water-soluble iron core with chloroacetate anion, as a substitute of common iron salts, is a better precursor in many ways. Another important advantage of this precursor complex is the pH of its aqueous solution that lies in between 2.5 and 3.0, which is also suitable [60,66] for the deposition of iron chalcogenides.

### 3.2. Structural characterization

Fig. 1 shows the XRD patterns of FeSe and FeS films, deposited on glass substrate and annealed at 250 °C for 1 h under argon atmosphere. The patterns of the crystalline products match quite well with the standard diffraction data of the pure tetragonal phase of FeSe (JCPDS: 850735) and FeS (JCPDS: 860389). In Fig. 1a, the peaks at 2θ values of 28.7, 37.2, 40.5, 51.2, 55.5, 61.0, 64.8 and 71.2 can also be indexed to the (1 0 1), (1 1 1), (1 0 2), (2 0 1), (1 0 3), (1 1 3), (2 1 2) and (2 0 3) planes of FeSe, respectively. While the peaks at 2θ values (Fig. 1b) of 34.5, 35.6, 38.9, 49.5, 53.2, and 62.5 can be indexed to the (1 1 0), (0 0 2), (1 1 1), (2 0 0), (2 0 1), and (2 0 2) planes of FeS, respectively. Therefore, XRD results reveal that the FeSe and FeS films were grown on glass substrates via the chemical bath deposition approach. This selective formation of FeSe or FeS could be attributed to the reduction of Fe<sup>3+</sup> to Fe<sup>2+</sup> and concomitant formation of FeSe or FeS from the trinuclear iron(III) complex. The average grain sizes of the nanomaterials, as calculated by the Debye–Scherrer equation ($D = 0.9\lambda/(\beta \cos \theta)$), were found to be around 20 nm for FeSe and 24 nm for FeS films, where D is the crystallite size (diameter), λ is the wave length of X-ray (1.540598 Å), β is the value of FWHM of the most intense peak (after correcting the instrumental broadening), and θ is Bragg’s angle.

The morphologies of FeSe and FeS films were investigated by FESEM measurements. Fig. 2a shows that the as-obtained FeSe has spherical morphology with an average diameter of about 30 nm. The FESEM image of FeS (Fig. 2b) reveals that the film consists of flower-like texture assembled by many interconnected monodispersed porous spherical particles with an average diameter of about 40 nm. The diameter of the micro-flower is about 400 nm. The particle sizes are somewhat higher than the values obtain from XRD pattern analysis. The size obtained from XRD pattern is the grain size, while the size observed from the FESEM image is the particle size. Since one particle may contain one or several grains, therefore, the size obtained from the FESEM image can be greater than that calculated from XRD pattern.

### 3.3. Optical properties

The UV–vis spectral analysis of FeSe and FeS films were carried out at room temperature from 900 to 300 nm using a glass substrate as reference. Sharp rises in absorbances were found to take place near 725 nm for FeSe film and 815 nm for FeS film (Fig. S1), respectively. The similar types of spectra were also obtained for FeSe or FeS film on ITO substrate using blank ITO as the reference. The recorded optical spectra were used to calculate the band gap energies of the nanomaterials. Fermi’s golden rule for fundamental interband electronic transitions was used for the relation between absorption coefficient and photon energy [67]: $[(\alpha h\nu)^{1/n} = A(h\nu - E_{g})]$ [68] (Fig. S1 inset), where, $h\nu$ is the incident photon energy, ‘A’ is a constant and ‘n’ is the exponent the value of which is determined by the type of electronic transition causing the absorption and can take the values 1/2 or 2 depending upon whether the transition is direct or indirect, respectively [69].

The band gap energies ($E_{g}$) were calculated as 1.78 eV (for FeSe) and 1.9 eV (for FeS) from the intercept of the straight line at $\alpha = 0$ in the plot of $(\alpha h\nu)^{2}$ vs. $h\nu$ [60]. A pronounced blue shift in the $E_{g}$ values from their corresponding bulk materials [66,70] (from 1.2 to 1.78 eV for FeSe and from 0.9 to 1.9 eV for FeS) may be attributed to the decreasing size of materials.

### 3.4. Peroxidase catalytic activities of the FeSe and FeS films

To investigate the peroxidase catalytic activity of the as-deposited FeSe film, the catalytic oxidation of peroxidase substrate, TMB, in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was carried out. We have also measured the peroxidase activity of the FeS film to compare their catalytic efficiency under similar conditions. TMB has been proved to be a noncarcinogenic derivative [71] and can be oxidized to a blue reaction product in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The catalytic reaction can be monitored by measuring the augmentation of TMB absorbance [47] at 653 nm (Fig. 3a), which is originated from the oxidation product of TMB (Fig. 3a inset). This behavior is similar to that observed for the commonly used enzyme horse radish peroxidase (HRP) [47]. To find out the optimum pH and temperature, at first we have examined the effect of pH and temperature on the catalytic oxidation of TMB in between the pH 2.0–8.0 and the temperature range 10–70 °C, respectively, with both FeSe and FeS films. The pH 4.0 and 30 °C were chosen as the optimum pH and temperature (Fig. S2), which are very similar to the values for HRP [47]. In Fig. 3b, the curves (i) and (ii) demonstrated that both FeSe and FeS films in the absence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could catalyze the oxidation of TMB probably by dissolved O<sub>2</sub> although the rates are inferior. Moreover, the rate of oxidation of TMB by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> shown in curve (iii) is not worth mentioning compared to curves (iv) and (v). This indicates that the interactions among the nano-films, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB are essential for the catalytic process. Fig. 3b, clearly demonstrates that the catalytic ability of FeSe film is greater than the FeS film under similar conditions. This enhanced catalytic activity is due to the smaller size of FeSe nanomaterial having a greater surface-to-volume ratio to interact with substrate. The enzyme like activity of the FeSe film may originate from the ferrous ions present at the surface of the film. These ferrous ions may interact with the substrate in the presence of peroxide, resulting in a colored reaction product. The mechanism most likely follows Fenton’s reaction [72] and can be written as follows:

$$Fe^{2+} + H_{2}O_{2} \rightarrow Fe^{3+} + OH\cdot + OH^{-} \text{ (3)}$$
$$Fe^{3+} + H_{2}O_{2} \rightarrow Fe^{2+} + OOH\cdot + H^{+} \text{ (4)}$$

The hydroxyl radical, formed in the course of reaction, may catalyze the oxidation of the TMB substrate, resulting in a blue colored product.

To investigate the mechanism of peroxidase catalytic activity of both FeSe and FeS film, we determined the apparent steady-state kinetic parameters for the reaction. The reaction rates at different concentrations of substrates were obtained by initial rate method, i.e. calculating the slopes of initial absorbance changes with time. After each measurement, the catalyst films can be recovered easily through washing it with double distilled water. The absorbance data were converted to corresponding concentration term by using the value $\epsilon = 39,000 \text{ M}^{-1} \text{ cm}^{-1}$ (at 653 nm) for the oxidized product of TMB [73]. In a certain range of TMB (Fig. 4a) and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Fig. 4b) concentrations, typical Michaelis–Menten curves were observed for both FeSe and FeS films. The data were fitted to the Michaelis–Menten kinetic model using a nonlinear least square fitting routine to obtain the catalytic parameters [Michaelis–Menten constant ($K_{m}^{app}$) and maximum initial velocity ($V_{max}$)] given in Table 1. These parameters were also evaluated from the Lineweaver–Burk [74] double-reciprocal plot ($1/V_{0}$ vs. $1/S_{0}$) (shown as insets in Fig. 4a and b) and gave almost identical values. $K_{m}^{app}$ is an indicator of enzyme affinity for its substrate. A high $K_{m}^{app}$ value indicates weak affinity, whereas a low value suggests a high affinity. It may be noted in Table 1 that, the $K_{m}^{app}$ value for the FeSe film with TMB as the substrate (0.04 mM) is less than that for the FeS film (0.08 mM) and is significantly lower than that of HRP [47]. As compared to the HRP and other recently reported nano-mimetics [47,52,53,75], the FeSe film has greater affinity for TMB. On the other hand, the $K_{m}^{app}$ values of FeSe and FeS films with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrates are 13.2 and 7.44 mM, respectively, which are higher than that obtained for HRP [47] as well as FeS nanosheet [52], but significantly lower than that of other previously mentioned nano-mimetics [47,53,75] (Table 1), suggesting the higher affinity for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. All these findings indicate that, our as-synthesized FeSe and FeS nano-films, which have additional facility of being easily recoverable and synthesized by more simple and low cost process, possessed intrinsic peroxidase-like activity and good affinity for both TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Hence they can be used as artificial peroxidases in addition to the other nano-powder-based peroxidase-mimetics.

### 3.5. Colorimetric glucose detection

On the basis of the superior intrinsic peroxidase property of the FeSe film, we designed a colorimetric method for detection of glucose content by utilizing the same chromogenic substrates studied above. Similar to the previous report [29,31–35,76–79], hydrogen peroxide, evolved from GOx oxidation of glucose, can oxidize TMB in the presence of the nano-film. This color change reaction can be used to measure indirectly the concentration of glucose. As the GOx denatured in pH 4.0, we have done this experiment in two steps. In the first step, the glucose oxidation reaction with GOx was performed in pH 7.0 buffer solutions to produce H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> while in the second step this H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was reduced by TMB co-substrate in the presence of FeSe film at pH 4.0. From the glucose concentration–response curve (Fig. 5), glucose can be detected as low as $5 \times 10^{-7} \text{ M}$ with a linear range from $2 \times 10^{-6}$ to $3 \times 10^{-5} \text{ M}$ (Fig. 5 inset). Compared with other nanomaterials [29,31–35] (Table 2), the FeSe nano-film shows either comparable or even better response toward glucose detection.

To examine the selectivity of this system toward glucose, detection experiments were carried out using buffer solution, 5 mM maltose, 5 mM lactose and 5 mM fructose in place of glucose (0.5 mM). The corresponding intensities of absorbance at 653 nm for all samples are shown in Fig. 6. A blue color solution was obtained in the presence of glucose, but no obvious blue color was observed for other compounds even if the concentration is increased as 10-fold that of glucose (Fig. 6 inset). All these observations indicate that our biosensing system has high selectivity toward glucose, which could be attributed to the high affinity of glucose oxidase for glucose [32].

Therefore, the as-synthesized FeSe nano-film, which is non-toxic, cheap and easily recoverable, might be a good candidate to assay glucose in clinical diagnostics, food industry or in other applications that require analysis of trace levels of glucose using this simple colorimetric method.

### 3.6. Electrochemical characteristics of the FeSe and FeS films, and amperometric detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>

In order to design FeSe/ITO as amperometric hydrogen peroxide sensor, at first cyclic voltammetric technique was applied for the study of its electro catalytic activity toward H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> reduction. Fig. 7 displays the cyclic voltammograms of as-deposited FeSe/ITO in PBS (0.1 M, pH 7.0) containing different amounts of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. In the absence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, one reduction peak at −0.5 V for FeSe/ITO was observed. When H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was added to PBS (pH 7.0) at a scan rate of 0.1 V s<sup>−1</sup>, the reduction peak current increased dramatically (Fig. 7) which demonstrates that the FeSe film possesses electro-catalytic behavior toward reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Similar phenomenon was also observed for FeS/ITO at −0.42 V as suggested by Dai et al. [52] (Fig. S3).

For amperometric detection, −0.5 V (vs. Ag/AgCl) was used as a working potential and the corresponding amperometric response of FeSe/ITO for each successive addition of different amounts of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is presented in Fig. 8. When an aliquot of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was added to the PBS (0.1 M, pH 7.0), the reduction peak current increased sharply to reach a steady-state value within 10 sec (achieving 95% of the steady-state current) and this response is faster than the reported results for HRP that immobilized on a β-type zeolite modified glassy carbon electrode (30 s) [80].

Calibration curve has been obtained between amperometric current and concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which is linear from 10 μM to 1000 μM (correlation coefficient 0.9982) with sensitivity 0.767 mA mM<sup>−1</sup> (upper left inset of Fig. 8) and the detection limit is estimated as 7.0 μM ($\sigma = 3$). The amperometric response of FeS/ITO was also studied at −0.4 V (Fig. S4) and the corresponding analytical parameters are summarized in Table 3 with several other recently reported H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensors [52,81–83]. The linear range of the FeSe/ITO is comparable or wider than that of other H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensors like HRP based electrode [81], FeS nanosheet/GC [52], AgNPs/ZnONRs/FTO [82], Cytochrome c/Au/C [83], etc. (Table 3). The detection limit of the sensor is suitable for the quantitative determination of hydrogen peroxide in the field of biomedicine and environmental chemistry. The enhanced catalytic efficiency of the new H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensor (FeSe/ITO) may be due to the smaller size of FeSe nanomaterial than that of FeS having a greater surface-to-volume ratio which can efficiently transfer the electrons from FeSe to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.

As the general enzymatic reaction, at H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration higher than 1000 μM, the calibration curves for the FeSe/ITO and FeS/ITO-based sensors showed a platform, indicating Michaelis–Menten type behavior. According to the electrochemical version of Lineweaver–Burk model, the $K_{m}^{app}$ values are calculated to be 3.8 and 1.5 mM for FeSe/ITO and FeS/ITO, respectively (shown as lower right insets, Figs. 8 and S4) which indicate that both have nearly similar affinity to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Therefore, from the amperometric response parameters for FeSe/ITO (Table 3), it may be concluded that our as synthesized FeSe nano-film based new sensor is good electrode material for detection of hydrogen peroxide and can be use in environmental chemistry, biotechnology and medical diagnostics.

The reproducibility and stability of the FeSe/ITO based new sensor was also studied by adding a fixed amount of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 mM) to the buffer solution. The relative standard deviation (RSD) of the sensor response was 3.2% for five successive measurements for the same electrode. When the FeSe/ITO electrode was stored at 4 °C for 1 week, the current response to 1 mM hydrogen peroxide remained 95% of its original value, suggesting the long-term stability of the electrode.

## 4. Conclusion

Nano-sized FeSe thin film has been successfully deposited on glass and ITO substrates through chemical bath deposition process using a trinuclear iron(III) precursor complex [Fe<sub>3</sub>(μ<sub>3</sub>-O)(O<sub>2</sub>CCH<sub>2</sub>Cl)<sub>6</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>3</sub>]NO<sub>3</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O and characterized by XRD, FESEM and UV–vis spectroscopic techniques. The resultant film possessed intrinsic peroxidase catalytic activity and showed typical Michaelis–Menten kinetics and high catalytic efficiency. We also demonstrated a simple colorimetric method for the detection and estimation of glucose in buffer solution. Furthermore, the FeSe film, on ITO substrate, performed as a good amperometric hydrogen peroxide sensor. All these studies suggest that our as-synthesized FeSe thin-film is new attractive enzyme mimetic and will facilitate the utilization in medical diagnostics and biotechnology.
