# Metal–organic frameworks-derived synthesis of porous FeP nanocubes: An effective peroxidase mimetic

Wenshu Yang <sup>a,b</sup>, Jinhui Hao <sup>a,b</sup>, Zhe Zhang <sup>a</sup>, Bailin Zhang <sup>a,∗</sup>

<sup>a</sup> State Key Laboratory of Electroanalytical Chemistry, Changchun Institute of Applied Chemistry, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Changchun 130022, PR China
<sup>b</sup> University of Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing 100049, PR China

### GRAPHICAL ABSTRACT

A facile process was demonstrated toward the synthesis of porous FeP nanocubes through thermal phosphidation reaction, using Prussian blue as iron-based metal–organic frameworks precursors. Then, the FeP nanocubes were exploited as a peroxidase mimetic.

### ABSTRACT

Starting with metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), a facile process was demonstrated toward the synthesis of porous FeP nanocubes. Herein, Prussian blue nanocubes were used as iron-based MOFs precursors, and converted into FeP nanocubes through the thermal phosphidation reaction. Then, the FeP nanocubes were exploited as a peroxidase mimetic, which not only has the advantages of low cost, high stability, and easy preparation, but also follows Michaelis–Menten behaviors and shows strong affinity to substrates. On the basis of high catalytic activity of FeP nanocubes, a rapid, and convenient approach was developed for the colorimetric detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> from 2 μM to 130 μM and with a detection limit of 0.62 μM. The good catalytic activity and high stability make the porous FeP nanocubes a useful catalyst for a wide range of potential applications in catalysis and biotechnology.

## 1. Introduction

Peroxidases are a large family of enzymes that typically catalyze the decomposition of peroxides to product hydroxyl radicals, which can oxide many organic substrates to reduce their toxicity [1]. With good catalytic performance, peroxidases have been applied in many fields [2–4], such as waste water treatment and adhesives in manufacturing processes [5]. Among them, horseradish peroxidase (HRP) is commonly used in techniques such as ELISA and immunohistochemistry, due to they can catalyze the conversion of chromogenic substrates (e.g., 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB)) into colored products [6,7]. However, natural enzymes, being protein-based macromolecules, bear some serious drawbacks in that they are unstable against denaturation or protease digestion, and their preparation and storage are expensive [8]. To overcome the stability and cost issues of biological catalyst, lots of efforts have been made to use stable enzyme mimetics as substitutes for natural enzymes. A variety of nanomaterials, such as noble metal [9,10], carbon materials [11,12], transition metal based alternatives [13–15], have been extensively developed as enzyme mimetics. Among these materials, some iron-based metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), like PCN-222 with porphyrinic Fe(III) centers [16], Fe(III)-based MIL-68 [17], MIL-100 [17] and Prussian blue [15], were recently found to be effective peroxidase mimetics.

MOFs are well-ordered crystalline porous materials that consist of metal clusters coordinated to organic ligands. The crystalline structures of MOFs make them have unusual properties such as permanent nanoscale porosity, and high surface area. Thus, MOFs have been explored extensively in fields of gas storage, sensing, and catalysis [18,19]. Although the structure of MOFs is beneficial to their applications [20], unfortunately, most MOFs are unstable under alkaline conditions or ambient atmospheric environment [21,22]. Prussian blue (PB) is considered as the first synthetic MOF, which is a mixed-valence iron(III) hexacyanoferrate(II) compound. It is well known that PB would converts into Fe(OH)<sub>3</sub> in an alkaline solution [21], which will limit their practical use in various catalysis reaction processes. Transition metal phosphides are a class of well-known catalysts and have recently attracted lots of research interest in catalysis because of their excellent catalytic performance over the wide pH range [23]. On the other hand, MOFs have been demonstrated as templates/precursors to construct carbon materials or metal oxides via thermolysis [24–26], and their pores are stable during elimination of the organic ligands. Inspired by these ideas, a phosphate reaction has been conducted using PB nanocubes as precursors, and porous FeP nanocubes were obtained. Interestingly, it has been found that the as-obtained FeP nanocubes possess excellent intrinsic peroxidase-like activity and can catalytically oxidize peroxide substrate 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to produce a typical color reaction. The peroxidase-like catalytic property of FeP was further investigated through steady-state enzyme kinetics theory and methods with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB as the substrates. Compared with PB precursor, FeP nanocubes have better stability under alkaline conditions. Finally, the FeP were successfully used as peroxidase mimetics for the colorimetric detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.

## 2. Experimental

### 2.1. Chemicals

3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) and sodium hypophosphite (NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub>) were obtained from Aladdin Industrial Inc. (Shanghai, China). Poly (N-vinyl-2-pyrrolidone) (PVP, relative molecular mass 30,000–40,000) and HRP was obtained from Shanghai Chemical Factory (Shanghai, China). Hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) and potassium hexacyanoferrate(II) trihydrate (K<sub>4</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O) were purchased from Beijing Chemical Reagent Factory (Beijing, China). All the reagents were used as received without further purification. All aqueous solutions were prepared with Milli-Q water (>18.2 MΩ cm) from a Milli-Q Plus system (Millipore).

### 2.2. Apparatus

X-ray diffraction (XRD) spectra were obtained on a D8 ADVANCE (Germany) using Cu Kα (0.15406 nm) radiation. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurement was performed on an ESCALAB-MKII spectrometer (VG Co., United Kingdom) with Al Kα X-ray radiation as the X-ray source for excitation. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and high-resolution TEM (HRTEM) images were obtained with a TECNAI G2 high-resolution transmission electron microscope (Holland) under an accelerating voltage of 200 kV, and a Hitachi H600 electron microscope (Japan) under an accelerating voltage of 100 kV. The sample for TEM characterization was prepared by placing a drop of prepared solution on a carbon coated copper grid and drying at room temperature. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images and energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) analysis were inspected on a Hitachi S-4800 microscope. Nitrogen adsorption and desorption isotherms were measured on an ASAP 2020 (Micromeritics, USA). Pore size distribution (PSD) curves were calculated by the Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) method from the absorption branch. UV–Vis detection was carried out on a PGENERAL T6 UV–Vis spectrophotometer (China).

### 2.3. Synthesis of porous FeP nanocubes

To synthesize FeP nanocubes, PB nanocubes were firstly prepared according to the literature [27]. In a typical procedure, PVP (3.00 g) and K<sub>3</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O (0.132 g) were added to a HCl solution (0.1 M, 40 mL) under magnetic stirring. After the mixture was stirred for 30 min, the bottle was then placed into an electric oven and heated at 80 °C for 20 h. The obtained blue product was washed several times with deionized water and finally dried at room temperature for further use. Secondly, the obtained PB nanocubes were transferred into a tubular furnace for phosphidation under N<sub>2</sub> flow. 0.05 g PB and 0.5 g NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub> powder were put at two separate positions in a fused silica tube with NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub> at the upstream side of the tubular furnace. Then the PB was heated to 450 °C at a rate of 3 °C min<sup>−1</sup> and maintained at this temperature for 2 h under protection of N<sub>2</sub>. Black FeP powder was obtained after cooled to ambient temperature under N<sub>2</sub> flow.

### 2.4. H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection using porous FeP nanocubes as peroxidase mimetics

To detect H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, experiments were carried out using 20 μg FeP nanocubes in a reaction volume of 2 mL NaAc–HAc buffer (pH 4.0) with 250 μM TMB as the substrate. The concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was 20 mM, unless otherwise stated. Reactions were monitored at 652 nm and recorded after 5 min at 40 °C.

### 2.5. The steady-state kinetic assays

The steady-state kinetic assays were carried out at room temperature in a 2 mL of TMB–H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>–FeP reaction system. The kinetic analysis of FeP nanocubes with TMB as the substrate was performed with TMB–H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>–FeP system with constant H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration of 5 mM and 10 μg/mL FeP nanocubes but varied TMB concentration (50–900 μM). The kinetic analysis with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrate was performed with constant TMB concentration of 0.1 mM and 10 μg/mL FeP nanocubes but varied H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration (1–20 mM).

All the reactions were monitored in timescan mode at 652 nm using the UV–Vis spectrophotometer. A series of initial reaction rates was calculated and applied to the double reciprocal of the Michaelis–Menten equation, $1/v = (K_m/V_{max}) \cdot (1/[S]) + 1/V_{max}$, where $v$ is the initial velocity, $[S]$ is the concentration of substrate, $K_m$ is the Michaelis–Menten constant and $V_{max}$ is the maximal reaction velocity.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. Preparation and characterization of FeP nanocubes

Porous FeP nanocubes were prepared through a facile route, which involved the synthesis of PB precursors and the subsequent phosphidation reaction. The as-prepared nanocomposites were initially characterized by XRD to verify the crystal structures. Fig. 1A and B show the XRD patterns for PB and the phosphidated product. The characteristic diffraction peaks of PB precursor are in agreement with Fe<sub>4</sub>[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS No.73-0687). In contrast to the sharp peaks of the original PB nanocubes, phosphidated product shows broadened and less-resolved peaks. The typical peaks of product can be identified clearly as orthorhombic FeP (JCPDS card No.78-1443) [28]. No impurity is observed, which indicates that the product is pure FeP phase.

Subsequently, the morphology of FeP nanocubes was observed by TEM. From Fig. 1C, it is noteworthy that the FeP nanocubes have a size of around 150 nm and possess a highly porous structure. HRTEM image (Fig. 1D) further displays that the labeled spacing of 0.242 nm corresponds to the (1 1 1) plane distance of FeP crystal, consistent with the XRD result. Furthermore, the samples before and after phosphidation were examined by SEM. The SEM images of PB and FeP nanocubes are shown in Fig. 2A and B. Comparing these two images, it can be found that the obtained FeP inherited the size and cubic morphology of PB precursor well, whereas their surfaces became very rough. The EDS spectrum (Fig. 2C) clearly demonstrates that both Fe and P elements exist in FeP nanocubes, which is different from the EDS results of PB, indicating the formation of FeP.

To obtain detailed information about elemental composition and chemical state of samples, XPS measurements were carried out. The survey XPS spectrum of FeP (Fig. S1) reveals the presence of P and Fe elements. High-resolution Fe 2p spectrum of PB (Fig. 2D) shows two peaks at 707.4 and 719.9 eV, corresponding to the Fe 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Fe 2p<sub>1/2</sub> states. However, there are two peaks (705.2 and 710.1 eV) in the Fe 2p<sub>3/2</sub> region for FeP (Fig. 2D). The peaks at 128.4 and 129.3 eV reflect the binding energy of P 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and P 2p<sub>1/2</sub> of FeP, respectively. The peaks at 705.2 and 128.4 eV are close to the binding energy for Fe and P in FeP [29], which indicates the formation of FeP species. The peaks at 710.1 and 133.4 eV can be assigned to oxidized Fe and P species arising from superficial oxidation of FeP exposed to air [30,31]. All these results also confirm the formation of FeP after phosphidation reaction.

Generally, the properties of nanomaterials are affected by the porous nature, morphology, and surface property. Thus, N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption measurement was conducted to characterize the porous property of FeP nanocubes (Fig. 3). The isotherm curves are identified as type III, which is characteristic of larger mesoporous and macroporous materials. The BET surface area of the FeP nanocubes was calculated to be 39.55 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Fig. 3). Moreover, the pore size of FeP nanocubes shows a relatively wide distribution (inset of Fig. 3). The average pore size distribution centers at ~12 nm, implying that the sample possesses the porous structure, which is in good agreement with the result of TEM (Fig. 1C).

### 3.2. Peroxidase-like activity of FeP

The peroxidase-like activity of FeP was investigated by studying the catalytic oxidation of peroxidase substrate, TMB, in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The UV–Vis absorbance maximum of reaction mixture was found at 652 nm, owing to the absorption of oxidized TMB. As shown in Fig. 4A, we find that the TMB solution have no well-developed absorption peaks in the range from 400 to 800 nm when either FeP or H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was present, meaning no oxidation reaction occurred. However, the TMB solution shows a strong absorption peak when both H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and FeP were added (Fig. 4A). The photographs (inset of Fig. 4A) for TMB solutions under different conditions have also shown the same result. All those observations indicate that FeP possesses peroxidase-like activity and can effectively catalyze the oxidation of TMB by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in an acidic solution [17,32].

To establish the appropriate analytical conditions, reaction conditions were optimized. The influences of working pH (Fig. 4B), reaction temperature (Fig. 4C) and the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Fig. 4D) on catalytic activity of FeP were investigated. Meanwhile, we compared the results with the activity found in HRP over the same range of parameters (Fig. 4). When the pH and temperature are approximately 4.0 and 40 °C, respectively, the FeP nanocubes show the best catalytic performance, which are very similar to the values for HRP. But the FeP nanocubes catalyzed reaction is inhibited at a higher concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to reach the maximum level of peroxidase activity than that of horse radish peroxidase (HRP) (Fig. 4D). In addition, the time-dependent absorbance change of TMB solution with FeP nanocubes as catalyst is higher than that of PB (Fig. S2A), which indicates that FeP nanocubes exhibit the better catalytic performance as peroxidase mimetics. To test the stability, we incubated both PB and FeP nanocubes in aqueous solution with pH values from 2 to 14 for 2 h (Fig. S3), and then measured the catalytic activity of FeP nanocubes under standard conditions (pH 4.0 and 40 °C) (Fig. S2B). The FeP nanocubes were indeed found to remain stable over a wide range of pH from 2 to 14 (Fig. S2B). However, from optical photographs, it can be found that the blue colors of PB nanocubes solution immediately disappeared at high pH (13 and 14) and became lighter at pH 10, 11 and 12 (Fig. S4), demonstrating the instability of PB under alkaline conditions. The reusability of FeP nanocubes was also evaluated on catalytic oxidation of TMB. After three cycles, ~90% of the initial catalytic activity of FeP nanocubes was retained (Fig. S4). These results show the high stability of FeP nanocubes.

To further investigate the kinetic mechanism of the peroxidase-like activity of FeP nanocubes, steady-state kinetic experiments were carried out. The velocity of the reaction at different concentrations of substrate was obtained by calculating the slopes of absorbance changes at 652 nm with time. In a certain range of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or TMB concentration, typical Michaelis–Menten curves were observed (Fig. 5A and B). The Michaelis–Menten constant ($K_m$) and maximum initial velocity ($V_m$) were calculated from Lineweaver–Burk plots and listed in Table S1. It is known that $K_m$ is an indicator of enzyme affinity to substrates and a smaller $K_m$ value reflects a stronger affinity of enzyme for substrate [33]. The apparent $K_m$ value of FeP nanocubes with TMB as the substrate is lower than those of reported Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> [34], Fe nanoparticles [35], and HRP [36], which suggests the FeP nanocubes have a higher affinity for TMB. The apparent $K_m$ value of the FeP with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrate is significantly higher than that for HRP (Table S1), consistent with the observation that a higher concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was required to observe the maximal activity for FeP nanocubes. The double-reciprocal plots (Fig. 5C and D) revealed the characteristic parallel lines of a ping-pong mechanism, as was observed for HRP [37].

On the basis of the intrinsic peroxidase property of FeP nanocubes, a colorimetric method for detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> using the FeP-catalyzed blue color reaction was designed. Since the absorbance of oxidized TMB is highly dependent on H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration, this method can be used to detect H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Fig. 6A shows a typical H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration–response curve under optimal conditions. The linear range of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> from 2 μM to 130 μM ($R^2 = 0.994$) was obtained, with a detection limit of 0.62 μM (Fig. 6B). This analytical performances show a wider linear rang and lower detection limit comparable to reported Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles based sensor [38] (linear range: 5–100 μM, detection limit: 3 μM).

## 4. Conclusion

In summary, porous FeP nanocubes were successfully fabricated via a facile phosphidation method. Different from precious reported works, PB nanocubes were used as precursor to synthesize FeP nanocubes. Through simple phosphidation reaction, PB nanocubes transformed into porous FeP nanocubes with the retention of the original shapes. Moreover, the as-obtained FeP nanocubes were found exhibiting peroxidase mimetic activities. It was thoroughly observed that the peroxidase activity of the FeP nanocubes show typical Michaelis–Menten kinetics and better stability than PB in alkaline solution. On the basis of the peroxidase-like property of FeP nanocubes, a simple colorimetric method for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection was achieved. It is believed that the FeP nanocubes may be a useful artificial peroxidase for biosensing and catalysis.
