# Structural effects of amphiphilic protein/gold nanoparticle hybrid based nanozyme on peroxidase-like activity and silver-mediated inhibition†

Yan Liu,* Yinping Xiang, Ding Ding and Rong Guo*

Understanding structure–property correlations of nanozymes is critical in the rational design and fabrication of nanozymes with effective activity and smart multifunction. Here, an amphiphilic protein, β-casein, was chosen as a flexible modifier to fabricate a β-casein–AuNP hybrid nanozyme. The physicochemical properties including protein surface density and particle sizes can be easily controlled by the molar ratio of [precursor]/[protein]. Then, the structure–property correlation of the nanozyme was systematically investigated. Both the surface density of the protein and the nanoparticle size influence the peroxidase-like activity and silver-mediated inhibition. β-Casein–AuNP with a moderate AuNP core size and protein surface density has the lowest apparent $K_{m}$ value of CM–AuNPs with TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, and hence the highest enzyme activity. Furthermore, the silver ion has been found to selectively inhibit the catalytic activity of β-casein–AuNPs due to the capping microenvironment provided by the protein. Since the bound silver ions can be effectively reduced to Ag<sup>0</sup> by protein on the AuNP surface, the highest inhibition potency by silver is obtained for a β-casein–AuNP nanozyme with the lowest protein surface density and largest particle size. Importantly, a β-casein–AuNP (4.3 nm) based silver sensor is highly sensitive for sensing silver ions with a wide linear range of 0.1–10 μM, and has a low detection limit of 10 nM. The fundamental understanding of the structure–property relationship of nanozymes could provide guidance in the further development of easily prepared, effective, and low cost nanozymes and their utilization in numerous areas.

## 1. Introduction

Nanomaterial-based artificial enzymes (nanozymes) have been attracting increasing attention in the past few years, since they exhibit several advantages such as low cost, stability against denaturing, and tunability in catalytic activities.<sup>1–6</sup> Because of their easy preparation, excellent biocompatibility, and unique optoelectronic properties, gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) have stimulated considerable interest in numerous fields.<sup>7–9</sup> Recently, AuNPs have been demonstrated to exhibit inherent peroxidase-mimic activity, so they hold potential applications for biosensor, cancer diagnostics/therapy, and pollutant detection/removal.<sup>10,11</sup> Unfortunately, the catalytic activity and stability of such nanozymes is usually relatively low, which limits the application of them in practice.

Proteins have been extensively explored to fabricate numerous functional nanomaterials due to several distinct characteristics, including mild reaction conditions, distinctive molecular structures, diverse functionality, highly-specific biorecognition, and flexible self-assembly abilities.<sup>12,13</sup> In addition, conjugation of proteins with nanocrystals can afford stabilization of the system, but more importantly, it introduces highly specific or multiple functionalities onto these hybrid nanoparticles for further multifunction application.<sup>14</sup> Natural enzymes exhibit high catalytic activity and selectivity, which depends much on the synergistic effect of hydrophobic interaction, electrostatic interaction as well as hydrogen bonding between substrates and enzymes. Thus, the large diversity of proteins provides high probability that proteins have the potential to recognize and interact with some substrates. Despite the significant advances in nanotechnology, little is known about the structure and catalytic properties relationship of protein/AuNP hybrid based nanozymes. Based on a fundamental understanding of catalytic processes, the rational design and fabrication of protein/AuNP hybrid based nanozymes with high ability, intentionally desired peroxidase-like activity and multifunction poses a great challenge.

As is well known, natural enzyme inhibitors are ubiquitous in all living systems. Generally, heavy metal ions are typical examples of enzyme inhibitors, due to the interaction between heavy metal ions and amino acid residues present in or near the active site of enzyme.<sup>15</sup> Accordingly, the property of protein/AuNP hybrid may also are labile and sensitive to interactions with heavy metal ions. Differently from natural enzyme, Huang and co-workers have recently discovered that mercury ions can dramatically stimulate the catalytic activity of citrate-capped AuNPs.<sup>16</sup> Apart from Hg<sup>2+</sup>, Chang et al. found that other metal ions, such as Ag<sup>+</sup>, Pb<sup>2+</sup>, and Bi<sup>3+</sup>, could also greatly enhance the catalytic activity of citrate-capped AuNPs.<sup>17</sup> Compared with citrate, proteins as biomacromolecules, have flexible molecular structure and many functional groups such as carboxyl, hydroxyl, and amine groups, so it is interesting to investigate the modulation of metal ions on the catalytic activity of protein/AuNP hybrid based nanozyme. The further comprehensive investigation of correlating different structures to silver-mediated activity is believed to provide important insight toward the rational design of high-performance nanozyme in biological and environmental application.

This article investigated the uses of β-casein as a model protein to prepare protein/AuNP hybrid based nanozyme, to elucidate the structural effects on peroxidase-like activity and silver-mediated activity of the nanozyme. β-Casein has a highly content of acid amino acid residues (such as glutamic acid and aspartic acid residues), and is negatively charged at neutral pH. It contains a negatively charged hydrophilic N-terminal region and a strongly hydrophobic C-terminal region.<sup>18,19</sup> Therefore, in contrast to typical “intrinsically unstructured/disordered” protein that remain in a monomeric state, β-casein self-assembles into micelles in solution, similar to those formed by amphiphilic block-copolymers and surfactants. Thus, β-casein can stabilize AuNPs by the electrostatic effect as well as the steric effect, and contribute to the affinity of substrate to protein/AuNPs hybrid via hydrophobic effect. The experimental results offer evidence that not only the peroxidase-like activity, but also the silver-mediated inhibition are strongly related to the structure of amphiphilic protein/gold nanoparticle hybrid. We have for the first time presented a fundamental understanding of the structure–property correlation of protein–AuNP hybrid based nanozyme. This work may provide guidance on designing efficient protein/metal nanoparticle based nanozyme with the desirable structure for specific applications.

## 2. Experimental section

### 2.1 Chemicals and materials

β-Casein was purchased from Sigma (>99%). Hydrogen tetrachloroaurate tetrahydrate (HAuCl<sub>4</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O, 99%) and 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) were purchased from Sigma. All other reagents used were of analytical grade from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Beijing Co., Ltd, and ultrapure Millipore water (18.2 MΩ cm) was used as the solvent. All glassware was washed with aqua regia (HCl : HNO<sub>3</sub> in 3 : 1 ratio by volume), and rinsed with ethanol and ultrapure water. β-Casein powders were dispersed in Milli-Q water under stirring at 50 °C, and the dispersions were stored at 4 °C overnight to allow complete hydration.

### 2.2 Synthesis of β-casein functionalized AuNPs

In a typical experiment, gold nanoparticles were prepared first by mixing 0.2 mL 25 mM HAuCl<sub>4</sub> solution with 10 mL of aqueous β-casein solution with a desired concentration (the final protein in the system being 0.02, 0.1 and 0.5 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>, respectively) and pH at room temperature. After stirring 30 min, 1.2 mL of ice cold 10 mM NaBH<sub>4</sub> was added under stirring. After the complete reaction (24 h), the solution was dialysed with water for two days to remove excess β-casein.

### 2.3 Characterization of β-casein functionalized AuNPs

The morphologies of products were examined by a transmission electron microscopy (TEM, Tecnai-12 Philip Apparatus Co.). UV-vis spectra (UV-2501, Shimadzu Corp., Japan) of products dispersed in water were measured in the range 300–800 nm. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectra were measured by a Thermo ESCALAB250 X-ray photoelectron spectroscope (Thermo, UK). Steady-state fluorescence experiments were performed with a RF-5301 luminescence spectrometer (Japan Shimadzu Company) equipped with a thermostated water-circulating bath.

### 2.4 β-Casein–AuNPs as peroxidase mimetics

To investigate the peroxidase-like activity of the as-prepared β-casein–AuNPs, the catalytic oxidation of the peroxidase substrate TMB in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was tested. In a typical experiment, 36 μL of 25.0 mM TMB, 150 μL of the 0.4 mM β-casein–AuNPs stock solution, and 90 μL of 10 M H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were added into 2.724 mL of 0.2 M pH 4.0 acetate buffer at 25 °C. The solution was then transferred for UV-vis scanning after incubating for 20 minutes.

### 2.5 Silver sensing

For Ag<sup>+</sup> sensing, 150 μL of β-casein–AuNPs were added into the solution which contains different amounts of Ag<sup>+</sup>. Two minutes later, the solution was diluted with 2.3 mL of pH 4.0 acetate buffer. 3,3′,5,5′-Tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) (36 μL, 25 mM) and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (90 μL, 10 M) were then added and left to react at 25 °C for 20 min prior to spectroscopy measurement.

### 2.6 Determination of Ag<sup>+</sup> in the lake water samples

Water samples were obtained from Slender West Lake near our university. The samples collected were first filtered through a 0.22 μm membrane to remove impurities. Then, 300 μL of the water sample was mixed with buffer, AuNPs, TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as described in the above procedure.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1 Characterization of β-casein–AuNPs

The prepared β-casein–AuNP (CM–AuNP) obtained in the presence of 0.1 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> β-casein appears a red wine color (inset in Fig. 1A), and shows a peak at 512 nm, which indicates the formation of Au isotropic dispersed nanocrystals (Fig. 1A). The transmission electron microscopy image in Fig. 1B shows that the as-prepared AuNPs are well monodispersed. The average diameter of the AuNPs is 4.2 ± 0.5 nm, as judged from image analysis of 300 individual particles. Fig. 1C shows the FT-IR spectra of native β-casein and β-casein stabilized AuNPs. For β-casein, the appearance of a band at 1645 cm<sup>−1</sup> indicates an unordered structure of β-casein.<sup>20</sup> However, the predominant band centering at 1656 cm<sup>−1</sup> indicates that the binding of β-casein to AuNPs has made the transformation from unordered structure to α-helix structure of β-casein. Furthermore, casein consists of a highly repetitive sequence of amino acids of aspartic acid (Asp) and glutamic acid (Glu) (17% Asp and 23% of all residues in casein), and the carboxylic group provides the potential for gold complexing. As expected, the band at 1447 and 1398 cm<sup>−1</sup> due to COO<sup>−</sup> of Asp and Glu residues changes much in the CM stabilized AuNPs (Fig. 1C), which indicates that Asp and Glu residues play important roles in the stabilization of AuNPs. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) data revealed that gold, carbon, nitrogen and oxygen are present at the surface of AuNPs, indicating that the AuNPs are capped by protein (Fig. S1†). As shown in Fig. 1D, the main peak (Au 4f<sub>7/2</sub>) of AuNPs demonstrates that the resulting AuNP solution does not include any unreacted species (such as AuCl<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>). Also, the main peak (Au 4f<sub>7/2</sub>) of AuNPs (83.0 eV) shifts toward a lower binding energy (ca. 1.0 eV) relative to that of metallic Au (84.0 eV) (Fig. 1D), suggesting the electrons transfer from the carboxylic group of the proteins to the Au surface. The CM–AuNPs are found to be stable over 6 months and do not show any signs of aggregation at 4 °C. Here, β-casein can stabilize AuNPs by both the electrostatic effect and the steric effect.

![Fig. 1 UV-vis spectrum (A) and TEM images (B) of CM–AuNPs obtained in 0.1 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> β-casein. Inset in (B) the size distribution histogram of CM–AuNPs. (C) FT-IR spectra of β-casein (down) and CM–AuNPs (up). (D) XPS spectrum showing the binding energy of Au 4f in CM–AuNPs.](figure1.png)

We also prepared CM–AuNPs of different sizes by changing protein concentrations. AuNPs prepared in the presence of 0.02 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> casein have an average size of 8.7 nm (Fig. 2A), and the AuNP size decreases to 2.8 nm when the casein concentration increases to 0.5 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> (Fig. 2B). The UV/vis absorption spectra of AuNPs synthesized at different protein concentration are shown in Fig. 2C. The occurrence of a red shift of λ<sub>max</sub> (from 512 nm at 0.1 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> to 535 nm at 0.02 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) further demonstrates that the size of AuNPs increases with the decrease of casein concentration to 0.02 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>. When the β-casein concentration increases to 0.5 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>, no sharper absorbance peak is observed within the UV-vis range, indicating the size of the gold colloids decreases to less than 4 nm.<sup>21</sup> The result of UV-vis absorption spectral measurement is very consistent with that of the TEM observation. Here, it is easy to deduce that the higher the casein concentration, the more effective the capping function of the casein, resulting in smaller AuNPs finally. For simplicity, we denote these β-casein–AuNPs as CM–AuNP (8.7 nm), CM–AuNP (4.2 nm) and CM–AuNP (2.8 nm), corresponding to the nanoparticles synthesized in the presence of β-casein with concentration being 0.02, 0.1, and 0.5 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>, respectively.

FT-IR and fluorescence spectra measurements were further used to understand the surface properties of as-prepared CM–AuNPs with different sizes. Fig. 2D shows the FT-IR spectra of CM–AuNPs obtained at different β-casein concentrations. Similar to CM–AuNP (4.2 nm), the appearance of the band at 1660 cm<sup>−1</sup> for CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) also indicates α-helix structure of β-casein on AuNP surfaces. Differently, the predominant band centering at 1648 cm<sup>−1</sup> for CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) indicates the unordered structure of β-casein. For CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) and CM–AuNP (4.2 nm), although the surface density of protein is low, the interaction between AuNPs and β-casein is strong due to the large size of AuNPs. This makes the natively unordered structure transform into α-helix structure of β-casein. For CM–AuNP (2.8 nm), the interaction between AuNPs and β-casein is the weakest due to the smallest size of AuNPs, so the natively unordered structure of β-casein is remained. The FT-IR results confirm that the interaction between β-casein and AuNP with the larger size is the stronger, which is consistent with some earlier studies.<sup>22,23</sup>

![Fig. 2 (A and B) TEM images of CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) and CM–AuNP (2.8 nm). UV-vis absorption spectra (C) and FT-IR spectra (D) of CM–AuNPs obtained at different casein concentration. The fluorescence emission spectra (E) and I<sub>1</sub>/I<sub>3</sub> ratio (F) of pyrene in the absence and the presence of CM–AuNPs.](figure2.png)

Pyrene has a very low solubility in water, and as a result, it is selectively solubilized in the hydrophobic region or microphases existing in aqueous medium. The intensity ratio of the first peak to the third peak ($I_{1}/I_{3}$) of the fluorescence spectrum of pyrene shows the microenvironmental polarity where the probe exists.<sup>24</sup> Fig. 2E shows the fluorescence spectra of pyrene in the absence and presence of CM–AuNP. Fig. 2F shows the effect of the $I_{1}/I_{3}$ ratio in the absence and presence of CM–AuNP. As can be seen, the ratio $I_{1}/I_{3}$ in aqueous solution has a value of 1.74 in the absence of CM–AuNPs, and the ratio $I_{1}/I_{3}$ in the formed CM–AuNPs decreases with the decrease of AuNP size. For CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) formed in the presence of 0.02 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> β-casein, only a few protein molecules are coated on the formed AuNP surfaces separately, so the hydrophobic domain of individual protein leads to a small decrease in the microenvironmental polarity around pyrene molecules ($I_{1}/I_{3}$ being 1.72) on CM–AuNPs surfaces. Because of its amphiphilic property, β-casein exhibit a strong tendency to self-assemble with the increase of protein concentration in aqueous solution.<sup>25</sup> Similarly, for CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) and CM–AuNP (4.2 nm), self-assembly of neighboring β-casein molecules occurs and a more hydrophobic domain is formed with the increase of surface density of protein. Therefore, pyrene molecules transfer into a more hydrophobic microenvironment, resulting in the ratio $I_{1}/I_{3}$ being 1.62 and 1.54 for CM–AuNP (4.2 nm) and CM–AuNP (2.8 nm), respectively. Thus, the fluorescence spectra of pyrene provide direct information that the prepared CM–AuNPs with the smaller size has the higher surface intensity of protein.

### 3.2 β-Casein–AuNPs as peroxidase mimetics

The peroxidase-like behavior of the synthesized CM–AuNPs was examined by using 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) as a chromogenic substrate. As the properties of nanomaterials are often dependent on their size, we investigate the peroxidase-like activity of the formed AuNPs synthesized at different β-casein concentrations. We found that the CM–AuNPs of all sizes can catalyze the reaction of the substrate TMB in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to produce a blue color, with maximum absorbance at 652 nm. The control experiments showed that the absorbance of the CM–AuNPs–TMB–H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> system was much higher than that of the TMB–H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> system, and the CM–AuNPs–TMB system had no absorbance at 652 nm. This indicates that both CM–AuNPs and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> are needed for the reaction, which is similar to horseradish peroxidase (HRP). Notably, the above XPS results indicate the resulting AuNPs solution does not include any unreacted species (such as AuCl<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>), so the catalytic activity originates from CM–AuNPs themselves. Fig. 3A and B shows time-dependent absorbance changes at 652 nm of TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and the catalytic relative activity of CM–AuNPs obtained at different concentrations, respectively. The enzyme activity of CM–AuNPs increases, in the order of CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) < CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) < CM–AuNP (4.2 nm). Generally, smaller AuNPs are more active because they have a larger numbers of surface atoms available for catalysis. As expected, among the three types of CM–AuNPs, the catalytic activity for CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) is the lowest due to its largest size. Surprisingly, CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) with the smallest size does not have the lowest enzyme activity. A reasonable explanation for this unique behavior is that the coated protein is affecting the proximity of substrates to the nanozyme core, leading to differences in the enzyme activity of nanozyme.

![Fig. 3 (A) Time-dependent absorbance changes at 652 nm of TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and (B) the catalytic relative activity of CM–AuNPs obtained in the presence of casein with different concentrations.](figure3.png)

Moreover, as shown in Fig. S2A and B,† similar to other nanomaterial-based peroxidase mimics,<sup>1,2</sup> the catalytic activity of CM–AuNPs relies on pH and temperature variation. The optimal pH and temperature are pH 4.0 and 25 °C, respectively. The steady-state kinetics investigated by the initial rate method was adopted to confirm the kinetic parameters for better assessing the peroxidase activity of CM–AuNPs. In a certain range of substrate concentration, typical Michaelis–Menten curves were obtained for both TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Fig. S3†). Michaelis–Menten constant ($K_{m}$) and maximum initial velocity ($V_{max}$) were obtained using Lineweaver–Burk plot,<sup>26</sup> and the results are shown in Table 1.

The apparent $K_{m}$ value of the CM–AuNPs with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> decreases, in the order of CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) > CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) > CM–AuNP (4.2 nm); and the apparent $K_{m}$ value of CM–AuNPs with TMB decreases, in the order of CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) > CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) > CM–AuNP (4.2 nm). The above fluorescence quenching results indicate that the modified protein can increase the affinity of substrate to CM–AuNPs and increase the enzyme activity. But, on the other hand, for CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) formed in the presence of 0.5 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> casein, the excess of surface protein density hinders the proximity of substrates to the nanozyme core.<sup>11,27</sup> This leads to the highest apparent $K_{m}$ value of CM–AuNPs with TMB, and hence a relative low activity of CM–AuNP (2.8 nm). Here, the enzyme activity of CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) depends much on the surface density of the proteins although the nanozyme core is the most active due to the smallest size (Scheme 1a). For CM–AuNP (8.7 nm), the least activity of nanozyme core as well as the relative high $K_{m}$ value of the casein–AuNPs with both TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> leads to the lowest enzyme activity (Scheme 1c). β-Casein–AuNP (4.2 nm) has the lowest apparent $K_{m}$ value of CM–AuNPs with TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, and the appropriate activity of the nanozyme core, resulting in the highest enzyme activity (Scheme 1b). Thus, our results demonstrate that the surface intensity of the modifiers and nanozyme core size are crucial in determining the activity of the nanozyme.

### 3.3 Silver-mediated inhibition on the peroxidase-like activity of β-casein–AuNPs

When a trace amount of Ag<sup>+</sup> was added, the catalytic activity of CM–AuNPs was dramatically inhibited (Fig. 4A). The optical absorption spectra of CM–AuNPs in the absence of (black) and in the presence of Ag<sup>+</sup> (red) are shown in Fig. S4.† Compared with the absorbance of pure CM–AuNPs, the position of the maximum absorption of AuNPs remains almost unchanged, and no significant broadening of the spectrum is observed, which indicates that CM–AuNPs do not experience aggregation into larger nanoparticles upon the addition of silver ions. That is to say, it is not the aggregation of CM–AuNPs that causes the surface properties of AuNPs to change and inhibits its catalytic activity. Furthermore, according to the quenching constant of the interaction between TMB and β-casein with and without Ag<sup>+</sup> (Fig. S5†), we can see that the presence of silver has no obvious influence on the binding of TMB to β-casein. Thus, the inhibition of silver on the activity of CM–AuNPs is mainly caused by the reduced active sites on CM–AuNPs. To confirm this, XPS data were used to study the existing state of silver on the surface of CM–AuNPs after incubating CM–AuNPs with Ag<sup>+</sup>. The peaks in Fig. 5A are attributed to Ag<sup>0</sup> 3d<sub>5/2</sub> (368.2 eV) and Ag<sup>0</sup> 3d<sub>3/2</sub> (374.2 eV), respectively. The peak positions of Ag 3d shift remarkably to the higher binding energy compared with that of Ag<sup>+</sup> (Ag 3d<sub>5/2</sub>, 367.5 eV) and (Ag 3d<sub>3/2</sub>, 373.7 eV), which indicates the reduction of Ag<sup>+</sup> to Ag<sup>0</sup>.

The surface property of AuNPs would influence the interaction between AuNPs and silver. Then, we substituted protein in the AuNP nanozyme system with citrate and cysteamine to evaluate the role of modifiers in the responsive process to silver. The responses of these nanozyme systems to the added silver are measured and shown in Fig. 6. It can be seen that unlike CM–AuNPs, citrate–AuNPs and cysteamine–AuNPs (Cys–AuNPs) show different responses to silver ions. The addition of silver has no influence on the peroxidase-like activity of cysteamine–AuNPs, but enhances the peroxidase-like activity of citrate–AuNPs to some extent. Here, the effect of silver ions on the enzyme activity of citrate–AuNPs is consistent with the earlier studies.<sup>17</sup> Thus, the modified protein is a crucial factor that can enable the nanozyme to respond to silver. The modified protein exhibits high affinity toward metal ions due to the presence of carboxyl, hydroxyl, and amine functional groups, and it can trap Ag<sup>+</sup>.<sup>28,29</sup> Also, some silver ions can interact with the AuNP core by metallic bond.<sup>16</sup> These bound silver ions can then be spontaneously reduced into Ag<sup>0</sup> atoms by protein catalyzed by AuNPs.<sup>28,29</sup> Ag<sup>0</sup> disperses on the surface of AuNPs and changes the surface properties of AuNPs, inhibiting their peroxidase-like activity (Scheme 2b).

The high specificity of silver and CM–AuNP interaction provides excellent selectivity toward Ag<sup>+</sup> over other metal ions. Fig. 4B shows that only Ag<sup>+</sup> can significantly inhibit the peroxidase-like activity of CM–AuNPs. In the absence of Ag<sup>+</sup>, none of Fe<sup>3+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Mn<sup>2+</sup>, Ni<sup>2+</sup>, Co<sup>2+</sup>, Cu<sup>2+</sup>, Zn<sup>2+</sup>, K<sup>+</sup>, Na<sup>+</sup> and Pb<sup>2+</sup> causes a detectable decrease in absorbance. In the presence of Hg<sup>2+</sup> ion, the absorption signals of the sensor solutions without Ag<sup>+</sup> increased dramatically, which is similar to citrate–AuNPs. Addition of low concentration of EDTA can mask the interference of Hg<sup>2+</sup>, while it shows little effect on the signal generated by Ag<sup>+</sup> (Fig. S6†). Therefore, quantitative detection of Ag<sup>+</sup> is also feasible in the presence of Hg<sup>2+</sup>.

Under the optimized conditions, CM–AuNPs were used to catalyze the TMB–H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> reaction after reaction with different concentrations of Ag<sup>+</sup>. Fig. 4C shows the gradual decrease at 652 nm of the TMB system when the concentration of Ag<sup>+</sup> increases from 0 to 10 μM. The absorbance at 652 nm against Ag<sup>+</sup> was linear in a range from 0.1 to 8.0 μM with a correlation coefficient of 0.999 (Fig. 4D). The limit of detection for Ag<sup>+</sup> (signal-to-noise ratio = 3) was calculated to be 10.0 nM. On the basis of the above results, the practicality of this method for the detection of Ag<sup>+</sup> in Slender West Lake water near our university was evaluated. Using the standard addition method, different concentrations of Ag<sup>+</sup> were respectively spiked in lake water samples and then analyzed with the method. As shown in Table S1,† the recoveries of the measurement were 96–107%, which suggest that this method would be suitable for the detection of Ag<sup>+</sup> in lake water.

We further investigate the effect of Ag<sup>+</sup> (silver concentration less than 2.0 μM) on the peroxidase-like activity of CM–AuNPs formed in the presence of different protein concentration. Fig. 7A–C shows the effect of Ag<sup>+</sup> concentration on the peroxidase-like activity of CM–AuNPs with different sizes. As shown, the inhibition effect of Ag<sup>+</sup> on the peroxidase-like activity of CM–AuNP (slope) strengthens, in the order of CM–AuNP (4.2 nm) < CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) < CM–AuNP (8.7 nm). Although CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) has the lowest peroxidase-like activity, the inhibition effect of Ag<sup>+</sup> on the peroxidase-like activity of CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) is the strongest.

As discussed above, CM–AuNPs catalyze the reduction of silver ions on AuNP surface by protein, which leads to the reduced active sites and peroxidase-like activity of CM–AuNPs. For CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) with the largest size among the three types of CM–AuNP, the interaction between protein and AuNP is the strongest and the protein can be fully contacted with AuNPs. Therefore, the bound silver ions are effectively reduced to Ag<sup>0</sup> with the help of protein, resulting in an effective inhibited activity of CM–AuNP (Scheme 2c). For CM–AuNP (4.2 nm) and CM–AuNP (2.8 nm), the interaction between protein and AuNP weakens, and protein molecules are not in contact with AuNPs fully with the decrease of nanozyme core size, so some of the chelated silver ions cannot be in proximity to the AuNP core and be reduced to Ag<sup>0</sup> effectively. XPS results show that the peak Ag 3d<sub>5/2</sub> for CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) system is about 0.2 eV higher than that for CM–AuNP (4.2 nm) and CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) systems, confirming the more effective reduction of silver ions for CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) system (data not shown). Thus, the inhibition potency by silver ions for CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) is stronger than that for CM–AuNP (4.2 nm) and CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) (Scheme 2). In addition, compared with CM–AuNP (4.2 nm), CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) has the higher activity to catalyze the reduction of silver ions by protein because of its smaller size, so Ag<sup>+</sup> inhibits the peroxidase-like activity of CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) more effectively. Notably, among the three types of CM–AuNP, the effect of silver on the activity of CM–AuNP (4.2 nm) exhibits the lowest slope, but the broadest linear range of 0.1 to 8.0 μM. Therefore, the silver sensor based on CM–AuNP (4.2 nm) is more suitable for use in practice.

Unexpectedly, LOD of the silver sensor based on the three types of CM–AuNP increases, in the order of CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) < CM–AuNP (4.2 nm) < CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) (Fig. 7D). Although the Ag<sup>+</sup> sensitivity (slope) of CM–AuNP (8.7 nm) is the highest (Fig. 7A), LOD of this sensor (20 nM) is two times higher than that of CM–AuNP (2.8 nm) and CM–AuNP (4.2 nm). This indicates that CM–AuNP has significantly different ability to catalyze the reduction of Ag<sup>+</sup> with different Ag<sup>+</sup> concentration. Here, CM–AuNP (4.2 nm) was used as a model to demonstrate the existing state of silver on the surface of CM–AuNPs after incubating CM–AuNPs with Ag<sup>+</sup> of different concentration. Compared with the XPS results shown in Fig. 5A, the peak Ag 3d<sub>5/2</sub> shifts from 368.2 to 367.9 eV with the increase of the added silver concentration (Fig. 5B). This suggests that some silver ions cannot be reduced to Ag<sup>0</sup> with the increase of silver concentration. At low silver concentration, the bound silver ions can be effectively reduced to Ag<sup>0</sup> with the help of protein, resulting in the peak Ag 3d<sub>5/2</sub> of 368.2 eV. With the increase of silver concentration, some of silver ions chelated by proteins cannot be in proximity to the AuNP core and be reduced to Ag<sup>0</sup> effectively, which makes the peak Ag 3d<sub>5/2</sub> shifts from 368.2 to 367.9 eV (Scheme 2b). Accordingly, at extreme low silver concentration, the bound silver ions are all in proximity to the AuNP core, so the catalysis effect of AuNPs on the reduction of silver ions by proteins depends mainly on the AuNP size. Thus, the reduction of silver ions by proteins weakens, which in turn makes LOD increase with the increase of the AuNP size. Here, silver mediated-inhibition to the nanozyme activity also depends on both the AuNP size and the surface density of protein.

## 4. Conclusion

This work develops a simple protein/gold nanoparticle based nanozyme to investigate the peroxidase-like activity and silver-mediated inhibition dependent on the structure of nanozyme. The structure–activity correlation and the fundamental reasons for the corresponding correlation were deduced through UV-vis absorption, transmission electron microscopy, Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy and fluorescence quenching analysis. The protein concentration greatly affects the physicochemical properties including particle sizes and surface protein density of CM–AuNPs. The most efficient CM–AuNPs nanozyme, consisting of AuNP with moderate size and surface protein of moderate density, exhibits the $K_{m}$ value of 0.023 mM toward TMB and 130 mM toward hydrogen peroxide. The peroxidase-like activity of CM–AuNPs can be inhibited by Ag<sup>+</sup> in aqueous solution, mainly through the reduction of Ag<sup>+</sup> by the modified protein on AuNP surfaces. CM–AuNPs based nanozyme is employed to explore colorimetric detection of Ag<sup>+</sup> with the limit of detection of 10 nM. More importantly, the responds towards silver ions also depends on the structure properties of CM–AuNPs. For CM–AuNP with the lowest protein density and the largest AuNP size, silver-mediated inhibition potency is the strongest, but LOD of silver ion is the highest. Thus, different from citrate–AuNPs, silver ion selectively inhibits the catalytic activity of β-casein–AuNPs due to the microenvironment provide by the coated protein. This indicates that the modifier of nanozymes plays important roles in determining the enzyme activity and metal ions-mediated inhibition. Here, the experimental results obtained help in understanding the strongly structure dependence of peroxidase-like activity and inhibition of nanozyme. This is central to the exploitation and optimization of protein/metal nanoparticle hybrid based nanozyme for various applications.
