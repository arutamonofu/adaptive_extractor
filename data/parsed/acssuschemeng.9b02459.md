**ABSTRACT:** Si doped CoO nanorods (denoted as Si-CoO) were prepared by the hydrothermal method. The Si-CoO composites were characterized by X-ray diffraction (XRD), transmission electron microscopy (TEM), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and X-ray photoelectron spectra (XPS), etc. The results suggest that Si element uniformly distributed onto CoO nanorods with a diameter of 50-100 nm. The as-prepared Si-CoO nanocomposite possessed an excellent peroxidase-like activity as well as quickly catalyzed the colorless substrate 3,3,5,5-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) to be oxidized into blue oxTMB by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> only in 20 s, which was easily visually observed. The electron spin resonance (ESR) data confirms that superoxide radicals (•O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>) play a key function in catalytic reaction. On the basis of the peroxidase biomimetic activity of Si-CoO, we constructed a new biosensor for detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and reduced glutathione (GSH) in the range of 1-100 μM with a limit of 0.22 and 0.45 μM, respectively.

**KEYWORDS:** SiO<sub>2</sub>; CoO; GSH; Peroxidase; Biosensor

**INTRODUCTION**

Natural enzymes, especially horseradish peroxidase (HRP), have been widely used for detecting biomacromolecule due to their excellent selectivity and sensitivity.<sup>1-2</sup> However, the high-cost and low stability in rigorous conditions obviously limited their industrial application.<sup>3</sup> Thus, a large number of cheap, stable and efficient artificial nanozymes have been exploited. Among these candidates, metal oxide-based mimics have been focused on, such as MoO<sub>3</sub>/C,<sup>4</sup> CoO,<sup>5</sup> MnO<sub>2</sub>,<sup>6</sup> NiO,<sup>7</sup> V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub><sup>8</sup> and CuO<sup>9</sup>, etc. With the development of peroxidase mimics, scientists further prepared composites to substitute natural peroxidases, including porphyrin modified oxides,<sup>10-11</sup> Porphyrin-based porous organic frameworks,<sup>12-13</sup> perylene diimide functionalized oxides,<sup>14</sup> some polymer-based nanomaterials,<sup>15</sup> and noble composites,<sup>16</sup> etc. Comparied with natural enzymes, artificial nanomimics exhibit more stability in harsh conditions (pH and temperature), and easy preparation.<sup>17</sup> Nevertheless, metal oxides as biomimetic nanoenzymes exhibited some disadvantages, including not only low stability and anti-poisoning ability, but also easier accumulation in solution,<sup>18</sup> significantly resulting in decreasing the activity of the nanoenzymes. Therefore, how to improve the catalytic performance of the artificial mimics is an urgent task.

Recently, as a typical p-type semiconductor, cobaltous oxide (CoO) with different morphologies, is widely used in lithium storage, catalysts, gas sensors, and magnetic materials, etc.,<sup>19-22</sup> due to its excellent electron transfer ability.<sup>23-24</sup> Nevertheless, CoO nanoparticles were inevitably apt to aggregation, which restricted its catalytic performance. To solve the aggregation of nanoparticles, some supports have been employed to prepare nanocomposites,<sup>25-26</sup> as well as coating on the surface of nanoparticles to improve the dispersity of nanoparticles in water. However, as far as the later concerned, the catalytic activity of the coated nanoparticles by silicon<sup>27</sup> or polymer<sup>28</sup> was dramatically decreased, due to reducing of active sites. Thus, doping is a popular method which can not only solve the aggregation of nanopartciles but also retain activity sites on the surface of nanoparticles. Moreover, the solubility of Si doped nano-oxides was greatly enhanced,<sup>29</sup> which was very important in study of biological fields. Based on the ideas on my mind, if CoO nanorods is doped with silicon in the process of preparation, the obtained Si doped CoO nanoparticles as peroxidase mimics will exhibit a highly catalytic activity. What’s more, rich oxygen vacancies greatly improved the catalytic activity of Si-CoO.

Herein, Si doped CoO nanorods (Si-CoO) were prepared by a hydrothermal method. The CoO nanorods doped with silicon possessed much excellent peroxidase performance, compared with that of pure CoO. The enhanced catalytic activity of Si-CoO is attributed to increasing of oxygen vacancy. Based on the peroxdase-like activity of Si-CoO, a colorimetric method was constructed for highly sensitive determination of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and glutathione (GSH) with high selectivity, respectively. ESR data reveal the catalytic mechanism is from superoxide radicals (•O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>).

**EXPERIMENTAL SECTION**

**Reagents and Materials.** Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)·6H<sub>2</sub>O, urea, tetraethoxysilane (TEOS), H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (30 wt%), various metal salts, ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid disodium salt (EDTA-2Na) and isopropyl alcohol (IPA) were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd (Shanghai, China). 3,3,5,5-tetramethylbenzidine dihydrochloride (TMB·2HCl) was purchased from Shanghai Macklin Biochemical Technology Co. Ltd. D-Leucine (Leu), Urio acid (UA), DL-ISOLeucine (ISO), L-Arginine (Arg), DL-Tryptophan (Try), D-Histidine (His) and glutathione were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Co. LLC. (Saint Louis, USA). The medical GSH pills (93.34%) was produced by Shandong Luye Pharmaceutical Co. All chemicals were analytical. Ultra-pure water was from Lichun water purification system.

**Apparatus.** The morphology of Si-CoO nanorods were systematically investigated by FE-SEM (BRUKER XFlash6100), TEM and HRTEM (JEOL JEM-2100F) and the elemental images were detected by using energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX). XRD patterns of the samples were obtained from a Rigaku X-ray diffractimeter with Cu Kα radiation (λ=1.5406 Å), and the 2θ range varying from 10° to 80°. The elementary valence state and composition were revealed by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) on a PHI Quantera SXM spectrometer with an Al Kα radiator (Thermo escalab 250XI, USA). The absorbance was recorded on UV-1810PC spectrophotometer (Shanghai, China). The production of O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> radical was detected by electron spin resonance (ESR) on a Bruker ESR A200 spectrometer with DMPO as the spin trap. Heat treatment was conducted in a tube furnace with a quartz tube (OTF-1200X, China). Contact angles were detected by DSA100 (Kruss, Germany).

**Synthesis of Si-CoO.** Si-CoO nanorods were prepared based on the publication with a minor modification.<sup>30</sup> Cobalt nitrate (0.6926 g), urea (0.6 g) and deionized water (50 mL) with different volume TEOS (50, 100, 150, 200, 250, 300 and 400 μL) were mixed thoroughly, transferred into Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclaves (100 mL) at 100 °C for 3 h. Then, the raw products were centrifuged and washed with pure water and ethanol in turn for 5 times. After that, the raw products were heated in nitrogen atmosphere from 20 to 500 °C and heating at 500 °C for 2 h with a ramp rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>.

**Peroxidase-like activity and steady-state kinetic assay of Si-CoO**

The peroxidase-like activity of Si-CoO was evaluated by catalyzing the oxidation of the chromogenic substrate TMB by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The maximum absorbance of oxTMB was tested at 652 nm.<sup>31</sup> Typically, 0.3 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> of Si-CoO was mixed in acetate buffer solution (1.4 mL, pH 5.0), followed by adding of 200 μL of 1 mM TMB solution and 200 μL of 0.25 M H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. After 3 min, the absorbance of reaction systems were measured by the UV-vis absorption spectroscopy. All the reactions were processed at room temperature under the conditions mentioned above unless otherwise stated.

The kinetic test of Si-CoO was performed by varying concentration of TMB while keeping H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration unchanged and vice versa. A series of initial reaction rates were calculated according to the Michaelis-Menten equation, $1/v = (K_m / V_{max}) \times (1/[S]) + 1/V_{max}$, where $K_m$ is the Michaelis constant, $v$ is the rate of conversion, $V_{max}$ is the maximum rate of conversion, and $[S]$ is the substrate concentration.

**Detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and GSH**

Based on the peroxidase performance of Si-CoO, a colorimetric detection platform was designed for detecting H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and GSH, respectively. The typical procedure of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection is as follows: 200 μL of 0.3 mg/mL Si-CoO, 200 μL H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with varying concentrations and 200 μL of 1mM TMB were incubated into buffer solution (1.4 mL, pH=5) and then tested the absorbance of the mixture after 3 min. The detection of GSH is as follows: It took 3 min for the mixed solution containing 200 μL of 0.3 mg/mL Si-CoO, 200 μL H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, 200 μL of 1 mM TMB and 1.4 mL acetate buffer solution (pH=5) to react. Immediately, add 200 μL of GSH with different concentrations into the above mixture, and measure on a UV-vis spectrophotometer.

**Active species capturing.**

The superoxide radicals (•O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>), holes (h<sup>+</sup>) and hydroxyl radicals (•OH) were probed by using sacrificial agents such as P-Benzoquinone (PBQ), 2-propanol (IPA) and disodium ethylene diamine tetraacetic acid (EDTA). This method is similar to detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, sacrificial agents are added to reaction systems before to add H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, respectively. Furthermore, •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> and •OH are captured by the 5, 5-dimethyl-1-pyrolin-N-oxide (DMPO) which can be devoted to detect radicals by ESR technique. Before determining two kinds of radicals (DMPO-•O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> and DMPO-•OH), 3 mg·Si-CoO, 1 mL H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and 240 μL DMPO were dissolve in 10 mL methanol or DI water and reacted for 3 min.

**RESULTS AND DISCUSSION**

**Characterization of Si-CoO**

Fig. 1 shows the XRD of pure CoO and Si-CoO, respectively. The peaks appeared at 2θ = 36.503°, 42.401°, 61.520°, 73.700° and 77.562° are corresponding to the crystal faces (111), (200), (220), (311) and (222) of CoO (JCPDS 43-1004). Notably, it is obviously that the peaks of Si-CoO are consistent with that of pure CoO, suggesting that the crystal form of CoO in the silicon doped CoO nanorods is not changed.

![Fig. 1 XRD patterns of CoO (a) and Si-CoO (b), respectively.](figure1_placeholder)

**Fig. 1** XRD patterns of CoO (a) and Si-CoO (b), respectively.

Furthermore, Fig. 2 shows XPS data can be confirmed the existence of Si element in the nanocomposite. The survey spectrum (Fig. 2a) shows that Si-CoO contain Si, O, and Co elements. Besides, Fig. 2b displays that two typical satellite peaks at 797.6 and 780.9 eV are attributed to the Co<sup>2+</sup>. As shown in Fig. 2c, it can be seen that one peak at 102.7 eV is attributed to Si 2p, similar to the reported result of SiO<sub>2</sub> (103.4 eV).<sup>32</sup> The little change of binding energy is caused by the higher electronegativity of Si in comparison with Co, indicating the existence of Co-O-Si bond. Moreover, as can be found in Fig. 2d, the high-resolution XPS spectrum of O1s shows three peaks (O1, O2 and O3) by deconvolution. The O1 peak at 532.5 eV is assigned to the lattice oxygen atoms of Si-O-Si bond, and the peak at 529.7 eV (O3) corresponds to the metal oxygen bond of Co-O-Co. Notably, the peak at 531.5 eV (O2) is attributed to oxygen defects from CoO.<sup>33</sup> Futhermore, compared with that in Fig. S2 (supporting information), the peak area for oxygen defects is obviously increased after doping Si, which is very important in enhancing the excellent catalytic activity of silicon doped CoO in the subsequent experiments. Based on the data described above, it is verified that the Si-CoO has been successfully prepared.<sup>34-35</sup>

![Fig. 2 XPS spectra of Si-CoO: survey spectra (a), Co 2p (b), Si 2p (c), and O1s (d), respectively.](figure2_placeholder)

**Fig. 2** XPS spectra of Si-CoO: survey spectra (a), Co 2p (b), Si 2p (c), and O1s (d), respectively.

Fig. 3 shows the morphology of the Si-CoO imaged by TEM and SEM, respectively. As displayed in Fig. 3a and 3c, it can be found that the Si-CoO nanorods with a width of 50-100 nm, are composed with lots of nanoparticles with ca. 15 nm in size. From Fig. 3b, it is clearly seen that the lattice fringe with ca. 0.21 nm in d-spacing, corresponds to the (111) lattice plane of CoO.<sup>19, 36</sup> Compared with pure CoO (Fig. S3, supporting information), the mophology of nanocomposites is negligible change, although silicon element is introduced in the process of preparation. Furthermore, the Co, Si and O are detected in the nanocmposites, confirming the formation of Si-CoO.

**Fig. 3** TEM (a), HRTEM (b), SEM images (c) and EDX mappings (d) of Si-CoO, respectively.

Generally, good surface wettability facilitates the dispersion in solution as well as the increase of the catalytic activity.<sup>37</sup> The contact angles were tested to compare the hydrophilic property of CoO nanorods with and without Si doping. As shown in Fig. 4, the contact angles of CoO and Si-CoO are 37.5° and 27.6°, respectively. It is suggested that the hydrophilic performance of Si-CoO is developed, due to the doping of Si.

![Fig. 4 Hydrophilic performance of CoO (a) and Si-CoO (b), respectively.](figure4_placeholder)

**Fig. 4** Hydrophilic performance of CoO (a) and Si-CoO (b), respectively.

Fig. S1 shows UV-vis diffuse reflection spectra (DRS) of CoO and Si-CoO, respectively. As shown in Fig. S1a, pure CoO (curve a) exhibits a significant absorption from ultraviolet to visible light region, while Si-CoO (red line) shows a slight weak. According to the Rauc plot equation, the band gap energy of Si-CoO is calculated to be 2.43 eV, similar to that of pure CoO (Fig. S1b), consistent with the previous results.<sup>38</sup> Combined with XPS data, it is demonstrated that Si doping changes the electronic structure of CoO and thereby improves the separation and transportation photoexcited charge carries.<sup>30</sup>

**Peroxidase-like activity of Si-CoO.**

To investigate the peroxidase mimetic activity of Si-CoO, TMB is chosen as chromogenic substrate in the oxidation reaction, displayed in Fig. 5. As shown in the figure, no obvious color change is distinguished in the absence (curve a and c) and presence (curve b) of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> before adding Si-CoO into solutions. Notably, TMB isn’t also oxidized without H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, though Si-CoO is added into the reaction system, indicating no oxidase-like activity of Si-CoO (curve d). From curve e in Fig. 5, it is clearly seen that the TMB is easily oxidized in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, suggesting that pure CoO possesses the peroxidase-like activity. Furthermore, from curve f, a much more obvious blue change is quickly observed, once Si-CoO is added in the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>/TMB system, verifying that the catalytic activity of Si-CoO is greatly improved, owing to Si doping. In fact, after Si-CoO was added into the TMB-H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> system for ca. 10 s, an obvious blue change was observed visually, suggesting the fast oxidation of TMB into oxTMB catalyzed by Si-CoO.<sup>39</sup> In brief, the peroxidase-like activity of Si-CoO is dramatically increased, compared with that of CoO.

**Influences of temperature, pH and the amount of TEOS**

Similar to the natural peroxidase (HRP) and some reported artificial peroxidase mimics,<sup>40-41</sup> temperature, pH together with the amount of TEOS obviously affect the catalytic performance of Si-CoO. As can be seen from Fig. 6a, the relative activity of Si-CoO quickly increases and then drastically reduces in the pH range from 2 to 7. As a result, the maximum of catalytic activity of Si-CoO achieves at pH 5. Thus, the weaker acidic environment is in favor of the catalytic activity of Si-CoO. As we know, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is easily decomposed into H<sub>2</sub>O and O<sub>2</sub> under the basic condition, causing the dramatic decrease of the amount of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and further resulting in the reduction of the catalytic performance of the composites in the reaction system. Obviously, as displayed in Fig. 6b, the Si-CoO kept high activity (>80%) in the temperature range of 15 - 40 °C, just fitting with the ambient temperature together with the body temperature, which is critical to apply Si-CoO in fields of environment and biology. Besides, the catalytic activity of Si-CoO reaches the maximum when the amount of TEOS is 150 μL. In general, the doped Si is in the form of SiO<sub>2</sub> with no catalytic performance. However, it is found that the moderate amount of TEOS develops the catalytic activity of CoO, owing to increasing of oxygen vacancy together with the water solubility. As the concentration of TEOS increasing, the catalytic activity of Si-CoO is reduced, owing to the absence of active sites in Si-CoO nanocomposites. Based on these results, the temperature 25 °C and pH 5 are selected for the subsequent experiments.

**Steady-state kinetic analysis of Si-CoO.**

To further investigate the peroxidase-like activity of Si-CoO, kinetic assay was performed. Fig. 7 shows that the catalytic behaviors are consistent with the typical Michaelis-Menten kinetics and double-reciprocal Lineweaver-Burk plots (inset of Fig. 7a and 7b) using TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrates. As shown in Table 1, the value of K<sub>m</sub> and V<sub>max</sub> for Si-CoO with TMB as the substrate are 0.0589 mM and 2.50×10<sup>−7</sup> Ms<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. For comparative investigates, some previous report results, such as Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, SiO<sub>2</sub>@Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> are listed in Table 1.<sup>42-45</sup> Notably, a significant lower or similar K<sub>m</sub> of the Si-CoO, implies that Si-CoO has a great affinity with TMB. On the other hand, the value of K<sub>m</sub> and V<sub>max</sub> for Si-CoO with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> are 0.023 mM and 1.04×10<sup>−8</sup> Ms<sup>−1</sup>, lower than that of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (140.07 mM),<sup>42</sup> Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (173.51 mM),<sup>43</sup> SiO<sub>2</sub>@ Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (25.2 mM)<sup>44</sup> and Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CeO<sub>2</sub> (7.09 mM),<sup>45</sup> indicating that less H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is required to achieve catalytic activity. To comparative study of the catalytic performance of mimic enzymes, k<sub>cat</sub>/K<sub>m</sub> value were also estimated. The larger the k<sub>cat</sub>/K<sub>m</sub> value, the stronger the peroxidase-like activity of mimic enzymes. From the data listed in Table 1, k<sub>cat</sub>/K<sub>m</sub> value of Si-CoO is higher than that of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and SiO<sub>2</sub>@Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. As a result, Si-CoO exhibits much better catalytic activity.

**Detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and GSH using Si-CoO as enzyme mimic.**

H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is one of the small molecule reactive oxygen species (ROS) in living organisms and a universal mark of oxidative stess.<sup>46,28</sup> Therefore, the detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is an important task. Based on the catalytic performance of Si-CoO as peroxidase mimic, convenient and ultrasensitive colorimetric detection systems of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and GSH are designed without using sophisticated instruments. Fig. 8. shows the response curves of the typical absorbance at 652 nm with various concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and GSH, respectively. As shown in Fig. 8a, the linear range of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration is in the range of 2-10 μM with the limit of detection (LOD) of 4.32×10<sup>−6</sup> M.

As a almighty protector, GSH plays a significance role for some protective enzymes including GSH peroxidase, glyoxalase and GSH S-transferases.<sup>47</sup> Besides, GSH can also protect cells against electrophiles and oxidative damage due to ROS that related to numerous clinical diseases (e.g., HIV, cancer, diabetes, Alzheimer’s disease).<sup>48-50</sup> Therefore, it’s meaningful to fast and accurate to detect GSH.<sup>51-52</sup> GSH reacted with oxTMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which caused the fading blue color, owing to its an excellent reducibility. As shown in Fig. 8b, the ΔA (ΔA = A<sub>blank</sub> - A<sub>GSH</sub>) increases with increasing of concentration of GSH, suggesting that the blue oxTMB was gradually changed to colorless TMB. It can be obtained that linear response of GSH is in the range of 1-5 μM, with a detection limit of 0.45 × 10<sup>−6</sup> M lower than that of Co-MOCP/CPE, Probe 1, NiS/MMT/GO, and MoS<sub>2</sub>/rGO, reported in Table 2.<sup>53-56</sup>

**Catalytic mechanism of Si-CoO**

The main active species formed in the Si-CoO/H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> system were determined for investigating the catalytic mechanism of Si-CoO. Three sacrificial agents including P-Benzoquinone (PBQ), 2-propanol (IPA) and disodium ethylene diamine tetraacetic acid (EDTA) are used to capture •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>, •OH and h<sup>+</sup>, respectively. It is obviously that the decrease of relative activity of Si-CoO is along with adding of PBQ, suggesting that •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> plays a major role in the catalytic system (Fig. 11).

![Fig. 11 Trapping experiments of active species during the catalysis of TMB over Si-CoO.](figure11_placeholder)

**Fig. 11** Trapping experiments of active species during the catalysis of TMB over Si-CoO.

In addition, DMPO spin trapping ESR spectrum further confirms the generation of •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> and •OH in the Si-CoO/H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> system. As shown in Fig. 12, when the Si-CoO/H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> system is exposed in the visible light, the signal intensity is stronger than that in dark. And the signal of •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> is more obvious than that of •OH either in dark or light (Fig. 12). As a result, three active species co-existed in Si-CoO/H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> system, and the •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> playes a major role in the catalytic activity of Si-CoO. According to the previous report,<sup>56</sup> the photogenerated charge carriers migrate to the surface of Si-CoO with a reduced recombination rate with holes and then a series of redox reactions occur at the solid-liquid interface. Based on the above results, the main reactions involved in the catalytic system can be concluded as follows:

O<sub>2</sub> + e<sub>CB</sub><sup>−</sup> → •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> (1)

H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + hν → 2•OH (2)

H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + h<sub>VB</sub><sup>+</sup> → 2•OH (3)

•O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> + 2H<sup>+</sup> + e<sub>CB</sub><sup>−</sup> → 2•OH (4)

•O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>, h<sup>+</sup>, •OH + TMB → oxTMB (5)

**CONCLUSION**

In summary, silicon doped CoO nanorods was successfully prepared and confirmed to possess the enhanced peroxidase-like activity. The superior catalytic activity of Si-CoO are ascribed to co-existence of •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>, •OH and h<sup>+</sup> in the TMB/H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>/Si-CoO reaction system. Based on the excellent peroxidase-like activity, stability and reusability of Si-CoO, a highly sensitive and selective colorimetric sensor was established for convenient detection of GSH with 0.45 μM of LOD. It is anticipated that our work will facilitate Si-CoO as the peroxidase mimic to be applied in bio-sensing, food and environment detection.
