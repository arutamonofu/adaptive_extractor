# Simultaneous enzymatic and SERS properties of bifunctional chitosan-modified popcorn-like Au-Ag nanoparticles for high sensitive detection of melamine in milk powder

### ABSTRACT

In this work, we suggest a chitosan-modified popcorn-like Au–Ag nanoparticles (CSPNPs) based assay for high sensitive detection of melamine, in which CSPNPs not only provide with an intrinsic peroxidase-like activity but also act as surface enhanced Raman scattering (SERS) substrates. CSPNPs can catalyze the oxidation of 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to the charge transfer complex (CTC), which contributes to a tremendous surface-enhanced resonant Raman scattering (SERRS) signals with 632.8 nm laser excitation. The target molecule melamine can generate an additional compound with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which means the available amount of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> for the oxidation of TMB reduced. Correspondingly, the SERRS intensity of CTC is decreased. The decreased Raman intensity is proportional to the concentration of melamine over a wide range from 10 nM to 50 μM ($R^2 = 0.989$), with a limit of detection (LOD) of 8.51 nM. Moreover, the proposed highly selective method is fully capable of rapid, separation-free detection of melamine in milk powder.

## 1. Introduction

Surface-enhanced Raman scattering (SERS) is a promising spectroscopic technique, which greatly extends the role of standard Raman Spectroscopy. In recent years, SERS spectroscopy has attracted enormous interest in the field of molecular multiplex detection [1], pathogen and live cell sensing [2], tissues diagnosis and in vivo bioimaging [3]. One of the advantages of SERS spectroscopy is that it can provide sufficient sensitivity for trace analysis. Taking advantage of the SERS signal of 4-mercaptobenzoic acid (4-MBA), Chen et al. [4] successfully detected rabbit IgG with the limit of detection of 1–0.1 fg/mL. In our group, α-thrombin was sensitively detected with the detection limit of 10 fM [5]. SERS intensity can be enhanced when the excitation-lasers’ length matches the optical absorption of Raman reporters. This phenomenon is called surface enhanced resonance Raman scattering (SERRS), which is able to make the enhancement factor increase about 100 times [6]. Furthermore, SERS substrates, acting as Raman signal amplifiers, their size distribution, geometry, chemical composition and surface chemistry produce an effect on the Raman enhancement ability. Gold (Au) and silver (Ag) nanoparticles (NPs) are the most commonly used Raman enhancing platforms. Various shapes and sizes of Au and Ag NPs [7–11] have been synthesized, such as nanospheres, nanoshells, nanorods and multibranched metal NPs. The formation of “hot spots” in the tips of multibranched metal NPs increases tremendously the Raman signal [12]. Besides, the increased surface area relative to that of a sphere of equivalent size allows more Raman reporter molecules to be attached on multibranched metal NPs, which give rise to a further increase of Raman signal. Typically, surfactants [7, 9, 13, 14], such as cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB), poly ethylene glycol (PEG) and poly (N-vinylpyrrolidone) (PVP), were necessary for the formation of multibranched NPs. However, the potential toxicity and difficulty of replacing these surfactants have a negative impact on their further applications.

Au NPs can not only act as excellent SERS substrates, but also have been demonstrated to serve as catalysts for a number of reactions under a range of experimental conditions. It has been demonstrated that when dispersed as ultrafine particles and supported on metal oxides, Au exhibits an extraordinary high activity for low-temperature catalytic reactions [15]. The smaller Au NPs acting as oxidase have been utilized to catalyze glucose oxidation [16]. Ramanavicien [17] and his co-workers demonstrated that Au NPs can act as redox mediator for glucose oxidase in a spectrophotometrical method. Their results indicated that electrons from GOx via Au NPs were transferred towards 2, 6-dichloroindophenol sodiumsalt hydrate (DCPIP) effectively, which was comparable with electron transfer via redox mediator N-methylphenazonium methyl sulfate (PMS) at some conditions. Other researchers have reported that Au NPs showed desirable catalytic ability towards some heterocyclic compounds as well. In our group [18], the synergetic effect between small Au NPs and 2,5-dimercapto-1,3,4-thiadiazole (DMcT)–Ag infinite coordination polymers (ICPs) was also proved to be provided with high catalytic activity towards the degradation of methylene blue even without adding cosubstrate. Xiong et al. [19] discovered that the degradation of Rhodamine B (RhB) was remarkably enhanced in the presence of graphene–Au nanocomposites.

In addition to catalyze the above mentioned chemicals, Au (or Au–Ag alloy) NPs can also serve as peroxidase mimetics to catalyze the oxidation of 2,2′-azinobis 3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid (ABTS) [20], 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) [21] and o-phenylenediamine (OPD) [22] in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Recently, Jv et al. [23] made a surprising discovery that positively-charged Au NPs with the average size of 34 nm possess an intrinsic peroxidase-like activity. Many peroxidase mimetics, including BSA-stabilized Au clusters [21], CuS NPs [24], water disposable silicon dots [25] and CoFe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> magnetic NPs [26], have been reported. The enzyme mimetics can overcome the drawbacks of nature enzymes, such as the catalytic ability and stability are susceptible to environmental conditions [27].

Melamine, with a chemical formula of C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>6</sub>N<sub>4</sub>, is commonly used in the synthesis of melamine formaldehyde resins for producing plastics, coatings, adhesives, laminates and commercial filters [28]. Unfortunately, melamine was once illegally added to dairy products to make a misleading result of high-level total nitrogen content, which can lead to the formation of insoluble crystals in kidneys causing the renal failure and even death. Classical detection methods for melamine, such as mass spectrometry (MS) [29], gas chromatography/mass spectrometry (GC–MS) [30], liquid chromatography/mass spectrometry (LC–MS) [31], and enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) [32], inevitably need time-consuming sample pretreatments and sophisticated instruments. Recently, more simple and sensitive methods in the field of spectroscopy have been created. Some groups detected melamine based on a colorimetric visual inspection of the NPs solution color change upon melamine interaction [33–36]. Sadly, the selectivity could not be ensured using the bare NPs because the existing interference substances can make a false positive response, while the modified NPs need tedious modification processes. Also, SERS spectroscopy has been explored in the detection of melamine. On one hand, the enhancement of Raman signal of melamine was realized through depositing NPs on solid materials [37–39], which usually suffered from lack of reproducibility due to the poor homogeneity of molecular distribution on the SERS substrates. On the other hand, SERS analysis of melamine has been demonstrated in liquid. Good result was obtained utilizing Ag NPs as SERS substrates with directly detecting the Raman signal of melamine [40].

On the other hand, a sensitive indirect method to detect melamine in milk via SERS was developed by using Au NPs as SERS substrates [41], but the linearity response of this method limited the practical application in routine assay.

The combination of SERS with peroxidase-catalyzed reaction will open up new opportunities for high sensitive detection of analytes. Recently, Yu et al. [42] successfully detected the human cardiac isoform of troponin T (cTnT) in human serum with a wider linear range and a more sensitive detection limit than traditional chromogenic tests by a method incorporated SERS technique and an enzyme catalysis-based bioassay. Ding et al. [43] detected melamine with Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> magnetic nanoparticles (MNPs) acting as peroxidase mimetics, which was built on the reaction between H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and melamine. However, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> MNPs only serve as catalysts and the detection limit is not low enough. In the present work, the chitosan-modified popcorn-like Au–Ag nanoparticles (CSPNPs) can be conveniently synthesized at room temperature without need of seeds and surfactants. The as-prepared CSPNPs possess highly intrinsic peroxidase mimetic catalytic ability for the oxidation of 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> producing charge transfer complex (CTC), which is most likely due to the following facts: the catalytic ability of Au in the NPs, the positively charged property [23] endowed by CS and high surface area of this new nanostructure. The proposed CSPNPs play a dual role of SERS substrates and peroxidase mimetics. Since melamine can generate an additional compound with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, the addition of melamine will lead to the decrease of the SERRS intensity of CTC. Thus, melamine can be determined indirectly. This work provides a simple, sensitive and selective method for the determination of melamine in milk powder.

## 2. Experimental

### 2.1. Chemicals and reagents

HAuCl<sub>4</sub> · 3H<sub>2</sub>O was purchased from Sigma. Ascorbic acid, silver nitrate, glucose, lactose, CaCl<sub>2</sub> · 2H<sub>2</sub>O, KCl, NH<sub>4</sub>Cl, ZnCl<sub>2</sub>, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (30 wt%) and melamine were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. All the above chemicals were analytical reagent grade and used without further purification. The milk powder was purchased from the local supermarket. Chitosan (CS) was purchased from Aladdin (deacetylated: 95%, viscosity: 100–200 mPa s). 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) (BR) and 2,2′-azinobis-(3-ethylbenzthiazoline-6-sulfonate) (ABTS) (BR) were purchased from Shanghai kayou Biological Technology Co., Ltd. Cysteine (BR), glycine (BR), and lysine (BR) were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. Ultrapure water with an electrical resistance larger than 18.2 MΩ cm was used throughout the experiment.

### 2.2. Instruments

A UV-2550 UV–vis spectrophotometer (Shimadzu) was used to record the absorption spectra of different reaction solutions. SERRS measurements were performed with a confocal microscope (Jobin–Yvon HR-800). He–Ne laser with 632.8 nm radiations was used for excitation. Spectra were collected with 50 long working distance lens. The signal was integrated for 10 s twice accumulations. Scanning Electron Microscope images were obtained with a Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) (SIGMA) microscope. Zeta potentials of CSPNPs were measured on a Nano-ZS ZEN3600 particle sizer (Malvern Instruments). Concentrations of Au/Ag were determined with an Inductively Coupled Plasma-Atomic Emission Spectrometry (ICP-AES, IRIS Intrepid II XSP, Thermo Electron Corporation). Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) and Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectroscopy (EDX) measurements for CSPNPs were performed on a JEOL JEM-2010 microscope.

### 2.3. Synthesis of CSPNPs

The CSPNPs were conveniently synthesized by ascorbic acid reduction of hydrogen tetrachloroaurate in the presence of chitosan and silver nitrate. Briefly 80 nm CSPNPs can be prepared as follows: 150 μL of 29 mM HAuCl<sub>4</sub> solution was added to 20 mL ultrapure water, and then 100 μL 10 mg/mL of chitosan (dissolved in 5% acetic acid) was added under stirring. One minute later, 60 μL of 6 mM silver nitrate was injected into the solutions, and 80 μL of 0.1 M ascorbic acid was added immediately. After the introduction of ascorbic acid, a colorless stage would maintain for about 5 s, followed by the emergence of blue color within 1 min indicating the formation of CSPNPs. No obvious color changes could be observed after 1 min. The mixture was kept stirring for 3 h. When the volume of CS was changed to 200 μL and 40 μL, 50 nm and 120 nm CSPNPs can be synthesized, respectively. The final products were purified using centrifugation at 3500 rpm for 15 min, and then washing and redispersing in ultrapure water. This process was repeated three times.

### 2.4. CSPNPs peroxidase mimetic-based melamine assay

The experiment conditions were as follows: 100 μL of melamine corresponding to a final concentration as 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 20, 30, 40 and 50 μM, 10 μL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (50 mM), 540 μL of 10 mM acetate buffer (pH 4.0), 150 μL of TMB (5 mM) and 200 μL of CSPNPs were incubated at 45 °C for 10 min. After that, the mixtures were kept in an ice-water bath for 10 min to terminate the reaction before Raman measurements. 6 μL of final solution was mounted in a capillary tube with 0.2 mm wall thickness and subsequently fixed on the sample stage of a spectrometer for focusing measurements. Finally the standard curve measurements were conducted based on the SERRS peaks of CTC at 1191 cm<sup>−1</sup> and the concentrations of melamine.

### 2.5. Kinetic analysis

The reaction kinetics for the catalytic oxidation of TMB was conducted by recording the absorption spectra at 652 nm within a 1 min interval using a Shimadzu UV-2550 spectrophotometer. The reaction was conducted at 45 °C with 200 μL CSPNPs in 540 μL of 10 mM NaAc–HAc buffer (pH 4) in the presence of varied concentrations of TMB or H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The apparent kinetic parameters were calculated according to the Michaelis–Menten equation:

$$\nu = \frac{V_{max} \times [S]}{K_m + [S]}$$

where $\nu$ is the initial velocity, $V_{max}$ is the maximal reaction velocity, $K_m$ is the Michaelis constant and $[S]$ is the concentration of substrate.

### 2.6. Selectivity comparison and recovery test for melamine detection

To illustrate the selectivity of the proposed method for melamine detection, control experiments were conducted. 100 μL of 20 μM melamine was incubated with 10 μL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (50 mM), 150 μL of TMB (5 mM) and 540 μL of 10 mM acetate buffer (pH 4.0) at 45 °C for 10 min. After that, the mixture was kept in a cold-ice bath for 10 min to terminate the reaction. For comparison, 100 μL of interfering substances (cystenine, Vitamine C, lysine, NH<sub>4</sub>Cl 200 μM and glucose, glycine, lactose, KCl, CaCl<sub>2</sub>, ZnCl<sub>2</sub> 0.2 mM) instead of melamine was separately conducted in a similar way. Then, 6 μL of final solution was mounted in a capillary tube with 0.2 mm wall thickness and subsequently fixed on the sample stage of a spectrometer for focusing measurements.

To test the recovery and practical applicability of the method for real samples, purified milk powder samples were tested in the method. Firstly, different amounts of melamine were added to 1.0 g of milk powder corresponding to a final concentration of melamine as 10, 20, 30, 40 μM. Secondly, the milk powder was treated by a simple extraction procedure. Specifically, 1 mL of acetonitrile, 1 mL of CCl<sub>3</sub>COOH, and 9 mL of water were added into 1.0 g of milk powder. Then the mixed solution was ultrasonically extracted for 20 min and then centrifugated at 2500 rpm for 10 min. 100 μL of supernatant instead of melamine was conducted in a way similar to the above mentioned about selectivity comparison.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. The synthesis of CSPNPs

The CSPNPs were conveniently synthesized by ascorbic acid reduction of hydrogen tetrachloroaurate (III) in the presence of chitosan and silver nitrate. Compared with other existing methods for the formation of multibranched NPs, CSPNPs were formed under room temperature with no need for seeds and surfactants. Besides, the as-prepared CSPNPs were environment friendly and biocompatible, because chitosan is a natural and nontoxic polysaccharide. From the SEM image (Fig. 1a), some “nanodots” with the size of about 10 nm, just like the petals of popcorns, can be observed on the surface of CSPNPs. TEM image (Fig. S1) further confirms the result. A hypothesized growth process is organized as follows: firstly, primary Au nuclei were formed due to the reduction of HAuCl<sub>4</sub>. Soon afterwards, silver ions preferentially deposited on Au nuclei and formed a silver layer due to the underpotential deposition (UPD) [44]. Then Au started growing via the concerted action of both reagent reduction and galvanic replacement. Secondly, the unstable Au crystals underwent agglomeration because of the requirement to reduce the overall surface energy [45] and the adhesion of CS. Thirdly, branches produced from the surface of the agglomerates due to anisotropic growth. This is because the growth of Au crystals followed more energetically favorable directions with the depletion of reactants [45]. The size of CSPNPs with a high monodispersity can be adjusted by changing the amount of regents. The composition of 80 nm CSPNPs with the original Ag/Au mole ratio of 0.08:1 was investigated by ICP-AES, EDX (Fig. S2). To conduct ICP-AES detection, CSPNPs were isolated by centrifugation at 3500 rpm in 15 min, washed three times and subsequently dissolved in aqua regia. ICP-AES data shows an Ag/Au mole ratio of 0.07:1, which indicates the exact amount of Au/Ag in the solution. EDX result shows an Ag/Au mole ratio of 0.06:1. These results are consistent with the hypothesis. Due to the fact that CS possesses the positively charged −NH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup> group under acidic conditions, zeta potential of the CSPNPs (80 nm) was positive (+38.47 mV).

### 3.2. Verification of the peroxidise-like activity of CSPNPs and sensing mechanism

To assess whether CSPNPs would exhibit peroxidase mimetic catalytic ability, we tested catalytic oxidation peroxidase substrates TMB and ABTS in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. As shown in Fig. 1B and Fig. S3, TMB and ABTS can be separately oxidized into a blue and green product in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and CSPNPs. In contrast, the mixture of other substances did not show characteristic color or only lightly. Furthermore, UV–vis spectroscopy was used to characterize the different solutions. Fig. 1C shows that CSPNPs, TMB and their mixture did not have absorptions at 370 nm and 652 nm. Compared with the absorption spectrum of the mixture of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB, strong absorptions at 370 nm and 652 nm were observed in the presence of CSPNPs in the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB solution, which demonstrates that the reaction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with TMB was greatly accelerated by CSPNPs. Besides, to prove the better catalytic and Raman signal enhancement ability of CSPNPs, different materials were compared, including sodium citrate modified popcorn-like NPs (Na<sub>3</sub>Ct-CSPNPs), chitosan (CS), sodium citrate modified 30 nm Au NPs (Na<sub>3</sub>Ct-AuNPs) and chitosan modified 30 nm AuNPs (CS-AuNPs). Two conclusions can be drawn from Fig. S4A, 4B, 4C. Firstly, positive-charged NPs (CSPNPs, CS-AuNPs) encompass a better catalytic ability than negative-charged NPs (Na<sub>3</sub>Ct-CSPNPs, Na<sub>3</sub>Ct-AuNPs), which consists with the former report [23]. Secondly, CSPNPs, though the total size is bigger than CS-AuNPs, show better results both catalytic and signal enhancement ability. The above mentioned facts can be explained in the following reasons. Au in the NPs greatly facilitated the catalytic reaction, which is line with the reports that Au can catalyze TMB [21], ABTS [20], OPD [22] by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and Au can act as redox mediator [17]. Besides, the positive charged property endowed by CS would influence the absorption of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and the particle-mediated electron transfer processes [23], which increased the catalytic ability of the NPs. Furthermore, the increased surface area owing to the appearance of “nanodots” enhanced the catalytic ability.

The oxidation of TMB by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> went through a two-step two-electron reaction (Fig. S5) [46]. The blue product belonging to charge transfer complex (CTC) [46] is attributed to the reversible complexation of the diamine and final diimine. According to a recent report, this CTC product can be analyzed by resonance Raman scattering using a laser excitation of 632.8 nm. In our present work, the Raman signal intensity was tremendously decreased by removing CSPNPs from the solutions in the method of centrifugation (Fig. S6), which indicated that CSPNPs act as not only catalysts but also SERS substrates. We further characterized the peroxidase-like activity of CSPNPs through Raman Spectroscopy (Fig. 1D). Raman bands assigned to CTC are summarized into Table S1. The analyses yielded consistent results as UV–vis did. All of the results demonstrated that the as-prepared CSPNPs did possess peroxidase mimetic catalytic ability.

### 3.3. Optimization of the reaction conditions

The catalytic ability of CSPNPs depends on pH, temperature, TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentrations and the size of CSPNPs. We measured the peroxidase-like activity of CSPNPs while varying pH from 2 to 11, the temperature from 25 °C to 65 °C. The optimal pH and temperature were approximately pH 4 and 45 °C (Fig. 2A, B), which were very similar to the values for horseradish peroxidase (HRP) [27]. The influence of TMB on the reaction is shown in Fig. 2 D. No significant change can be observed after 150 μL, thus the best volume of TMB was 150 μL. The concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was critical to our experiment. To ensure sensitivity, the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was optimized following two steps. Firstly, certain amounts of melamine were added to a series of mixtures of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, TMB while varying H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration from 10 mM to 100 mM. For comparison, control experiments were conducted similarly without adding melamine. Those, with the same concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> while exhibit color differences, were chosen as candidates to conduct Raman detection. Secondly, the final optimal value was established by the sensitivity of Raman (Fig. 2E). The value of y shows that the differences of SERRS intensity of CTC at 1191 cm<sup>−1</sup> between experiments with the same concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> divided the amounts of melamine. Apparently, 50 mM should be chosen as the optimal H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration. The effect of the particle size of CSPNPs on our experiments is shown in Fig. 2F, the SERRS intensity of CTC at 1191 cm<sup>−1</sup> was the strongest when the size of CSPNPs was about 80 nm, which was a concerted action of catalysis and SERS enhancement. In addition, to ensure the reaction can be completed within the shortest time, the best amounts of CSPNPs were 200 μL (Fig. 2C). The reaction time was also observed. As shown in Fig. S7, the Raman signal intensity at 1191 cm<sup>−1</sup> gradually increased from 2 min to 10 min, but no significant change was observed after 10 min. Therefore, we consider that the reaction can be terminated at 10 min.

### 3.5. Analysis of melamine with high sensitivity and selectivity

Under the optimized conditions above, qualitative and sensitive detection of melamine was achieved by monitoring the SERRS peaks of CTC at 1191 cm<sup>−1</sup>. As shown in Fig. 3A, the color gradually became lighter with the increasing amounts of melamine. As a result, the SERRS intensity of CTC at 1191 cm<sup>−1</sup> also decreased gradually, which can be seen from Fig. 3B. A linear relationship (Fig. 3c) was obtained between the SERRS intensity of CTC at 1191 cm<sup>−1</sup> and the melamine concentration from 10 nM to 50 μM ($R^2 = 0.989$) with a detection limit of 8.51 nM ($LOD = 3.3\text{ SD}/S$, where SD is the standard deviation of the response well-known from statistics and S is the slope of the calibration curve in the initial stages). This detection limit is far lower than the safety limits of melamine ingestion (20 μM in the US and EU; 8 μM in infant formula in China). Compared with the detection using Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> MNPs [43], the proposed method provides a much lower detection limit and a broader linear range. Also, the detection limit obtained in our work was better than the results reported by using the SERS [39, 50] and colorimetric methods [35, 36].

Considering that the existence of some potential interfering substances, such as glucose, lactose, Vitamin C, common ions and some nitrogen containing organic molecules, might affect the detection of melamine in real samples analysis, the selectivity of this method was investigated. As shown in Fig. 3D, compared with other species, only melamine induces a dramatic decrease of Raman signal intensity. These results indicate that the proposed method is amenable to selectively detect melamine.

### 3.6. Application in real samples

The recoveries of the real samples are in the range 90.86–106.29% with the relative standard deviation of 1.66–4.29%. The desirable results obviously demonstrated the feasibility of the proposed method for the determination of melamine in milk powder samples.

## 4. Conclusions

In summary, CSPNPs were conveniently synthesized which possessed a higher intrinsic peroxidase-like activity. Furthermore, trace melamine was sensitively detected based on the peroxidase mimetic ability of CSPNPs and reaction of melamine with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. In our system, CSPNPs not only acted as catalysts but also served as enhancement substrates. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that melamine could be detected by combining peroxidase-catalyzed reaction with SERRS. We expect our findings will be utilized to a wide range of new potential applications including biotechnology, environmental chemistry and medicine.
