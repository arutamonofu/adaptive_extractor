# Highly sensitive colorimetric detection of arsenite based on reassembly-induced oxidase-mimicking activity inhibition of dithiothreitol-capped Pd nanozyme

### ABSTRACT

Given that hypertoxic arsenite (As<sup>3+</sup>) poses a serious threat to the environment and global health, developing high-performance, low-cost and easy-to-use approaches to specifically monitor As<sup>3+</sup> in environmental matrices turns to be of great importance. In the present work, we reported a novel colorimetric assay of As<sup>3+</sup> based on the reassembly-induced oxidase-like activity inhibition of dithiothreitol-capped Pd nanoparticles (Pd-DTT). The Pd-DTT nanozyme exhibits good oxidase-like catalytic activity to trigger the oxidation of colorless 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) in the presence of O<sub>2</sub>, forming a blue product TMBox. When As<sup>3+</sup> exists, it can chelate the sulfydryl groups in DTT more strongly, leading to a reassembly of Pd-DTT. Compared with the original nanozyme, the reassembled one has much less oxidase-like activity, resulting in the significantly suppressed TMB reaction. Based on this principle, highly sensitive detection of As<sup>3+</sup> was achieved, providing a wide linear range from 33 ng L<sup>−1</sup> to 333.3 μg L<sup>−1</sup> and an ultralow detection limit of 35 ng L<sup>−1</sup>. With the help of EDTA as a chelating agent, the method could also offer excellent selectivity for the determination of As<sup>3+</sup> against other metal ions. Besides, our colorimetric assay was employed to accurately measure the analyte in spiked drinking and river water, revealing its great promise in environmental analysis application.

### 1. Introduction

It is well known that inorganic arsenic has drawn great concern across the world due to its hypertoxicity and carcinogenicity [1,2]. The arsenic pollution mainly originates from mineral exploration and industrial waste. After a long time of accumulation, it is ultimately deposited in the environment and generates a serious threat to global health. In view of the high toxicity of arsenic, its maximum level in drinking water has been limited as 10 μg L<sup>−1</sup> (0.13 μM) by the World Health Organization (WHO). Inorganic arsenic exists mainly as trivalent arsenite (As<sup>3+</sup>) and pentavalent arsenate (As<sup>5+</sup>). In comparison with the latter, As<sup>3+</sup> can chelate sulfydryl in bio-enzymes and proteins [3], and such that it shows a higher toxicity (about 60 times higher than As<sup>5+</sup>) [4]. Therefore, it is very necessary to develop effective approaches for high-performance monitoring of As<sup>3+</sup> in environmental matrices.

Currently, the detection of arsenic mainly depends on ICP-MS [5], ICP-OES [6] and AFS [7]. These conventional methods can accurately determine the arsenic levels in various matrices. However, they need expensive instruments and tedious operation, seriously limiting their use in on-site monitoring. Instead, optical methods like colorimetric analysis have been explored for arsenic sensing [8–15]. The colorimetric approaches exhibit advantages of facile operation, low cost and versatile readout [16], and are very promising for environmental field analysis. Generally, colorimetric detection of arsenic is based on the color changes of foreign labels (e.g., colloidal Au NPs) triggered by the target [8,10,17]. In comparison with ICP-MS and AFS, colorimetric assays usually have inferior sensitivity [8,10], which hinders their utilization in monitoring low-level arsenic. Thus, exploring facile colorimetric strategies with high sensitivity for arsenic detection is highly desired.

As an emerging class of nanomaterials that show enzyme-like features, nanozymes have attracted considerable attention in the past few years [18–22]. Compared with natural bio-enzymes, nanozymes exhibit superiorities of excellent stability, easy mass preparation, low cost and adjustable catalytic activity [20,23]. Given these, nanozymes acting as alternatives to natural enzymes have been widely applied in the sensing field [24–33]. For toxic inorganic ions, one can utilize the target-induced activity regulation of a nanozyme to realize their sensing [34–43]. Different from traditional colorimetric strategies [8,10,12], nanozyme-based colorimetric analysis is a typical catalytic reaction-based method, which can generate amplified signals [44]. Thus, it is able to provide favorable sensitivity for the detection of low-level targets. For instance, recently our group developed a nanozyme-based colorimetric sensor for the ultrasensitive determination of Hg<sup>2+</sup> [37]. The sensor was based on the target-stimulated peroxidase-like activity of cysteine-decorated Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> particles, and the limit of detection (LOD) could be down to 5.9 pM. The attractive characteristic inspires us to explore nanozyme-based colorimetric assays for As<sup>3+</sup> sensing.

Nanostructured Pd is a common nanozyme that can exhibit peroxidase-like/oxidase-like activity [16,35,41,45]. In this work, we explored nanosized Pd as an oxidase mimic for the highly sensitive detection of As<sup>3+</sup>. Compared with peroxidase mimics, oxidase-like nanozymes are more desirable because of their simpler reaction conditions (e.g., no H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is required) [46], which can also avoid the oxidation of As<sup>3+</sup> to As<sup>5+</sup> during measurement. Our colorimetric sensor is based on the reassembly-induced oxidase-like activity inhibition of dithiothreitol-capped Pd nanoparticles (Pd-DTT). As illustrated in Scheme 1, Pd-DTT exhibits favorable oxidase-mimicking catalytic activity to induce the oxidation of colorless 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) to blue TMBox with the participation of dissolved O<sub>2</sub>. When As<sup>3+</sup> is added, it is able to chelate the sulfydryl groups in DTT, resulting in a reassembly of the Pd-DTT nanozyme. In comparison with Pd-DTT, the reassembled nanozyme (Pd-DTT-As<sup>3+</sup>) shows much lower catalytic activity, thus leading to a significant suppression of the TMB reaction. With the strategy, highly sensitive and selective determination of As<sup>3+</sup> was realized.

### 2. Experimental

#### 2.1. Chemicals

NaBH<sub>4</sub>, TMB and EDTA were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent. DTT, K<sub>2</sub>PdCl<sub>6</sub>, As<sup>3+</sup>, As<sup>5+</sup>, Hg<sup>2+</sup>, Pb<sup>2+</sup> and other ion standard solutions were obtained from Shanghai Aladdin Biochemical Technology. All the other chemicals were of analytical grade. Deionized water was utilized throughout the study.

#### 2.2. Preparation and characterization of Pd-DTT

Pd-DTT was prepared via reducing the K<sub>2</sub>PdCl<sub>4</sub> precursor by NaBH<sub>4</sub> according to the previous report with a little modification [34]. Briefly, 10 mL DTT (1 mM) and 10 mL K<sub>2</sub>PdCl<sub>4</sub> (25 mM) were first mixed in 10 mL deionized water and stirred at room temperature for 1 h. Then, 10 mL NaBH<sub>4</sub> (125 mM) was dropwise added to the above mixture. The solution was kept under a mild stir for 0.5 h. The products were washed with deionized water and collected. After dried in a vacuum oven at 50 °C for 12 h, the Pd-DTT nanozyme was obtained. 5 mg dried Pd-DTT was ultrasonically dispersed in 25 mL deionized water and stored at 4 °C for use.

The prepared Pd-DTT was featured by several methods. X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were obtained by a 6100 diffractometer (Shimadzu). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images were gained by a JEM-2100 microscope (JEOL). Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectra were obtained by a Nicolet Nexus 470 instrument (USA Nicolet). X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed on an ESCALAB-MKII spectrometer (Thermo-Fisher Scientific).

#### 2.3. Oxidase-mimicking feature of Pd-DTT

The oxidase-mimicking feature of Pd-DTT was investigated by employing TMB as a substrate. All photometric measurements were performed on a Cary 8454 ultraviolet-visible (UV–vis) spectrometer (Agilent Technologies). Typically, 100 μL Pd-DTT solution (0.2 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) and 100 μL TMB solution (5 mM, dissolved in ethanol) were added to 2,800 μL NaAc-HAc buffer (pH 4, 0.2 M). After reaction for 20 min, the system was detected. The generated superoxide radicals were detected by electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR). In detail, 100 μL Pd-DTT solution (0.2 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) was first dispersed in 2,900 μL NaAc-HAc buffer (pH 4, 0.2 M). Then, 20 μL 5,5-dimethyl-1-pyrroline N-oxide (DMPO) solution was added to the above solution to capture superoxide radicals. After that, the sample was collected by capillary and measured by EPR immediately. The apparent kinetics parameters were calculated based on the Michaelis-Menten equation $v = V_{max}[S]/(K_m + [S])$, where $v$ is the reaction velocity, $V_{max}$ is the maximum reaction velocity, $[S]$ is the substrate concentration, and $K_m$ is the Michaelis-Menten constant. The reaction velocity ($v$) was obtained by a transformation of measured data using the Beer-Lambert equation $c = A/\epsilon b$, where $c$ is the concentration of TMBox, $A$ is the absorbance at 652 nm, $b$ is the thickness of the solution (1 cm), and $\epsilon$ is the molar absorption coefficient of TMBox (39,000 M<sup>−1</sup> cm<sup>−1</sup>).

The robustness and stability of the oxidase-mimicking Pd-DTT were studied against harsh incubation pH and temperature, inorganic salt concentration, and storage time. For the robustness of the nanozyme, it was first incubated in buffers with different pH values or at different temperatures for 2 h, and then its oxidase-like activity was tested under standard conditions (0.2 M NaAc-HAc buffer, pH 4, room temperature). For the stability of the nanozyme, it was used to catalyze the TMB reaction in the presence of different concentrations of NaCl or over storage time, and its catalytic activity was evaluated by UV–vis.

#### 2.4. Reassembly-induced activity inhibition of Pd-DTT by As<sup>3+</sup> for As<sup>3+</sup> sensing

Typically, 100 μL As<sup>3+</sup> solution (its concentration varied from 1 μg L<sup>−1</sup> to 10 mg L<sup>−1</sup>) was added to the mixture of 100 μL Pd-DTT solution (0.2 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) and 2,700 μL NaAc-HAc buffer (pH 4, 0.2 M). After incubation at room temperature for 2 min, 100 μL TMB solution (5 mM, dissolved in ethanol) was added to the above mixture. After incubation for 20 min, the system was detected.

The practicability of our colorimetric assay was tested by using it to detect As<sup>3+</sup> spiked in real water samples. In this work, Yangtze River water, drinking water and purified water were collected and filtered by 0.22 mm microfiltration membrane. After spiked with different levels of As<sup>3+</sup>, they were measured according to the above procedure.

### 3. Results and discussion

#### 3.1. Preparation and characterization of Pd-DTT

The Pd-DTT nanozyme was synthesized via reducing the K<sub>2</sub>PdCl<sub>4</sub> precursor with NaBH<sub>4</sub>, during which a certain amount of DTT was added as a stabilizer and modifier. The crystal structure of the obtained products was characterized by XRD. As depicted in Fig. 1A, the material provides three characteristic peaks at 39.9°, 46.5° and 66.7°, which are assigned to the (111), (200) and (220) planes of face-centered cubic Pd (JCPDS No. 88-2335), respectively [47]. The shape of Pd-DTT was observed by TEM (Fig. 1B). A large number of Pd nanoparticles with a mean diameter of 3.8 nm (the inset in Fig. 1B) are observed. The high-resolution TEM image (Fig. S1, Supporting Information) displays that the interplanar spacing of the synthesized Pd-DTT is 0.227 nm, which is in consistent with the theoretical value (0.224 nm). These particles are linked each other because of the interaction between Pd and the dual sulfydryl groups in DTT (Fig. 1B). This feature was further confirmed by FTIR (Fig. 1C). In Pd-DTT, the peaks at 2970 and 2930 cm<sup>−1</sup> are attributed to the alkyl groups in DTT, indicating that DTT has been successfully anchored onto Pd nanoparticles. However, the peak at 2550 cm<sup>−1</sup> originating from the dual sulfydryl groups in DTT cannot be identified in Pd-DTT. This supports that the dual sulfydryl groups in DTT have bound to two Pd particles, forming a Pd–DTT–Pd structure. In the full XPS (Fig. 1D), the elements O, Pd, C and S can be well observed, also indicating the successful modification of DTT on Pd surface. The fine Pd 3d XPS and S 2p XPS (Fig. S2, Supporting Information) also confirm the capping of DTT onto Pd surface via the Pd–S binding.

#### 3.2. Oxidase-mimicking activity of Pd-DTT

To verify the oxidase-mimicking feature of the synthesized Pd-DTT, the UV–vis spectra of different combinations of Pd-DTT and TMB were recorded. As compared in Fig. 2A, no notable color change is found in TMB or Pd-DTT solution, indicating that the two species are stable when they are separated. However, when TMB and Pd-DTT are incubated together, a significant chromogenic reaction from colorless to deep blue occurs, giving a maximum absorption peak at about 652 nm. This chromogenic reaction is due to the catalytic oxidation of TMB to TMBox. The time-dependent absorbance changes of the three systems (Fig. S3, Supporting Information) also confirm that the Pd-DTT material can accelerate the oxidation of colorless TMB to blue TMBox. These results demonstrate the oxidase-like catalytic characteristic of Pd-DTT. To further uncover the TMB reaction catalyzed by Pd-DTT, possible intermediates generated during the reaction were identified. As exhibited in Fig. 2B, obvious EPR signals attributed to superoxide radicals are captured when using DMPO as a trapping agent, revealing that superoxide radicals are produced during the reaction. Thus, the chromogenic reaction observed in the TMB + Pd-DTT system can be deduced as follows (the inset in Fig. 2B): O<sub>2</sub> dissolved in buffer is first activated by the Pd-DTT oxidase mimic to generate superoxide radicals, and then the produced radicals with a strong oxidizing ability lead to the oxidation of TMB to TMBox.

Similar to other oxidase mimics [44,48,49], the oxidase-mimicking catalytic activity of Pd-DTT highly depends on the buffer pH. As exhibited in Fig. S4 (Supporting Information), when the buffer pH increases from 3 to 9, the enzymatic activity of Pd-DTT first rises and then falls, reaching the maximum at pH 4. Therefore, the NaAc-HAc buffer with pH 4 was utilized in the following study. To better understand the Pd-DTT oxidase mimic, its steady-state catalytic kinetics toward TMB was further investigated. As shown in Fig. S5 (Supporting Information), a typical Michaelis-Menten curve of Pd-DTT catalyzing the TMB substrate is obtained. The $K_m$ and $V_{max}$ are determined to be 0.15 mM and $3.8 \times 10^{−8}$ M s<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Since the catalytic efficiency of Pd-DTT is comparable or even better than that of other oxidase mimics [50,51] (Table S1, Supporting Information), it can be used as an oxidase-like nanozyme for promising applications. Compared with natural bio-enzymes, nanozymes are more popular because of their excellent resistance against harsh environments. In the present work, the robustness of the Pd-DTT oxidase mimic was studied according to our previous report [16]. As shown in Fig. S6 (Supporting Information), the absorbance of the TMB chromogenic reaction catalyzed by Pd-DTT has no notable change after the nanozyme is incubated at harsh pH or temperature, suggesting that the Pd-DTT nanozyme is very robust against harsh conditions. Besides, the nanozyme shows favorable stability upon inorganic salt concentration (Fig. S7, Supporting Information) and storage time (Fig. S8, Supporting Information). This favorable feature will endow it with wide use under various conditions.

#### 3.3. As<sup>3+</sup> inhibits the oxidase-mimicking activity of Pd-DTT via inducing reassembly

It is interestingly found that the TMB chromogenic reaction catalyzed by the Pd-DTT oxidase mimic can be suppressed by As<sup>3+</sup>. As displayed in Fig. 3A, when a certain amount of As<sup>3+</sup> is added to the Pd-DTT + TMB system, the UV–vis absorption signal decreases significantly. Because As<sup>3+</sup> has no enough reducing capacity to turn the generated blue TMBox to colorless TMB again, the suppressed reaction can only result from the inhibition of the oxidase-mimicking activity of Pd-DTT by As<sup>3+</sup>. Previous reports have revealed that As<sup>3+</sup> is easy to chelate sulfydryl to form the As–S bond [3,14]. Given that the DTT modifier in Pd-DTT has two sulfydryl groups, it is reasonably speculated that the addition of As<sup>3+</sup> triggers a reassembly of the nanozyme, during which its active surface is blocked for the suppressed chromogenic reaction.

To verify the above speculation, the catalytic kinetics of Pd-DTT toward TMB with the presence of As<sup>3+</sup> or not were investigated. In general, the catalytic inhibition process of an enzyme has an important impact on its $V_{max}$ and/or $K_m$. As shown in Fig. S9 (Supporting Information), the Pd-DTT nanozyme provides typical Michaelis-Menten curves toward the TMB substrate before and after interacting with As<sup>3+</sup>. Compared with the original Pd-DTT, the $K_m$ after the addition of As<sup>3+</sup> remains nearly unchanged, while the $V_{max}$ has a remarkable decrease. This means that the affinity of the nanozyme toward TMB does not change, while its catalytic efficiency is significantly reduced after interacting with As<sup>3+</sup>. The result indicates that the inhibition belongs to a noncompetitive process [52]. It is further suggested that the immanent cause for the oxidase-mimicking activity inhibition of Pd-DTT originates from the blocked active sites induced by As<sup>3+</sup> [52]. Further, the FTIR spectra, XPS, and TEM images of Pd-DTT before and after interacting with As<sup>3+</sup> were compared. As shown in Fig. S10 (Supporting Information), a weak FTIR peak of arsenite at 860 cm<sup>−1</sup> is found after the addition of As<sup>3+</sup>, indicating that As<sup>3+</sup> has been adsorbed onto the nanozyme via chelating the sulfydryl groups in Pd-DTT. The XPS of Pd-DTT after interacting with As<sup>3+</sup> was also recorded. As displayed in Fig. S11 (Supporting Information), the element As can be observed in XPS, also suggesting the binding of As<sup>3+</sup> onto the Pd-DTT nanozyme. Moreover, the binding capacity of Pd-DTT was studied. After incubation for 2 min, the As<sup>3+</sup> concentration in the supernatant was detected by ICP-MS. It is found that the level of As<sup>3+</sup> has a significant decrease (from an initial concentration of 333 μg L<sup>−1</sup> to a final concentration of 153 μg L<sup>−1</sup>), proving again that As<sup>3+</sup> has been adsorbed onto the nanozyme to induce the reassembly process. Additionally, the reassembly kinetics of Pd-DTT was investigated. As shown in Fig. S12 (Supporting Information), the absorbance decreases with the increasing incubation time of Pd-DTT and As<sup>3+</sup>. It can further be concluded that the As<sup>3+</sup>-induced reassembly of Pd-DTT is a relatively rapid process, because in the first two minutes the catalytic activity of Pd-DTT has been reduced by ~80%. After interacting with As<sup>3+</sup>, the size of the nanozyme particles remains almost unchanged, and they are still linked each other (Fig. S13, Supporting Information). All the results suggest the reassembly-induced oxidase-mimicking activity inhibition mechanism of Pd-DTT by As<sup>3+</sup>, as illustrated in Fig. 3B: due to the larger bond dissociation energy of As–S (433.2 ± 12.5 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>) than Pd–S (197 ± 6 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>), As<sup>3+</sup> can break the original interaction of Pd and sulfydryl in Pd-DTT by chelating the sulfydryl groups more strongly, forming a more stable As–(S)<sub>3</sub> structure rapidly. Namely, the addition of As<sup>3+</sup> leads to a reassembly process of Pd-DTT. In the reassembled nanozyme, the active sites are blocked by the As<sup>3+</sup> species, resulting in the sharp decline of its oxidase-mimicking activity.

#### 3.4. High-performance colorimetric detection of As<sup>3+</sup>

Given that As<sup>3+</sup> can inhibit the oxidase-like activity of Pd-DTT, the finding can be employed to sense the hypertoxic As<sup>3+</sup> species. As displayed in Fig. 4A, the UV–vis profiles of the Pd-DTT + TMB system with the presence of different levels of As<sup>3+</sup> are recorded. As expected, the UV–vis signals of the TMB chromogenic reaction catalyzed by Pd-DTT decrease with the increasing concentrations of As<sup>3+</sup> (Fig. 4A). It is further found that the absorbance at 652 nm has a logarithmic relationship with the level of As<sup>3+</sup> ranging from 33 ng L<sup>−1</sup> to 333.3 μg L<sup>−1</sup> (Fig. 4B), providing a fitting equation of $A = −0.04Log[As^{3+}] + 0.24$ ($R^2 = 0.994$). The LOD is determined to be as low as 35 ng L<sup>−1</sup> based on signal-to-noise of three (S/N = 3). This LOD can completely meet the demand provided by the WHO (10 μg L<sup>−1</sup>). Compared with previously reported approaches (Table S2, Supporting Information), our colorimetric assay is preeminent in terms of LOD and linear range. The outstanding performance mainly originates from the catalytic reaction-based strategy, which can provide amplified response for high-sensitivity detection [37]. Besides, our assay does not require any expensive recognizer (e.g., aptamer), greatly reducing the detection cost. In addition, the colorimetric signal can be easily read by naked eyes or smartphone, making it more attractive in on-site analysis. These advantages endow our assay with great promise for As<sup>3+</sup> monitoring.

**Fig. 3.** (A) compares UV–vis spectra of the Pd-DTT + TMB system with the addition of As<sup>3+</sup> or not, and the inset shows the corresponding photograph. (B) illustrates the reassembly-induced oxidase-mimicking activity inhibition process of Pd-DTT by As<sup>3+</sup>.

Additionally, the selectivity of our colorimetric assay for As<sup>3+</sup> detection was studied. As presented in Fig. S14 (Supporting Information), the interference influences of all the potential ions at a low concentration (333.3 μg L<sup>−1</sup>) can be negligible. However, some of these species will affect the selective determination of As<sup>3+</sup> when they are at a higher level (3.3 mg L<sup>−1</sup>). In detail, since both Hg<sup>2+</sup> and Pd<sup>2+</sup> can also chelate sulfydryl on Pd-DTT surface and regulate the oxidase-mimicking activity of the nanozyme, they suppress the TMB chromogenic reaction to different degree. Fe<sup>3+</sup> itself has a certain enzymatic activity, and such that it can promote the chromogenic reaction slightly. To avoid the interferences from these high-level ions, EDTA acting as a chelating agent was employed to chelate these dissociative species, including Hg<sup>2+</sup>, Pd<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup>, for selective detection of As<sup>3+</sup> [53]. As shown in Fig. 4C and D, with the addition of EDTA (0.33 mM), the interference influences of Hg<sup>2+</sup>, Pd<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup> at a high concentration (3.3 mg L<sup>−1</sup>) can be completely eliminated, while the added EDTA has no obvious effect on the As<sup>3+</sup> signal. Hence, with the help of EDTA, the Pd-DTT nanozyme can be utilized to selectively detect As<sup>3+</sup>.

Finally, we used the fabricated assay to measure the As<sup>3+</sup> levels in environmental water. Yangtze River water, drinking water and purified water were collected and spiked for measurement. The detection results are listed in Table 1. It is found that our assay can provide good recoveries for the detection of As<sup>3+</sup> in spiked samples. Moreover, our detection results are in agreement with the data obtained by ICP-MS. This result reveals its reliability for As<sup>3+</sup> sensing in practical matrices.

### 4. Conclusions

In summary, a new colorimetric assay has been proposed for As<sup>3+</sup> sensing based on the reassembly-triggered oxidase-mimicking activity inhibition of Pd-DTT. The assay can provide outstanding sensitivity and ultralow LOD for the determination of As<sup>3+</sup>. With the assist of EDTA as a specific chelating agent, our method is also highly selective to the As<sup>3+</sup> species against other common ions. In addition, it can be used to detect the target in drinking and river water accurately. These attractive analytical properties suggest our sensor as a facile and feasible tool to monitor hypertoxic As<sup>3+</sup> in environmental matrices. Besides, the concept of reassembly-induced activity regulation of nanozymes will inspire future design and development of other nanozyme-based chemosensors.
