# One-step solvothermal synthesis of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite as magnetically recyclable mimetic peroxidase

### ABSTRACT

Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite was prepared by a simple and facile one-step solvothermal method and characterized by XRD, SEM, TEM, EDS and XPS techniques. The Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite showed peroxidase-like activity, it could catalyze the oxidation of typical peroxidase substrates, o-phenylenediamine (OPDA) and 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) in the presence of hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>). A simple colorimetric method for the detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could be established based on the intrinsic peroxidase-like activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite. In addition, the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite displayed good separation ability, stability and reusability, which could be easily separated from the reaction system by an external magnet and reused for the next round of reaction without significant decrease of its catalytic activity. We expect that the as-prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite may have the potential to replace natural peroxidase in the fields of catalysis, green chemistry, biosensors, environmental monitoring and protection.

## 1. Introduction

Natural enzymes, due to their high efficiency and mild reaction conditions, have been extensively studied and widely used in the field of medicine, chemical industry, food processing and agriculture [1,2], environmental monitoring and protection [3,4]. However, natural enzymes have their own intrinsic drawbacks and disadvantages, such as degradation by protease, denaturation under strong acidic and alkaline conditions, and at high temperature conditions, as well as the requirement of specials storage conditions [5,6]. Moreover, the preparation, purification and storage of natural enzymes are usually expensive and time-consuming [7,8]. Therefore, the synthesis of artificial enzyme mimics has long been a major concern of chemists and biochemists [9–13].

Recently, Gao et al. has reported that Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles possess intrinsic enzyme activity similar to that of natural peroxidases such as horseradish peroxidase (HRP), which opens the door to the development of nanoscaled inorganic materials in biochemical field [14]. Since this report, a number of nanoscaled metal oxides, metals and metal alloys, such as Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-nanorods [15], VO<sub>2</sub> nanobelts [16], Ag<sub>2</sub>O nanoparticles [6], Cu nanoclusters [17], Au nanoparticles [18], Au@Pt nanostructures [19] and Au@PdPt alloy nanorods [20], have been investigated and found to possess intrinsic peroxidase-like activity.

Noble metal nanoparticles, such as Pt, Au and Ag nanoparticles, have attracted intensive research interest because of their unique electronic structures and excellent catalytic properties [21–23]. However, the use of noble metals is limited to some degree due to their high cost and limited supply. Compared with noble metals such as Au and Ag, Cu is a much cheap metal [24]. In the past years, much attention has been paid to prepare nanoscaled Cu, Cu<sub>2</sub>O, CuO and Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O composites and to investigate their application in the field of catalysis [25,26] and environmental protection [24,25,27]. For example, Liu and co-workers fabricated porous CuO micro-/nanostructures by a two-step route and probed their peroxidase-like activity [28]. Zhou and co-workers developed a two-step solvothermal method to prepare flower-like Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposites and found that some of the Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposites exhibited higher photocatalytic performance than pure Cu<sub>2</sub>O [29]. Ai and co-workers developed an interfacial hydrothermal method for the preparation of Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O core–shell composite microspheres and investigated their visible-light-driven photocatalytic activity [30]. Kou and co-workers synthesized porous Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanoparticles through a two-step strategy involving dealloying and surface oxidation, and investigated the photocatalytic performance and adsorption behavior of the as-prepared nanoparticles [24,25]. In order to overcome the problem of recycling Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanoparticles from the reaction system, Wang and co-workers introduced magnetic Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> into the Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanoparticles and prepared magnetically recyclable Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu<sub>2</sub>O@Cu nanocomposites by a two-step method [31]. However, some of the materials are synthesized through complicated and multi-step procedures, and some need toxic organic solvent, or the synthesized materials are difficult to be recycled. Therefore, it is still necessary for material researchers to find a simple and convenient method to prepare Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O composite with easily recyclable properties. Furthermore, to the best of our knowledge, the peroxidase-like activity of Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O composite has not yet been systematically studied, although there are some publications about the peroxidase-like activity of Cu and CuO [17,28,32,33].

In this study, we reported a simple and facile one-step solvothermal method for the preparation of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite, and investigated the peroxidase-like activity of the nanocomposite with 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) and o-phenylenediamine (OPDA) as model peroxidase substrates. It was demonstrated that the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite exhibited intrinsic peroxidase-like activity, as it could catalyze the oxidation of TMB and OPDA to produce colored products in the presence of hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>). Additionally, the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite can be easily magnetically recycled and reused without significant loss of the catalytic activity, indicating that the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite has the potential for the replacement of natural peroxidases in practical applications.

## 2. Experimental

### 2.1. Materials

Copper sulfate pentahydrate (CuSO<sub>4</sub>·5H<sub>2</sub>O), iron chloride hexahydrate (FeCl<sub>3</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O), sodium acetate trihydrate (NaAc·3H<sub>2</sub>O), ethylene glycol (EG), o-phenylenediamine (OPDA), 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB), hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, 30 wt%) were purchased from Chendu Kelong Chemical Reagent Company (Chengdu, China). All reagents were of analytical reagent grade and used as received. Ultrapure water was utilized for the preparation of all aqueous solutions.

### 2.2. Preparation of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite

The Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite was prepared by a facile one-step solvothermal method. Typically, 1 mmol CuSO<sub>4</sub>·5H<sub>2</sub>O (0.25 g), 2 mmol FeCl<sub>3</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O (0.54 g) and 20 mmol NaAc·3H<sub>2</sub>O (2.7 g) were dissolved in 25 mL EG solution by magnetic stirring. Then the resulting solution was transferred into a 50 mL Teflon-lined stainless autoclave and heated at 200 °C for 10 h. After cooled down to room temperature naturally, the product was collected by an external magnetic field, washed with water and ethanol three times, and dried at 60 °C.

### 2.3. Characterization

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was taken on an FEI Inspect F50 microscope. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) were carried out on an FEI Tecnai G2 F20 field-emission high-resolution transmission electron microscope. Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) was performed by an energy dispersive X-ray spectrometer (Oxford instrument X-Max). X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) was collected by a Rigaku Dmax/Ultima IV diffractometer with monochromatized Cu Kα radiation ($\lambda = 1.5418 \text{ Å}$). X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed on an XPS spectrometer (Kratos XSAM800); the binding energies were adjusted with respect to the C1s binding energy of 284.8 eV.

### 2.4. Peroxidase-like activity

The peroxidase-like activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite was examined by the catalytic oxidation of OPDA and TMB in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Typically, the reaction of OPDA (or TMB) with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was performed at 25 °C in 100 mM phosphate buffer (pH 8.0) or 100 mM acetate buffer (pH 4.0) in the presence of 0.05 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite. Unless otherwise stated, the concentrations of OPDA and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were 0.5 and 2.0 mM respectively for the oxidation of OPDA, and the concentrations of TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were 0.2 and 10 mM respectively for the catalytic oxidation of TMB.

### 2.5. Kinetics measurements

Kinetics measurements were performed at 25 °C with 0.05 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite in 100 mM phosphate buffer (pH 8.0) by varying the concentration of OPDA (0.2–1.5 mM) at a fixed concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (2.0 mM) or vice versa by varying the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0.2–6.0 mM) at a fixed concentration of OPDA (1.0 mM). When TMB was used as chromogenic substrate, experiments were carried out in 100 mM acetate buffer (pH 4.0) by varying the concentration of TMB (0.025–0.3 mM) at a fixed concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (20 mM) or by varying the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (2–100 mM) at a fixed concentration of TMB (0.2 mM). The steady-state reaction rates of OPDA and TMB oxidations were obtained by monitoring the absorbance increase at 416 and 652 nm using molar absorption coefficients of 16.7 [34] and 39 mM<sup>−1</sup> cm<sup>−1</sup> [35], respectively. The apparent kinetic parameters were determined by nonlinear least square fitting of the absorbance data to Michaelis-Menten equation ($v = V_{max} \cdot [S] / (K_m + [S])$) or Lineweaver-Burk double reciprocal model ($1/v = (K_m / V_{max}) \cdot (1/[S]) + 1/V_{max}$), where $v$ is the reaction rate, $V_{max}$ is the maximum rate, $[S]$ is the substrate concentration and $K_m$ is the Michaelis constant which represents the affinity of enzyme toward substrate, the value of $K_m$ is equal to the substrate concentration at which the reaction rate is half of the maximum reaction rate.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. Characterization of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite

Fig. 1 shows the XRD pattern of the as-prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O sample. The diffraction peaks at 2θ values of 43.30, 50.43, 74.13° can be ascribed to the reflections of (111), (200) and (220) planes of cubic Cu (marked with ♥) with lattice constant $a = 3.615 \text{ nm}$ (JCPDS No. 65-9743). The peaks at 2θ values of 30.10, 35.46, 56.98, 62.57° can be ascribed to the reflections of (220), (311), (511), (440) planes of cubic Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (marked with ♠) with lattice constant $a = 8.391 \text{ nm}$ (JCPDS No. 65-3107). Additionally, a weak peak at 36.50° (marked with ♦) can be indexed to the (111) plane of Cu<sub>2</sub>O (JCPDS No. 65-3288). The relatively lower intensity for the diffraction peaks of Cu<sub>2</sub>O may be due to the tiny amounts of Cu<sub>2</sub>O. Therefore, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O composite sample was successfully prepared by a simple one-step solvothermal method.

The SEM images show that the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O composite are composed of uniform spherical-like morphology (Fig. 2A and B). The mean diameter of the spherical-like particles is about 50 nm as revealed by the TEM images (Fig. 2C and D). Fig. 2E shows a typical HRTEM image of the composite and the enlarged view of area F in Fig. 2E is depicted in Fig. 2F. The fringe spacings of crystallites (I) and (III) in Fig. 2F were about 0.485 and 0.293 nm, respectively, which was in agreement with the (111) plane distance of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (0.484 nm tabulated from XRD) and the (110) plane distance of cubic Cu<sub>2</sub>O (0.301 nm tabulated from XRD), respectively, while the zone (II) was the superposition area of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and Cu<sub>2</sub>O.

Elemental analysis of the as-prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite was performed with an energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS). The results show that the Cu, Fe and O elements coexist in the nanocomposite (Fig. S1A, Supporting information). The EDS elemental mapping results further confirm that the Cu, Fe elements are uniformly distributed in the nanocomposite (Fig. S1B and C, Supporting information).

The elemental composition and chemical status of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O composite were further analyzed by means of X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). XPS results indicated that the composite contained Fe, Cu, O, and C (Fig. 3A). The carbon peak was due to the adventitious hydrocarbon from the XPS instrument itself. The Fe, Cu and part of O peaks were from the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O sample. Fig. 3B shows the XPS spectrum of Fe 2p of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O sample, the peaks at binding energies (BE) of 712.1 and 724.8 eV were characteristics of Fe<sup>3+</sup>, while the peaks at BE of 709.8 and 722.8 eV were characteristics of Fe<sup>2+</sup>. The satellite signal at 717.6 eV is attributed to the combination of Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup>, for the satellite signals of Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup> are at 715.5 and 719.0 eV, respectively [36,37]. The presence of Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup> in the sample further confirms the formation of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>.

The O 1s core-level spectrum is shown in Fig. 3C. The peak at 530.1 eV is in agreement with the lattice oxygen in Cu<sub>2</sub>O [38–40] and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> [41], the peak at 531.5 eV can be assigned to the presence of hydroxyl species as a result of the surface-adsorbed water on the air-exposed surface of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O composite according to previous studies [38,42–44].

The XPS spectrum of Cu 2p core level region for the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite displays main peaks at 933.3 and 953.1 eV which are attributed to the binding energy of Cu 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Cu 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, respectively (Fig. 3D). The broad peaks of Cu 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Cu 2p<sub>1/2</sub> can be further divided into two different peaks at 932.8, 934.9 eV and 952.6, 955.1 eV, respectively. The peaks at 932.8 and 952.6 eV can be attributed to Cu/Cu<sub>2</sub>O (Cu<sup>0</sup>/Cu<sup>+</sup>) [45], whereas the peaks at 934.9 and 955.1 eV can be assigned to CuO/Cu(OH)<sub>2</sub> (Cu<sup>2+</sup>) [44,46]. Cu<sup>2+</sup> can also be distinguished by the appearance of the shake-up satellite features at ~940.6–943.5 eV [47] and ~961.9 eV (denoted by arrows), which evidenced the open 3d<sup>9</sup> shell of Cu(II) [46,48]. The binding energy at 934.9 for Cu(II) was higher than the previously reported values for CuO in the range of 933.4–933.9 eV [40,48]. It has been reported that when the content of CuO is low, the binding energies of Cu<sup>2+</sup> will shift to higher values [49]. Thus the binding energy of CuO at 934.9 eV in the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O samples may be due to the small content of Cu<sup>2+</sup> on the sample surfaces [50]. From these results it is inferred that the surface of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O sample is oxidized after being exposed to air conditions. In fact, it has been reported that tiny amounts of Cu<sub>2</sub>O on the surface could be oxidized to CuO and/or Cu(OH)<sub>2</sub> species when the surface is exposed in air with humidity [30], and the tiny surface CuO/Cu(OH)<sub>2</sub> can be detected by XPS, but not by XRD. Similar phenomenon has also been observed in our previous work that Cu<sup>2+</sup> signal can be detected by XPS on the surface of Cu<sub>2</sub>O, but CuO and Cu(OH)<sub>2</sub> are not detected by XRD [27]. Based on the differences between the XRD and XPS results, it can be reasoned that XPS is much more sensitive than XRD for surface detection. It has also been reported that XPS only detects signals within the upper 5 nm thickness on the surface, while the XRD can detect signals in micro-order thickness [30,51].

### 3.2. Peroxidase activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite

The peroxidase-like activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite was examined by the catalytic oxidation of OPDA and TMB in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. OPDA and TMB have been widely used as typical chromogenic substrates for investigating the oxidase- and peroxidase-like activities of natural enzymes or mimetic enzymes [6,34,52]. As shown in the insets of Fig. 4A and B, the solutions of OPDA and TMB could react with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to present yellow and blue color, respectively, within 60 min in the presence of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite, whereas, the solutions of OPDA and TMB were still colorless in the absence of the nanocomposite.

The absorption spectrum of OPDA oxidized by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in the presence of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite displayed a characteristic absorption band at ~416 nm in the visible region (Fig. 4A) due to the formation of 2,3-diaminophenazine (Fig. 4C) [34], while the TMB oxidation product showed two characteristic bands at ~370 and ~652 nm in the UV–Vis spectrum (Fig. 4B) due to the formation of diamine and diimine as shown in Fig. 4D [52]. However, the solutions of OPDA and TMB showed no obvious absorption in their UV–Vis spectra in the absence of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite (Fig. 4A and B). These results suggested that the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite possesses intrinsic peroxidase-like activity and can catalyze the oxidation of peroxidase substrates OPDA and TMB in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.

### 3.3. Identification of active component

Previously, it has been reported that Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles have peroxidase-like activity, which can catalyze the oxidation of peroxidase substrates such as OPDA and TMB in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> [14]. In order to identify the active component in the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite, we prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O samples (see Supporting information and Fig. S2) and measured their peroxidase-like activity. As shown in Fig. 5 and Fig. S3, the activity of Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O, without the presence of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, was a little bit lower than that of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O. However, without the presence of Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O, the peroxidase-like activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> was dramatically decreased. In addition, the activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O was also a little bit higher than that of the sum of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O. These results suggest that the peroxidase-like activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O mainly come from Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O and synergistic effect may exist between Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O composite.

It was worth of pointing out that the peroxidase-like activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles reported here was much lower than that of previous report [14]. By carefully comparing the experiment conditions, we found that the size of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles in our experiment is about 50 nm, while ~30 nm in the literature, and the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration used in our experiment was 2 mM, which was much lower than that used in the literature (530 mM). Therefore, the lower peroxidase-like activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles reported here should be due to the larger particle size and the lower H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration used in our experiment.

### 3.4. Effects of pH, catalyst concentration, temperature and time of incubation

As we know, the catalytic activity of natural enzymes, such as HRP, is dependent on solution pH, catalyst concentration and temperature, etc. So we examined these factors on the peroxidase-like activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite. The effect of pH was firstly examined with the catalytic oxidation of OPDA in 100 mM phosphate buffer or acetate buffer. The solution pH was adjusted by addition of a small amount of HCl or NaOH solution. For the oxidation of OPDA, the reaction activity increased with increase in solution pH from 4.0 to 8.0, and then decreased with further increase the solution pH up to 10.0 (Fig. 6A). As of TMB oxidation, the highest reaction activity was observed at pH 4.0 (Fig. S4). This was similar to the effects of pH on the peroxidase activity of HRP and other inorganic materials [53]. The effect of catalyst concentration was studied at the fixed concentrations of OPDA (0.5 mM) and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (2 mM). When Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O was varied in the range of 0.02–0.05 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>, the reaction rate was almost proportional to the catalyst dose, but the rate was not significantly increased when further increase in catalyst concentration up to 0.075 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> (Fig. 6B).

The effect of temperature on the peroxidase-like activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite was investigated by varying temperature in the range of 25–60 °C. As shown in Fig. 6C, the activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O increased with temperature below 45 °C, while decreased with further increase in temperature. It was worth pointing out that when the oxidation reaction was carried out at 60 °C, the color of the oxidation product began to fade after several minutes, indicating that the colored product was easily to be degraded by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in the presence of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite. This might be one of the reasons that the catalytic activity was decreased at high temperature, especially higher than 50 °C. Similar phenomena are also observed by other researchers when Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles [14] and CuS nanoparticles [54] were used as mimetic peroxidases.

The storage of natural enzymes (proteins), especially enzyme solutions, usually requires low temperature (Usually −80 to 4 °C), for natural enzymes is apt to be deteriorated at high temperature, even at room temperature conditions. To test the temperature stability of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite, we measured the peroxidase-like activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite after incubation in water at 40 °C. More than 87% catalytic activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite could be retained after 30 h of incubation (Fig. 6D). Furthermore, the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite could still retain more than 90% of its initial peroxidase-like activity within a storage period of three weeks in water at room temperature, further indicating the good temperature stability of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite as mimetic peroxidase.

### 3.5. Steady-state kinetics study

Steady-state kinetics assays were carried out to further understand the mechanism of the peroxidase-like activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite. The rates of product formation of OPDA and TMB oxidations were determined from the increases in the absorbances at 416 [34] and 652 nm [35], respectively. The experiments were performed with fixed concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and varying the concentration of OPDA (or TMB) or vice versa in the presence of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite. As shown in Fig. 7 and Fig. S5, the plots of reaction rate vs. OPDA (or TMB) and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration show typical Michaelis-Menten behavior [55]. The kinetic parameters, such as the Michaelis constant ($K_m$) and maximum velocity ($V_{max}$) were obtained by using the nonlinear least square fitting of the data to Michaelis-Menten equation and also by using the Lineweaver-Burk plots of the double reciprocal model. Data from both models were in good agreement and were summarized in Table 1 and Table S1. By comparing with the kinetic parameters of other reported inorganic materials and natural peroxidase HRP (Table 1 and Table S1), it can be seen that the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite possesses intrinsic peroxidase-like activity and shows good affinity toward OPDA, TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. For example, The $K_m$ and $V_{max}$ values were 0.85 mM and $13.1 \times 10^{−8} \text{ M s}^{−1}$ for OPDA and 2.3 mM and $11.9 \times 10^{−8} \text{ M s}^{−1}$ for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, respectively. These values are comparable to those of natural peroxidase HRP (Table 1).

In addition, the change of the maximum absorbance of OPDA oxidation at 416 nm was linear increase with the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> from 0.4 to 1.5 mM with limit of detection of 0.2 mM (Fig. S6A). When TMB was used as chromogenic substrate, the absorbance change at 652 nm was linear increase with the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> from 4 mM to 50 mM with a limit of detection of 2 mM (Fig. S6B). These results suggest that a colorimetric method for the detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could be developed based on the intrinsic peroxidase-like activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite.

### 3.6. Recyclability and reusability

To test the recyclability and reusability of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite, the catalytic oxidation of OPDA in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was examined. After the first round of experiment, the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O catalyst was separated by an external magnetic field from the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O-OPDA-H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> reaction system and washed with water three times, then the recycled Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O was reused to catalyze the oxidation of OPDA under the same experimental conditions as the first round. As shown in Fig. 8A, the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite could be successfully recycled by a magnet. The catalytic activity was not significantly lost with three cycles (Fig. 8B).

## 4. Conclusion

In summary, we demonstrated a facile and simple one-step method for the preparation of magnetically recyclable Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite. The as-prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite exhibited highly efficient peroxidase-like activity, it can catalyze the oxidation of peroxidase substrates such as OPDA and TMB in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. A simple colorimetric method for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection could be established based on the peroxidase-like activity of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite. Furthermore, the as-prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite can be easily recycled and reused without significant loss of its catalytic activity. The recyclability, reusability and nonpolluting nature of the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocomposite would have potential applications in the field of catalysis, biosensor, environmental monitoring and protection.

### Preparation of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and Cu@Cu<sub>2</sub>O
