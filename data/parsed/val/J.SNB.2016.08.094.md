# Montmorillonite-loaded ceria nanocomposites with superior peroxidase-like activity for rapid colorimetric detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>

## ABSTRACT

An efficient novel colorimetric sensor for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> based on peroxidase-like CeO<sub>2</sub>-montmorillonite (MMT) nanocomposites that were successfully prepared by a facile one-pot approach, was realized. In comparison with other nanomaterials as peroxidase mimics, the catalytic reaction by the well-dispersed CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites was in accordance with typical Michaelis–Menten kinetics, which exhibited a higher affinity to 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, bringing about a superior peroxidase-like activity (a distinct blue color change was observed rapidly within 30 s, by the naked eye.) than that of pure CeO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles, MMT and their hybrid materials, respectively. Under the optimized conditions, CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites were used to establish a colorimetric biosensor for the detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in a relative wide range of 9 × 10<sup>−6</sup> M to 5 × 10<sup>−4</sup> M with a lower detection limit of 7.8 × 10<sup>−6</sup> M. The sensor was successfully applied in H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection in milk samples.

## 1. Introduction

It is well known that hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) is not only the intermediate or final product in many processes such as food, medicine, enzyme, chemistry, biology, and environmental industry. Consequently, there are many analytical methods to be developed for monitoring of hydrogen peroxide, such as chemiluminescence [1], electrochemistry [2], fluorescence [3], bioelectrochemistry [4], liquid chromatography [5], and colorimetric assay [6]. Among these determined methodologies, the colorimetric assay has been drawn attention due to its absolute advantages in timesaver, low-cost, pretty simplicity and sensitivity as well as practical application. Furthermore, the detection of analytes using the colorimetric method can also be immediately interpreted by the naked eye to realize visual detection without any expensive or sophisticated instrumentation [7].

Recently, based on the colorimetric method described above, people have focused on artificial enzymes which are applied in biosensing and pharmaceutical processes, owing to their better design flexibility, high efficiency and substrate specificity, as well as excellent stability under mild reaction conditions, compared with that of natural enzymes. Because preparation of metal oxide in nanoscale becomes governable and the lattice matching between metal oxides is not difficult to achieve, more and more studies have reported that the nano-oxides can be used as artificial peroxidases to catalyze oxidation of colorless substrates and give different colored products in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which can be used to detect H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> observed by the naked eye. For example, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles were firstly found to possess the peroxidase activity and were used to detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> [8]. In the subsequent studies, various oxides including Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles [9], CuO nanoclusters [10], and V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> nanowires [11] have also been proven to possess the effective activity of peroxidase mimics [12].

Among the various metal oxides, nanoceria has been widely applied in various fields, such as catalysis, electrochemistry, photochemistry, metal polishing agents, fuel cells, gas sensors, and luminescence, etc. [13–19]. In addition, it is known that ceria (CeO<sub>2</sub>) has a wide band gap (3.4 eV) with high isoelectric point (IEP, 9.0) and excellent retention of biological activity for enzyme binding [20,21]. Furthermore, ceria has been considered as a promising material for the fabrication of mediator-less electrochemical biosensors because of its ability to act as a redox couple [22]. Just as the reported oxides, CeO<sub>2</sub> is capable of mimicking the activity of several natural redox enzymes, largely based on the Ce<sup>4+</sup> ↔ Ce<sup>3+</sup> redox switch in biomimetic catalytic applications, besides possessing the high biocompatibility [23–28]. However, pure CeO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles with smaller size are easy to agglomerate, resulting in the decrease of their catalytic activity. In order to avoid of agglomeration of CeO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles, it is necessary to find a support to disperse the nanoparticles.

Montmorillonite (MMT), a layered material, has been widely applied in catalysis, separation, optical and electrical devices due to its versatile properties, such as large specific surface area, chemical and mechanical stability, layered structure and high cation exchange capacity [29]. Furthermore, the MMT layer consists of an octahedral sheet sandwiched between two opposing tetrahedral sheets (TOT), which is called a 2:1 phyllosilicate [30]. So far, a variety of nanoparticles have been prepared using MMT as a support, such as oxides (TiO<sub>2</sub>, ZnO and Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) [31–34] and metals (Cu, Ag, Pd, Pt as well as Co) [35–38]. However, to the best of our knowledge, there is no report on preparation of CeO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles supported with MMT. With these in mind, it is deduced that CeO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles using MMT as a support maybe potentially possess the enhanced peroxidase activity in catalyzing the oxidation of substrates in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.

Herein, CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites were prepared via a “one-step” hydrothermal route and used as novel nanocatalysts to establish a novel H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensor. In addition, in order to comparatively investigate, the pure CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs without MMT, and their hybrid material have been prepared under the vigorous stirring for 3 h, due to the large specific surface area and adsorbed properties of MMT. As a result, The CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites demonstrated higher stability, dispersity and affinity to the peroxidase substrate 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB). The CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites were proved to possess the enhanced peroxidase activity and rapidly catalyze to oxidize TMB into a blue product in the present of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which can be observed by the naked eye. This method provides the sensitive colorimetric detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. With the aid of this simple method, as low as 7.8 μM H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could be detected with a wider linear range from 9 to 500 μM. Thus, we develop a facile colorimetric method for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection based on the peroxidase activity of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites.

## 2. Experimental section

### 2.1. Materials

Ce(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>•6H<sub>2</sub>O, montmorillonite (MMT) and TMB were purchased from Solarbio (Tianjin, China). Hydrogen peroxide (30% wt%, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>), sodium acetate (NaAc) and acetic acid (HAc) were purchased from Gucheng Reagent Co. (Shanghai, China), Terephthalic acid (TA) was purchased from Chengdu cologne chemical reagent factory. The reagents, above all, were of analytical reagent grade and used without further purification.

### 2.2. Characterization

The crystal structures of the products were determined by means of the powder X-ray diffractometer (XRD) with graphite monochromatized Cu Kα radiation (D/Max2500PC, Rigaku). The morphology and shape of the nanoparticles were examined on the transmission electron microscope with an acceleration voltage of 200 kV (TEM JEM-2100, JEOL, Japan). In addition, the Fluorometric data were recorded on a Cary Eclipse spectrofluorophotometer (Varian, Inc, USA), and the UV–vis spectra were carried out by a MAPADA UV-3200PC spectrophotometer (Shanghai, China). The Micro-electrophoresis Apparatus-JS94H was used to measure zeta potential.

### 2.3. Preparation of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites

Firstly, 0.5 g MMT was dissolved in 30 mL distilled water under the vigorous stirring for 18 h to form a homogeneous suspension. After that, 0.217 g (ca. 0.5 mM) Ce(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>•6H<sub>2</sub>O solution as a precursor serving Ce<sup>3+</sup> cation was dropwise added into the above solution under continuously stirring for another 2 h. Then, 2 mL hydrogen peroxide (30%) was cautiously dropwise added into the obtained solution. After the solution being stirred for one more hour, the mixture was sealed in a polytetrafluoroethylene-lined stainless steel autoclave and heated at 150 °C for 6 h. Subsequently, the solution was naturally cooled to room temperature, separated by centrifugation and washed with deionized water for three times. Finally, the products were collected and dried under atmospheric condition at 60 °C for 10 h. The CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites were obtained and used in the following experiments.

For comparative investigation, we have prepared the CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs and CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT hybrids, respectively. For example, the preparation of CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs was similar to that of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites in the absence of MMT. The CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT hybrids were prepared by mixing MMT with CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs in the distilled water under vigorous stirring for 3 h.

### 2.4. Catalytic oxidation of TMB by CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites

The peroxidase-like activity of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites was evaluated in measuring the catalytic capability of the peroxidase substrate TMB•2HCl in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. In a series of representative experiments, 0.2 mL of TMB (1 mM) in sodium acetate buffer solution (pH 3.8) was dealt with different reaction systems, listed in Table 1. Each of systems, the total volume 2 mL, was measured by monitoring the absorbance (at 637 nm) in time-scan mode and absorption spectra from 300 to 800 nm, respectively.

### 2.5. Kinetics measurements

The reaction kinetic measurements were recorded in time course mode on a UV–vis absorbance spectrophotometer. Catalytic experiments were carried out as follows: 0.3 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites in HAc-NaAc buffer solution (pH 3.8) treated with 0.2 mL of TMB (0.1–1 mM) as a substrate and 0.2 mL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0.25 M), or 0.2 mL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (10–1000 mM) as a substrate and TMB (1 mM) with a total reaction volume of 2 mL at room temperature. The Michaelis–Menten constant ($K_m$) was calculated by the Lineweaver–Burk plot: $1/v = K_m/V_{max} (1/[S] + 1/K_m)$, where $v$ is the initial velocity, $V_{max}$ is the maximal reaction velocity, $K_m$ is the Michaelis constant and $[S]$ is the concentration of the substrate [39].

### 2.6. Detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> using CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites

The colorimetric detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> using CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites as catalysts was investigated by measuring capability of the catalytic oxidation of the peroxidase substrate TMB (0.2 mL, 1 mM) in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0.2 mL), together with 1.4 mL sodium acetate buffer solution (pH 3.8) and 0.2 mL of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites solution (0.3 mg·mL<sup>−1</sup>).

Fig. 1b shows the XRD patterns of the as-prepared CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs without MMT as a support. The characteristic planes (1 1 1), (2 0 0), (2 2 0), (3 1 1), (4 0 0), (4 2 0) were attributable to the face-centred cubic fluorite structure of CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs (JCPDS34-0394), which shows spiculate peaks related to the high crystallinity. From Fig. 1c, it can be seen that the characteristic but relatively weak peaks (marked with *) at 2θ = 28.5°, 32.9°, 47.4°, 56.8° of CeO<sub>2</sub> from the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites were in accordance with pure CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs (Fig. 1b), suggesting that CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs were deposited on the layers of MMT.

The microstructure and morphology of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites (Fig. 2A), the pure CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs (Fig. 3A) and hybrid materials (Fig. 3B) were imaged by TEM. The HRTEM image shown in Fig. 2B reveals clear lattice fringes with a lattice spacing of 1.63 nm corresponding to (1 1 1) plane of CeO<sub>2</sub>. However, the pure CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs without MMT were easy to aggregate (Fig. 3A), due to the absence of a support. Moreover, the aggregated CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs had a relatively better physical dispersity on the layers of MMT by employing MMT as the support under continuous stirring for a relatively long time at room temperature, shown in Fig. 3B. The dispersed CeO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles on layers of MMT are name as the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanohybrids.

### 3.2. Peroxidase-like activity of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites

The peroxidase-like activity of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites was evaluated by the catalytic oxidation of TMB, a representative peroxidase substrate, in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. As shown in Fig. 5a, when CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites were added into H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-TMB system, a distinct blue change of the system (TMB + CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) was observed rapidly within 30 s, by the naked eye (Scheme 1). In contrast, the experimental system without CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites, shown in Fig. 5e, exhibits a negligible color change. These results indicate that CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites possesses the superior peroxidase-like catalytic activity. In addition, the control experiments (Fig. 5a, c and d) showed that the catalytic activity of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites is higher than that of MMT or CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs alone. The reasons are as follows: One is probably due to the synergistic effects between MMT and CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs. The other is that the smaller CeO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles with a stability in aqueous solution well dispersed onto the layers of MMT, shown as in Fig. 4. Moreover, it can be also seen that the system of TMB + CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites did not demonstrate a color change, suggesting that the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites can not oxidize the substrate TMB without H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Furthermore, from Fig. 6, it can be found that the substance with the highest peroxidase-like activity was CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites, among four comparative materials in this study. After the aggregated CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs were well dispersed onto the layers of MMT (CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT hybrids), the catalytic activity of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT hybrids were higher than that of pure CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs while still lower than that of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites obtained by a “one-step” method.

### 3.3. Experimental condition optimization

Furthermore, the catalytic activity of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites depends on pH, temperature, the amount of catalysts and the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, similar to that of the natural enzyme HRP and other nanomaterial-based peroxidase mimics [40–43a]. The peroxidase-like activity of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites is investigated by varying the pH from 1.7 to 8, the temperatures from 23 °C to 70 °C, the catalyst concentration from 0 to 0.03 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> and the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration from 2.5 mM to 250 mM, respectively. As shown in Fig. 7A, the catalytic activity of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites increased with increasing the pH from 1.7 to 3.8, and then decreased. Thus, the optimal pH was approximately 3.8, indicating that the oxidation of TMB occurs easily under acidic conditions [43b]. In addition, it may be ascribed to that ceria is unstable under strong acidic conditions to decompose the surface superabundant peroxide species into hydroxyl radicals [43c]. Besides, the preferable adsorption as well as chemical activity of MMT under strong acidic conditions are worth noting [43d]. From Fig. 7B, it can be seen that the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites remained the quite commendable catalytic activity from 50 °C to 65 °C, indicating that the nanocomposites possessed a higher temperature resistance. Fig. 7C shows that the reaction rate increased with the increase of the amount of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites. Similarly, the higher the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, the higher the catalytic activity of nanocomposites. However, the rate growth became slow under high H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration, shown in Fig. 7D. In order to enhance the efficiency of the catalysts as well as avoid the self-decomposition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at higher concentrations, a H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration of 25 mM was adopted for the following assays [43e].

**Fig. 7.** The peroxidase-like activity of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites depends on pH (A), temperature (B), catalyst concentration (C), and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration (D), respectively. The maximum point in each curve (A–D) was set as 100%.

### 3.4. Kinetic studies of the peroxidase-like activity of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites

To investigate the mechanism of the peroxidase activity of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites, the apparent steady-state kinetic parameters ($K_m$ and $V_{max}$) for the initial reaction that conformed to the typical Michaelis–Menten model were determined with a suitable concentration range of TMB or H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as substrates by changing the concentration of one substrate and keeping the other constant (Fig. 8A and 8B). All of the parameters were also regressed to the Lineweaver–Burk double-reciprocal model ($1/v_0$ vs $1/[S]$), which also gave the analogous values (Fig. 8C and D) [44]. As shown in Table 3, the $K_m$ value of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrate was 3.399 mM, slightly lower than that of horseradish peroxidase HRP reported in the previous publication [28], indicating that CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites had a bit better affinity for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> than that of HRP. However, the $K_m$ value with TMB (1 mM) as the substrate was 0.0105 mM, 41 times lower than that of HRP (0.434 mM) and much lower than other catalysts listed in Table 3, suggesting that CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites have a quite higher affinity to TMB. In addition, the pure MMT showed a higher affinity to the substrate TMB as well, due to the larger surface area of the layered MMT with a stronger adsorption ability to TMB.

### 3.5. Mechanism of the peroxidase-like activity of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites

The previous studies have reported that the catalytic mechanism of nanocomposites is the generation of hydroxyl radicals (•OH) from the catalytic decomposition of acidified H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> [46]. As mentioned above, it is believed that CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites would facilitate the electron transfer between H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB and then catalyze H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to decompose into hydroxyl radicals under acidic conditions, which oxidizes TMB into a blue product, as illustrated in Scheme 1. To confirm this assumption, the •OH formation was evaluated by adding a fluorescent probe, the terephthalic acid (TA), into the reaction system of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposite. The TA molecule easily reacted with •OH to form a strong fluorescent product, 2-hydroxyterephthalic acid (TAOH), which emitted unique fluorescence around 435 nm [47,48]. 0.35 mM TA, 0.25 M H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and the different amount of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites have been incubated in HAc-NaAc buffer (pH 3.8) at 40 °C for 15 min. The incubated solutions were used to investigate fluorescence intensity. The results show that the fluorescent intensity of the experimental systems gradually enhanced with the increasing amount of CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites (Fig. 9), suggesting that the more the nanocomposites, the more the produced •OH.

On account of the intrinsic peroxidase-like activity of the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites is H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration dependent, the system investigated above could be applied to detect H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with a simple colorimetric method. A typical dose-response curve at 637 nm depending on the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> from 1 μM to 800 μM under the optimal conditions was exhibited in Fig. 10A. From Fig. 10, it can be found that an obvious trend of absorbance gradually increased with increasing of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration. The corresponding linear calibration plot is shown in Fig. 10B, revealing a good linear relationship between the absorbance of oxidized TMB and the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in the range of 9–500 μM with a detection limit of 7.8 μM. ## 4. Conclusion

In conclusion, CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites firstly were prepared by a facile “one-step” method and demonstrated to possess an intrinsic peroxidase-like activity, which was much higher than that of pure CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs, pure MMT and CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanohybrids, respectively. CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites displayed higher dispersity and stability in water. The catalytic behaviors of the nanocomposites were from the generation of the hydroxyl radicals. The activity of the artificial enzyme was dependent on the pH, temperature, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and catalyst concentration. Additionally, the CeO<sub>2</sub>-MMT nanocomposites exhibited a higher affinity to TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Furthermore, on the basis of the above results, a colorimetric method for the detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is developed. The colorimetric assay demonstrates not only a higher sensitivity but also a wider responding range of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, suggesting that the designed sensor of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> has a promising application in food detection, biochemical analysis and environmental monitoring.
