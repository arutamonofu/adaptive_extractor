**Intrinsic peroxidase-like activity of mesoporous nickel oxide for selective cysteine sensing†**

Chaiti Ray, Soumen Dutta, Sougata Sarkar, Ramkrishna Sahoo, Anindita Roy and Tarasankar Pal*

Mesoporous nickel oxide nanoflowers (NiO NFs) can be easily synthesized by a two-step synthetic procedure based on modified hydrothermal (MHT) treatment of nickel acetate and ethanol amine in water followed by thermal decomposition at 350 °C for 4 h. After thermal treatment, the porosity is increased by 18% with retention of parental nickel hydroxide size. In this study, for the first time, a new catalytic application of NiO NFs has been revealed in terms of peroxidase-like activity where colorless 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) is oxidized to blue color product in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at room temperature. Comparative study confirms that mesoporous NiO NFs exhibit superior catalytic activity to the parent analogues, i.e. Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> or bulk NiO. This intrinsic peroxidase-like activity from an easily synthesized inorganic nanomaterial provides an alternative to horseradish peroxidase (HRP) enzyme. The lower Michaelis constant ($K_m$) value indicates that the catalyst NiO NFs bind efficiently to the test substrate, i.e. TMB. Interestingly, the NiO NFs-catalyzed TMB oxidation, i.e. blue color formation, has been found to be selectively and successively inhibited by a variable amount of cysteine among a set of 21 congeners. Thus our adopted simple, low-cost and novel colorimetric assay stands to be a highly efficient approach for selective detection of cysteine with a limit of detection (LOD) value of ~1.1 μM using a simple UV-vis spectrophotometer. The proposed method also exhibits outstanding selectivity and accuracy for N-acetyl cysteine (an analogue of cysteine) estimation in real pharmaceutical samples.

**Introduction**

As a result of their exclusive optical and electronic properties,<sup>1</sup> nanomaterials, especially functional nanomaterials, are of great importance in a wide range of applications including effective energy storage,<sup>2</sup> sensing,<sup>3</sup> nanodevices,<sup>4</sup> and catalysis.<sup>5</sup> Among different types of catalysis, artificial enzyme mimetics have attracted tremendous attention in recent years.<sup>6,7</sup>

Peroxidase is one type of redox enzyme that catalyzes decomposition of peroxide and oxidation of some peroxidase substrates. Horseradish peroxidase (HRP) is an example of a natural peroxidase enzyme, but it has some fundamental disadvantages that hinder its large-scale applications, such as low stability, a requirement for suitable reaction conditions, and high cost in preparation and purification. In the search for alternatives, comprehensive investigations have been performed using a variety of nanoparticles, functional polymers, and hybrid composites as catalysts. In the literature, the inherent peroxidase activity of numerous nanomaterials, including Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs,<sup>6</sup> Co–Al ELDH,<sup>7</sup> V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> NWs,<sup>8</sup> polymer-coated CeO<sub>2</sub> NPs,<sup>9</sup> BiFeO<sub>3</sub> NPs,<sup>10</sup> TiO<sub>2</sub> NPs,<sup>11</sup> FeS and FeSe NPs,<sup>12</sup> Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs,<sup>13,14</sup> bimetallic alloys NPs,<sup>15,16</sup> CuS NPs,<sup>17</sup> Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>–Au NCs,<sup>18</sup> Pt NTs,<sup>19</sup> graphene oxide (GO),<sup>20</sup> Au-reduced graphene oxide composite,<sup>21</sup> carbon nanodots (C-dots),<sup>22</sup> and single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs),<sup>23</sup> has been established. However, complex reaction strategy and high cost for the reported cases have encouraged further attempts to find a new, low-cost, easily synthesized nanomaterial with high peroxidase mimic activity.

Of the various transition metal-based nanomaterials, nickel hydroxide (Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub>) has received a good deal of attention because of its easy synthesis to obtain porous architecture, which enhances its potential applications in the areas of superconductors<sup>24</sup> and photocatalysis.<sup>25</sup> Moreover, Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> is also a preferable precursor for synthesis of NiO, which is a p-type semiconductor. In this work, β-nickel hydroxide nanoflowers (Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs) were synthesized using a cost-effective, aqueous solution-based hydrothermal process, followed by calcinations to produce mesoporous NiO NFs. Nickel oxide is also an interesting material with varied well-known applications in solar cells,<sup>26</sup> devices,<sup>27</sup> gas sensors,<sup>28</sup> and catalysis.<sup>29</sup> Our synthesized NiO nanostructures demonstrate a new catalytic activity as a peroxidase mimetic, where colorless peroxidase substrate 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) transforms into its blue color oxidized form in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Thus, NiO NFs-driven oxidation of colorless to blue colored product gives a good measure for colorimetric detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.

Cysteine (Cys) is a semi-essential amino acid, containing sulfur as an active donor site. It is found in many proteins in the body, and forms a special disulfide bond.<sup>30</sup> This disulfide compound is essential in construction of the secondary structure of protein.<sup>31</sup> Hence, Cys has vital roles in the human body in protein synthesis, detoxification, and metabolism, and Cys deficiency causes slow cell growth, liver damage, hair depigmentation, and malabsorption syndromes.<sup>32</sup> N-acetyl cysteine (NAC), a well-known derivative of cysteine, is a pharmaceutical drug and nutrition supplement. It is used as a dietary supplement for its antioxidant and liver protecting actions. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), NAC is listed as an essential medicine for the basic health system. Therefore, detection of Cys and NAC are noteworthy for human health.

To date, scenario detection of Cys has been considered to be extremely significant and demanding. Hence, immense effort has been given to detection of Cys by various techniques, including UV-vis spectroscopy,<sup>33,34</sup> fluorescence spectroscopy,<sup>35</sup> FTIR spectroscopy,<sup>36</sup> electrochemical voltammetry,<sup>37</sup> high performance liquid chromatography,<sup>38</sup> mass spectroscopy,<sup>39</sup> and so on. However, in most of the procedures, expensive instrumentation is required and complicated sample preparations are followed, which suggests limited practical applications. Hence, a colorimetric detection process would be desirable for its simplicity and cost-effectiveness and routine analysis. In this work, developed peroxidase-like activity by NiO NFs behaves as a selective sensor towards Cys, with an extension on NAC detection in pharmaceutical samples with limit of detection ~1 μM.

**Experimental section**

**Materials and instruments**

The related information is presented as S1 in the ESI.†

**Synthesis of β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> and NiO NFs.** In typical β-nickel hydroxide nanoflowers (Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs) synthesis, 0.8 mL ethanol amine was dripped into 4.2 mL 0.1 M nickel acetate (Ni(OAc)<sub>2</sub>) solution in a screw-capped test tube of 25 mL capacity to form a blue colored nickel–ethanol amine complex. Then, with a submerged glass slide (2.5 cm × 0.7 cm), the resulting blue colored solution was sealed in the screw-capped test tube and subjected to overnight (~12 h) heating under modified hydrothermal (MHT)<sup>40</sup> conditions at 110 °C. After a specific time of 12 h, a uniform greenish-white thin film on the glass slide was deposited and the solid film was recovered by decanting the clear supernatant solution. Then, the thin film was washed several times with water and dried in an air oven for 1 h at 60 °C.

To obtain gray-black nickel oxide nanoflowers (NiO NFs), the greenish-white precursor was annealed at 350 °C for 4 h under ambient conditions in a tube furnace.

**Catalytic oxidation of TMB.** To study the peroxidase activity of NiO NFs, catalytic oxidation of 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) in acetate buffer was performed in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. To investigate the catalytic activity of NiO for TMB oxidation, a 25 μL TMB solution (0.01 M) was diluted by 2.5 mL acetate buffer solution (0.01 M and pH 4.0), and was sequentially treated by 0.5 mg catalyst and 100 μL H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (~0.1 M). Kinetic analyses with TMB as substrate were carried out by taking a fixed amount of NiO (0.5 mg) as catalyst and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (100 μL) with various TMB concentrations (0–200 μM). Similar kinetic analyses with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as substrate were performed using 0.5 mg catalyst with a fixed amount of TMB (25 μL) and different H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentrations (9–74 mM). The Michaelis–Menten parameters were evaluated from a Lineweaver–Burk plot of $1/V_0$ vs. $1/S_0$.

**Detection of cysteine using NiO.** At room temperature, the TMB solution (25 μM, 0.01 M) and cysteine (Cys) in various concentrations (10–100 μM) were added to 2.5 mL acetate buffer solution (pH 4.0). Then, 0.5 mg NiO catalyst and 100 μL H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0.1 M) were added into the mixture. The variation in blue color of the reaction mixture was monitored and quantified spectrophotometrically, which gives a quantitative measure of the Cys concentration. Naked eye detection of Cys concentration is also possible from a color chart or from the spectrophotometric results.

Again, for quantitative colorimetric detection of N-acetyl cysteine (NAC) at room temperature in 2.5 mL acetate buffer solution (pH 4.0), the TMB solution (25 μM, 0.01 M) and NAC at various concentrations (5–60 μM), 0.5 mg NiO catalyst, and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (100 μL, 0.1 M) were added and absorbance was measured using a UV-vis spectrophotometer.

All the catalytic experiments were performed four times to investigate reproducibility.

**Results and discussion**

In this work, we report synthesis of walnut-like β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs hydrothermally using Ni(OAc)<sub>2</sub> and ethanol amine as precursor compounds. NiO NFs were obtained after calcination of β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs at 350 °C for 4 h. NiO NFs are introduced as a new catalyst for oxidation of TMB and ortho-phenylene diamine (OPDA) in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Scheme 1).

![Scheme 1](Scheme1.png)
**Scheme 1** Schematic representation of stepwise formation of NiO NFs showing peroxidase-like activity toward TMB and OPDA as substrates in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.

The crystallographic structural characteristics of the as-obtained greenish-white β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs and gray-black NiO NFs were investigated using XRD analysis. The as-synthesized β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs have single-phase hexagonal structure (Fig. 1a, JCPDS 14-0117). The absence of any other peaks authenticates the synthesis of pure β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs. The gray-black NiO NFs were obtained after calcination of β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs at 350 °C for 4 h in air (Fig. 1b, JCPDS 71-1179). No peaks were found for β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs in the XRD pattern of the calcined sample, which confirms the complete and quantitative transformation of β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs to NiO NFs.

In the XPS study of Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> in Fig. 1b, the presence of Ni 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Ni 2p<sub>1/2</sub> peaks of Ni(II) at 856.2 eV and 873.9 eV indicates the formation of phase pure Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub>. In addition, the extra two peaks at 861.4 eV and 879.1 eV are ascribed as the two satellite peaks of Ni 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Ni 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, respectively. In Fig. 1c, the XPS spectrum of NiO shows two major peaks at 854.03 eV and 871.8 eV for Ni 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Ni 2p<sub>1/2</sub> of Ni(II), with corresponding satellite peaks at 860.1 eV and 878.4 eV, respectively. It is interesting to note that XPS spectra do not contain any other peaks related to Ni(III), which substantiates the formation of pure Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> and NiO in respective cases.

FTIR analysis was performed to obtain additional evidence of the as-synthesized β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs and conversion to NiO NFs after calcination (Fig. 1d). The band at 459 cm<sup>−1</sup> appeared for Ni–OH stretching frequency, but disappeared in the case of NiO with a new band arising near 400 cm<sup>−1</sup> for Ni–O stretching frequency. The presence of a band at ~517 cm<sup>−1</sup> (in plane deformation mode of free hydroxyl groups, δ<sub>O–H</sub>) confirms the β-phase of Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub>. At ~1380 cm<sup>−1</sup>, a band appears for the τ(NH<sub>2</sub>), twisted vibration. The peak at ~1445 cm<sup>−1</sup> indicates the presence of carbonate ion because of the surface adsorption of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>. The band near 1630 cm<sup>−1</sup> is ascribed to stretching and bending vibrations of surface adsorbed (hydrogen bonded) water molecules. The sharp peak at ~3647 cm<sup>−1</sup> and a broad peak at ~3438 cm<sup>−1</sup> are assigned as a non-hydrogen bonded free hydroxyl group and a hydrogen bonded hydroxyl group, respectively, in spectrum a. The absences of the peak at ~3647 cm<sup>−1</sup> and the comparatively low intense peak at ~3438 cm<sup>−1</sup> indicate formation of NiO after calcination of β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub>.

The morphology of the light-green colored β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs was examined using different microscopic techniques. Fig. 2a and b shows corresponding FESEM images at low and medium resolution, which indicate that the architectures have average diameter ~3–5 μm and consist of interconnected, smooth surfaced wavy plates of ~15 nm thickness. The morphology of Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> resembles a flower structure. Thus, that a unique hierarchical, well-defined and discrete micro-flower with multilayered texture can be synthesized easily using this facile process is also authenticated by FESEM analysis. Fig. 2c–e shows corresponding TEM, SAED, and HRTEM images of the synthesized Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs. In a low-magnification TEM image, a single walnut structure was observed (Fig. 2c). Fig. 2d indicates the layered structure of Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs. The difference in color shade confirms the porous nature of the product. From HRTEM analysis the observed lattice gap ~0.227 nm has good agreement with the d-spacing value of (101) XRD planes (Fig. 2e). In a SAED pattern (inset), bright spots indicate the crystalline nature of the material.

The morphological analyses were performed with the calcined product of β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs, i.e. NiO NFs. The size of the walnuts almost remains the same, but the interplate spacing is different, which has already been mentioned. This increased spacing increases the porous nature of the material, NiO NFs. From the FESEM images (Fig. 3a and b), the highly porous nature of the NiO NFs can be observed. The TEM analysis was executed to understand the crystallinity and texture of the sample. TEM images (Fig. 3c and d) at medium and low resolution indicate abundantly formed flower-like structures, organized into distinguishable nanopetals. The darker central part with fading towards the edges reveals the preferential compactness of the nanopetals at the centre rather than the exterior. At the exterior, the difference in contrast of the petal edges and the areas underneath authenticates the porous nature of the sample. In HRTEM analysis (Fig. 3f) the fringe spacing is observed ~0.19 nm, which indicates the (200) XRD plane, and the SAED pattern shows the highly crystalline nature of NiO NFs.

The porous nature of β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs and calcined product NiO NFs are evident from FESEM and TEM analyses. Nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherm measurements were studied to investigate their surface porosity. Fig. 4 represents the nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms of β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> and NiO NFs. The calculated Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas are 50.363 and 113.707 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> for β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> and NiO NFs, respectively. The Barret–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) method is used for measurement of pore diameter distribution of β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> and NiO NFs (inset of Fig. 4). The pore size for β-Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs is 3.1 nm, which has been increased to 3.8 nm for NiO NFs.

**Catalysis**

In recent years the topic of artificial enzymes in biocatalysis has become more popular. In this paper, we demonstrate an approach for detection of bioactive molecules based on catalytic activity of a new enzyme-like nanomaterial. Herein, NiO NFs show excellent catalytic property for oxidation of peroxidase substrate, TMB, to produce a blue color in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. In addition, this peroxidase-like reaction is used for selective and quantitative detection of cysteine.

To investigate the catalytic property of NiO NFs for oxidation of TMB in the presence or absence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, an oxidation reaction was performed under five different conditions: (i) TMB + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + NiO, (ii) Control 1; TMB + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub>, (iii) Control 2; TMB + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + bulk NiO, (iv) Control 3; TMB + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, (v) Control 4; TMB + catalyst. Fig. 5a shows that the Control 3 system remains colorless, and the Control 4 shows an insignificant color change. Again, in the presence of different catalysts, the TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> mixture shows blue coloration with different color intensity exhibiting different absorbance values that follow the order: bulk NiO < Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs < NiO NFs. These results can be explained in terms of their BET surface area. Synthesized NiO NFs possess more catalytically active surface area than Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs as suggested by BET analyses. The catalytic oxidation of TMB by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in the presence of NiO is shown in Fig. 5b.

A leaching experiment was performed to rule out the possibility that the oxidation is caused by leached out Ni(II) ions. To perform the leaching experiment, NiO was incubated in solution for 30 min at room temperature under ultrasonication. After centrifuging, the supernatant was used for the experiment. The insignificant absorbance at the spectrophotometer ensured that the peroxidase-like activity initiated from NiO rather than leached out Ni(II) ions (Fig. S1, ESI†).

Similarly, oxidation of another well-known peroxidase substrate ortho-phenylene diamine (OPDA) was investigated in the presence of synthesized NiO NFs, and the results are shown in Fig. S2 (ESI†). Within a few minutes, orange coloration occurs with an absorbance peak ~443 nm in the UV-vis spectra, which is characteristic of OPDA oxidation.

The peroxidase-like catalytic activity of NiO NFs towards TMB oxidation is dependent on pH and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration, like other natural enzymes. The catalytic activity was studied with a wide pH range, 3.6–8.0, and showed maximum activity at pH ~4.0 (weak acidic condition) (Fig. 6a). Thus, 10<sup>−2</sup> M acetate buffer with a pH of 4.0 was selected as an optimal reaction condition to achieve higher catalytic activity. The effect of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration on the peroxidase process was investigated by varying the concentration in the ~10 to 75 mM range (Fig. 6b). Based on peroxidase-like activity of NiO NFs, a colorimetric assay was developed for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection. Although the absorbance of oxidized TMB is proportional to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration, it is a simple way for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection using a spectrophotometer at λ = 653 nm. Fig. 6b shows the increase of absorbance of oxidized TMB with increase in H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration (18.6–74.5 mM). The inset represents a calibration curve of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration with a range of 18–56 mM, with limit of detection (LOD) 1 mM (Fig. 6c).

**Steady-state kinetic analysis of peroxidase substrate oxidation**

The peroxidase-like catalytic behavior of the as-synthesized NiO NFs and Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs was investigated spectrophotometrically at room temperature upon addition of catalysts into TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> mixture separately. The progress of the reaction was examined by following the increase in absorbance value of blue colored oxidized product at λ<sub>max</sub> = 653 nm.

Furthermore, the kinetics of the oxidation process were investigated applying steady-state kinetics conditions. A series of experiments was performed by varying concentration of one substrate and keeping the other one constant. The absorbance values, i.e. the experimental results, were converted to the corresponding concentration terms using the ε value of oxidized blue product (39 000 M<sup>−1</sup> cm<sup>−1</sup>). In a certain concentration range of TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, a typical Michaelis–Menten curve was observed for the NiO catalyst (Fig. 7).

The as-obtained data of the kinetic study were fitted into the Michaelis–Menten model.

$$V_0 = \frac{V_{max} S_0}{S_0 + K_m} \tag{1}$$

All these kinetic parameters are listed in Table S1 (ESI†). These data are evaluated from the Lineweaver–Burk plot ($1/V_0$ vs. $1/S_0$).

$$\frac{1}{V_0} = \frac{1}{V_{max}} + \frac{K_m}{V_{max} S_0} \tag{2}$$

In both eqn (1) and (2), $V_0$ is the initial velocity, $S_0$ is the substrate concentration, $K_m$ is the Michaelis constant, and $V_{max}$ is the maximal reaction velocity.

After steady-state kinetic study, it was found that the oxidation process follows the Michaelis–Menten model with NiO and Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs as catalysts for both the substrates TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.

The Michaelis constant ($K_m$) assesses binding affinity between enzyme and substrates.<sup>41</sup> The $K_m$ value for NiO NFs is 0.018 mM, lower than the $K_m$ value for Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs, 0.023 mM, and much lower than the $K_m$ value for HRP enzyme towards TMB as substrate. The lower value of $K_m$ in the case of NiO NFs authenticates the better binding affinity to TMB than Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs and HRP enzyme.<sup>42</sup> Additionally, the $K_m$ values of NiO and Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> NFs with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as substrate are 1.77 and 1.76 mM, respectively, two times lower than that of HRP, 3.7 mM. A comparison of $K_m$ values of NiO and Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> nanostructures with other reported catalysts is shown in Table 1.

**Cysteine detection**

In the presence of NiO NFs, oxidation of common peroxidase substrate TMB by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was effectively and successively suppressed after introduction of a trace amount of L-cysteine (L-Cys) solution into the reaction mixture. Following this serendipitous discovery, a method for L-Cys sensing has been proposed. The proposition widens the boundary of NiO catalysis. In Fig. 8a, the typical plot of UV absorbance at λ = 653 nm versus time (min), shows that an increase in Cys concentration decreases the absorbance value. As a result, the absorbance versus Cys concentration plot in Fig. 8b gives a linear graph within the 20–100 μM range ($R^2$ = 0.9965) with a limit of detection of 1.1 μM. This estimated LOD value for cysteine is more significant in comparison with the reported procedure using various techniques (Table 2).

A similar process was followed for colorimetric assay of NAC. In this case the absorbance versus NAC concentration plot pursues linearity in the 5–60 μM range with a 1.004 μM limit of detection.

**Mechanism**

L-Cys is a semi-essential amino acid with redox active thiol side chain which is prone to oxidation to give disulfide linking in Cys. Cys has antioxidant properties, and has an important role in restricting the oxidation process of many biomolecules such as tripeptide glutathione. In this work, the presence of a trace amount Cys inhibited the usual H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-mediated oxidation of TMB by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The hindrance in blue color evolution occurs because of thermodynamics-driven preferential oxidation of Cys by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> leaving aside TMB. In such a competition Cys is oxidized by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to its disulfide form.<sup>49,50</sup> Here, oxidation of Cys by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is more feasible than oxidation of TMB, and this proclamation is explained by following $E^0$ values (Table 3). Thus, a favorable thermodynamic parameter forces H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to first oxidize Cys into its disulfide form and oxidation of TMB is inhibited accordingly. This inhibition process is Cys concentration-dependent, i.e. with higher Cys concentration, hindrance of oxidation happens more effectively. Depending on this concentration-dependent inhibiting property, simple sensing of Cys process is manifested.

To investigate the selectivity of this colorimetric assay, we studied the catalytic oxidation of TMB in the presence of other amino acids. Fig. 9 shows no color inhibition in the presence of other amino acids and this is because other amino acids do not have any interaction with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> like cysteine. Thus, the established sensing system has higher selectivity towards Cys than other amino acids as Cys is vulnerable to oxidation by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in the presence of NiO NFs. Notably, methionine (Met), a sulfur-containing amino acid, did not significantly inhibit oxidation of TMB. The thiol group in cysteine is more prone to oxidation by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> than thioether in methionine. Again, other amino acids having N-type ligand, such as histidine (His) and lysine (Lys), did not show any interaction with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Even at much higher concentrations of different amino acids, the absorbance remained the same, with color difference from Cys easily noted using the naked eye.

The selectivity and applicability of our sensor for NAC detection were investigated with co-existing compounds in two pharmaceutical samples. The result showed that responses for other additives were absent in colorimetric detection of NAC. After addition of a certain concentration of medicine solution in water, an obvious decrease in absorbance was observed compared with the blank test (without any biomolecules). The concentration of NAC in those medicines was calculated from a calibration plot of NAC (Fig. 8c). Results in Table 4 indicate the similarity of results from the biosensor and those claimed in medicines. For reproducibility, the sensing experiments were performed five times under similar conditions and relative standard deviations (RSDs) were calculated. This implies that the sensor is appropriate for reliable determination and analysis of NAC in pharmaceutical drug samples.

**Conclusion**

In conclusion, mesoporous NiO NFs, synthesized by a straightforward method, have been shown to exhibit peroxidase-like activity for the first time. Thus, the difficulty of horseradish peroxidase (HRP) enzyme manipulation is removed typically. Significant improvement of substrate binding with the catalyst, NiO NFs, is authenticated from the low $K_m$ value in comparison with HRP, while we considered a typical Michaelis–Menten model. Again, the NiO catalyzed oxidation of TMB by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was inhibited in the presence of cysteine and also N-acetyl cysteine (NAC). Thus, NiO becomes a biosensor, exhibiting low detection limit, excellent selectivity, and accurate detection of Cys and NAC. This method was successfully applied for NAC detection in pharmaceutical preparations. Overall, according to spectrophotometric measurements, NiO NFs demonstrate outstanding potential application for routine nonenzymatic cysteine sensing.
