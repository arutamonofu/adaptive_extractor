**Self assembly of organic-inorganic hybrid nanoflower as an efficient biomimetic catalyst for self-activated tandem reaction**

Yanyan Huang<sup>a,b</sup>, Xiang Ran<sup>a,b</sup>, Youhui Lin<sup>a,b</sup>, Jinsong Ren,*<sup>a</sup> and Xiaogang Qu*<sup>a</sup>

Received (in XXX, XXX) Xth XXXXXXXXX 20XX, Accepted Xth XXXXXXXXX 20XX
DOI: 10.1039/b000000x

BSA-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O hybrid nanoflower was used as one biomimetic catalyst with excellent catalytic activity, durability and stability. When GOx was served as the protein componment, this composite could implement self-activated cascade catalysis. Our nanoflower system could also be used to decompose organic pollutants with high efficiency.

Natural enzymes, with immense catalytic capability and high substrate specificity, have attracted lots of research interest for the exploration in medical, biological and food industry fields. Despite these extensive developments, natural enzymes often suffer from inherent shortcomings such as high cost for preparation and purification, low operational stability, sensitivity of catalytic activity to environmental conditions and difficulties of recovering.<sup>1</sup> These drawbacks all limit their practical applications. To address these problems, novel alternative candidates have been explored.<sup>2</sup> Numerous functional nanomaterials have been verified to possess unique enzyme mimic catalytic activities. For instance, some inorganic nanomaterials, such as gold nanoparticles, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, CeO<sub>2</sub> and grapheme oxide et al, have been discovered to possess intrinsic enzymatic activities.<sup>3</sup> In addition, many organic nanomaterials, such as cyclodextrins and polypyrrole further expand the group of biomimetic catalysts.<sup>4</sup> Although they have held great promise as enzyme mimics for the applications in environmental detection and biomedical fields, searching for innovative, highly-efficient and stable catalysts is still of great interest.

Due to the well-defined structures, hybrid composite materials have been widely explored to realize the combination of the characteristics of each component or to achieve cooperatively enhanced features.<sup>5</sup> A variety of hybrid complexes incorporated with different matrices have been found to possess promising features, such as novel functionalities, enhanced biocompatibility and improved mechanical and chemical properties.<sup>2b</sup> By adjusting the structure and interfacial interactions within the complexes, novel materials could be achieved. In this way, these novel nanocomposites have shown potential applications for various future technologies, such as protein separation and detection, heterogeneous catalysis and multimodal imaging.<sup>6</sup> Among them, organic-inorganic hybrid materials have attracted an explosion of interests in the applications of biological and electrochemical fields.<sup>7</sup> For example, Traversa’s group found that by hybridizing nanoceria with polymer scaffolds, the complex could promote the growth of stem cell with enhanced biological activity and better adhesion.<sup>7d</sup>

As an advanced nanotechnology, self-assembly can spontaneously integrate different components together to achieve desired hybrid materials.<sup>8</sup> Recently, Zare et al presented a method of protein-inorganic hybrid nanoflowers through self-assembly.<sup>9</sup> When laccase (a copper-containing oxidase) was used as the protein component, in comparison with free enzyme, the hybrid nanoflower exhibited enhanced laccase activity and stability. However, these studies only used the properties of natural enzymes while the potential applications of the inorganic framework have not been further explored yet. Previous studies have demonstrated that some copper compounds or iron compounds can act as Fenton-like reagent in acid environment which can catalyze the substrate to produce a colored product in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<sup>3f</sup>

Inspired by these unique features, herein, for the first time, we found that protein-inorganic hybrid materials with flower-like morphology possessed unique peroxidase-like activity (Scheme 1A and 1B). In our work, we used bovine serum albumin (BSA) as the model protein to form BSA-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower. The catalytic mechanism was likely followed Fenton’s reaction (Scheme 1B). The catalytic activity of this nanoflower was associated with Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O framework while protein was only used to induce the nucleation of the Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O crystals to form the flower morphology. It was worth mentioning that, compared with the natural horse radish peroxidase (HRP), our nanoflower had excellent thermal stability to implement high temperature catalytic reactions. More interestingly, the catalytic activity of nanoflower enhanced with the increasing of temperature. In addition, based on that, we replaced BSA with glucose oxidase (GOx) as the protein component to obtain GOx-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower. In this way, two different types of enzymes (artificial and natural enzyme) in a complex system were successfully integrated. Through the communication between artificial enzyme and natural enzyme, our system could achieve a two-step self-activated cascade reaction (Scheme 1C). This artificial enzymatic cascade system could mimic natural enzymatic cascade reactions, a common phenomenon in natural society but much more difficult to implement in artificial self-activated systems.<sup>10</sup> We believe that our system may promote the development of artificial enzymes and self-organized cascade reaction in an integrated system. Our system could also be used to decompose organic pollutants with high efficiency. Our system may have the potential applications for the treatment of waste water.

To validate our design, the BSA-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower was firstly prepared. After 3 days incubation, blue precipitates were formed with flower-like morphology and the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images were shown in Fig. S1. When the concentration of protein was 0.5 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>, the nanoflower had an average diameter of 5 μm with a narrow particle size distribution. After decreasing the protein concentration, the obtained nanoflower became larger with an average size of 10 μm. The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images further confirmed the nanoflower morphology with branch-like structure (Fig. S2). The energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) analysis of the nanoflower indicated the presence of Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub> and BSA components in the hybrid material (Fig. S3). Fig. S4 was the X-ray diffraction (XRD) pattern of the BSA-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower powder. The results demonstrated that the diffraction peaks of the nanoflower fitted those obtained from the JCPDS card (00-022-0548).

Having established the successful fabrication of the hybrid nanoflower, we next investigated the peroxidase-like activity of this novel nanoflower. The oxidation of peroxidase substrate 2,2-azinobis (3-ethylbenzothiozoline)-6-sulfonic acid (ABTS) was firstly evaluated (Fig. 1A). After incubating H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with the nanoflower and ABTS for 10 minutes at 37 °C, the color of the solution turned to green with a strong absorption at 417 nm. Additional control experiments revealed that the solution containing nanoflower or substrate alone could not induce any color change, indicating that our hybrid nanoflower possessed peroxidase-like catalytic activity (Fig. 1B). It was necessary to explore the possible catalytic mechanism of our catalyst. As shown in Fig. S5, the solution containing protein only did not have a color change while solutions with Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O crystal or protein-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower could induce a green color change. This result demonstrated that the peroxidase-like catalytic activity was originated from Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O framework while the protein was only used to induce the nucleation of the Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O crystals. It was also essential to rule out the possibility that the catalytic activity was due to the leaching of copper ions into acidic solution. To verify this, we incubated the nanoflower in acidic solution and then centrifuged to remove the precipitates. Fig. S6 demonstrated that the leaching solution had no catalytic activity, revealing that the observed peroxidase-like activity was not associated with an ion-leaching process but the intrinsic properties of the hybrid nanomaterials.

It is well known that the enzymatic activity is dependent on the substrate concentrations and reaction conditions. Similar to HRP, pH, temperature and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration could also influence the catalytic abilities of our catalyst. As shown in Fig. S7, the catalytic activity decreased gradually in the pH range from 4 to 8, demonstrating that optimal pH was at weakly acidic condition (pH 4.0). The effect of temperature on enzymatic activity was also investigated. In the range from 15 to 65 °C, this material showed a high-temperature resistance while HRP lost the catalytic activity under the same condition (Fig. S8). This result demonstrated that the nanoflower had good thermal stability and could achieve high-temperature catalytic reaction. More interestingly, with the increasing of temperature, the catalytic activity of nanoflower enhanced dramatically. Fig. 1C was the influence of substrate concentrations on the biocatalytic capability of nanoflower. The reaction rate enhanced with the increase of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration in a certain range. The liner calibration plot of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection was shown in Fig. S9 and the response was liner in the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration range 1-2000 μM with a detection limit of 0.45 μM. A time-dependent absorbance change under different catalyst concentrations was also observed. As shown in Fig. 1D, the intensity of absorption dramatically increased when increasing the catalyst concentration.

To further investigate the mechanism of the catalytic activity of the hybrid nanoflower, we determined apparent steady-state kinetic parameters for the reaction. Within the suitable range of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentrations, typical Michaelis-Menten curve was observed for our nanoflower (Fig. S10). Maximum initial velocity ($V_{max}$) and Michaelis-Menten constant ($K_m$) were obtained using Lineweaver-Burk plot (Table 1). The attractive catalytic activity of BSA-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O hybrid nanoflower made it plausible as a nanobiocatalyst. To verify this, their durability was further investigated. Fig. 1E was the plot of stability against the number of catalytic reactions. It was clear that the nanoflower only lost 20% of their catalyst activity over the course of seven rounds of reaction, demonstrating a high durability. It was worth noting that our catalyst remained the original flower-like structure after room-temperature reaction (Fig. S11A). More importantly, after high-temperature reaction, the nanoflower still maintained their structural integrity (Fig. S11B). Taken together, all the above results confirmed that the BSA-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower could be used as one biomimetic catalyst with excellent catalytic activity, thermal stability and durability.

Before verifying the feasibility of cascade reaction, we first replaced BSA with GOx to obtain GOx-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower. Fig. S12A was the image of assembles with an average size of 6 μm. Subsequently, the catalytic activity of GOx was evaluated. GOx can catalyze the oxidation of glucose to produce gluconic acid, with the concomitant formation of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Fig. 2A).<sup>11</sup> Therefore, we validated the reaction products and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was first interrogated via chromogenic reaction. In the presence of HRP and ABTS, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could oxidize ABTS to produce a colored product ABTS<sup>•+</sup> while no obvious color changes were observed in other conditions (Fig. 2B). Next, gluconic acid, the other product was interrogated with a specific colorimetric assay.<sup>12</sup> As Fig. 2C displayed, the color of the solution turned red after incubated with Fe<sup>3+</sup> and hydroxylamine. Control experiments indicated that the solution containing glucose or catalyst alone could not produce any color changes. With the increasing of glucose concentration, the reaction rate increased (Fig. 2D). As an organic acid, gluconic acid could decrease the ambient pH. To verify this hypothesis, we decreased the concentration of phosphate buffer and then used pH meter to monitor the final pH values of the solutions. As shown in Fig. S13, when glucose was incubated with GOx-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower in 0.1 mM pH 7.4 buffer, the pH of the solution dropped to around 4. This result conformed that gluconic acid generated from the redox reaction could decrease the ambient pH. Similar to BSA-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower, our GOx-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O hybrid nanoflower was remained the original structure after oxidation reaction (Fig. S12B). After 6 reaction cycles, the GOx-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower still maintained about 50% activities which indicated our nanoflower was recyclable and could be used for long-term operating (Fig. 2E).

The feasibility of cascade reaction was then verified. Since GOx would be denatured in pH 4.0 buffer solution,<sup>10a, 11</sup> while the optical activity of our nanoflower was at acidic pH (Fig. S7), therefore, it was incompatible to realize the glucose-based cascade reaction. One solution to this problem was to decrease the concentration of buffer. Our initial reaction system contained GOx-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflowers and glucose in 0.1 mM pH 7.4 phosphate buffer. In the presence of oxygen, GOx could catalize glucose to yield gluconic acid and hydrogen peroxide. As gluconic acid produced in the system could decrease the ambient pH, therefore, the acid environment could further activate the peroxidase-like catalytic activity of Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O component. Thus, the other product H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could be catalytically reduced by Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O component in the presence of ABTS at this acid condition (Fig. 3A). In this way, both of GOx and Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O could catalyze its corresponding reaction without losing their capability. Fig. 3B showed the pH changes for different conditions. When 30 μg mL<sup>−1</sup> GOx-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower and 4 mM glucose were incubated in 0.1 mM pH 7.4 phosphate buffer at 37 °C, the pH of solution decreased to around 4 while there were no pH changes in other conditions. As shown in Fig. 3C, after adding ABTS, an obvious green color product was obtained with a high absorbance at 417 nm in the solution which contained glucose and GOx-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower. Taken together, these results indicated that GOx-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O hybrid nanoflower could realize the self-activated cascade reaction in one integrated system.

To explore the potential of the hybrid nanoflower in real applications, herein, we applied our BSA-Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O nanoflower to catalyze the decomposition of organic pollutants. In our work, we used Rhodamine B (RhB) as a typical organic waste (Fig. S14). Under acidic conditions, the nanoflower could catalyze H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to generate hydroxyl radicals. When the nanoflower and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were incubated in the RhB solution for 4 hours, about 97% of the RhB could be removed (Fig. S14A). The relative absorbance spectra of the final solutions were shown in Fig. S14B. Neither nanoflower nor H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> alone could decompose the organic dye. Fig. S14C and S14D demonstrated that with the increasing of concentrations of catalyst or H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, the decomposition efficiency enhanced.

In summary, for the first time, we found the Cu<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O framework of protein-inorganic hybrid nanoflower possessed intrinsic peroxidase-like activity. As one hybrid material, compared with traditional organic or inorganic nanoenzymes, our organic-inorganic hybrid material possessed excellent durability, thermal and structural stability with the flexible control of the construction. With these advantages, this hybrid material could also achieve high-temperature reaction without destroying the original morphology. More interestingly, when increasing the temperature, the catalytic activity of nanoflower enhanced dramatically. By using their intrinsic peroxidase-like activity, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could be detected as low as 0.45 μM. Most importantly, when GOx was used as the protein component, this composite could realize the communication between artificial enzyme and natural enzyme and implement a self-activated cascade reaction. This artificial enzymatic cascade system could mimic natural enzymatic cascade reactions. Our studies may push forward the development of novel artificial enzymes and broad up the way to realize self-activated cascade reactions in one incorporated system. We hope our work will contribute to the further understanding the complicated enzymatic reactions in natural and biological systems. This nanoflower system could also be used to decompose organic dye with high efficiency. We prospect that our system may have the potential applications for the treatment of waste water.
