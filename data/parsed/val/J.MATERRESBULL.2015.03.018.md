### ABSTRACT

α-MnSe crystals as green and cost-efficient peroxidase mimics were successfully synthesized via a facial hydrothermal route in a glycerol/water solution system. They were first found to catalyze the reaction of peroxidase substrate 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to induce a blue color change, indicating an intrinsic peroxidase-like activity. The steady-state kinetic analysis showed that α-MnSe crystals had higher affinity for TMB and lower affinity for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> compared with horseradish peroxidase. The crystal structure, morphology, and catalytic activity could be tailored by adjusting the starting raw materials. The usage of sodium dodecyl sulfate and glycerol could decrease the particle size of α-MnSe crystals to enhance the peroxidase-like activity. Furthermore, benefiting from the chemical and crystal stability, α-MnSe crystals have two potential advantages: long-term stability and ease of recovery, and thus have potential applications as a peroxidase mimic in chemical/bio detection.

### 1. Introduction

Natural peroxidases are generally proteins with high substrate specificity and high efficiency under mild conditions [1,2]. They are significant in analytical diagnostics, such as H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, glucose, and melamine detection [3]. However, they possess some serious disadvantages [1], such as (a) lack of stability and loss of activation under harsh conditions and (b) time-consuming and expensive preparation and purification, which restrict their applications to some extent. As a result, low-cost and stable artificial peroxidase mimics are becoming a research interest. Since Gao et al. [4] reported intrinsic peroxidase-like activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles similar to that found in natural horseradish peroxidase, a variety of inorganic materials have been developed as peroxidase mimics, including transition metal oxides or chalcogenides (CeO<sub>2</sub> [5], Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> [6,7], MnO<sub>2</sub> [8], V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> [9], CuO [10], FeS [11], CuS [12,13], FeSe [14], etc.), noble metals (Au [15,16] and Pt [17]), and carbon-based nanomaterials (fullerene [18], carbon nanotubes [19], and graphene [20]). Previous reported peroxidase mimics are commonly nanoparticles, which can take advantage of large surface-to-volume ratios for effective interaction with substrates. However, they usually suffer from other two drawbacks [3]. One is the disposable way of usage, which brings economic and environmental concerns; the other is that the uncontrolled tendency of nanomaterial aggregation weakens the reproducibility and reliability of the analytical results. Thus, the development of peroxidase mimics with stable structure is highly desirable. Stable crystals may be good candidates.

α-MnSe has a stable rocksalt (cubic NaCl-type) crystal structure under ambient conditions and possesses unique magnetic properties as an antiferromagnetic semiconductor with a band gap of 2.65 eV [21]. In particular, α-MnSe possesses strong *sp-d* band electron exchange interactions between electron/hole band states and Mn<sup>2+</sup> 3*d* electron state for the introducing of Mn<sup>2+</sup> ions [22]. Considering the stable crystal structure and the specific electron transferability of α-MnSe, it is expected to be a good candidate for natural peroxidase mimics. However, the potential of α-MnSe as a peroxidase mimic has not been explored. In this work, we demonstrate for the first time that α-MnSe crystals exhibit peroxidase-like activity. Besides, benefiting from the specific stable crystal structure, α-MnSe crystals are easy to separate, reuse, and store, all of which highlight their potential use in bio/chemical detection.

### 2. Materials and methods

#### 2.1. Materials

A standard 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) liquid substrate system for peroxidases was purchased from Beijing Boisynthesis Biotechnology Co., Ltd. Other analytical reagent-grade chemicals were supplied by Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. Milli-Q water was used in the experiments (Millipore, USA).

#### 2.2. Synthesis of α-MnSe

α-MnSe crystals synthesized with different starting raw materials at 180 °C for different hydrothermal times and the corresponding abbreviation are tabulated in Table 1. In a typical synthesis of α-MnSe crystals (MnSe-4), 4 mmol of Mn(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>2</sub>·4H<sub>2</sub>O (Mn(Ac)<sub>2</sub>), 4 mmol of SeO<sub>2</sub>, 2 g of sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), 10 mL of glycerol, and 10 mL of N<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O (80%) (HZ) were added into 60 mL of water under vigorous stirring condition at room temperature for 10 min. The mixture was transferred into a teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave and heated at 180 °C for 36 h. The final sample was collected by filtration, washed with water and absolute ethanol, and then dried at 60 °C for 24 h in air.

#### 2.3. Characterization of α-MnSe

X-ray diffraction (XRD) measurement was performed on a Rigaku Ultima IV X-ray diffractometer using monochromatized Cu Kα radiation ($\lambda = 1.54056$ Å) with $2\theta$ ranging from 10° to 80° (40 kV, 40 mA) (Japan). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images were taken from a Hitachi S-4800 scanning electron microscopy (Japan). The samples were also subjected to energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) during the SEM measurement.

#### 2.4. Peroxidase-like activity of α-MnSe

The peroxidase-like activities of the synthesized α-MnSe crystals were evaluated using a standard TMB liquid substrate system for peroxidases. In a typical process, 10 mg of the synthesized sample was added into 1 mL water and then the mixture was ultrasonicated for 20 min to obtain a stable colloidal suspension. 200 μL of the mixture was added into 1 mL of TMB liquid substrate and after 2 min, the absorbance at 652 nm was recorded using a Hitachi U2900 UV–vis spectrometer (Japan).

The steady-state kinetic analysis experiments were carried out at room temperature using 0.40 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> MnSe-4 sample in phosphate buffer solution (50 mM, pH 5.0) by varying the concentration of TMB from 0 to 1 mM at a fixed concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or vice versa by varying the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> from 0 to 500 mM at a fixed concentration of TMB. All measurements were observed in time-scan mode at 652 nm using a Hitachi U2900 UV–vis spectrometer. The apparent kinetics parameters were determined by nonlinear fitting of the absorbance data to the Michaelis–Menten equation, $v = V_{max} [S] / (K_m + [S])$, where $v$ is the initial reaction rate, $V_{max}$ is the maximal reaction rate, $[S]$ is the substrate concentration, and $K_m$ is the Michaelis–Menten constant.

### 3. Results and discussion

#### 3.1. Characterization of α-MnSe

Fig. 1 shows XRD patterns of the synthesized α-MnSe samples with different starting raw materials at 180 °C for different hydrothermal times. In each case, except for the MnSe-5 sample (Fig. 1e), all peaks in the pattern could be indexed to the pure NaCl-structured α-MnSe phase and no other characteristic peaks corresponding to impurities were detected. The lattice constants were determined to be $a = b = c = 5.454$ Å, all of which are in good agreement with that of α-MnSe (JCPDS 11-0683). It could also be observed in Fig. 1 that the characteristic diffraction peak intensity varied with the starting raw materials. Fig. 1a shows that α-MnSe with high crystallinity could be synthesized in the absence of alcohol and SDS. In the pure water solution, the use of SDS slightly decreased the peak intensity of the MnSe-2 sample (Fig. 1b), while in the glycerol/water solution system (Fig. 1c), the peak intensity of the MnSe-3 sample decreased significantly, suggesting that glycerol can help decrease crystalline degree. Due to the synergistic effect of glycerol and SDS, in the glycerol/water solution with 2 g of SDS (Fig. 1d), the peak intensity of the MnSe-4 sample decreased compared with the MnSe-1 and MnSe-2 samples (Fig. 1a and b).

In order to further investigate the effect of the starting raw materials on the crystallinity of the resultant α-MnSe, the alcohol types and the SDS concentrations were varied (Fig. 1e–h). As shown in Fig. 1e, in the ethanol/water solution containing 2 g of SDS (MnSe-5), except for the characteristic diffraction peaks of α-MnSe, some other diffraction peaks appeared. We attempted, but failed to index these peak. This impurity may be a type of Mn complex produced under the given hydrothermal condition. When glycerol was replaced by methanol (MnSe-6), pure phase α-MnSe could be obtained (Fig. 1f). Moreover, the peak intensity of the MnSe-5 and MnSe-6 samples is stronger than that of the MnSe-4 sample, suggesting that glycerol is a more suitable assistant agent to obtain α-MnSe with low crystallinity compared with ethanol and methanol. In the glycerol/water solution with 0.5 g (MnSe-7) and 5 g of SDS (MnSe-8), pure phase α-MnSe could be obtained (Fig. 1g and h). Moreover, Fig. 1 shows that the peak intensity of the MnSe-7 sample (Fig. 1g) is stronger than that of the MnSe-4 and MnSe-8 samples (Fig. 1d and h), suggesting that the increasing of the SDS concentration can help reduce crystallinity degree of the synthesized α-MnSe crystals.

![Fig. 2. SEM images of the synthesized α-MnSe samples with different starting raw materials at 180 °C for 36 h (a: MnSe-1; b: MnSe-2; c: MnSe-3; d: MnSe-4; e: MnSe-5; f: MnSe-6; g: MnSe-7; h: MnSe-8).](figure2.png)

As mentioned above, in the alcohol/water solution with SDS, the synergistic effect of glycerol and high concentration SDS is the key to obtain α-MnSe with low crystallinity. SEM was used to further investigate the effect of the starting raw materials on the morphology of the resultant α-MnSe and the results are shown in Fig. 2. As shown in Fig. 2a, the MnSe-1 sample had polyhedral shapes and the average size was found to be approximately 12 μm. The average size of the MnSe-2 sample decreased to be approximately 8 μm with SDS. When glycerol was used, the average particle size further decreased (Fig. 2c). In the glycerol/water solution with 2 g of SDS, α-MnSe crystals with particle size distribution between 1 and 4 μm could be synthesized (Fig. 2d). As glycerol was replaced by ethanol (Fig. 2e), the MnSe-5 sample with polyhedral shape could be obtained and the morphology became similar to that of the MnSe-1 sample (Fig. 2a), while in the presence of methanol (Fig. 2f), the synthesized α-MnSe crystals (MnSe-6) had polyhedral shapes and the particle size distribution became narrower than that of the MnSe-5 sample (Fig. 2e). Moreover, the surface and the particle size of the MnSe-5 and MnSe-6 samples became smoother and larger than that of the MnSe-4 sample, respectively. These results indicate that the use of glycerol can help form rough surface and reduce the particle size. As shown in Fig. 2g, in the glycerol/water solution containing low concentration SDS (0.5 g), α-MnSe crystals (MnSe-7) with irregular shape and serious agglomeration can be obtained. In comparison, in the mixed solution containing high concentration SDS (5 g), α-MnSe crystals (MnSe-8) with special crooked belt shape could be obtained (Fig. 2h). These results suggest that the morphology of α-MnSe crystals can be easily tailored by adjusting the concentration of SDS and high concentration SDS facilitates the formation of special belt crystals. Moreover, the particle size of the MnSe-4 sample is also smaller than that of the MnSe-7 and MnSe-8 samples. The EDS results demonstrated that the molar ratios of Mn/Se of the synthesized α-MnSe crystals were close to 1. This ratio was consistent with the stoichiometric composition of α-MnSe. These results are in good agreement with the XRD results (Fig. 1).

To investigate the effect of the time evolution, we varied the hydrothermal time from 12 h to 48 h with glycerol and 2 g of SDS. Fig. 3 shows the XRD patterns of the resultant samples synthesized at different times. As shown in Fig. 3a, after 12 h reaction, the as-prepared MnSe-9 sample was composed of two phases: MnSe<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS 20-0722) and α-MnSe. Fig. 3b–d shows that α-MnSe became the main product with the elongation of the reaction time. After 36 h reaction (MnSe-4), pure phase α-MnSe could be produced and no reflection peaks of MnSe<sub>2</sub> could be observed (Fig. 3c). These results show that MnSe<sub>2</sub> will be converted to α-MnSe under given reaction temperature and time, which can be attributed that NaCl-structured α-MnSe is more stable than MnSe<sub>2</sub> with pyrite structure [23]. According to the literature [23], SeO<sub>2</sub> could be deoxidized by hydrazine to Se quickly upon heating, which had high reactivity and was easy to be disproportionated into Se<sup>2−</sup> ions under alkaline conditions (Eqs. (1) and (2)). Free Mn<sup>2+</sup> ions could react with Se<sup>2−</sup> ions to form MnSe (Eq. (3)). Glycerol can act as a complexing reagent to react with free Mn<sup>2+</sup> ions to form a complex, which can affect the crystal growth process and decrease the particle size.

$$SeO_2 + N_2H_4 \rightarrow Se \downarrow + N_2 \uparrow + 2H_2O \tag{1}$$
$$3Se + 6OH^- \rightarrow 2Se^{2-} + SeO_3^{2-} + 3H_2O \tag{2}$$
$$Mn^{2+} + Se^{2-} \rightarrow MnSe \downarrow \tag{3}$$

#### 3.2. Peroxidase-like activity of α-MnSe

TMB is a typical peroxidase substrate because it is colorless and is oxidized by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> very slowly. Natural peroxidase can catalyze the oxidation of TMB substrate in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> by one electron transfer and the consequent color change of the TMB oxidation product can be monitored from absorbance augmentation of the TMB oxidation product at 652 nm [1,2]. Fig. 4 shows that the synthesized α-MnSe crystals could catalyze the oxidation of TMB substrate with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and produced a nice blue color (the inset of Fig. 4). Fig. 4 also shows that the peroxidase-like activities of the resultant α-MnSe crystals varied with the starting raw materials. The MnSe-4 sample exhibited the highest catalytic activity, which can be attributed to its smallest particle size and special rough crystal morphology. These advantages may have more reaction active sizes, such that H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB can be easily adsorbed onto the sample surface to benefit the reaction.

The peroxidase-like activity of the MnSe-4 sample was further investigated by steady-state kinetic experiments and the analysis results are shown in Fig. 5. As shown, by plotting the initial reaction rate against concentration, the typical Michaelis–Menten curves for TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could be obtained in Fig. 5A and B. $K_m$ was obtained by using Lineweaver–Burk plots (Fig. 5C and D). The $K_m$ value for α-MnSe crystals with TMB as the substrate was 0.0348 mM, which was much lower than the reported value of horseradish peroxidase (HRP, 0.434 mM) [4], suggesting that α-MnSe crystals had higher affinity for TMB than HRP. On the other hand, the $K_m$ value for α-MnSe crystals with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrate was 87 mM, significantly higher than that of 3.7 mM for HRP [4], suggesting that α-MnSe crystals had lower affinity for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> than HRP.

As an inorganic enzyme mimic, α-MnSe may exhibit high chemical and thermal stability. To investigate this advantage, recycling experiments were performed, and peroxidase-like activity was monitored on the basis of its absorbance at 652 nm in each cycle. The cycle performance of the MnSe-4 sample is shown in Fig. 6. The results reveal no clear loss of activity was observed, even after 10 cycles, suggesting that as a crystal-based peroxidase mimic, α-MnSe crystals have two potential advantages, including (1) stable activity and (2) ease of recovery and reuse. After 10 cycles, the sample was reclaimed and analyzed by XRD and SEM. The results are shown in Figs. 7 and 8, respectively. As shown in Fig. 7, all characteristic diffraction peaks could be indexed to the α-MnSe phase. The SEM image reveals that the morphology of the reclaimed sample was similar with that of the original sample before 10 cycles (Fig. 8). These results provide strong evidence that α-MnSe crystals possess excellent robustness, stable activity, and good reusability.

### 4. Conclusions

In summary, α-MnSe crystals have been prepared through a facile hydrothermal process in a glycerol/water solution system using SeO<sub>2</sub> as selenium source and hydrous hydrazine as reducing agent. Their peroxidase-like activity was first demonstrated. The particle sizes and morphologies of the resulting samples could be tailored by adjusting the starting raw materials. SDS and glycerol can help decrease the particle size of α-MnSe crystals to enhance the peroxidase-like activity. The steady-state kinetic analysis shows α-MnSe crystals had higher affinity for TMB and lower affinity for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> compared with HRP. Moreover, α-MnSe peroxidase mimic had long-term stability (no activity loss after 10 cycles) and ease of recovery and reuse. Because this route is easily accessible, α-MnSe peroxidase mimic has great potential for practice use in chemical/bio detection.
