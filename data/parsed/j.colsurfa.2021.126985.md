### ABSTRACT

In this work, the Pt nanoclusters with an average size of $1.7 \pm 0.3$ nm were prepared by a facile and green method using polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) as both stabilizer and reductant. The oxidase-like activity of PVP-stabilized Pt nanoclusters (PVP-Pt NCs) was investigated by the catalytic oxidation of o-phenylenediamine (OPD). The mechanism studies indicated that PVP-Pt NCs performed as oxidase enzyme mimics by catalyzing the dissolved oxygen to generate the reactive oxygen species (singlet oxygen and superoxide anion) intermediates. Ascorbic acid (AA) could also be oxidized to dehydroascorbic acid (DHA) which could react with OPD to generate a fluorescent compound. On the basis of the robust oxidase-like activity of PVP-Pt NCs, both colorimetric assay and fluorescence method for AA determination were established. Compared with colorimetric assay, the fluorescence method showed higher sensitivity and selectivity for AA detection. The proposed fluorescence method with the LOD of 1.17 μM could overcome the interference of most reducing substances and showed promising applications in biosensor.

### 1. Introduction

Ascorbic acid (AA), known as vitamin C, is widely found in fruits and vegetables. AA plays an important role in human metabolism processes such as the metabolism of amino acids, the utilization of metal ions, the growth of bones and teeth, the formation of antibodies, and so forth [1, 2]. The deficiency of AA is closely linked to many diseases like scurvy, Alzheimer’s disease, cancer, cardiovascular disease and Parkinson’s disease [3–6]. Unfortunately, humans can not produce AA themselves because of the absence of gulonolactone oxidase. As a water-soluble vitamin, AA flows away through metabolism every day. Therefore, the AA should be supplemented in daily diet [7]. Owing to its biosafety and antioxidant properties, AA is widely used as an additive in beverages, food and medicines. Considering the crucial roles of AA in human health, food and drug safety, it is significant to develop a fast, convenient and selective method for the determination of AA.

Traditional methods for AA determination including electrochemistry [8], chemiluminescence [9], spectrophotometry [10] and high performance liquid chromatography [11], usually suffer from some drawbacks such as bulky and expensive instruments, complicated operation, time consuming, or insufficient selectivity or sensitivity. Fluorescence method has drawn considerable interest due to its simplicity and high sensitivity. A large number of fluorescent nanoprobes such as metal nanoclusters [12], carbon nanomaterials [13] and semiconductor quantum dots [14], have been synthesized and used to determine AA in recent years. Most of these fluorescence methods are at the foundation of the charge transfer between nanoprobes and AA which could change the fluorescence intensity of nanoprobes. Hu and coworkers [15] synthesized high fluorescent carbon dots which were employed for AA determination. The Cr<sup>6+</sup> ions could quench the fluorescence property of carbon dots. Once introducing AA which could reduce Cr<sup>6+</sup> to Cr<sup>3+</sup>, the fluorescence property of carbon dots was recovered.

Because of the low-cost, rapidity and easy detection by naked eyes, colorimetric assay has attracted intense attention on biosensing, environmental monitoring and food safety [16]. A variety of nanomaterials such as metal, metal oxide, and carbon materials, with intrinsic peroxidase-like or oxidase-like property have been used for colorimetric detection of AA [17–21]. Pt nanomaterials, which possessed multi-enzyme mimics including oxidase, peroxidase, catalase and superoxide dismutase, have been widely explored and applied in biosensing, environmental monitoring [22,23]. The monodisperse Pt nanoclusters with extremely high enzymatic activities were more attractive as biosensors in recent years. However, some expensive template molecules such as polyamidoamine dendrimer, protein, heparin and yeast extract, were needed to inhibit the growth of nucleation and prevent the aggregation of Pt nanoclusters in the synthesis process [24–27]. Hence, it’s important to develop a low-cost and simple method for preparation of monodisperse Pt nanoclusters. Polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP), a commercial nonionic surfactant with high water solubility and high biosafety, is an excellent stabilizer in noble metal nanomaterials. PVP could protect Pt nanocrystals from aggregation due to the steric hindrance effect of PVP molecules and the Pt-N bond interaction between PVP and Pt nanocrystals [28]. It’s reported that PVP stabilized Pd nanoclusters, Ir nanoclusters and supported Pt nanoclusters have been successfully synthesized through the alcoholic reduction method [29–32]. Herein, we reported a green method for preparation of Pt nanoclusters by employing PVP as both reductant and stabilizer. The prepared Pt nanoclusters exhibited high oxidase-like activities, which could be used for colorimetric detection of AA.

The mentioned fluorescence methods and colorimetric assays for determination of AA mostly utilized the reducibility of AA to change the fluorescence properties of fluorescent nanoprobes or inhibited the color change of chromogenic substrates, respectively. Some interferential substances with reducibility like glutathione (GSH), cysteine, dopamine (DA) and sodium sulfite, could significantly affect the detecting results of AA [33]. Therefore, it’s essential to improve the selectivity of fluorescence methods and colorimetric assays for AA detection to meet the demand of practical applications.

In this work, a high selective method for AA detection was established by combining the colorimetric signal and the fluorescence property of reaction solution based on the oxidase-like activity of Pt nanoclusters. The monodisperse Pt nanoclusters were synthesized in one-pot reaction by using PVP as both stabilizer and reductant. The oxidase-like activity of PVP-stabilized Pt nanoclusters (PVP-Pt NCs) was investigated by catalyzing the oxidation of OPD. The AA could also be oxidized to dehydroascorbic acid (DHA) by the robust oxidase-like activity of Pt nanoclusters. Both the colorimetric assay and the fluorescence method for AA detection were developed on the basis of the oxidase-like activity of PVP-Pt NCs. The fluorescence method based on the specific reaction between DHA and OPD displayed enhanced selectivity for AA detection, which could exclude the interference of reducing substances.

### 2. Experimental

#### 2.1. Materials

Chloroplatinic acid hexahydrate (H<sub>2</sub>PtCl<sub>6</sub>⋅6H<sub>2</sub>O), Polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP, M<sub>w</sub> = 30,000), ascorbic acid and glutathione (GSH) were purchased from Meryer Technologies (Shanghai, China). Na<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>HPO<sub>4</sub>, NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> and H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> were obtained from Yongda Chemical Reagent (Tianjin, China). Bovine serum albumin (BSA) was supplied by Guangzhou Zhanchen Biotechnology (Guangzhou, China). Glucose, lactose, terephthalic acid (TA) and o-phenylenediamine (OPD) were bought from Macklin Reagent (Shanghai, China). KCl, NaCl, CaCl<sub>2</sub>, sodium acetate trihydtrate and urea were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent (Shanghai, China). Threonine, glycine and serine were obtained from Sigma-Aldrich (Shanghai, China). Carotene and superoxide dismutase (SOD) were bought from Yuanye Biotechnology (Shanghai, China). Lemon soda beverage and vitamin C effervescent tablets were bought from local supermarket and drugstore, respectively. Ultrapure water with a resistivity of 18.25 MΩ cm was used throughout the experiments.

#### 2.2. Synthesis of PVP-Pt NCs

The PVP stabilized Pt nanoclusters (PVP-Pt NCs) were synthesized by reduction of H<sub>2</sub>PtCl<sub>6</sub>⋅6H<sub>2</sub>O in pure water using PVP as both reductant and stabilizer. In a typical synthesis procedure, the sodium acetate trihydtrate was added into 40 mL of H<sub>2</sub>PtCl<sub>6</sub>⋅6H<sub>2</sub>O solution (62 mg, 0.12 mmol) to adjust the pH value of 7.0. Then, 20 mL PVP solution (M<sub>w</sub> = 30,000, 2.7 g) was added dropwise under vigorous stirring. After stirring for 2 h, the obtained light yellow solution was immersed in oil bath and heated to reflux for 4 h at a temperature of 110 °C. The obtained black colloid was centrifugalized by 15 mL ultracentrifugal filter devices (Millipore Amicon Ultra-15, 50 kDa) to remove the residual PVP and washed by ultrapure water for several times. The concentration of PVP-Pt NCs, which was verified by the inductive coupled plasma optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES), was adjusted to 1 mg/mL (counted as Pt content) for further use.

#### 2.3. Oxidase-like activity of PVP-Pt NCs

OPD substrate, which could be used for both colorimetric assay and fluorescent detection due to the color and the fluorescent property of OPD<sub>ox</sub>, was selected to evaluate the oxidase-like activity of PVP-Pt NCs [34]. Briefly, 5 μL PVP-Pt NCs dispersion and 10 μL OPD solution (100 mM) were added into 485 μL sodium phosphate buffer solution (pH = 4.0, 0.1 M) at the same time. The absorbance spectra were recorded by a UV–vis spectroscopy. The effects of pH value of buffer solution (2.0–8.0), temperature (0–90 °C), concentration of PVP-Pt NCs (1–30 μg/mL) and concentration of OPD (0.1–5 mM) to the oxidase-like activity of PVP-Pt NCs were carried out to optimize the reaction conditions. The steady-state kinetic experiments were executed by varying the concentration of OPD. The kinetic parameters were calculated by fitting equation of Lineweaver-Burk plot which was derived from Michaelis-Menten equation [35].

#### 2.4. Detection of AA

A colorimetric assay for AA developed based on the inhibition of AA to the oxidation of OPD. Experimentally, 10 μL of OPD solution (100 mM), 10 μL of AA solution with different concentration and 10 μL of PVP-Pt NCs dispersion were mixed into sodium phosphate buffer solution (0.1 M, pH = 4.0) with a final volume of 500 μL. The absorbance intensity at 448 nm of reaction solution was monitored after reaction for 10 min at 75 °C.

The PVP-Pt NCs could catalyze the oxidation of AA due to their oxidase mimetic property. Meanwhile, the produced DHA could react with OPD to produce a fluorescent production, based on which a fluorescence method for selective detection of AA was established. Typically, 50 μL of OPD solution (120 mM) and 50 μL of AA solution with different concentration were mixed into sodium phosphate buffer solution (2.80 mL, 0.1 M, pH = 7.0). Then, 100 μL of PVP-Pt NCs dispersion was added to catalyze the reaction. The fluorescence intensities at 434 nm and 564 nm were monitored by an excited wavelength of 370 nm when the reaction solution changed from colorless to yellow.

The selectivity of the proposed methods for AA detection was carried out in the presence of interferential substances like carbohydrates, metal ions, and amino acids. The content of AA in lemon soda beverage, Vitamin C effervescent tablets and simulated serum was diluted and analyzed to evaluate the practical application.

### 3. Results and discussion

The mechanism of the mimetic oxidase property of PVP-Pt NCs and the determination of AA were well illustrated in Scheme 1. The as-prepared PVP-Pt NCs possessed intrinsic oxidase-like property by catalyzing dissolved oxygen to reactive oxygen species (ROS, singlet oxygen and superoxide anion) which could oxide the OPD to OPD<sub>ox</sub> (Fig. S1a). The inhibition of the catalytic oxidation of OPD by AA was employed to establish the colorimetric assay for AA determination. AA could also be oxidized to DHA by ROS intermediates (Fig. S1b), and the DHA could react with OPD to form a quinoxaline derivative (QD) (Fig. S1c) with a fluorescence emission peak at 434 nm. The fluorescence method for AA detection was carried out when the solution changed to yellow color which was the signal of the reaction terminal. Comparing with colorimetric assay, the fluorescence method exhibited enhanced sensitivity and selectivity.

#### 3.1. Characterization of PVP-Pt NCs

The PVP-Pt NCs were synthesized by one-pot reaction in aqueous solution utilizing the PVP as both reductant and stabilizer (Fig. 1a), which was a simple, green and economic synthetic procedure. The usage of NaBH<sub>4</sub>, a common reducing agent which is harmful to organisms and the environment, was avoided. Besides, compared with polyamidoamine dendrimers, heparin and mercaptans, PVP as stabilizer was cheaper and more eco-friendly. The color of reaction solution gradually changed from light yellow to black after 30 min reflux, indicating the formation of PVP-Pt NCs (Fig. S2). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images displayed that PVP-Pt NCs were monodisperse and mostly quasi-spherical (Fig. 1b and Fig. S3). No agglomeration was observed, which could be ascribed to the steric hindrance effect of the PVP stabilizer on the surface of Pt nanoclusters. The average size of PVP-Pt NCs was approximate 1.7 nm by statistically analyzing more than 200 nanoparticles from TEM images (Fig. S4). The high resolution TEM (HRTEM) image inset into Fig. 1b displayed that the crystal lattice space of PVP-Pt NCs was 0.224 nm, corresponding well to the (111) facet of metallic Pt. The X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed to investigate the chemical states of as-prepared PVP-Pt NCs. In the survey spectrum, the strong peaks of Pt 4f, C 1s, N 1s and O 1s were clearly observed at 74, 285, 399 and 531 eV, respectively (Fig. S5). The C, O and N elements were mainly from PVP molecules. The high resolution XPS spectra of Pt 4f were presented in Fig. 1c. The peaks of binding energies at 70.7 and 74.1 eV corresponded to the 4f<sub>7/2</sub> and 4f<sub>5/2</sub> of metallic species of Pt(0), respectively. The peaks at 71.7 and 76.1 eV could be ascribed to the oxidized species of Pt<sup>2+</sup> [36]. No Pt<sup>4+</sup> species were observed in PVP-Pt NCs, indicating the complete reduction of H<sub>2</sub>PtCl<sub>6</sub>. In the sedimentation experiment (Fig. S6), the PVP-Pt NCs dispersion was still uniform and there was no apparent precipitate after six months of placement, which suggested the good water soluble of as-prepared PVP-Pt NCs.

#### 3.2. Oxidase-like activities of PVP-Pt NCs

The oxidase-like activities of PVP-Pt NCs were explored by catalytic oxidation of OPD in sodium phosphate buffer solution. As a typical chromogenic substrate, the colorless OPD could be oxidized to bright yellow 2, 3-diaminophenazine (OPD<sub>ox</sub>), and the reaction equation was present in Fig. S1a. As shown in Fig. 2, the colorless OPD solution gradually changed to yellow color after adding PVP-Pt NCs. And the absorption spectrum of reaction solution showed a maximum absorbance intensity at 448 nm, implying the production of OPD<sub>ox</sub> and the high oxidase mimetic property of PVP-Pt NCs. Neither color change, nor absorption peak at 448 nm was observed in the absence of PVP-Pt NCs, demonstrating that the PVP-Pt NCs were accountable for the catalytic oxidation reaction of OPD. After catalytic reaction, PVP-Pt NCs was centrifugalized and reused by a 4 mL ultracentrifugal filter device (Millipore Amicon Ultra-4, 10 kDa). The recycled PVP-Pt NCs remained high activity after five cycles reaction (Fig. S7) and the TEM image showed no aggregation of the recycled PVP-Pt NCs (Fig. S8), indicating the good stability and reusability of PVP-Pt NCs.

To elucidate the mechanism of the oxidase-like property of PVP-Pt NCs, the effect of dissolved oxygen was firstly carried out. Compared with the control experiment (in air), the color of the OPD and PVP-Pt NCs in the N<sub>2</sub>-saturated solution was much lighter (Fig. 3a). #### 3.3. Colorimetric detection of AA

The oxidation of OPD could be inhibited in the presence of AA due to its strong reducibility, which could be used to develop a colorimetric assay for AA detection. The colorimetric assay for AA was carried out at the optimal reaction condition of oxidase-like property of PVP-Pt NCs with varied concentration of AA. The absorption spectra of reaction solutions were also presented at Fig. S12b. The ΔA and concentration of AA exhibited a good linear relationship with the correlation coefficient (R<sup>2</sup>) of 0.9976 at the concentration of AA ranging from 100 to 500 μM (Fig. 4a). The limit of detection (LOD) was calculated to be 18.9 μM with equation of LOD = 3.3δ/S (S and δ stand for the slope and the standard error of the intercept of the linear fitting equation, respectively.). The selectivity of colorimetric assay for AA detection was investigated by adding different interferential substances including amino acids, metal ions and carbohydrates. The results were shown in Fig. S13. The color of reaction solution with AA concentration of 500 μM was light yellow. While, most of other solution with interferential substances displayed little change compared to control experiment which presented bright yellow color. Exceptionally, the reaction solution with cysteine or GSH also displayed light yellow color, indicating the great influence of cysteine or GSH to the accuracy of AA detection. The rational reason of the influence of cysteine or GSH could be attributed to their reductive property which inhibited the catalytic oxidation of OPD similar to AA. The colorimetric assay for AA detection has the advantages of simplicity and direct eye observation, but poor selectivity to reducing substances.

#### 3.4. Fluorescence detection of AA

To solve the problem of low selectivity of the colorimetric assay for AA detection, a fluorescence method based on the reaction between DAH and OPD was developed. The pH value of reaction solution was an important factor on the catalytic oxidation of AA by PVP-Pt NCs. The maximum absorption wavelength and molar absorption coefficient of AA vary with pH value (Fig. S14a). The average catalytic oxidation reaction rate of AA per milligram of PVP-Pt NCs was calculated by Beer-Lambert Law, using 0.1 mM AA in sodium phosphate buffer with different pH value as reference solution. As presented in Fig. S14b, the oxidation rate of AA increased with the increasing of pH value, and displayed highest reaction rate at pH value range of 4.5–5.0. The reaction between 0.1 mM of DHA and 2 mM of OPD was carried out to valuate the influence of pH value. The relative activity of reaction improved with the increase of pH value, and the reaction maintained high activity in the pH range of 6–8 (Fig. S15). The reaction extent between DHA and OPD determined the fluorescence intensity of reaction solution and the AA was unstable under alkaline condition. Therefore, the pH value of 7.0 was chosen in the following fluorescence experiments. The influence of reaction time was performed with 0.1 mM of AA, 2 mM of OPD and 10 μL of PVP-Pt NCs at a pH value of 7, and the results were shown at Fig. S16. The fluorescence intensity at 434 nm increased with time, but gradually decreased after 20 min. At the beginning, AA was catalytically oxidized to DHA and the produced DHA reacted with OPD to generated a quinoxaline derivative (QD) with a fluorescence emission peak at 434 nm at the same time. The reaction equations were presented at Fig. S1. When AA was completely consumed, the PVP-Pt NCs began to catalyze the oxidation of OPD and the reaction solution gradually changed to yellow color. The produced OPD<sub>ox</sub> could partially absorb the fluorescent light generated by QD, which led to the decrease of fluorescence intensity at 434 nm. Besides, another fluorescent peak appeared at 564 nm, which was ascribed to the OPD<sub>ox</sub>. Interestingly, the sum of the fluorescence intensities (FLs) at 434 nm and 564 nm almost remained constant (Fig. S17) after 20 min. While, the oxidase-like activity of PVP-Pt NCs was low at pH value of 7, which left enough time for the reaction between DHA and OPD. Therefore the color change could be considered as a signal of the complete reaction between DHA and OPD.

On the foundation of the color signal and the fluorescence property of QD and OPD<sub>ox</sub>, a fluorescence method for AA detection was established. As presented in Fig. 5a, the FLs intensity and the concentration of AA displayed a good linear correlation with the AA concentration range of 2–50 μM with R<sup>2</sup> of 0.99551. And the fluorescence spectra of reaction solutions were shown in Fig. S18. The LOD for AA was estimated to be 1.17 μM with equation of LOD = 3.3δ/S, which exhibited good performance for AA detection comparing with many previous reports (Table S2). The selectivity of the proposed detection method was also explored by adding some potential interfering substances such as metal ions, amino acids and carbohydrates. As shown in Fig. 5b, the relative fluorescence intensity had no significant change comparing to control experiment, when the same concentration of interferential substance and AA were added together. Without AA, the reaction solution only showed a weak fluorescence peak at 564 nm which was ascribed to OPD<sub>ox</sub>, and there was no fluorescence peak at 434 nm. Comparing with colorimetric assay, the proposed fluorescence method for AA detection exhibited higher selectivity which could excluded the interference of reducing substances like GSH and cysteine.

#### 3.5. Detection AA in real samples

To further evaluate the feasibility of the proposed detection methods in practical applications, both fluorescence method and colorimetric method for AA detection in real samples were performed. Considering the cysteine concentration in healthy people’s serum or plasma is 200–300 μM, the 5 wt% BSA solution containing 300 μM cysteine was used to imitate serum sample. Vitamin C effervescent tablets and lemon soda beverage were diluted to the linear range of proposed detection methods and analyzed respectively. The colorimetric detection results were listed in Table S3. The results of vitamin C effervescent tablet and lemon soda samples were consistent well with the specifications with the recoveries of 102.80–104.21%. However, the detection results in simulated serum (BSA sample) were much higher than adding content, which could be put down to the strong influence of cysteine. The results of fluorescence method for AA detection were displayed in Table 1. The AA contents in simulated serum, vitamin C tablets and lemon soda beverage acquired by our experiments were agreement with the specifications. In addition, the recoveries reached to 97.39–101.37% with the relative standard deviation (RSD) of 1.12–3.44%. Taken together, the fluorescence method with good selectivity and sensitivity was feasible and dependable for AA detection in some real samples.

### 4. Conclusion

The PVP-Pt NCs with high oxidase-like activity were synthesized by a one-pot and green process utilizing the PVP as both reductant and stabilizer. Mechanistic studies suggested that singlet oxygen and superoxide anion radicals generated by catalyzing the dissolved oxygen were responsible for the oxidase-like activity of PVP-Pt NCs. The colorimetric assay for AA was established and used in some real samples but had poor selectivity to reducing substances. To improve the selectivity, a fluorescence method for AA detection based on the color signal of OPD<sub>ox</sub> and the fluorescence property of QD was developed, which was free of the effect of reducing substances. The successful application on AA detection in real samples of the proposed fluorescence method demonstrated the potential application in pharmacy, food safety and biosensor.
