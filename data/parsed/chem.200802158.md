# Nanostructured FeS as a Mimic Peroxidase for Biocatalysis and Biosensing

Zhihui Dai,<sup>[a]</sup> Shaohua Liu,<sup>[a]</sup> Jianchun Bao,*<sup>[a]</sup> and Huangxian Ju*<sup>[b]</sup>

**Abstract:** Artificial enzyme mimics have attracted considerable interest due to easy denaturation and leakage of enzymes during their storage and immobilization procedure. Herein we describe the design of a novel mimic peroxidase, a nanostructure of sheet-like FeS prepared by a simple micelle-assisted synthetic method. Such a nanostructure has a large specific surface area and high peroxidase-like activity, and was thus further used as a mimic enzyme for the development of biocatalysts and amperometric biosensors. The sheet-like FeS nanostructure showed typical Michaelis–Menten kinetics and good affinity to both H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and 3,3’,5,5’-tetramethyl benzidine. At pH 7.0 the constructed amperometric sensor showed a linear range for the detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> from 0.5 to 150 μM with a correlation coefficient of 0.9998 without any electron transfer mediator. The H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensor based on the sheet-like FeS showed more sensitive response than those based on spherical FeS nanostructure, and resulted in a better stability than horseradish peroxidase when they were exposed to solutions with different pH values and temperatures. These excellent properties made the sheet-like nanostructured FeS powerful tools for a wide range of potential applications as an “artificial peroxidase” as biosensors and biotechnology.

**Keywords:** amperometric biosensors · biosensors · enzyme catalysis · FeS nanostructure · peroxide mimic

---

### Introduction

Nanostructured materials have attracted considerable interest for biomedical and biosensing applications due to their unique optical, electric, chemical and mechanical properties.<sup>[1]</sup> In bioassay applications these nanomaterials are mainly used as supports for immobilization of biomolecules such as enzymes, antibodies and DNA. They are generally considered to be biologically and chemically inert,<sup>[2]</sup> though the catalytic behavior of some carbon-based nanomaterials and metal nanoparticles have been used to accelerate the electron transfer for construction of amperometric biosensors.<sup>[3]</sup> These resulting enzyme-based devices usually show high sensitivity due to the high loading of enzymes on the nanoparticles.<sup>[4, 5]</sup> However, their stability is limited due to easy denaturation<sup>[6]</sup> and leakage of enzymes during their storage and immobilization procedure. Furthermore, the preparation and purification of enzymes are usually time consuming and expensive.<sup>[7]</sup> Therefore, the syntheses of artificial peroxidases with highly catalytic properties and a wide range of practical applications are becoming a significant field for different purposes.

Many peroxidase mimics including haematin,<sup>[8]</sup> porphyrin<sup>[9]</sup> and cyclodextrin<sup>[10]</sup> have been applied in different fields. Some iron-based compounds such as Prussian blue<sup>[11]</sup> have been used as electrocatalysts in H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> reduction due to the expected conversion of Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup>, which occurs in the reaction centers of a number of peroxidase enzymes.<sup>[12]</sup> Although Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles have been used for cathodic determination of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[13]</sup> their peroxidase activity is always ignored until a recent report, in which the intrinsic peroxidase-like activity of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles was observed to catalyze the breakdown of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<sup>[2]</sup> Afterwards, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles have been presented as a mimic peroxidase for application in H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and glucose detection.<sup>[14]</sup> In order to obtain more support for potential use as nanomaterials, particularly Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup>-contained nanostructures, as artificial peroxidases to prepare biosensors and biocatalysts, this work describes the design of a novel nanostructure of sheet-like FeS by a simple micelle-assisted synthetic method under N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere. This nanostructured FeS could provide the enzymatic active center of Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> for electron transfer.

FeS possesses a specific electron-transfer ability<sup>[15]</sup> and good adsorption, and more importantly, has a lower band gap than FeO,<sup>[16]</sup> which favors electron transfer. Thus, the FeS nanostructure has the potential for novel applications in biocatalysis and biosensing. The synthesized sheet-like FeS nanostructures showed attractive performance of intrinsic peroxidase-like activity, which was confirmed by the ability to catalyze the oxidation of organic substrates to produce a color change. Also, this H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensor had more sensitive response than those based on spherical FeS nanoparticles. In comparison with HRP, the mimic peroxidase showed better stability in exposure to extreme pH values and high temperature. The good stability, ease of production and special properties of the sheet-like FeS implied that this material has a wide range of potential applications in biosensor, biotechnology and environmental chemistry.

### Results and Discussion

**Formation and characterization of the nanostructured FeS:** Figure 1 shows the X-ray diffraction (XRD) pattern of the as-prepared sample. The diffraction peaks in the range of $15 < 2\theta < 80^{\circ}$ can be indexed as hexagonal structure FeS (100), (101), (102), (110), (112), (200), (203), (211) and (315) (ASTM standard 80-1027). The sharp peaks indicate that the product is well crystallized. In addition, some diffraction peaks of the Fe<sub>0.975</sub>S (ASTM standard 80-1033), marked with asterisks, are also observed, which indicate a little Fe<sub>0.975</sub>S existed besides FeS.<sup>[17]</sup> The EDS analysis of the sample displays that the atom ratio of Fe to S is about 0.99:1; this suggests that FeS is formed.

![Figure 1. XRD pattern of the as-prepared FeS nanostructure. The diffraction peaks marked with asterisks result from Fe<sub>0.975</sub>S.](figure1.png)

The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image of the as-prepared FeS nanostructure is shown in Figure 2A. It can be seen that the particles are of the sheet shape. There exist some regions with color difference between the edge and the middle of the sheets due to the wrap and crinkle (such as marked arrows). The sheet shape can be further confirmed with the field emission scanning electron microscopic (FESEM) analysis (Figure 2B). The thickness of the sheet-like particles is about 8 nm, while the length and width cannot be exactly determined due to the wrap and crinkle. Corresponding selected area electron diffraction (SAED) of the FeS (inset in Figure 2A) demonstrates that the sheet-like FeS nanostructure is polycrystalline.

![Figure 2. A) TEM and FESEM images of the FeS nanostructure. Inset in A): SAED pattern.](figure2.png)

The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of the sheet-like FeS nanostructure calculated from N<sub>2</sub> adsorption isotherm was 48.2 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, which was much larger than 2.0 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> of bulk FeS<sup>[18]</sup> and 14.9 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> of core-shell FeS nanoparticles with carbon as the shell.<sup>[19]</sup> Such a nanostructure with a large surface area might be of great significance for its potential application in sensing, optical devices and catalysis.

Although different FeS nanostructures, such as spherical particles,<sup>[20]</sup> have been prepared, to the best of our knowledge, the sheet-like nanostructure has not been reported yet. On the other hand, dodecylamine is often used as a stabilizing agent<sup>[21]</sup> or a structure-directing agent<sup>[22]</sup> in organic solvent-containing system for the preparation of nanostructures. However, due to its poor solubility in water only few works use it to prepare nanostructures in aqueous solution. This work used dodecanamine hydrochloride (DAHCl), a water-soluble compound, as a directing agent to prepare FeS nanostructure.

In the absence of DAHCl, the precipitation reaction of Fe<sup>2+</sup> ions with S<sup>2−</sup> ions produced irregular particles. In presence of 0.02 M DAHCl, this reaction formed regular spherical particles with a diameter of about 50 nm (Figure 3). With the increasing concentration of DAHCl, some sheet-like particles could be observed in the product. At the DAHCl concentration of 0.06 M, the product contained about 60% spherical FeS and 40% sheet-like FeS particles. When its concentration was increased to 0.14 M, sheet-like FeS with yield of at least 95% was obtained. Thus it can be concluded that the DAHCl plays an important role in the formation of the sheet-like nanostructure.

Micelles have been used as temples for fabrication of various types of nanoparticles. It is known that DAH<sup>+</sup> can aggregate in aqueous solutions to form rod-like or lamellar micelles when its concentration is higher than the critical micelle concentration (CMC) of 0.0125 M.<sup>[23]</sup> Furthermore, the micelles remain more or less spherical even at high surfactant concentrations.<sup>[24]</sup> Thus at a concentration of 0.02 M the formed spherical micelles could act as a directing agent to produce regular spherical particles. With further increase of the DAHCl concentration, the sphere-to-sheet-like transition of micelles took place. The formed product contained sheet-like nanostructure. When the concentration was increased to 0.14 M, much higher than the CMC, the DAH<sup>+</sup> micelles were mainly sheet-like, so the precipitation reaction on the micelles produced the sheet-like FeS. This method to form sheet-like nanostructure was similar to that used for preparation of Co-based nanosheets.<sup>[25]</sup>

![Figure 3. TEM image of the FeS prepared by the reaction of Na<sub>2</sub>S with (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Fe(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub> in the 0.02 M of DAHCl aqueous solution.](figure3.png)

**Catalytic behavior of sheet-like FeS nanostructure:** It is well known that peroxidase can catalyze the oxidation of a peroxidase substrate to produce a color change and the color reaction can generally be quenched by H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>. In order to characterize the peroxidase-like activity of the sheet-like FeS nanostructure, similar experiments were carried out. The suspension of sheet-like FeS with black color (Figure 4A) could catalyze the oxidation of a peroxidase substrate, TMB, by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to produce a blue color (Figure 4B). The resulting solution showed a maximum absorbance at 652 nm, which originated from the oxidation product of TMB. When H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> was added into the mixture of TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, the color reaction could be quenched. Upon addition of the sheet-like FeS only an orange solution was observed (Figure 4C), which showed the maximum absorbance at 450 nm. Obviously, the sheet-like FeS possessed peroxidase-like activity towards typical peroxidase substrates.

The apparent steady-state kinetic parameters for the peroxidase-like enzymatic color reaction could be determined by changing the concentrations of TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in this system, respectively. The $K_{m}^{app}$ value for the sheet-like FeS with TMB was 0.13 mM, while the value of $K_{m}^{app}$ for HRP with TMB was 0.4 mM. The latter coincides with that reported previously;<sup>[2]</sup> the former was slightly larger than that of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles.<sup>[2]</sup> The three times lower $K_{m}^{app}$ value of the sheet-like FeS nanostructure than that of HRP suggested that it had a higher affinity to TMB than HRP. This may be due to the fact that an HRP molecule has only one iron ion, in contrast to the surface of sheet-like FeS nanostructure. On the other hand, the $K_{m}^{app}$ value of the FeS with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was 7.2 mM, slightly higher than that of 3.7 mM for HRP in solution. But the $K_{m}^{app}$ value was much lower than that of 154 mM for Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles,<sup>[2]</sup> indicating a better affinity of the sheet-like FeS nanostructure to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> than Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles.

![Figure 4. Images of the suspension of sheet-like FeS nanostructure (A), mixture of TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> after catalytic reaction by sheet-like FeS nanostructure (B), mixture of TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> after adding H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> to quench the catalytic reaction by sheet-like FeS nanostructure (C).](figure4.png)

**Electrocatalytic activity of sheet-like FeS nanostructure toward reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>:** The direct electrochemistry of haeme-containing proteins on nanoparticles modified electrodes<sup>[12, 13]</sup> has well been studied in order to understand the electron-transfer process of proteins and develop reagentless amperometric biosensors for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. For use of sheet-like FeS nanostructures as biocatalysts for reagentless amperometric biosensors, its electrocatalytic activity toward reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was examined. The cyclic voltammogram of the sheet-like FeS modified GCE in 0.1 M pH 7.0 PBS showed a weak reduction peak at −0.424 V at 0.1 V s<sup>−1</sup> in spite of the unobvious oxidation peak (Figure 5). Upon addition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, the reduction peak current increased dramatically (Figure 5), which was much greater than the sum of the reduction peaks of the sheet-like FeS modified GCE and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with the same concentration at bare GCE (not shown), displaying an obvious electrocatalytic behavior of the sheet-like FeS nanostructure toward the reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Thus, the sheet-like FeS nanostructure could catalyze the reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.

In addition, similar to horseradish peroxidase, which is known to catalyze the reduction of oxygen, the sheet-like FeS modified GCE also showed electrocatalytic behavior towards the reduction of oxygen. Its cyclic voltammogram in oxygen-saturated 0.1 M pH 7.0 PBS showed obvious reduction wave starting at −0.1 V with a peak at about −0.45 V, while bare GCE did not show such a peak (not shown).

![Figure 5. Cyclic voltammograms of sheet-like FeS nanostructure modified GCE in 0.1 M pH 7.0 PBS containing a) 0, b) 10, c) 30, d) 40, and e) 50 μM H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (from bottom to top) at 0.1 V s<sup>−1</sup>.](figure5.png)

Furthermore, the electrocatalytic peak current increased with the increasing H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration. The amperometric response of the sheet-like FeS nanostructure modified GCE to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is shown in curve a in Figure 6. Upon addition of an aliquot of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to the buffer solution, the reduction current increased steeply to reach a stable value. The electrode achieved 95% of the steady-state-current in less than 10 seconds. The results clearly demonstrated that the electrocatalytic response was very fast. At pH 7.0 the linear range of the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensor was from 0.5 to 150 μM with a correlation coefficient of 0.9998 (inset in Figure 6), which was much wider than 1.0 to 73 μM for a HRP-based biosensor in zirconia nanoparticles enhanced grafted collagen tri-helix scaffold.<sup>[26]</sup> The detection limit was estimated to be $9.2 \times 10^{-8}$ M at a signal to noise of 3, which was lower than 0.65 μM at a HRP/gold nanoparticles/chitosan modified electrode<sup>[27]</sup> and 4 μM for gold nanotube ensembles.<sup>[28]</sup> The attractive performance of the proposed H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensor illustrated the advantage of the sheet-like FeS nanostructure-based “artificial peroxidase” as a transducer for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> detection and preparation of reagentless amperometric biosensors.

![Figure 6. Amperometric responses of a) sheet-like and b) spherical FeS nanostructure modified GCEs at −0.40 V upon successive additions of 5 μL 0.5 mM H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> into 5.0 mL 0.1 M pH 7.0 PBS. Inset: plot of electrocatalytic current vs H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration at sheet-like FeS modified electrode.](figure6.png)

For comparison, the amperometric response of a spherical FeS modified GCE was shown in curve b in Figure 6. Upon the addition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, the reduction current also increased. However, the catalytic responses were smaller than those at sheet-like FeS modified GCE, indicating that sheet-like FeS had a better catalytic activity, which might result from the larger surface area of sheet-like FeS than about 28.1 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> of the spherical FeS. In addition, the sheet-like nanostructure had a high fraction of surface atoms located on the facets and edges, which might result in an improved catalytic activity.<sup>[29]</sup>

As the general enzymatic reaction<sup>[12b]</sup> and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles-catalyzed reaction,<sup>[3]</sup> at H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentrations higher than 150 μM, the calibration curve of the sheet-like FeS nanostructure-based sensor showed a platform, indicating a Michaelis–Menten type response. The $K_{m}^{app}$ value for electrocatalyzing the reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at 25 °C was estimated to be $54.3 \pm 1.85$ μM, which was much smaller than 160 μM of HRP immobilized on Nafion–cysteine modified gold electrode,<sup>[30]</sup> indicating a higher affinity of the immobilized sheet-like FeS to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The maximum catalytic current was 61.2 μA. From the coverage of the sheet-like FeS and the slope of plot of catalytic reduction current vs H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration, the catalytic velocity constant of the sheet-like FeS modified electrode toward H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was calculated to be 51.3 M<sup>−1</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>.

**Effects of pH and temperature on the sheet-like FeS nanostructure-based sensor for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>:** The effects of pH and temperature on the electrocatalytic activity of the sheet-like FeS nanostructure are shown in Figure 7, in which the relative activity was defined as the ratio of the electrocatalytic response at a given condition to the maximum electrocatalytic response. With the increasing pH of detection solution from 2.0 to 10 the electrocatalytic activity toward the reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> increased and then decreased. The maximum catalytic activity occurred at approximately pH 7.0. The dependence of the electrocatalytic activity on temperature in the range of 10 to 70 °C also showed similar appearance. The maximum catalytic activity was at 40 °C. These optimal conditions were similar to the values for the electrocatalytic reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> by cytochrome c immobilized on NaY zeolite.<sup>[31]</sup>

**Stability and reproducibility of the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensor:** The FeS nanostructure is an inorganic nanomaterial and is expected to be more stable than natural peroxidases. To examine its stability, both HRP and the sheet-like FeS nanostructure modified electrodes were exposed to different temperatures ranging from 10 to 70 °C and solutions with pH ranging from 2.0 to 10.0 for 2 h; their relative activities toward the electrocatalytic reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were then measured at pH 7.0 and 40 °C. The sheet-like FeS nanostructure showed much better stability than HRP in the measured temperature and pH ranges (Figure 8). After exposed to pH 2.0 and 10.0 solutions for two hours the nanostructure could remain 40 and 56% of the peroxidase activity, respectively, while the HRP denatured completely at pH values lower than 3.0 and remained only 30% of its activity at pH 10.0. The effect of the temperature on its stability showed similar phenomena. In the temperature range of 20 to 60 °C the nanostructure was almost stable, but the HRP showed a very narrow temperature range for remaining its activity, and when it was exposed to temperature higher than 70 °C the complete denaturation was observed.

![Figure 7. Effects of A) pH and B) temperature on the electrocatalytic response of sheet-like FeS nanostructure to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> reduction.](figure7.png)

![Figure 8. Electrocatalytic activity of a) sheet-like FeS nanostructure and b) HRP to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at pH 7.0 and 40 °C after exposed to different temperatures and pHs for 2 h.](figure8.png)

In addition the sheet-like FeS modified GCE also had a good storage stability. When it was stored in 0.1 M pH 7.0 PBS at 4 °C for a week, no obvious decrease in the response to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was observed. After a month, the sensor retained 99% of its initial response. The good stability of FeS made it more suitable for a broad range of applications in the biomedicine and environmental chemistry fields.

The current determination using four proposed FeS-modified electrodes, made independently, at 4.0 μM H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> showed a relative standard deviation (RSD) of 4.3%. The mean steady-state current of 4.0 μM H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> for six determinations using one FeS-modified GCE was 1.1 μA, which showed a RSD of 4.6%. Thus the fabrication reproducibility and detection precision of the sheet-like FeS-modified GCE was acceptable.

### Conclusion

This work reports a sheet-like FeS nanostructure that can be easily synthesized by adjusting the concentration of DAHCl in the reaction system. This novel FeS nanostructure has large specific surface area and can be considered as a mimic peroxidase. Its peroxidase activity has been demonstrated by following facts: 1) it can catalyze the oxidation reaction of TMB by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>; 2) the FeS nanostructure-catalyzed reaction shows the behavior similar to the general peroxidase and nanoparticles-catalyzed reactions; 3) it shows the electrocatalytic activity toward reduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to produce a fast, sensitive and low-cost H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensor; 4) the catalytic behaviors show a typical Michaelis–Menten kinetics and good affinity to both H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB; 5) the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sensor based on the sheet-like FeS had more sensitive response than those based on spherical FeS; 6) the electrocatalytic activity is dependent on pH and temperature; and 7) the sheet-like FeS nanostructure has much better stability than HRP when exposed to different solution pHs and temperatures, and the resulting sensor for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> also shows good stability and reproducibility. The novel sheet-like FeS nanostructure can be used as an “artificial peroxidase” for the potential development of amperometric transducers and biocatalysts.

### Experimental Section

**Materials and synthesis:** HRP (EC 1.11 1.7, RZ > 3.0, > 250 U mg<sup>−1</sup>) was from Sigma (USA) and used as received. Hydrogen peroxide (30% w/v solution) was from Shanghai Biochemical Regent Co. (China). Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA, average degree of polymerization, $1800 \pm 100$) was from Shanghai Laize Factory of Fine Chemicals (China). DAHCl was from Shanghai Chem. Co. (China). 3,3’,5,5’-Tetramethyl benzidine (TMB) was from Beijing Cellchip Biotechnology Co. (China). All chemicals were of analytical reagent grade and used as received. All solutions were prepared with doubly distilled water deoxygenated by bubbling highly pure nitrogen. 0.1 M phosphate buffer solutions with different pHs were prepared by mixing the stock standard solutions of Na<sub>2</sub>HPO<sub>4</sub> and NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> and adjusting the pH with 0.1 M H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> or NaOH.

**Typical synthesis of sheet-like FeS nanostructure:** The synthesis was operated in a glove box under N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere. (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Fe(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O (0.39 g, 1 mmol) and DAHCl (0.77 g, 3.47 mmol) were added to distilled water (25 mL) to obtain solution A. Solution B was obtained by dissolving Na<sub>2</sub>S·9H<sub>2</sub>O (0.12 g, 0.5 mmol) and DAHCl (0.12 g, 0.5 mmol) into distilled water (25 mL). Then solution B was added to solution A under stirring to produce black precipitate. The produced precipitate was centrifuged, collected, washed with water and water/acetone for several times, and then dried under vacuum, which was demonstrated to have a sheet-like FeS nanostructure. The obtained nanostructured FeS was stored under N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere.

**Preparation of nanostructured FeS modified electrodes:** Nanostructured FeS (30 mg) was dispersed into distilled water (10 mL) to obtain a suspension of FeS (3 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>). 100 μL of the obtained suspension was then mixed with a 3% PVA solution (5 μL) of ethanol/water 1:1 to produce a colloid solution of FeS nanostructure. The glassy carbon electrodes (GCE, 3.0 mm in diameter) were firstly polished with 0.3 and 0.05 μm alumina slurry (Buehler) followed by thoroughly rinsing with water. The colloidal solution (2 μL) of the nanostructure was then dropped on the pretreated GCE surface and allowed to dry under ambient conditions for 3 h to obtain nanostructured FeS modified electrodes.

**Apparatus and procedures:** The phase characterization was performed by means of X-ray diffraction (XRD) using a D/Max-RA diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation. The morphology and particle sizes of the samples were characterized by JEM-200CX transmission electron microscopy (TEM) working at 200 kV and LEO1530 VP field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) at an acceleration of 15 kV. Energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) was performed on the FESEM with a PV9100 scanning electron microanalyzer. Nitrogen adsorption isotherms were obtained using an ASAP 2000 instrument (Micromeritics, Norcross, GA). Before adsorption measurements, the samples were degassed for 2 h at 150 °C.

The catalytic experiments of the sheet-like FeS to the oxidation of TMB were carried out in a 1.5 mL tube with 10 μg sheet-like FeS or 1.0 ng HRP in 500 μL of 0.1 M PBS (pH 7.0) in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, using TMB as the substrate. Hitachi UV 2010 spectrophotometer was used to measure the apparent Michaelis–Menten constant ($K_{m}^{app}$) of the sheet-like FeS.

Cyclic voltammetric and amperometric measurements were performed on CHI 660 (CHI, USA). A three-electrode system comprising a platinum wire as auxiliary, a saturated calomel electrode as reference and the FeS-modified electrode as working electrodes was used for all electrochemical experiments. The electrochemical experiments were carried out in a cell containing 5.0 mL 0.1 M PBS at room temperature ($26 \pm 2$ °C). All experimental solutions were deoxygenated by bubbling highly pure nitrogen for at least 10 min and maintained under nitrogen atmosphere during the measurements. In amperometric experiments, the current–time data were recorded after a constant residual current had been established and successive additions of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> solution into the buffer were done. It was carried out by applying a potential of −0.4 V on a stirred cell at room temperature. The sensor response was measured as the difference between total and residual currents.
