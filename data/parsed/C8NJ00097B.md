**Modulation of biomimetic properties of cerium nanoparticles by hypoxic tumor microenvironment: Steering towards tumor specificity**

Preethi Sridharan<sup>a,†</sup>, G. Vinothkumar<sup>b,†</sup>, Pooja Pratheesh<sup>a,*</sup>, K. Suresh Babu<sup>b,*</sup>

<sup>a</sup> Central-Interdisciplinary Research Facility, Mahatma Gandhi Medical College and Research Institute, Pondicherry – 607 403, India.
<sup>b</sup> Centre for Nanoscience & Technology, Madanjeet School of Green Energy Technologies, Pondicherry University, Kalapet, Pondicherry – 605 014, India.

**Abstract**

The low oxygen tension characterizing the core glioblastomas (GBMs) and other solid tumors are known to cause a metabolic shift within tumors. This lowers the pH and simultaneously increases the ROS (O<sub>2</sub><sup>●−</sup>, HO<sup>●</sup>, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> etc.,) accumulation which eventually facilitates the tumor resistance to chemo/radiotherapy. Herein, we report the biomimetic antioxidant properties of cerium oxide nanoparticles (CNPs) under physiological and tumor microenvironment like conditions *in vitro* using GBM cell line. CNPs were synthesized in a single step combustion method conferred selective cytotoxicity under tumor microenvironment like conditions. Further, the peroxidase-like activity of CNP nanoparticles were modulated by tumor microenvironment like conditions (pH 6.5 and 1.5% O<sub>2</sub>) due to the redox transformation between Ce<sup>3+</sup> ↔ Ce<sup>4+</sup> states of cerium oxide nanoparticles under different conditions. When incubated with CNPs, a remarkable variation in the oxidative stress was observed from the measured MDA levels. Combined results from peroxidase assay, MDA assay and TBARS assay revealed size and pH dependent activity which could have significant implications towards the tumor specific activity. The results are promising for use of CNPs for tumor therapy and warrant further studies both *in vivo* and *in vitro* models on the possible therapeutic potential.

**Keywords**

Ceria; hypoxia; glioblastoma; cytotoxicity; pH-dependent; peroxidase; antioxidant

**1. Introduction**

Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is a group of heterogeneous and highly lethal brain tumors for which conventional therapies like surgical resection, chemotherapy and radiotherapy provide only palliation<sup>1</sup>. The hierarchy of the heterogeneous neoplastic compartments and a tumor core with low pH and low oxygen tension (hypoxia) contributes to the pathogenesis of GBMs. A hypoxic microenvironment contributes to the formation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) through the release of superoxide anion radical (O<sub>2</sub><sup>●−</sup>), hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>), and hydroxyl radical (<sup>●</sup>OH) from the mitochondrial electron transport chain. Chemotherapy and radiation therapy employed as standard palliative therapy also generate ROS, which at higher levels are toxic to cells. Nevertheless, sub-lethal ROS generated by these treatments have shown to promote cancer invasion and metastasis<sup>2,3</sup>. Though the detailed mechanism still remain elusive, the metabolic shift associated with chronic hypoxia and the resultant increase in ROS generation is likely to facilitate the GBM resistance to chemotherapy, radiotherapy and enhances tumor angiogenesis and metastatic features<sup>4–6</sup>. Recent studies have suggested that ROS and hypoxia-inducible transcription factor 1α (HIF-1α) are potential mediators of hypoxia-mediated tumor progression. Glioma stem cells (GSCs) are also shown to be enriched in this hypoxic regions and this microenvironment facilitates the functional ability of GSCs to self-renew and propagate tumors<sup>7</sup>. Therefore, a pragmatic approach would be to design GBM therapeutic modalities keeping hypoxic and ROS enriched microenvironment in consideration.

Various physicochemical methods are presently being investigated to specifically target tumor hypoxic niche. Among numerous experimental approaches, nanoparticles based tumor therapeutics have shown promise<sup>8–10</sup>. Cerium oxide (CeO<sub>2</sub>) is one of the rare-earth oxide material that exist in Ce<sup>3+</sup> and Ce<sup>4+</sup> oxidation state<sup>11</sup>. Nanoceria or cerium oxide nanoparticles (CNPs) are gaining interest in biomedicine due to their differential biomimetic properties under diverse physico-chemical environments<sup>12,13</sup>. The concentration of Ce<sup>3+</sup> and Ce<sup>4+</sup> on the surface of the ceria largely depends on the factors like synthetic procedures, ambient chemical environment, size, morphology etc. The transition between the Ce<sup>3+</sup> and Ce<sup>4+</sup> valence states and vice versa is found to be the reason behind the biomimetic activity of CNPs<sup>14</sup>. The partial reduction of cerium oxide from Ce<sup>4+</sup> to Ce<sup>3+</sup> state with the formation of oxygen vacancies in the ceria lattice can be written using the Kröger – Vink notation as,

$$O_{o}^{x} + 2Ce_{Ce}^{x} \rightarrow V_{o}^{\bullet \bullet} + 2Ce_{Ce}^{'} + \frac{1}{2}O_{2} \quad (1)$$

where $V_{o}^{\bullet \bullet}$ is the oxygen vacancy having doubly positive charge, $O_{o}^{x}$ and $Ce_{Ce}^{x}$ corresponds to Ce<sup>4+</sup> and $Ce_{Ce}^{'}$ corresponds to Ce<sup>3+</sup> in the ceria lattice. When two Ce<sup>4+</sup> ions are reduced to Ce<sup>3+</sup> ions an oxygen vacancy is generated in the lattice<sup>15</sup>. In nano regime, cerium oxide easily loses oxygen at a low oxygen pressure which can be represented as $2CeO_{2} \leftrightarrow Ce_{2}O_{3} + \frac{1}{2} O_{2}$, transforming the Ce<sup>4+</sup> state into partially reduced Ce<sup>3+</sup> state which is highly reactive in nature<sup>16</sup>.

The functional characteristics of CNPs modulate with the ambient physicochemical parameters. Recently, Alili *et al.*, reported, pH-dependent pro-oxidant behavior of nano ceria on squamous tumor cell lines while being non-toxic to stromal cells<sup>8,17</sup>. Das *et al.*, and Chigurupati *et al.*, demonstrated the induction of angiogenesis by ceria through the regulation of intracellular oxygen environment to promote wound healing<sup>9,18</sup>. Nanoceria acts as an anti-angiogenic therapeutic agent which prevents the growth and metastasis of solid tumors<sup>19,20</sup>. Moreover, nanoceria in combination with chemotherapeutic drugs such as doxorubicin and cisplatinum improved its therapeutic potential<sup>10,21</sup>. At low pH, CNPs are found to display oxidase like activity and generate harmful ROS in living systems and become cytotoxic to cancer cells<sup>22</sup>. However, at physiological pH, CNPs mimic superoxide-dismutase (SOD) mimetic activity<sup>23</sup>, catalase mimetic activity<sup>24</sup> and the capacity to scavenge nitric oxide radicals<sup>25</sup> thereby exhibiting antioxidant properties. Reports highlight the protective effects of antioxidant nanoceria in neurological disorders and radioprotection to healthy cells<sup>26,27</sup>. Although nanoceria has been studied extensively as SOD and catalase mimetics, research on peroxidase-like activity remains scant<sup>28</sup>. The superoxide anion radical and hydrogen peroxide plays an important role in regulating the HIF-1α. Towards the antioxidant based tumor therapy, over-expression of enzyme based antioxidants has shown promising results. Overexpression of manganese superoxide dismutase (MnSOD) is shown to overturn the malignancy of cancer cells, including gliomas<sup>29–32</sup>. In contrast, depletion of MnSOD is shown to result in increased cell proliferation *in vitro* leading to more aggressive tumor growth *in vivo*<sup>33</sup>. These results are encouraging for the future therapeutic use of antioxidant enzymes *in vivo*.

However, it is of utmost importance that the biomimetic activities of these nanostructures are evaluated in terms of the mechanism of action of these materials under conditions prevalent *in vivo*. Since the physicochemical parameters in and around the tumors, specially solid tumors and gliomas vary widely, including variations in pH, oxygen tension, ionic concentration etc., it is pertinent to investigate the therapeutic potential of these nanozymes under conditions mimicking tumor microenvironment. Through this work, we have addressed this questions and our results show that the CNPs exhibit peroxidase like enzymatic activity under tumor core like conditions. This leads to significant reduction in the ROS level. Our results indicate that the ability of CNPs to alleviate ROS induced stress may allow the chemotherapeutics work more effectively for a longer duration, thereby enhancing the chemotherapy efficacy. Hence, the peroxidase-like activity along with the ability of reduce oxidative stress makes CNPs a lucrative candidate for developing CNP based adjunct therapeutics for GBM. To our knowledge, this is the first report to discuss hypoxic and low pH tumor microenvironment as a modulator of CNPs peroxidase like activity and possible role of nanozymes mediated reduction of ROS levels in achieving better therapeutic outcome for GBMs in future.

**2. Experimental methods**

**2.1. Materials**

Cerium nitrate hexahydrate (Ce(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>. 6H<sub>2</sub>O, Himedia) and glycine (H<sub>2</sub>NCH<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>2</sub>H, Fisher Scientific) were used for the synthesis of CNP. Human GBM cell line U251 was obtained from National Center for Cell Sciences, Pune (India). Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium, high glucose (DMEM), Penicillin (50 IU/ml), streptomycin (50 IU/ml), Thiazoyl blue tetrazolium bromide (MTT) and cell culture grade Dimethyl Sulfoxide (DMSO) were purchased from Sigma, USA. Fetal Bovine Serum was purchased from Gibco BRL. Glutamine was purchased from Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA. Phosphate Buffered Saline (PBS) was prepared in house using Sodium Chloride (NaCl), Sodium Phosphate monobasic (Na<sub>2</sub>HPO<sub>4</sub>) and Potassium Phosphate monobasic (KH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>) all purchased from Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA. Potassium chloride (KCl), Thiobarbituric acid (TBA), acetic acid, butanol and pyridine used for MDA assay were purchased from Fisher Scientific, USA and sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS), tetramethoxy propane were purchased from Sigma, USA. Plastic cell culture dishes, flasks and plates were purchased from Corning Incorporation, USA. 0.22µm PES membrane filter and syringe filters were purchased from PAL, USA. The hypoxia studies were carried out in Heracell Vios 160i CO<sub>2</sub> incubator.

**2.2. Synthesis and Characterization of CNPs**

Cerium oxide nanoparticles of two different sizes were synthesized by solution combustion method by varying the concentration of oxidizer (cerium nitrate hexahydrate) and fuel (glycine). Typically, 100 mL of aqueous solution of cerium nitrate (1 M) and glycine (0.6 M) were mixed thoroughly by stirring at room temperature. The homogeneous solution turned into a transparent gel upon incubation at 80 °C in an oven to evaporate the water. The gel was transferred to a hot plate where it was maintained at 300 °C for ignition to start. Upon the auto-ignition of gel, highly exothermic reaction resulted in crystalline and pale yellow colored cerium oxide nanoparticle. The formation of cerium oxide through combustion reaction at different fuel-oxidizer ratio (Φ = 0.6 and 1.1, labelled CNP-6 and CNP-12, respectively) can be written as follows,

$$Ce(NO_{3})_{3} \cdot 6H_{2}O + \Phi NH_{2}CH_{2}COOH + \frac{1}{4}O_{2} \rightarrow CeO_{2} + \frac{7}{3}N_{2} + \frac{10}{3}CO_{2} + \frac{61}{6}H_{2}O \quad (2)$$

The resultant particles were washed and centrifuged at 10,000 rpm with water/ethanol mixture to remove any unreacted precursors and dried overnight at 80 °C. All the experiments were further carried out by preparing the cerium oxide stock solution at a concentration of 1mg/mL after sonicating the solution for 1 hour. The solution became milky white and was found to be stable upto 3 days without settling.

The X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis was carried out to identify the crystalline nature and average crystallite size using a Rigaku Ultima IV X-ray diffractometer equipped with Cu-Kα radiation at a scan rate of 4° per min. The morphology and composition of the samples were analyzed with Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM, Hitachi S-3400N) and Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy (EDS) operated at 30 kV. The morphology and size distribution of the cerium oxide nanoparticles were obtained using high resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM), FEI-Tecnai G2 20 S-TWIN operated at 200 kV attached with Bruker XFlash 6T130 detector. Raman spectra of the samples were recorded to determine the oxygen vacancy concentration of the nanoceria with Renishaw Laser Confocal Raman microscope 2000 consists of Ar<sup>+</sup> ion laser source at an excitation wavelength of 785 nm. UV-visible absorption spectra of cerium oxide nanoparticles was acquired in a Perkin Elmer Lambda 650S UV-Vis spectrophotometer.

**2.3. Preparation of cell culture medium with different pH**

The DMEM medium was supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, penicillin and streptomycin (50 IU/ml) and 2mM glutamine (henceforth referred to as complete medium). The pH was adjusted at 7.4 using sodium bicarbonate and at pH 6.5 using 1N HCl. The media was filter sterilized by passing through 0.22 µm PES membrane filter.

**2.4. Cell culture**

The cells were cultured in complete medium. The cells were cultured at 37°C under 90% humidity and 5% CO<sub>2</sub> (referred to as normoxic conditions) till the cells were 80% confluent. The cells were washed twice with phosphate-buffered saline (pH 7.4), and trypsinized with 0.05% trypsin-ethylenediaminetetraacetic (EDTA). Complete medium was freshly added to neutralize the trypsin action and cells were centrifuged at 2,000 rpm for 10 minutes. The cell viability was checked using Trypan blue staining and 1X10<sup>4</sup> cells were plated per well for cell viability assay. The cells were allowed to adhere for up to 20 hours at 37 °C under 90% humidity and 5% CO<sub>2</sub> (referred as Normoxia) For exposure to hypoxia, the cells were maintained at 37 °C, 90% humidity 5% CO<sub>2</sub>, and 1.3% O<sub>2</sub> in Heracell Vios 160i CO<sub>2</sub> incubator.

**2.5. Cell viability test**

Cell viability was measured by the MTT (3-(4-5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide) assay. The CNP-6 nm and CNP-12 were dispersed at five different concentrations (50, 125, 250, 500 and 1000 µg/ml) in complete medium at pH 7.4 and 6.5 and added to wells. The plates were incubated at normoxia or hypoxia for 24 hours. For control, cells were incubated at normoxic or hypoxic conditions in complete medium at pH 7.2 and 6.5 without CNPs. At the end of exposure, 40 µl of MTT solution (2 mg/ml) was added to each well and the cells were incubated for 4 hours at 37 °C. This was followed by addition of 150 µl of DMSO to dissolve the formazan crystal and the absorbance was quantified at 570nm using a multimode reader (Molecular devices, Spectramax M5, USA). The viability of the treated group was expressed as the percentage of control group that was assumed to be 100%.

**2.6. IC<sub>50</sub> Value Calculation**

The IC<sub>50</sub> represents the concentration of a drug that is required for 50% inhibition of cell growth/metabolic and/or biochemical function. Here, IC<sub>50</sub> values for CNP-6 and CNP-12 were calculated under different test conditions.

**2.7. Peroxidase assay**

The peroxidase-mimicking activity of the CNPs was evaluated by monitoring the catalytic reaction between H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and 3,3′-diaminobenzidine (DAB) in citrate buffer solution (eqn. 1)

$$DAB + H_{2}O_{2} \rightarrow DABox + 2H_{2}O + O_{2} \quad (3)$$

The assay was carried out as reported by Fahimi<sup>34</sup> *et al.* Briefly, CNP-6 and CNP-12 were dissolved in citrate buffer at pH 4.0, 6.5 and 7.4 at the concentration of 2mg/ml. For the assay, 100 µl of each CNPs was taken into a microfuge tube and 100 µl of DAB (0.8mg/ml) along with 5µl of 30% hydrogen peroxide and 0.1% gelatin was added. The mixture was incubated at 37 °C for varying time points and the OD at 460 nm was noted using a spectrophotometer. Assays were performed under physiological (pH 7.4) and tumor (pH 6.5) conditions as described.

**2.8. MDA and TBARS assay**

An increase in levels of MDA (Malondialdehyde) is a known marker of oxidative stress. The extent of lipid peroxidation due to oxidative stress induced by hypoxia was determined by the method of Okhawa<sup>35</sup> *et al.* The U-251 cells were treated with CNP-6 and CNP-12 (Table 2) under physiological and tumor micro environmental conditions and CNP untreated cells under similar experimental conditions were taken as control. After 24 h incubation, the cells were homogenized in 1ml of 0.15M potassium chloride. In a reaction tube, 0.2 ml of 0.31% thiobarbituric acid solution was added in the succession. To this reaction mixture, 0.2ml of the cell homogenate was added and heated in the boiling water bath for 60minutes. After cooling to RT, 5ml of butanol: pyridine (15:1) solution was added and then centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 15minutes. The upper organic layer was aspirated and the intensity of the resulting pink colour was read at 532nm using tetramethoxy propane as an external standard. The results were expressed as nmoles of MDA formed/ 10mg of protein.

TBARs assay was used to confirm the findings from MDA assay using TBARS assay kit (Cayman, USA). The assay was performed with cells grown under hypoxic and normoxic conditions, with and without CNPs. The U-251 cells were treated with CNP-6 and CNP-12 as given in Table 2 under physiological and tumor micro environmental conditions and CNP untreated cells under similar experimental conditions were taken as control. Briefly, 100uL of cell lysate was mixed with 100uL of SDS solution and 4mL of colour solution. The mixture was kept in boiling water bath for 1 hour and then immediately kept on ice for 10 minutes. The contents were then centrifuged at 1600 x g for 10 minutes and the supernatant was used to measure the absorbance at 530nm. The results were expressed as nmoles of MDA formed/ 10mg of protein. The results were expressed as nmoles of MDA formed/ 10mg of protein.

**2.9. Statistical Analysis**

All the assays were repeated at least three times and the data are presented as mean ± standard error. In all experiments, CNPs treated samples were compared with their respective controls and the mean difference was calculated using one way analysis of variance (ANOVA). Along this, mean difference between two groups was assessed during the two-tailed Student’s *t*-test and *P* < 0.05 was considered as statistically significant in all the experiments.

**3. Results**

**3.1. X-ray diffraction**

The X-ray diffraction pattern of cerium oxide nanoparticles synthesized by solution combustion method with different fuel-oxidizer ratio (Φ = 0.6 and 1.1) is shown in Fig. 1. The peaks located at 28.5°, 33.1°, 47.5°, 56.2° and 59.3° correpsonds to (111), (200), (220), (311) and (222) of cubic fluorite cerium oxide crystallographic planes (ICDD card no. 00-34-0394), respectively<sup>36</sup>.

The peaks obtained for CNP-6 were found to be broader in comparison to CNP-12. The mean crystallite size and lattice strain was estimated using Williamson-Hall equation<sup>37</sup>,

$$\frac{\beta \cos \theta}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{d} + \frac{\eta \sin \theta}{\lambda} \quad (4)$$

where β is the full width at half maximum (FWHM), θ is the diffraction angle and λ is the wavelength of X-ray (0.14517 nm), *d* is the average crystallite size (nm) and η is the lattice strain. When the fuel-oxidizer ratio increases, the adiabatic flame temperature in the combustion process also increases<sup>38</sup>. Thus, a mean crystallite size of 6 nm and 12 nm was obtained at a low fuel ratio (Φ = 0.6) and high fuel ratio (Φ = 1.1), respectively (Table 1). A shift in the peak position towards lower 2θ was observed for CNP-6 as shown in Fig. 1 which indicates a lattice expansion at smaller crystallites size.

![Fig. 1 XRD pattern of CNP-6 and CNP-12 prepared at different fuel ratio Φ = 0.6 and 1.1, respectively.](figure1_image)

**Fig. 1** XRD pattern of CNP-6 and CNP-12 prepared at different fuel ratio Φ = 0.6 and 1.1, respectively.

The lattice parameter was found to be 5.420 and 5.416 nm for CNP-6 and CNP-12, respectively, which is slightly higher than the reported value of 5.411 for bulk cerium oxide<sup>39</sup>. The expansion of the ceria lattice can be attributed to the presence of Ce<sup>3+</sup> ions and oxygen vacancies (as represented in equation 1). Deshpande *et.al*, observed an increase in the lattice parameter with the decrease of particle size and the lattice expansion was attributed to the generation of large amount of oxygen vacancies and associated Ce<sup>3+</sup> concentration in the ceria lattice<sup>40</sup>. Moreover, using molecular dynamics simulation, Ma *et.al*, showed that the oxygen vacancy formation energy decreases with an increase in ceria lattice parameter<sup>41</sup>. With the generation of large amount of oxygen vacancies and Ce<sup>3+</sup> (ionic radius = 0.114 nm), distortion in the local symmetry of the cerium oxide lattice with Ce<sup>4+</sup> (0.097 nm) ions produce higher lattice strain in smaller crystallites<sup>42</sup>. The calculated lattice strain for CNP-12 was found to be very low compared to CNP-6 in consistent with the fact that lattice parameters and lattice strain are directly proportional. The ratio Ce<sup>3+</sup>/Ce<sup>4+</sup> in the ceria lattice can be controlled by oxygen vacancies are crucial parameters that determine the reactivity of the cerium oxide nanoparticles.

**3.3. Raman and UV-Vis spectroscopic analysis**

The Raman spectra of bulk cerium oxide exhibit a single sharp corresponding to Ce-O<sub>8</sub> vibration at 465 cm<sup>-1</sup> which is highly sensitive to the lattice defects<sup>36</sup> as shown in Fig. 4a. The Raman spectra of CNP exhibit a sharp asymmetric band at 464 cm<sup>-1</sup> correspond to the triply degenerate F<sub>2g</sub> mode of vibration with cubic fluorite structure arising from the symmetric breathing mode of the Ce-O vibration which is consistent with the XRD observations. The F<sub>2g</sub> peak become broad, asymmetric and shifted towards 463 cm<sup>-1</sup> when the crystallite size reduced to 6 nm.

The broadening and shift in the F<sub>2g</sub> band position can be attributed to factors such as smaller crystallite size, formation of defects such as oxygen vacancies in the lattice, lattice strain and phonon confinement<sup>43</sup> etc. Broad and weak band centered at 600 cm<sup>-1</sup> is observed for both the samples indicating the formation of non-stoichiometric intrinsic oxygen vacancies which depends on the size of the nanoparticles. The other weak band around 260 cm<sup>-1</sup> belongs to the second order optical mode (2LO) of vibration<sup>44</sup>. The nature of shift in the peak position can be written as,

$$\Delta \omega = - \frac{3 \gamma \omega_{o} \Delta a}{a_{o}} \quad (5)$$

where ω<sub>o</sub> is the Raman frequency of bulk CeO<sub>2</sub>, a<sub>o</sub> is the bulk ceria lattice constant (0.5411 nm), Δa is the difference in the lattice constant and γ is the Gruneisen constant with a value of 1.24 for cerium oxide. The shifting nature of F<sub>2g</sub> band with respect to size can be correlated to the weakening of Ce-O coordination leads to the breaking of bonds. As a result oxygen vacancies are generated to maintain the charge imbalance due to bond breaking. Further the oxygen vacancy concentration in nanoceria is calculated using Spatial Correlation model (SCM). The concentration of oxygen vacancies of nanoceria can be estimated using the spatial correlation model from the relationship between correlation length and grain size,

$$L(nm) = \sqrt{\left[ \frac{\alpha}{2d_{g}} \right]^{2} ([d_{g} - 2\alpha]^{3} + 4d_{g}^{2}\alpha)} \quad (6)$$

where *L* is the correlation length which is the average distance between two lattice defects, α is the radius of the CeO<sub>2</sub> units (0.34 nm) and d<sub>g</sub> is the grain size. The oxygen vacancy concentration *N* (cm<sup>-3</sup>) is calculated using the following relation,

$$N(cm^{-3}) = 3/4\pi L^{3} \quad (7)$$

The oxygen vacancy concentration of 2.14 x 10<sup>21</sup> cm<sup>-3</sup> and 1.46 x 10<sup>21</sup> cm<sup>-3</sup> was obtained for CNP-6 and CNP-12, respectively. The higher oxygen vacancy concentration in CNP-6 indicates the presence of higher Ce<sup>3+</sup> concentration in comparison to CNP-12 which is in accordance with our XRD results. Moreover, the optical characteristics of the synthesized nanoparticles were studied using UV-Vis absorption spectroscopy. Fig. 4b depicts the UV-vis spectra of CNP-6 and CNP-12 recorded from 200 nm to 800 nm range. It can be observed that the maximum absorption and band edge of the spectra lies between 200 nm and 500 nm. The peak at 255 nm corresponds to the O<sup>2-</sup> to Ce<sup>3+</sup> charge transfer transitions and peak at 341 nm indicates the interband transitions. The absorption band edge, particularly of CNP-6 extends well into the visible region of the spectrum compared to that of CNP-12. The absorption in the visible region of the spectrum reveals the presence of defects such as oxygen vacancies in the ceria lattice and increased Ce<sup>3+</sup> concentration while phonon confinement, lattice strain are the other factors contribute to this effect<sup>36</sup>.

**3.5. Peroxidase assay**

The peroxidase like activity of CNP-6 and CNP-12 was tested using the chromogenic substrate DAB in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. To check whether CNPs demonstrated any pH dependent peroxidase like activity, the assay was performed at tumor like condition (pH 6.5, 1.5% O<sub>2</sub> tension), physiological condition (pH 7.5) and pH 4.0 (positive control). Further, the concentration of the chromogenic substrate DAB was varied from 5-40 mM and the absorbance values were noted for 60 min at an interval of 10 minutes. The change in the absorbance was monitored at 460 nm is shown in Fig. 6. The oxidation of DAB in the presence of hydrogen peroxide and gelatin led to the development of brown color as shown in the inset of Fig. 6.

The steady-state kinetics of the peroxidase like enzymatic activity of CNP were determined using Michaelis-Menten model as shown in Fig. 6(c-d). The reciprocal of initial velocities plotted against the inverse of substrate concentration resulting in a linear plot (inset of Fig. 6c & 6d) was utilized to evaluate the kinetic parameters of the enzymatic activity using double reciprocal (Lineweaver-Burk method),

$$\frac{1}{V} = \frac{K_{m}}{V_{max}[S]} + \frac{1}{V_{max}} \quad (8)$$

where the K<sub>m</sub> (the Michaelis constant, a measure of affinity towards substrate), *V* is the initial velocity and V<sub>max</sub> (the maximum velocity for converting substrate to product).

**4. Discussion**

Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM), a highly aggressive and lethal brain tumor is characterized by a severe hypoxia with pO<sub>2</sub> reducing to the level of ≈ 0.1% and elevated glycolytic flux in the presence of a reversed pH gradient between the tumor and the microenvironment<sup>45,46</sup>. While the physiological pH is ~7.4, malignant tumors harbor a pH of ~6.8-7.4<sup>47</sup>. Glioma stem cells (GSCs) responsible for self-renewal and tumor propagation are known to reside and renew in hypoxic niches. Hence, for any GBM therapeutic approach to be successful, it is important to target the hypoxic tumor core. In order to achieve this, an ideal therapeutic formulation should include the hypoxic tumor core as one of its target.

There are contradictory reports on whether CNPs act as an anti-oxidant with ability to scavenge free radicals and protect the cells from oxidative damage or as a pro-oxidant causing cytotoxicity by the generation of ROS<sup>15,24</sup>. Since the biomimetic behavior of CNPs changes according to their redox state<sup>1</sup>, which in turn depends on various factors including the environmental pH, size, surface charge, reactive crystalline facets, partial pressure of oxygen etc., it is pertinent to validate the activity and efficacy of these nano-formulations under circumstances mimicking the *in vivo* pathological conditions. Previous studies have shown that CNPs are capable of acting as both a SOD mimetic to scavenge superoxide radicals and a catalase mimetic to scavenge H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and that CNPs can act as “sensitizers” for radiotherapy<sup>48</sup>. In the present work, the CNPs were prepared by solution combustion method by varying the fuel-oxidizer ratio which controls the size and surface Ce<sup>3+</sup>/Ce<sup>4+</sup> ratio associated with the oxygen vacancies in the ceria lattice. In accordance with the various characterization techniques like XRD, TEM and Raman (Section 3), the obtained pure ceria of smaller size (CNP-6) possess higher surface defects/oxygen vacancy concentration which corroborates the presence large amount of reactive Ce<sup>3+</sup> surface sites compared to that of CNP-12.

To study overall tolerance of cells for the prepared CNPs, MTT assay was carried out under physiological and tumor micro-environment mimicking conditions (Fig. 5). We observed high IC<sub>50</sub> values for both the CNPs at physiological conditions (Normoxia/pH 7.4) indicating that the nanoparticles were well tolerated by the cells (Table 2). However, at tumor like conditions (Hypoxia/pH 6.5), the IC<sub>50</sub> values decreased which indicated that under such conditions, cells could not tolerate CNPs well. It is well known that, lowering pH shifts the redox state of ceria from Ce<sup>4+</sup> to Ce<sup>3+</sup> making them more reactive. Literature, though scant, also suggests that under conditions of low oxygen tension *in vitro* redox state changes from Ce<sup>4+</sup> to Ce<sup>3+</sup>. Shcherbakov *et.al*, explained the variation in the redox state of Ce<sup>3+</sup> and associated pro-oxidant [if E(CeO<sub>2</sub>) < E(H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>)], anti-oxidant [if E(CeO<sub>2</sub>) < E(H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>)] nature of CNP at different pH and oxygen conditions using an electrochemical method when the electrode potential as follows<sup>49</sup>.

$$E(CeO_{2}) = 1.66 + 0.0591(\log([CeO_{2}]/[Ce^{3+}]) - 4pH) \quad (9)$$

$$E(H_{2}O_{2}) = 0.695 + 0.02958(\log(pO_{2}/[H_{2}O_{2}]) - 2pH) \quad (10)$$

A large difference in the IC<sub>50</sub> value was observed for CNP-6 under normoxic and hypoxic conditions compared to that of CNP-12 (Table 2). This is an encouraging indication suggesting that these CNPs could have potential for selective targeting of the hypoxic core The difference in the IC<sub>50</sub> values of CNP-6 and CNP-12 at different pH conditions could be attributed to the differential regenerative capacity of CNPs during the reaction.

Further, the biomimetic peroxidase like activity of CNP-6 and CNP-12 under physiological and tumor micro-environment like conditions were analyzed *in vitro*. It is commonly observed that the elevated levels of hydrogen peroxide can be found in the cancer cells. The increased production of cellular H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> has been linked to metastasis and stabilization of hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF-α)<sup>50</sup>. The hydrogen peroxide can be detoxified using CNPs catalase and peroxidase mimetic activity. Pirmohamed *et.al*, demonstrated that the catalase activity of the CNP at physiological pH is excellent but gradually decreased at acidic pH conditions. Thus, activation of pH selective catalase and peroxidase mimetic activity of CeO<sub>2</sub> and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles have been reported<sup>24,51</sup>. Hence, it was decided to study the peroxidase like activity of the CNPs at physiological (pH 7.5) and hypoxic tumor core mimicking conditions (pH 6.5 and pH 4.0). A modified protocol reported by Fahimi *et al.*, was used for the assay<sup>34</sup>. The formation of deep brown color in the assay at different pH indicates the CNPs catalyzed oxidation of DAB substrates by CNPs. Our results showed that both the CNPs exhibited better peroxidase- like activity under hypoxic tumor microenvironment like conditions. The steady state kinetics analysis for the enzyme activity showed linearly dependent relationship between the initial reaction rate and the substrate concentration which is similar to that of natural enzyme (Fig. 6). Our results demonstrate that the reaction catalyzed by CNPs is in accordance with the typical Michaelis-Menten behavior. To cross-check any oxidase like activity of CNPs as reported<sup>52</sup>, CNP-6 and CNP-12 were incubated with DAB alone for 60 minutes under hypoxic and normoxic conditions. In either case, the assay did not develop any color indicating that the DAB was not oxidized by CNPs and hydrogen peroxide is required for the oxidation (data not shown).

The kinetics parameters K<sub>m</sub> and V<sub>max</sub> were obtained by the linear fitting of double reciprocal Lineweaver-Burk plot as shown in Fig. 6. Under hypoxic tumor like conditions, the K<sub>m</sub> value for CNP-6 was lower (1.87 mM) when compared to CNP-12 (12.45 mM). The lower K<sub>m</sub> value indicates higher affinity of the nanozymes towards the substrate. According to the available literature, enhancing the number of surface defects (Ce<sup>3+</sup> sites) on nanoceria increases the sensitivity of the peroxidase mimetic activity<sup>25</sup>, as the surface defects on CNPs are primarily the catalytic sites. Hence, a high fraction of surface Ce<sup>3+</sup> would correspond to a large number of surface defects, thereby imparting better catalytic activity to the CNPs<sup>38</sup>. This could explain our observations with CNP-6 which showed a better peroxidase like activity under hypoxic tumor microenvironment like conditions. The peroxidase activity of the ceria can be written as follows,

$$Ce^{3+} + H_{2}O_{2} + H^{+} \rightarrow Ce^{4+} + OH^{\bullet} + H_{2}O \quad (11)$$

$$OH^{\bullet} + H_{2}O_{2} \rightarrow HO_{2}^{\bullet} + H_{2}O \quad (12)$$

$$Ce^{4+} + HO_{2}^{\bullet} \rightarrow Ce^{3+} + O_{2} + H^{+} \quad (13)$$

From the above equations, the tumor like acidic conditions favor the regeneration of Ce<sup>3+</sup> redox state of the CNP thereby providing higher catalytic activity. CNP-12 on the other hand, due to their intrinsically low Ce<sup>3+</sup> /Ce<sup>4+</sup> were less reactive than CNP-6. Therefore the better peroxidase like activity of CNPs at tumor like pH is attributed to the higher surface defects (Ce<sup>3+</sup>/Ce<sup>4+</sup>) than at physiological pH *in vitro*.

Since in our experiments CNPs showed peroxidase like property, we further explored their role in alleviating oxidative stress by their peroxidase-like activity. Increased level of the MDA level has been a known indicator of oxidative stress<sup>53,54</sup>. MDA assay and TBARS assay were performed to assess the anti-oxidant behavior of CNPs. The results from MDA assay and TBARs assay confirmed that the ROS levels are increased under hypoxia and can be alleviated by treatment with CNPs, especially of small crystallite size. The observed reduction in oxidative stress by CNPs may be used to sensitize the GBM tumor cells which turn drug resistant owing to high intracellular ROS levels<sup>55</sup>. Comprehensive analysis of the entire data indicates that CNP-6 have better therapeutic potential when compared to CNP-12 and the same could be attributed to their differential surface charge regenerative property under studied conditions<sup>56</sup>.

In summary, our results clearly demonstrate that the peroxidase-like property of CNPs is modulated under physiological and hypoxic tumor like conditions. Nanozymes with the enhanced peroxidase like activity at hypoxic tumor like conditions are promising as they could reduce the ROS levels induced by hypoxia prevalent in tumor microenvironment. Since ROS is known to induce resistance to chemo/radiotherapy in tumors, by alleviating ROS in tumors, CNP based therapeutic modalities may sensitize GBM tumors towards better chemotherapeutic outcome.

**5. Conclusion**

Present study reports on the cytotoxicity and biomimetic peroxidase like activity of the cerium oxide nanoparticles (CNP) in hypoxic tumor micro environment (low pH-low O<sub>2</sub> tension) and normoxic conditions (pH 7.4). Overall, the CNPs were well tolerated well by the cells. CNPs exhibited anti-oxidant peroxidase like activity that increased under conditions mimicking hypoxic tumor core like conditions. This was further corroborated by the MDA assay which showed that these CNPs were able to bring down the ROS levels induced by the hypoxia exposure. Since moderately high ROS levels are known to induce drug resistance in tumors, these CNPs by virtue of their better anti-oxidant activity are expected to reduce the ROS levels, thereby “sensitizing” the GBM tumor cells for a better therapeutic outcome in future.
