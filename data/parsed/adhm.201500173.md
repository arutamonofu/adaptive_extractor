Recently, there are intense interests in the use of nanomaterials for a wide range of biomedical and biotechnological applications.<sup>[1]</sup> Among known nanomaterials, nanomaterials exhibiting inherent enzyme-like properties have been active area of research over the last decade.<sup>[2]</sup> Considering extraordinary stability of enzyme-mimetic nanomaterials under harsh environments such as high temperature and extreme pH compared with native enzymes,<sup>[3]</sup> point-of-care (POC) bioassays would be one of the most promising applications of enzyme-mimetic nanomaterials. Thus, enzyme-mimetic nanomaterial-based bioassay systems have been recently developed for ultrafast and ultrasensitive detection of analytes.<sup>[4]</sup> However, they require specialized equipments and complicated operations that will limit their applications. Integration of enzyme-mimetic nanomaterials into paper-based disposable platforms that are portable and easy-to-use would resolve such issues and enable enzyme-mimetic nanomaterials to be more widely used for POC applications.

Lateral flow immunoassay (LFIA) strips (**Figure 1a**) are one of the most widely used forms of paper-based POC bioassay devices for many applications including pregnancy tests, cardiac marker tests, food and environmental monitoring and defense against bioterrorism.<sup>[5]</sup> Liquid sample applied on a sample pad of a LIFA strip flows by capillary force to a conjugation pad loaded with nanoparticles (NPs, typically dark-colored NPs such as gold or carbon) conjugated with detection antibodies (Ab–NPs, Figure 1ai). In a conjugation pad, binding of target analytes in sample liquid to the Ab–NPs occurs to form analyte/Ab–NP complexes (Figure 1aii). On a test pad, analyte/Ab–NP complexes will be captured by antibody immobilized on a test line (T-line) by forming sandwich-like structures (Figure 1aiii) while the rest of Ab–NPs will be captured by antibody immobilized on a control line (C-line, Figure 1aiv). As a result, two lines (both T-line and C-line) will be visible by the naked eye if the concentration of target analytes is sufficiently high; otherwise, only one line (C-line) will by visible. It is extremely simple to use LFIA strips; the presence or absence of a target analyte can be visually inspected within 5–20 min after a drop of sample is dispensed onto a sample pad. However, applications of LFIA have been limited due to its low sensitivity.<sup>[5b,6]</sup> Sensitivity has been increased using various signal-enhancement methods based on fluorescence,<sup>[7]</sup> chemiluminescence,<sup>[8]</sup> and heat generation by plasmonic nanoparticles;<sup>[9]</sup> but the modified LFIAs require specialized devices to detect the signal, so their POC applications are also limited.

To overcome these limitations, we integrated enzyme-mimetic nanoparticles into LFIA strips and developed a simple and sensitive bioassay system (**Figure 1b**). The enzyme-mimetic NPs use a peroxidase-like reaction to amplify signals by generating colors on the lines of LFIA strips (Step 2 in Figure 1b), thereby achieve much higher sensitivity than conventional LFIA and make the signal detectable by the naked eye. Furthermore, quantitative information of the analytes can be readily extracted from digital images of LFIA strips acquired by a smart cellular phone, which is being ubiquitously used in the world.

To be integrated into LFIA strips, the diameter of NPs should be small enough (typically less than 40 nm<sup>[5b]</sup>) to efficiently migrate through the porous cellulose nitrate membranes of LFIA strips. In addition, for maximum enzyme-like activity, the total surface area of NPs must be maximized. To satisfy these criteria, Hierarchically structured Pt nanoparticles (H-Pt NPs) with 30-nm diameter and large surface area were synthesized using a seed-mediated growth method<sup>[10]</sup> in aqueous solution and characterized using X-ray diffraction (XRD), a scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and a high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HR-TEM). The XRD pattern (**Figure 2a**) of the synthesized H-Pt NPs had diffraction peaks at 2θ = 39.3, 45.7, 66.6, and 80.1, which were assigned to the reflections of (111), (200), (220), and (311) planes of platinum (JCPDS, no. 88–2343), respectively. Based on the broadening of the XRD peaks, the average crystallite size calculated using the Debye–Scherrer equation was 6.24 nm. The (111)/(200) peak ratio of the Pt NPs was 1.5 times higher than that of the bulk Pt structure, meaning H-Pt NPs exhibited (111)-oriented growth.<sup>[11]</sup> The synthesized H-Pt NPs were monodispersed (**Figure 2b,c**) with a diameter of ≈30 nm (**Figure 2c**), and had rough, porous surfaces (**Figure 2d**) with 0.212 nm spacing between fringes, which is consistent with the presence of (111) planes (inset, Figure 2d). Considering rough and porous surfaces, the 30 nm H-Pt NPs may be aggregates of smaller Pt NP crystallites. Indeed, the crystallite size of H-Pt NPs calculated from the XRD peaks (6.24 nm) was about 5 times smaller than the diameter of H-Pt NPs measured from the HR-TEM images (30 nm), further supporting our hypothesis.

Enzymatic activity of H-Pt NPs was compared with horseradish peroxidase (HRP), an enzyme that is widely used in immunoassays for signal amplification, by performing the peroxidase-catalyzed 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) oxidation reaction in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. As shown in Figure S1 (Supporting Information), the solution was much deeper blue after reaction with H-Pt NPs than with HRP when the concentration of NPs and HRP were the same ($5 \times 10^{-11}$ M); therefore H-Pt NPs have significantly higher catalytic activity than does HRP. To quantitatively assess peroxidase-like activity of H-Pt NPs, the initial reaction rate of TMB oxidation reaction mediated by H-Pt NPs was measured with various [TMB], where [·] indicates “concentration of”. The kinetic data were described well by the Michaelis–Menten equation (Figure S2, Supporting Information). Kinetic parameters of H-Pt NPs were compared with those of HRP and commercially-available Pt NPs measured in our previous study<sup>[12]</sup> (**Table 1**). $K_{m}$ of H-Pt NPs was ≈2 times higher than that of HRP; this means that TMB binding affinity of H-Pt NPs is half that of HRP. However, $k_{cat}$ value of H-Pt NPs was ≈14 times higher than that of HRP; this means that H-Pt NPs convert TMB ≈14 times faster than does HRP. Therefore, in the presence of sufficient amounts of substrate, the catalytic reaction of H-Pt NPs would be an order of magnitude faster than that of HRP. Fast reaction rate is essential for urgent POC diagnosis applications. Furthermore, the H-Pt NPs exhibited ≈50 times higher catalytic efficiency per particle ($k_{cat}/K_{m}$) than commercially-available Pt NPs (10 nm) used in our previous study.<sup>[4]</sup> Catalytic efficiency per mass of H-Pt NPs was also twice as high as that of the commercially-available Pt NPs, possibly due to the rough and porous surfaces of H-Pt NPs.

Because H-Pt NPs are inorganic, they are likely to be more stable than HRP in harsh environments. To test this possibility, H-Pt NPs and HRP were incubated at a wide range of temperatures ($T = 4, 15, 22, 34, 45, 60, and 75 °C$) and pHs (2–12) for 3 h, then their catalytic activities were measured using a colorimetric TMB oxidation reaction. Relative activities of H-Pt NPs or HRP treated with various conditions with respect to untreated H-Pt NPs or HRP were calculated and plotted in **Figure 2e,f**. The H-Pt NPs retained good catalytic activity over the entire ranges of $T$ (**Figure 2e**) and pH (**Figure 2f**), whereas that of HRP decreased significantly at $T > 45 °C$, and at pH < 6 or pH > 12. Taken together, the evidence indicates that H-Pt NPs had higher enzymatic activity and better stability than did HRP.

With these promising properties, next H-Pt NPs were integrated into LFIA strips (**Figure 1b**). Commercially-available LFIA strips for the detection of human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), which are available in pharmacy for pregnancy tests, were modified and used. First, anti-hCG antibodies (Abs) were attached on the surfaces of H-Pt NPs to form Ab-H-Pt NPs. Then, conjugation pads of the conventional LFIA, which contains Au NPs conjugated with anti-hCG Abs, were replaced with conjugation pads loaded with Ab-H-Pt NPs. In this way, performance of H-Pt NPs for sensitivity enhancement of LFIAs can be easily assessed without fabricating entire LFIA strips, and sensitivity of H-Pt NP-based LFIAs can be directly compared with that of commercially-available Au NP-based LFIA chips. Two-step sandwich immunoassays (**Figure 1b**) were performed using Ab-H-Pt NP-loaded LFIA chips. In Step 1, a small droplet of sample liquid containing hCG was applied to the sample pad. The sample liquid flows through the conjugation pad containing Ab-H-Pt NPs; during this process, hCG binds to Ab-H-Pt NPs. The hCG/Ab-H-Pt NPs complex is immobilized in the T-line by antibodies that capture hCG, whereas free Ab-H-Pt NPs is immobilized in the C-line by antibodies that bind to anti-hCG antibodies immobilized on Ab-HPt NPs. In Step 2, a small droplet containing H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and 3-amino-9-ethylcarbazole (AEC), another substrate that generates red precipitate when oxidized by peroxidases, was added to the test pads and allowed to react for a defined duration. Finally, images of strips were acquired using a smart cellular phone and quantitative information was extracted by image processing (Figure S3, Supporting Information).

Representative digital images of LFIA strips before and 5 min after the addition of AEC were shown in **Figure 3a**. The grey-colored T-line and C-line were each converted to a red line, confirming that successful enzymatic reaction had occurred on the strips. In addition to the color change, the intensity of T-line was also ≈3 times increased in this particular condition ([hCG] = 10 ng mL<sup>−1</sup>, [AEC] = $3800 \times 10^{-6}$ M) further confirming signal amplification by enzyme-like reaction. To assess enzymatic performance of H-Pt NPs on the strip, kinetic assays were performed on the LFIA strips. First, 10 ng mL<sup>−1</sup> of hCG was added to sample pads, then various [AEC] were dispensed onto test pads. Digital images of the test pads were acquired using a smart cellular phone every 1 min after AEC addition, and the intensity of the T-line was plotted over time (Figure S4, Supporting Information). For the first 5 min, the intensity of T-line increased linearly for all [AEC] examined, so initial rates of the enzymatic reaction were extracted from the slopes these line. Similar to TMB oxidation, reaction kinetics data of AEC oxidation on LFIA strips were fitted well by Michaelis–Menten equation (**Figure 3b**). AEC underwent oxidation by H-Pt NPs immobilized on the T-line, so [H-Pt NP] for AEC oxidation could not be determined; therefore only $K_{m}$ values can be calculated by the equation fitting. The $K_{m}$ value of AEC oxidation by H-Pt NPs on LFIA strip was $1.328 \times 10^{-3}$ M, close to that of TMB oxidation by H-Pt NPs in solution ($1.361 \times 10^{-3}$ M). These results confirm that Ab H-Pt NPs were successfully integrated into LFIA strips to intensify signals by enzyme-like reaction-mediated signal amplification.

Finally, we assessed the detection sensitivity of the LFIA strips based on H-Pt NPs. Various [hCG] (0.1–9 ng mL<sup>−1</sup>) were applied to the sample pads of H-Pt NP-based LFIA strips or to commercially-available Au NP-based LFIA strips. For H-Pt NP-based LFIA strips, sodium acetate buffer ($0.1 \times 10^{-3}$ M, pH 4.5) was applied 5 min after the addition of hCG, to wash away unbound H-Pt NPs and to provide acidic environments in the strips for the peroxidase-like reaction. Then, $3.8 \times 10^{-3}$ M AEC solution in sodium acetate buffer was added to the test pads to amplify the signals; digital images were acquired 5 min after the addition of AEC using a smart cellular phone (top panel of **Figure 4a**). For conventional Au NP-based LFIA strips, digital images of the strips were acquired 15 min after the addition of hCG (bottom panel of **Figure 4a**). The H-Pt NP-based LFIA strips were much more sensitive to hCG than were the Au NP-based LFIA strips: the lowest [hCG] visible by the naked eye was 0.3 ng mL<sup>−1</sup> for H-Pt NP-based LFIA strips, but 9 ng mL<sup>−1</sup> for Au NP-based LFIA strips. Typically, digital image processing provides better sensitivity than naked-eye examination.<sup>[13]</sup> Therefore, digital images acquired by a smart cellular phone were processed (Figure S3, Supporting Information) to extract the intensity information of each strip. To compensate for potential variations in intensities depending on acquisition conditions such as light illumination and camera setting, the background was subtracted, and signal was quantified using the ratio (T/C) of the intensity of T-line to the intensity of C-line. T/C of H-Pt NP-based LFIAs and conventional Au NP-based LFIAs increased linearly with [hCG] (**Figure 4b**). The limit of detection (LOD) was defined as [hCG] that corresponds to the T/C value of (average background + 3(standard deviation of background)).<sup>[14]</sup> LODs were 0.2 ng mL<sup>−1</sup> for H-Pt NP-based LFIAs and 3.7 ng mL<sup>−1</sup> for Au NP LFIAs. These results agree well with the naked-eye observations. Taken together, H-Pt NP-based LFIA strips were ≈20 times sensitive than conventional Au NP-based LFIA strips. In addition, H-Pt NP-mediated coloration reaction was not altered by biological substances in urine or serum (Figure S5, Supporting Information), meaning H-Pt NP-based LFIAs can be used for real biological samples.

In summary, we demonstrated that LFIA strips integrated with H-Pt NPs could detect analytes within 15 min by easy operation and sensitivity ≈20 times higher than that of conventional LFIA strips. In addition, by processing digital images of LFIA strips acquired by a smart cellular phone, which is being ubiquitously available, quantification of analytes could be performed. Considering that H-Pt NPs stored in harsh environments maintained peroxidase-like activity, the H-Pt NP-loaded LFIA strips developed in this study will be useful for many POC bioassay applications including military/biodefense applications<sup>[15]</sup> and disease diagnostics in tropical countries.<sup>[16]</sup>
