# Detection of mercury ions based on mercury-induced switching of enzyme-like activity of platinum/gold nanoparticles†

Chao-Wei Tseng,<sup>a</sup> Hsiang-Yu Chang,<sup>a</sup> Jia-Yaw Chang<sup>b</sup> and Chih-Ching Huang*<sup>a</sup>

Received 4th July 2012, Accepted 3rd September 2012
DOI: 10.1039/c2nr31716h

In this study, bimetallic platinum/gold nanoparticles (Pt/Au NPs) were found to exhibit peroxidase-like activity, and the deposition of mercury was found to switch the enzymatic activity to a catalase-like activity. Based on this phenomenon, we developed a new method for detecting mercury ions through their deposition on bimetallic Pt/Au NPs to switch the catalytic activity of Pt/Au NPs. Pt/Au NPs could be easily prepared through reduction of Au<sup>3+</sup> and Pt<sup>4+</sup> by sodium citrate in a one-pot synthesis. The peroxidase catalytic activity of the Pt/Au NPs was controlled by varying the ratios of Pt to Au. The Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs (prepared with a [Au<sup>3+</sup>]/[Pt<sup>4+</sup>] molar ratio of 9.0/1.0) showed excellent oxidation catalysis for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-mediated oxidation of Amplex Red (AR) to resorufin. The oxidized product of AR, resorufin, fluoresces more strongly (excitation/emission wavelength maxima *ca.* 570/585 nm) than AR alone. The peroxidase catalytic activity of Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs was switched to catalase-like activity in the presence of mercury ions in a 5.0 mM tris(hydroxymethyl)aminomethane (Tris)-borate solution (pH 7.0) through the deposition of Hg on the particle surfaces owing to the strong Hg–Au metallic bond. The catalytic activity of Hg–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs is superior (by at least 5-fold) to that of natural catalase (from bovine liver). Under optimal solution conditions [5.0 mM Tris-borate (pH 7.0), H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (50 mM), and AR (10 μM)] and in the presence of the masking agents polyacrylic acid and tellurium nanowires, the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs allowed the selective detection of inorganic mercury (Hg<sup>2+</sup>) and methylmercury ions (MeHg<sup>+</sup>) at concentrations as low as several nanomolar. This simple, fast, and cost-effective system enabled selective determination of the spiked concentrations of Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup> in tap, pond, and stream waters.

## 1. Introduction

Natural peroxidase enzymes are protein-based enzymes that act as catalysts in various biological processes by donating electrons for binding to specific substrate substances.<sup>1</sup> In particular, peroxidase enzymes degrade hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>), which is a naturally occurring byproduct of oxygen metabolism in the body.<sup>2</sup> Catalase is an antioxidant enzyme found in living organisms. It catalyzes the decomposition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (formed in eukaryotic cells as a byproduct of various oxidase and superoxide dismutase reactions) to water and oxygen (2H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> → 2H<sub>2</sub>O + O<sub>2</sub>).<sup>3</sup> Catalase is present in high concentrations in the liver, kidneys, and erythrocytes, and its expression is regulated at various levels.<sup>3</sup> It is known that catalase and peroxidase enzymes can catalyze oxidative and decomposition reactions under very mild and favorable biological conditions.<sup>4</sup> However, the widespread application of these enzymes is restricted by their intrinsic properties such as easy denaturing under extreme pH conditions and high temperatures as well as their digestibility by protease.<sup>5</sup> In addition, high cost and rigorous storage requirements also limit their applications.<sup>5</sup> Thus, the development of artificial enzyme mimetics has been attracting much attention. Although numerous peroxidase and catalase mimetics including hematin, cyclodextrin, porphyrin, DNAzyme, molecularly imprinted hydrogels, and functional polymers have been applied in different fields, the catalytic activities of these molecules are much lower compared with those of natural enzymes.<sup>6</sup>

Nanomaterials have become one of the most interesting functional materials owing to their unique size-, shape- and composite-dependent electronic, optical, and magnetic properties.<sup>7</sup> Recently, the increasing availability of nanomaterials has created widespread interest in their use as high-efficiency catalysts because of their large surface-to-volume ratio and high surface energy.<sup>8</sup> After the first report demonstrating that Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> magnetic nanoparticles (NPs) possess intrinsic peroxidase-like activity similar to horseradish peroxidase (HRP),<sup>8a</sup> cerium oxide NPs,<sup>9</sup> Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs,<sup>10</sup> carbon nanotubes,<sup>11</sup> graphene oxide (GO),<sup>12</sup> FeS nanosheets,<sup>13</sup> cubic Pt nanocrystals,<sup>14</sup> V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> nanowires (NWs),<sup>15</sup> calcined layered double hydroxides,<sup>16</sup> positively charged gold NPs (cysteamine-modified Au NPs),<sup>17</sup> carbon nanodots,<sup>18</sup> and BSA-stabilized Au nanoclusters<sup>19</sup> have also been found to possess peroxidase-like activities and have demonstrated potential in biomedical and environmental detection. More recently, bimetallic NPs and hybrid nanomaterials such as hemin–graphene oxide nanosheets,<sup>12a</sup> AgM (M = Au, Pd, Pt),<sup>20</sup> carbon nitride dots,<sup>21</sup> CoFe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>,<sup>22</sup> BiFeO<sub>3</sub>,<sup>23</sup> iron-substituted SAB-15 (Fe-SBA-15),<sup>24</sup> Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs–carbon nitride nanotube hybrids,<sup>25a</sup> iron phosphate (FePO<sub>4</sub>) microflowers,<sup>25b</sup> ferritin–Pt NPs,<sup>25c</sup> Au@Pt or Au@Pd/Pt (core@shell) nanorods (NRs),<sup>26</sup> Au NPs/GO and Au@Hg NPs<sup>27</sup> have been synthesized to further improve peroxidase activity and stability by their specific synergetic effects, thus effectively reducing the poisoning effect. However, the synthesis of some of these hybrid and bimetallic nanomaterials has been complicated because most of them require more than two steps of preparation. Accordingly, developing a new, simple, and one-pot preparation of bimetallic NPs with peroxidase-like activity is highly desired. Moreover, although cerium oxide, Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, ferritin–Pt NPs, carbon nanodots, and Au@Pt NRs have recently been reported to exhibit more than one enzyme-like activity, the enzymatic activities of these nanomaterials (*e.g.*, Au@Pt NRs, carbon nanodots, cerium oxide NPs) were not tuneable.<sup>9,10,18,25c,26</sup>

Peroxidase-like nanomaterials have exhibited suitable performances in the traditional enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay for the analysis of specific proteins as well as in other colorimetric assays for the detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, glucose, glutathione, melamine, xanthine, and single nucleotide polymorphism by oxidizing the substrates *o*-phenylenediamine (OPD), 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB), and 2,2′-azinobis(3-ethylbenzthiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) diammonium salt (ABTS) to colored products.<sup>8–26</sup> Almost all enzyme-like nanomaterials reported to date are based on a colorimetric method. In the present study, based on a fluorescence assay that exhibited superior sensitivity accompanying the formation of a fluorescent oxidized product,<sup>28</sup> a fluorogenic substrate is employed to screen NP-based enzyme-like activity. To the best of our knowledge, artificial peroxidase-like NPs have not been applied to heavy metal ion sensing by switching their enzyme-like activity until now. Although mercury ions (Hg<sup>2+</sup>) have been demonstrated to stimulate and inhibit the peroxidase activities of Au NPs and Au@Pt NRs, respectively, the detection of Hg<sup>2+</sup> in real samples is limited because of their poor sensitivity, selectivity, and stability.<sup>26b,27b</sup>

Mercury and its organometallic compounds (mainly methylmercury) are the most toxic pollutants in aquatic ecosystems.<sup>29</sup> Inorganic Hg<sup>2+</sup>, which is water soluble, has a strong affinity for many inorganic and organic ligands, especially those containing sulfur atoms. They are caustic and carcinogenic materials that exhibit high cellular toxicities.<sup>30</sup> Methylmercury ions (MeHg<sup>+</sup>) are produced by specific bacterial organisms in surface waters; the bacteria convert inorganic mercury into MeHg<sup>+</sup>, which poses a significant threat to human health.<sup>31</sup> For example, MeHg<sup>+</sup> is a potent neurotoxin that impairs the central nervous system and in severe cases, causes irreversible damage to the brain.<sup>32</sup> Consequently, the US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has set the maximum allowable level of Hg<sup>2+</sup> in drinking water at 2 ppb and the reference dose of MeHg<sup>+</sup> at 0.1 μg kg<sup>−1</sup> d<sup>−1</sup> (*i.e.*, 0.1 μg of MeHg<sup>+</sup> per day for each kilogram of a person’s body mass).<sup>33</sup> Therefore, the development of reliable and rapid routine methodologies for the determination of Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup> is an attractive challenge.

In the present investigation, we prepared a series of bimetallic Pt/Au NPs whose peroxidase activity could be controlled by varying the ratios of Pt to Au. The Pt/Au NPs were prepared through citrate-mediated reduction of HAuCl<sub>4</sub> and K<sub>2</sub>PtCl<sub>6</sub> in a one-pot solution. The fluorogenic substrate Amplex Red (denoted as AR) was used to screen the catalytic ability of as-prepared Pt/Au NPs. The AR reagent is a colorless substrate that reacts with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with a 1 : 1 stoichiometry to produce highly fluorescent resorufin (excitation/emission maxima ~570/585 nm).<sup>28</sup> AR–Pt/Au NPs were employed as sensors for the analysis of mercury ions in aqueous solution. The operation of this probe is based on the fact that mercury ions are deposited on the surfaces of Pt/Au NPs as a result of strong metallophilic interactions, subsequently inducing the inhibition of Pt/Au NP catalysis toward AR (see Scheme 1). Moreover, we found that the enzymatic property of the Pt/Au NPs was switched from peroxidase-like to catalase-like activities after reaction with mercury ions. We have evaluated the roles of the Pt/Au ratio, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and AR concentrations, pH, and masking agents [polyacrylic acid (PAA) and tellurium nanowires (Te NWs)] in determination of the selectivity and sensitivity of the AR–Pt/Au NP probe for detecting mercury ions (Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup>). In addition, we validated the practicality of this approach through the analysis of mercury ions in natural water samples (in this case, stream, pond, and tap waters).

## 2. Experimental

### 2.1 Chemicals

Acetic acid, tris(hydroxymethyl)aminomethane (Tris), trisodium citrate, hydrochloric acid, boric acid, and all metal salts used in this study were purchased from Mallinckrodt Baker (Phillipsburg, New Jersey, USA). Hydrogen tetrachloroaurate(III) trihydrate (HAuCl<sub>4</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O), sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), potassium hexachloroplatinate (K<sub>2</sub>PtCl<sub>6</sub>), and catalase (from bovine liver; 2000–5000 units per mg protein) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, Missouri, USA). One unit decomposes 1.0 μmol of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> per minute at pH 7.0 at 25 °C, whereas the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration decreases from 10.3 to 9.2 mM, which is measured by the rate of decrease of *A*<sub>240</sub>. AR was purchased from Invitrogen (Eugene, Oregon, USA). Phosphoric acid, sodium phosphate monobasic monohydrate, sodium phosphate dibasic anhydrous, and sodium phosphate tribasic dodecahydrate obtained from J. T. Baker (Phillipsburg, New Jersey, USA) were used to prepare a phosphate buffer (5.0 mM, pH 3–11). Milli-Q ultrapure water was used in each experiment. A Tris-borate (50 mM) buffer was prepared from a 50 mM Tris solution adjusted to pH 7.0 with 200 mM boric acid. All other reagents used in this study were purchased from J. T. Baker. All solvents and chemicals were of the highest purity commercially available and were used without further purification. A stock solution of inorganic metal ions (1.0 mM) was prepared in 10 mM HNO<sub>3</sub> and diluted to 1 nM–100 μM in ultrapure water. Methylmercury (MeHg<sup>+</sup>; 100 mM) ions were prepared in ethanol and diluted to 1 nM–100 μM in ultrapure water.

### 2.2 Synthesis and characterization of Pt/Au NPs

Pt/Au NPs were prepared through citrate-mediated reduction of HAuCl<sub>4</sub> and K<sub>2</sub>PtCl<sub>6</sub>. Various molar ratios of Pt to Au ions at a constant total ion concentration (1.0 mM) were brought to a vigorous boil while stirring in a round bottom flask fitted with a reflux condenser, and trisodium citrate (4.0 mM) was rapidly added to the solution. The solution was heated for an additional 15 min while the color changed from pale yellow to deep red or blue. The solution was cooled to room temperature with continuous stirring.

The sizes of the Pt/Au NPs were verified by high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HR-TEM; Tecnai 20 G2 S-Twin TEM, Philips/FEI, Hillsboro, Oregon, USA). The samples for TEM measurements were prepared by placing 20 μL of the Pt/Au NPs solution on a carbon-coated Cu grid, followed by drying at room temperature. Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) analysis of the Pt/Au NPs using a 0.7 nm-diameter electron probe was employed to determine their chemical identities. A Synergy 4 Multi-Mode Microplate Reader (Biotek Instruments, Winooski, Vermont, USA) was used to measure the absorbance of the Pt/Au NPs solution. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed with a PHI 5000 VersaProbe (Physical Electronics, Minnesota, USA).

For simplicity, we denoted the concentration of as-prepared Pt/Au NPs as 1× (~20 nM). The mixture of 900 μL DI water and 100 μL Pt/Au NPs (1×) is denoted as 0.1× Pt/Au NPs. Prior to surface-assisted laser desorption/ionization time-of-flight mass spectrometry (SALDI-TOF MS) measurements, an aliquot (10 μL) of the Au NPs (1×) or Pt/Au NPs (1×) was cast onto a stainless steel 384-well target plate and dried in air at room temperature. Mass spectrometry experiments were performed in the reflectron positive-ion mode using an Autoflex III MALDI-TOF mass spectrometer (Bruker Daltonics, Bremen, Germany). The samples were irradiated with a Nd:YAG laser (output: 355 nm) at 100 Hz. Ions produced by laser desorption were energetically stabilized during a delayed extraction period of 30 ns and then accelerated through the TOF chamber in the reflection mode before entering the mass analyzer. The available accelerating voltages ranged from +20 to −20 kV. The instruments were calibrated with Au clusters ([Au<sub>*x*</sub>]<sup>+</sup>; *x* = 1–3). A total of 1000 pulsed laser shots were applied to obtain signals from 10 MALDI target positions under a laser power density of 3.82 × 10<sup>4</sup> W cm<sup>−2</sup>. Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) with an Elan 6000 instrument (Perkin-Elmer) revealed the contents of mercury atoms/ions on the Pt/Au NP surfaces.

The interactions of AR (500 nM) with Au NPs or Pt/Au NPs (1.5 nM) were also investigated. After incubation for 1 h at room temperature, the mixtures (500 μL) were centrifuged (RCF 30 000g; 20 min) to remove excess AR. The supernatants (400 μL) were mixed with horseradish peroxidase (HRP; 1 mU mL<sup>−1</sup>, 50 μL) and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0.4 mM, 50 μL). After incubation for 30 min, the mixtures were subjected to fluorescence measurements, with excitation and emission wavelengths of 540 and 585 nm, respectively.

### 2.3 Preparation of tellurium nanowires

We slowly added TeO<sub>2</sub> powder (16 mg) to N<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O (10 mL) in a beaker at room temperature under constant magnetic stirring. The powder was completely dissolved within 10 min. The color of the solution sequentially changed from colorless to amber, purple, and eventually blue within 1 h. The blue color indicated the formation of Te NWs. The solution was diluted fivefold with 10 mM SDS and then stirred for another 30 min. The average diameter and length of the Te NWs were 12.1 (±1.9) and 456 (±110) nm, respectively. The corresponding concentration of as-prepared Te NWs was 3.0 nM.

### 2.4 AR–Pt/Au NPs probe for mercury ion sensing

Aliquots (400 μL) of the Tris-borate solution (5 mM, pH 7.0) containing Pt/Au NPs (0.01×) and Hg<sup>2+</sup> or MeHg<sup>+</sup> (1.0 nM to 1 μM) were equilibrated at room temperature for 30 min. H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (50 mM) and AR (10 μM) were then added and left to react for 2 h prior to fluorescence measurements with excitation at 540 nm (Synergy 4 Multi-Mode Microplate Reader). In this paper, the final concentrations of the species are provided.

### 2.5 Analysis of real samples

Water samples collected from a pond or stream on the campus of National Taiwan Ocean University and the local tap water were filtered through a 0.2 μm membrane. For the detection of mercury ions, aliquots of the water samples (500 μL) were spiked with standard mercury ion solutions (1.0 nM to 1.0 μM). The spiked samples were then diluted to 1.0 mL with Tris-borate (pH 7.0) containing AR (10 μM) and Pt/Au NPs (0.01×) and were left to react for 2 h prior to fluorescence measurements with excitation at 540 nm. All spiked samples were also analyzed by ICP-MS.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1 Preparation of catalytic Pt/Au NPs

Pt/Au NPs were synthesized by simple citrate-mediated reduction of HAuCl<sub>4</sub> and K<sub>2</sub>PtCl<sub>6</sub> in a one-pot solution. We obtained different sizes of Pt/Au NPs upon varying the [Au<sup>3+</sup>]/[Pt<sup>4+</sup>] molar ratio from 9.5/0.5 to 7.0/3.0. TEM images (Fig. 1 and Fig. S1†) indicated that the Au NPs and Pt/Au NPs prepared from sodium citrate (4.0 mM) at [Au<sup>3+</sup>]/[Pt<sup>4+</sup>] molar ratios of 10/0, 9.5/0.5, 9.0/1.0, 8.0/2.0, and 7.0/3.0 had average diameters of 13.3 (±0.6), 13.0 (±0.5), 11.6 (±0.7), 7.2 (±1.2), and 6.5 (±1.7) nm (calculated from 100 counts of each type of NP), respectively. Thus, as the [Pt<sup>4+</sup>]/[Au<sup>3+</sup>] molar ratio increased, the sizes of the Pt/Au NPs decreased, primarily because of the higher seeding rate in the presence of Pt<sup>4+</sup>.<sup>34</sup> The branched structure became more condensed at a [Au<sup>3+</sup>]/[Pt<sup>4+</sup>] ratio of 7/3 (Fig. S1, ESI†). This is because the growth induced by Pt had a different spatial orientation.<sup>35,36</sup> The compositions of these Au and Pt/Au NPs measured by ICP-MS were 100/0, 95.05/4.95, 90.09/9.91, 80.15/19.85, and 70.21/29.79, which are very similar to the added [Au<sup>3+</sup>]/[Pt<sup>4+</sup>] ratios of 10/0, 9.5/0.5, 9.0/1.0, 8.0/2.0, and 7.0/3.0, respectively. Therefore, for simplicity, these as-prepared Pt/Au NPs were denoted as Pt<sub>0.05</sub>/Au NPs, Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs, Pt<sub>0.2</sub>/Au NPs, and Pt<sub>0.3</sub>/Au NPs that were prepared at the [Au<sup>3+</sup>]/[Pt<sup>4+</sup>] molar ratios of 9.5/0.5, 9.0/1.0, 8.0/2.0, and 7.0/3.0, respectively.

The corresponding UV/Vis absorption spectra of these as-prepared Au and Pt/Au NPs indicate that the typical surface plasmon resonance (SPR) bands of the Au and Pt/Au NPs were centered from 520 to 575 nm (Fig. 2). The broader band and larger red shift of Pt<sub>0.3</sub>/Au NPs were due to their higher Pt ratio, larger size distribution, branched structure, and aggregation.<sup>36</sup> We noted that at room temperature, some particles readily settled in the Pt<sub>0.3</sub>/Au NPs solution after two days. To investigate catalytic activity, we chose AR, which has been used as a fluorogenic substrate for the demonstration of the oxidation activities of numerous natural oxidase and peroxidase enzymes in the presence of O<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, respectively.<sup>37</sup> H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was an electron acceptor of the Pt/Au NPs during the oxidation of AR with an 1 : 1 stoichiometry that yielded a highly colored (extinction coefficient: 54 000 cm<sup>−1</sup> M<sup>−1</sup>) and fluorescent (quantum yield: 0.83) resorufin (7-hydroxy-3*H*-phenoxazin-3-one) (excitation/emission maxima = 570/585 nm).<sup>37</sup> Pt/Au NPs likely catalyzed a one-electron oxidation of a non-fluorescent AR to form a non-fluorescent AR radical. Two radical molecules subsequently underwent an enzyme-independent dismutation reaction to form fluorescent resorufin. In a control experiment without Au NPs or Pt/Au NPs in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and an AR solution, negligible fluorescence formation was observed (data not shown). This result indicates that our NPs behave similarly to peroxidase toward typical peroxidase substrates. The NP-based peroxidase and oxidase activities in previous reports were mainly composed of metal ion compounds on their surfaces, in which the enzyme-like activity was highly dependent on the valence (oxidation) variations of metal ions.<sup>8–26,38</sup> Fig. 3 reveals that the catalytic activities of the Au NPs and Pt/Au NPs decreased in the order Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs ≈ Pt<sub>0.05</sub>/Au NPs > Pt<sub>0.2</sub>/Au NPs > Pt<sub>0.3</sub>/Au NPs > Au NPs. The fluorescence intensity developed by the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs is almost 40-fold that by the Au NPs. We noted that as-prepared Pt/Au NPs aggregated easily when prepared from higher molar ratio of [Pt<sup>4+</sup>]/[Au<sup>3+</sup>]. Owing to decreases in the surface area, active sites, and insufficient Au atoms or ions on the particle surface, the Pt/Au NPs prepared from higher molar ratios of [Pt<sup>4+</sup>]/[Au<sup>3+</sup>] (>0.1) cause a decrease in their catalytic activity. We also investigated the interactions between the AR and Au NPs or Pt/Au NPs. At constant concentrations of AR (500 nM) and the Au NPs or Pt/Au NPs (1.5 nM), we determined the number of AR molecules per Au NP, Pt<sub>0.05</sub>/Au NPs, Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs, Pt<sub>0.2</sub>/Au NPs, and Pt<sub>0.3</sub>/Au NPs (*n* = 3) as 295 ± 11, 290 ± 12, 302 ± 14, 220 ± 8, and 161 ± 10, respectively. The interaction between AR molecules and Au NPs or Pt/Au NPs were controlled by a combination of electrostatic and π–π interactions between the negative citrates moieties and the positive groups of the dye. In addition, the coordinate interaction between AR and Au NPs or Pt/Au NPs through N–Au interactions could not be excluded. Increasing the [Pt<sup>4+</sup>]/[Au<sup>3+</sup>] molar ratio leads to a decrease in the binding affinity of AR with Pt/Au NPs that is probably due to the smaller particle size, ease of aggregation and smaller Au fraction. Although Au NPs show a high affinity for AR, the lack of various valence (oxidation) states of Au<sup>+</sup>/Au<sup>0</sup> and Pt<sup>0</sup> on the particle surfaces limits their catalytic activity.

From high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HR-TEM) measurements, the calculated lattice spacing of the (111) plane of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs was 0.231 nm (Fig. S2, ESI†), which lies between those of pure Pt and Au, indicating the formation of an alloy. The X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs (Fig. S3b, ESI†) show a face-centered-cubic (fcc) structural characterization that exhibited diffraction peaks of (111), (200), (220), and (311) at 2θ values of 38.5, 44.7, 66.0, and 78.4, respectively. In contrast, the XRD peaks of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs were broader and shifted slightly to higher 2θ values, implying that the smaller particles incorporate Pt atoms into the Au fcc lattice.<sup>39</sup> No Pt metal peaks appeared (Fig. S3b†), indicating that bimetallic nanostructures or alloys were formed.<sup>40</sup> Scanning transmission electron microscopy/energy dispersive X-ray mapping (STEM–EDS, Fig. S4, ESI†) also shows that Pt and Au coexisted and were well dispersed in the NPs, which is in agreement with the HR-TEM and XRD results. We confirmed that Pt/Au alloys were well formed on the surface of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs by performing highly reproducible SALDI-MS analyses. The peaks observed (Fig. S5, ESI†) at *m/z* 390.89, 391.89, and 392.89 corresponding to [<sup>196.93</sup>Au + <sup>193.96</sup>Pt]<sup>+</sup>, [<sup>196.93</sup>Au + <sup>194.96</sup>Pt]<sup>+</sup>, and [<sup>196.93</sup>Au + <sup>195.96</sup>Pt]<sup>+</sup> ions, respectively, strongly support the formation of Pt/Au alloys on the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NP surfaces. The X-ray photoelectron spectra (Fig. S6, ESI†) of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs show a Au 4f<sub>7/2</sub> peak of 84.3 eV between 83.9 (Au<sup>0</sup>) and 85.0 eV (Au<sup>+</sup>) as well as a Pt 4f<sub>7/2</sub> peak of 71.5 eV (Pt<sup>0</sup>), which reveals that a Au atom/ion and a Pt atom coexisted on the particle surface. It is well known that several metal ions such as Pt and Au ions may catalyze redox reactions such as H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and luminol. It has been suggested that nanoparticles transfer electrons between pairs of different oxidation states of metal ions to drive their catalytic activity.<sup>26,41</sup> Therefore, the various valence states of Au<sup>+</sup>/Au<sup>0</sup> and Pt<sup>0</sup> on the Pt/Au NP surfaces on the particle surfaces accounted for their high peroxidase-like activity.

### 3.2 Influence of AR and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentrations on reaction rate

Kinetic data were obtained by changing the concentration of one substrate (AR or H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) and maintaining the concentration of the other constant. The curves display a Michaelis–Menten mechanism, which is one of the earliest descriptions of enzyme action. The data were fitted to the Michaelis–Menten equation [$1/v = K_M/v_{max} \times (1/[S] + 1/K_M)$], in which the basic parameters can be obtained from Lineweaver–Burk double reciprocal plots (Fig. S7, ESI†). For comparison, the kinetic data, including the Michaelis constant (*K*<sub>M</sub>), maximal velocity (*v*<sub>max</sub>), and catalytic constant (*K*<sub>cat</sub>, the turnover number) of Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs and horseradish peroxidase (HRP) are listed in Table 1. *K*<sub>cat</sub> is equal to *v*<sub>max</sub>/[E]<sub>0</sub>, where [E]<sub>0</sub> is the total enzyme concentration (both bound and unbound). *K*<sub>M</sub> and *K*<sub>cat</sub> represent the affinity of a given enzyme toward the substrate and the enzymatic activity, respectively. The Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs had a larger *K*<sub>M</sub> for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> than for HRP, which is in agreement with the fact that the maximum activity for the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs was obtained at a higher H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration compared with that of HRP. Although the apparent *K*<sub>M</sub> value of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs with AR as the substrate was only slightly better than that of HRP, our AR (10 μM)–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs (0.01×) allow detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentrations as low as 100 nM (Fig. S8, ESI†). Although the *K*<sub>cat</sub> value of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was smaller than that reported for peroxidase-like NPs,<sup>8</sup> the preparation of Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs is much simpler, involving a one-pot, aqueous reduction process. We also note that the *K*<sub>cat</sub> value of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs with AR was smaller than that of HRP; however, the catalytic activity of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs was more stable at various pH values and temperatures (Fig. S9, ESI†).

### 3.3 Switching of enzymatic activity

Fig. 4a reveals the poorly developed fluorescence intensity of the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs (0.01×) system in the presence of Hg<sup>2+</sup> (1.0 μM). The slight increase in the absorbance of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs (0.1×) solution [see Fig. 4b] in the presence of Hg<sup>2+</sup> (10 μM) suggests that Hg was deposited on the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs *via* strong Hg–Au aurophilic interactions. The EDS and XPS spectra further supported that Hg was deposited on the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs (Fig. S10, ESI†). In addition, we used ICP-MS to quantify the amount of Hg species on the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs and obtained a value of approximately 340 Hg atoms/ions per Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NP. We believe that the deactivation of the peroxidase-like activity of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs by mercury ions is mainly due to the strong interaction between Hg and the active sites of the Pt/Au shell.

This Hg<sup>2+</sup>-activated catalase-like activity of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs was identified by electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) of the hydroxyl radical by employing dimethyl pyridine *N*-oxide (DMPO) as the spin trap (Fig. S11, ESI†). The EPR spectra displayed a characteristic peak of the typical DMPO–OH adduct with an intensity ratio of 1 : 2 : 2 : 1 in the presence of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs (0.1×) under UV irradiation. These signals decrease sharply with the addition of Hg<sup>2+</sup> [Fig. S11b†], revealing decomposition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs after deposition of Hg<sup>2+</sup>. We also visually observed the dynamic process of O<sub>2</sub> gas bubble production by both catalase and Hg–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs in the form of bubbles in the vial (Fig. S12, ESI†). In contrast, no bubbles were observed in the control H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sample [Fig. S12a†]. The catalase-like activity of the Hg–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs was further demonstrated using a commercial Amplex® UltraRed catalase assay kit (Molecular Probes, Inc.) (Fig. S13, ESI†). In the assay, the Hg–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs first reacted with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to produce H<sub>2</sub>O and O<sub>2</sub>. Next, the Amplex® UltraRed reagent reacted with a 1 : 1 stoichiometry with unreacted H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in the presence of HRP to produce a highly fluorescent oxidation product. Therefore, as catalase activity increased, the signal from the Amplex® UltraRed product decreased. Our results (Fig. S13†) indicate that the activity of Hg–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs is superior (by at least 5-fold) to that of natural catalase (from bovine liver). Moreover, Hg–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs may find potential applications such as resistance against oxidative stress and biosensors in catalase-related fields such as the textile industry. Pt NPs have been used with potent catalase-like activity for scavenging superoxides, free radicals, and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<sup>42</sup> Therefore, we suggest that the formation of Hg–Au aurophilic bonds on the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NP surfaces inhibits the peroxidase catalytic activity from Au atom/ions and activates the catalase-like activity from Pt. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first report on the switching of enzymatic activity of metallic NPs by heavy metal ions.

### 3.4 Parameters for sensing mercury ions

We further examined the effect of Hg<sup>2+</sup> (1.0 μM) on the peroxidase activities of the Au NPs and various Pt/Au NPs. From the relative decrease in fluorescence intensity (*I*<sub>F0</sub> − *I*<sub>F</sub>)/*I*<sub>F0</sub> at 585 nm, where *I*<sub>F0</sub> and *I*<sub>F</sub> are the fluorescence intensities of the AR (10 μM)–Pt/Au NP (0.01×) solution in the absence and presence of Hg<sup>2+</sup>, respectively, we found that the presence of Hg<sup>2+</sup> provided greater deactivation for the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs than for the Au NPs and other Pt/Au NPs (Fig. S14, ESI†). This result is mainly owing to the high catalytic activity of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs and their strong interaction with Hg<sup>2+</sup>. Similar to the results of previous reports,<sup>27</sup> we found that Hg can stimulate the peroxidase-like activity of the Au NPs. The fluorescence intensity of the AR–Au NPs in the presence of Hg (1.0 μM) is 5.2-fold higher than that in the absence of Hg<sup>2+</sup>. To optimize this fluorescence sensor, we examined the concentrations of both H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and AR. As shown in Fig. S15 (ESI†), our results suggest that the optimized conditions were obtained in the 5.0 mM Tris-borate solution (pH 7.0) in the presence of the Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs containing H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (50 mM) and AR (10 μM).

### 3.5 Selectivity and sensitivity

To verify that the catalytic sensor was highly selective toward mercury species, we determined the values of (*I*<sub>F0</sub> − *I*<sub>F</sub>)/*I*<sub>F0</sub> for the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs in the presence of 21 different metal species (1.0 μM). Fig. S16 (ESI†) reveals that the addition of Co<sup>2+</sup>, Au<sup>3+</sup>, Pt<sup>4+</sup>, Pb<sup>2+</sup>, and the mercury species Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup> to solutions of the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs resulted in apparent fluorescence changes; in contrast, the remaining ions showed insignificant effects under the same experimental conditions. These results are similar to those of our previous report, suggesting that Au<sup>3+</sup>, Pt<sup>4+</sup>, and Pb<sup>2+</sup> could be deposited on the Au NPs.<sup>43</sup> Free Co<sup>2+</sup> has been shown to react with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and substrates,<sup>44</sup> causing interference of the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs in sensing of mercury ions. Next, we investigated the effects of masking reagents – PAA [Fig. S16b†] and Te NWs [Fig. S16c†] as well as mixtures of PAA and Te NWs [Fig. S16d†] – on the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs sensing system. However, our sensor was specific for Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup> over the other metal ion species (*P* < 0.01) in the presence of 0.1% PAA [see Fig. S16b†]. In particular, PAA has been reported to be applicable to wastewater treatment for the removal of several heavy metal ions through strong complexation.<sup>45</sup> PAA forms stable complexes with metal ions, which enables suppression of the interference caused by heavy metals. In the presence of 0.1% PAA and 0.3 nM Te NWs, the selectivities of AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs probe toward MeHg<sup>+</sup> over other metal ions and Hg<sup>2+</sup> were greater than 200- and 50-fold, respectively.

To improve the selectivity of the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs for MeHg<sup>+</sup>, we used Te NWs as a second masking agent. Te possesses a strong affinity toward inorganic metal ions such as Hg<sup>2+</sup>;<sup>46</sup> in contrast, its interactions with organomercury species are relatively weak. Therefore, the use of as-prepared Te NWs as a masking reagent significantly suppressed the interference from Hg<sup>2+</sup>, allowing the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NP sensor to exhibit excellent selectivity for MeHg<sup>+</sup>. As indicated in Fig. S16d,† the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs (0.01×) in the 5.0 mM Tris-borate solution (pH 7.0) containing masking reagents (0.1% PAA and 0.3 nM Te NW) responded with high selectivity for MeHg<sup>+</sup> (1.0 μM) compared with that for Hg<sup>2+</sup> (>50-fold) and the other metal ions (>200-fold) at the same concentration.

Under optimal conditions, we investigated the sensitivity of the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NP sensor toward Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup> alone. The fluorescence intensity of the AR (10 μM)–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs (0.01×) in a 5.0 mM Tris-borate solution (pH 7.0) containing H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (50 mM) and masking agents (0.1% PAA) decreased gradually with increase in the Hg<sup>2+</sup> concentration [see Fig. 5a]. A linear correlation (*r* = 0.98) existed between the value of (*I*<sub>F0</sub> − *I*<sub>F</sub>)/*I*<sub>F0</sub> and the concentration of 10 nM–1 μM. The limit of detection (LOD) of Hg<sup>2+</sup> was 2.5 nM (with a signal-to-noise ratio of 3), which is lower than the maximum level (2.0 ppb) of mercury permitted in drinking water by the United States EPA. When using a solution of AR (10 μM)–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs (0.01×) containing H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (50 mM) and masking agents PAA (0.1%) and Te NW (0.3 nM), we obtained a linear relationship (*r* = 0.97) between the value of (*I*<sub>F0</sub> − *I*<sub>F</sub>)/*I*<sub>F0</sub> for the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs and the concentration of MeHg<sup>+</sup> over the range 10 nM to 1.0 μM. The LOD for MeHg<sup>+</sup> was approximately 4.0 nM [Fig. 5b]. Relative to other Au-NP-based sensors for mercury ions,<sup>47–50</sup> our label-free fluorescence-based assay for mercury is simpler, more cost-effective, and more selective. Note that most other techniques require the conjugation of alkanethiol ligands, thiolated oligonucleotides, and protein or dye molecules to Au NPs. In addition, our AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs sensor allows the selective detection of Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup> ions in an aqueous solution.

### 3.6 Detection of Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup> in real samples

To validate the practicality of our proposed sensing strategy for the analysis of environmental samples, we used our AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs to determine the concentrations of Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup> spiked in tap, pond, and stream water samples. Prior to the analysis, the samples were filtered through a 0.2 μm membrane and diluted twofold in a 5.0 mM Tris-borate buffer (pH 7.0). Here, we obtained linear correlations (*r* = 0.94–0.98) among the values of (*I*<sub>F0</sub> − *I*<sub>F</sub>)/*I*<sub>F0</sub> and the concentration of mercury ions over the ranges 0–1.0 μM (Fig. S17, ESI†). In these measurements, the two probes provided recoveries of 95–106% for mercury ions. The minimum detectable concentration of Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup> by our probes in these water samples was approximately 10 nM. Neither our sensing approach nor the ICP-MS-based system detected the presence of Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup> in the original water samples. Thus, our proposed method is applicable to the practical screening of mercury species in environmental samples.

## 4. Conclusions

We have developed a highly sensitive and selective approach for the detection of Hg<sup>2+</sup> and MeHg<sup>+</sup>. We prepared bimetallic Pt/Au NPs whose peroxidase catalytic activity could be controlled by varying the ratios of Pt to Au. As-prepared Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs exhibited the most efficient peroxidase-like activity and catalyzed the oxidation of AR in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The developed AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs probe allowed quantitative determination of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentrations as low as 100 nM. Furthermore, we used the AR–Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs system to detect mercury ions, with LOD ≈ 2.5 nM, on the basis of the switching of the peroxidase-like activity of Pt<sub>0.1</sub>/Au NPs to catalase-like activity by the ions. We improved the selectivity of the sensor by adding masking agents (PAA and/or Te NWs) to the sample solutions. It was found that in the presence of 0.1% PAA and 0.3 nM Te NWs, the selectivities of this system toward MeHg<sup>+</sup> over other metal ions and Hg<sup>2+</sup> were greater than 200- and 50-fold, respectively. In addition, we demonstrated the feasibility of using the developed sensor for the rapid determination of mercury species in tap, pond, and stream waters.
