# Intercalation-Activated Layered MoO<sub>3</sub> Nanobelts as Biodegradable Nanozymes for Tumor-Specific Photo-Enhanced Catalytic Therapy

Zhan Zhou<sup>+</sup>, Yanlong Wang<sup>+</sup>, Feng Peng<sup>+</sup>, Fanqi Meng<sup>+</sup>, Jiajia Zha, Lu Ma, Yonghua Du, Na Peng, Lufang Ma, Qinghua Zhang, Lin Gu,* Wenyan Yin,* Zhanjun Gu,* and Chaoliang Tan*

**Abstract:** The existence of natural van der Waals gaps in layered materials allows them to be easily intercalated with varying guest species, offering an appealing strategy to optimize their physicochemical properties and application performance. Herein, we report the activation of layered MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts via aqueous intercalation as an efficient biodegradable nanozyme for tumor-specific photo-enhanced catalytic therapy. The long MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts are grinded and then intercalated with Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O to obtain the short Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sub>2</sub>O co-intercalated MoO<sub>3−x</sub> (NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>) nanobelts. In contrast to the inert MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts, the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts exhibit excellent enzyme-mimicking catalytic activity for generation of reactive oxygen species, which can be further enhanced by the photothermal effect under a 1064 nm laser irradiation. Thus, after bovine serum albumin modification, the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts can efficiently kill cancer cells in vitro and eliminate tumors in vivo facilitating with 1064 nm laser irradiation.

## Introduction

Layered materials are a class of functional materials with highly anisotropic bonding, in which each layer is strongly bonded via covalent bonding and adjacent layers are only weakly bonded via van der Waals interactions.<sup>[1–3]</sup> One of the unique advantages for layered materials is the existence of natural van der Waals gaps, allowing them to be easily intercalated with varying guest species, such as cations, anions, organic molecules and conductive polymers.<sup>[4–6]</sup> By taking this advantage, intercalation has been developed as one of the most appealing strategies to tune/optimize the physical, chemical and electronic properties of layered materials and/or boost their performance in various promising applications.<sup>[7–9]</sup> For example, organic cation intercalation has been proposed as a fascinating approach to remarkably boost the thermoelectric and electronic device performance of layered TiS<sub>2</sub> and black phosphorus, respectively.<sup>[10, 11]</sup> Furthermore, the pre-intercalation of metal cations (e.g., Na<sup>+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup> and Mg<sup>2+</sup>) or conducting polymers (e.g., polyaniline) into layered metal oxides, such as MoO<sub>3</sub>, MnO<sub>2</sub> and V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>, can expand their interlayer spacings, regulate their electronic properties and/or generate rich oxygen vacancies and thus could dramatically enhance their performance in rechargeable batteries, including Li-ion, Na-ion and Zinc-ion batteries.<sup>[12–19]</sup> Although the intercalation has been widely used as a promising strategy for structural engineering of layered materials, they mainly focused on the optimization of their performance in thermoelectric devices, electronic devices and rechargeable batteries. Therefore, how the intercalation affects the performance of layered nanomaterials for biomedical applications, such as photo-thermal and catalytic therapy, still remain unexplored.

Nanomaterials with enzyme-mimicking oxidoreductase activities including peroxidase (POD), oxidase (OXD), and catalase (CAT), so-called "nanozymes", have been one of central topics in nanomedicine in the last decade since they can provide an ideal platform to realize highly efficient tumor diagnostic and/or therapeutic outcomes.<sup>[20–24]</sup> One of the major concerns for nanozymes is that their undesired off-target activity leads to unpredictable toxicity.<sup>[25, 26]</sup> Hence, great effort has been devoted to the development of tumor microenvironment (TME)-responsive nanozymes that only exhibit catalytic reaction-based therapeutic effects, while minimum side effects in normal tissues.<sup>[27–29]</sup> Promisingly, recent studies have demonstrated that some nanozymes exhibit TME-responsive (e.g., H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>- or pH-responsive) enzyme-mimicking catalytic activity and thus can achieve tumor-specific therapy since the microenvironment in tumors, such as the high H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration and acidic pH value, is different from that of in normal tissues.<sup>[30–33]</sup> Among them, molybdenum oxide nanomaterials have been proven to be efficient nanozymes for tumor-specific catalytic therapy because of their low toxicity, variable oxidation states of Mo ions and excellent biodegradability.<sup>[34–38]</sup> For example, MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanourchins and Fe-MoO<sub>v</sub> nanoparticles have been developed as efficient nanozymes for tumor-specific catalytic therapy and the structural engineering, including morphology tuning or defect engineering, has been proven to be essential to regulate their enzyme-mimicking catalytic activity.<sup>[36, 37]</sup> However, the current reported molybdenum oxide-based nanozymes still suffer from the relatively low catalytic activity or need to introduce additional active metal elements. Therefore, it is quite urgent to develop simple but effective strategies to prepare highly active redox nanozymes that are competitive to natural enzymes for catalytic therapy.

In this contribution, we report the intercalation as a promising strategy to activate the enzyme-mimicking catalytic activity of layered MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts, making it an efficient biodegradable nanozyme for tumor-specific photo-enhanced catalytic therapy. The hydrothermal-synthesized micrometer-long MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts are ball-milled and then co-intercalated with Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O via aqueous solution intercalation to obtain the dark-blue co-intercalated MoO<sub>3−x</sub> (denoted as NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>) nanobelts (Figure 1). Importantly, the co-intercalation not only can partially reduce the Mo<sup>6+</sup> to Mo<sup>5+</sup> and generate rich defects on the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts, but also can boost its absorption in the near-infrared (NIR)-II region because of the band gap narrowing. Thus, the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts exhibit excellent enzyme-mimicking catalytic activity for generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) including •OH and •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> in the TME in comparison with the inert MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts (Figure 1). Moreover, the enzyme-mimicking catalytic activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts can be further enhanced by the photothermal effect under a 1064 nm laser irradiation. Thus, after modification with bovine serum albumin (BSA), the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA nanobelts exhibit remarkable tumor ablation when used as a nanozyme for the photo-driven tumor-specific catalytic therapy (Figure 1). Interestingly, the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA nanobelts are able to degrade rapidly and be excreted from the mice body, minimizing the in vivo toxicity for normal organs. This work provides a promising example on activating layered MoO<sub>3</sub> nanomaterials via intercalation as nanozymes for highly efficient tumor-specific catalytic therapy.

## Results and Discussion

Single-crystalline MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts with micrometer lengths were prepared by a hydrothermal approach according to the previously reported method.<sup>[39]</sup> The X-ray diffraction (XRD) pattern of the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts is shown in Figure 2a and all the peaks match well with the standard reference of the layered orthorhombic MoO<sub>3</sub> (α-MoO<sub>3</sub>) (PDF 05-0508), confirming its crystal structure. As shown in the scanning electron microscope (SEM) image (Figure 2b), the prepared MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts have a length up to 5–10 μm and a width of hundreds of nanometers. The morphology and crystal structure of the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts were further verified by transmission electron microscope (TEM) characterization (Figure S1). In order to shorten its length, the micrometer-long MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts were grinded for 2 h via a dry ball milling process to obtain short MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts (denoted as MoO<sub>3</sub>-BM). As shown Figure 2c, the SEM image shows that the MoO<sub>3</sub>-BM nanobelts have a much shorter length, which is around 50–200 nm. The crystal structure of the MoO<sub>3</sub>-BM nanobelts maintain well, which is evidenced by the scanning transmission electron microscope (STEM) and its corresponding atomic resolution STEM images (Figure S2). As shown in Figure S3, the XRD pattern of MoO<sub>3</sub>-BM nanobelts matches well with the standard reference of the α-MoO<sub>3</sub> crystal (PDF 05-0508). All the aforementioned characterization results suggest that the balling milling mainly shortens the length of MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts.

The MoO<sub>3</sub>-BM nanobelts were then intercalated with Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O by reacting with sodium molybdate and sodium dithionite in aqueous solution to obtain the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts.<sup>[40]</sup> As shown in the XRD pattern, all the XRD peaks of the intercalated sample match well with the simulated peaks based on the crystal structure of hydrated molybdenum bronze [Na(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>2</sub>]<sub>0.25</sub>MoO<sub>3</sub> (Figure 2a).<sup>[40]</sup> This hydrated molybdenum bronze can be considered as the layered α-MoO<sub>3</sub> co-intercalated with Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O. It is worth pointing out that the XRD peak corresponding to the (020) planes of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts shows an obvious shift to lower angle in comparison with that of the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts. The peak position of (020) planes in the XRD pattern indicates the interlayer distancing of layered α-MoO<sub>3</sub>. The obvious shift of this peak to lower angle suggests the expansion of the interlayer distancing of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts induced by the co-intercalation with Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O.

The SEM image clearly shows that the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts have a uniform size of 100–300 nm (Figure 2d). The STEM image of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts (Figure 2e) displays a similar result with the SEM image. A continuous lattice fringe can be seen from the high-resolution STEM image (Figure 2f) of a typical NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelt (inset in Figure 2f), indicating its single-crystalline characteristic. The measured lattice distance is ≈0.38 nm, which is assignable to the (100) planes of the [Na(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>2</sub>]<sub>0.25</sub>MoO<sub>3</sub> crystal. The Figure 2g shows the atomic resolution STEM image of MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelt viewing from y-axis, suggesting that the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelt a layered structure and single-crystalline nature. The measured interlayer distancing is ≈0.72 nm (Figure 2g), which is corresponding to the (020) planes of the layered α-MoO<sub>3</sub> crystal. Similarly, the atomic resolution STEM image of a typical NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelt viewing from y-axis also displays its layered structure (Figure 2h). However, the measured interlayer distancing for the (020) planes is ≈0.87 nm (Figure 2h), which is much larger than that of layered α-MoO<sub>3</sub> (i.e. 0.72 nm). Such results clearly prove the obvious expansion of the interlayer distancing of layered NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts as compared to the layered α-MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts, which is consistent with the XRD results. It is worth pointing out that not all the interlayer spacings are intercalated with Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O since some parts of interlayer distancing remain the same as the layered α-MoO<sub>3</sub> (Figure 2h). Meanwhile, some defects (indicating by dash circles) can be also seen from the atomic resolution STEM image (Figure 2h), suggesting that the intercalation process also gives rise to some defects on the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts.

X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was used to further characterize the structural changes of MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts caused by the co-intercalation. The high-resolution XPS Mo 3d of the MoO<sub>3</sub> and NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts are shown in Figure 3a. As shown in Figure 3a, the XPS Mo 3d spectrum of the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts gives two peaks at 236.0 and 232.9 eV, which is attributed to the Mo<sup>6+</sup> of MoO<sub>3</sub>.<sup>[41, 42]</sup> In contrast, the XPS Mo 3d spectrum of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> presents two shoulder peaks at 234.7 and 231.6 eV, assignable to the Mo<sup>5+</sup> in the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts, in addition to the two main peaks at 236.0 and 232.9 eV.<sup>[41, 42]</sup> Such a result indicates that the Mo atoms were partially reduced from Mo<sup>6+</sup> to Mo<sup>5+</sup> during the intercalation process. According to the deconvolution of the high-resolution XPS Mo 3d spectrum, the calculated percentage of Mo<sup>5+</sup> is about 25.5% in the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>, which is consistent with the chemical formula of intercalated Na<sup>+</sup> (Figure 2c). Similarly, a new shoulder peak at 531.4 eV can be observed in the XPS O 1s spectrum of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts in addition to the main peak at 530.7 eV in comparison with the XPS O 1s spectrum of the MoO<sub>3</sub> (Figure 3b), which might be due to oxygen vacancies caused by the partial reduction.<sup>[41, 42]</sup> Importantly, in contract the non-detectable peak in XPS Na 1s spectrum of the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts (Figure 3c), an obvious peak at 1071.8 eV corresponding to the Na<sup>+</sup> can be detected in XPS Na 1s spectrum of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts, confirming the presence of Na<sup>+</sup> in the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts.

The MoO<sub>3</sub> and NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts were also characterized by electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy. As shown in Figure 3d, both of the MoO<sub>3</sub> and NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts display peaks at g values of 2.0 and 1.91, which are corresponding to the signals from oxygen vacancies and the Mo<sup>5+</sup>, respectively.<sup>[43]</sup> It can be clearly observed that the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts give much stronger intensity for both peaks than that of the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts, suggesting that much more oxygen vacancies and Mo<sup>5+</sup> ions in the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts than those of the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts.<sup>[43]</sup> The EPR results are consistent with analysis from other characterizations, including TEM and XPS. Furthermore, both of the MoO<sub>3</sub> and NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts were characterized by the Mo K-edge X-ray absorption near-edge structure (XANES) spectra. The radial atomic distribution around Mo atoms for both of the samples are evidenced by the Fourier transformed extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) spectra (Figure 3e). Note that the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts show slightly peak position shift and much lower peak intensity as compared to the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts (Figure 3e). The much lower peak intensity suggests the significantly increased structure distortion and the peak position shift indicates the slightly changes on the bond lengths around Mo atoms, which can be attributed to the partial reduction of the Mo atoms and crystal structure induced by the intercalation, respectively.<sup>[44]</sup>

As shown in Figure 3f, the Raman spectrum of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts gives different characteristic peaks with much weaker intensity as compared to that of the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts.<sup>[39]</sup> In addition, a few new characteristic peaks can be observed between 850–1000 cm<sup>−1</sup>. The Raman spectrum change should be originated from the intercalation. Therefore, it was found that multiple structural changes can be achieved by the simple co-intercalation with Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O into layered MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts, including partially reducing Mo<sup>6+</sup> to Mo<sup>5+</sup>, expanding the interlayer gaps, generating rich defects (e.g. oxygen vacancies) and changing crystal structure, to obtain short, interlayer gap-expanded and intercalated NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts.

The intercalation process can also give rise to a color change on the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts from white to dark blue. As shown in Figure 4a, the photographs of powders and solutions of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts show an obvious dark blue color, distinctive from the white color of the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts. Therefore, the UV/Vis-NIR absorption of the MoO<sub>3</sub>, MoO<sub>3</sub>-BM, and NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts are measured at the same concentration (100 ppm). The MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts exhibit weak absorption peaks in the range of 400 to 1300 nm and its absorption intensity gradually decreased could be attributed to light scattering induced by its micrometer-long length (Figure 4b). Note that the short MoO<sub>3</sub>-BM nanobelts show negligible absorption intensity after 600 nm since the light scattering no longer exists because of the reduced size to hundreds of nanometers (Figure 4b). Interestingly, the absorption of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts gradually increase along with the wavelength, resulting in strong absorption in the NIR-II window (Figure 4b), which is consistent with the UV/Vis-NIR spectra calculated by density functional theory (DFT) (Figure 4c). In order to figure out the mechanism behind the absorption change, the electronic band structure and optical property were simulated by first principles DFT. We can find that the MoO<sub>3</sub> has a band gap of 2.592 eV, in which the valence band maximum (VBM) located at the high symmetry U point and the conduction band minimum (CBM) located at the X point (Figure 4d). In contrast, based on the calculation, the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> has a band gap of 1.793 eV (Figure 4e) which decreases 0.8 eV compared with the pristine MoO<sub>3</sub>. It can be seen from the band structure that the conduction band of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> also moves downward to the Fermi level as compared to the pristine MoO<sub>3</sub>, since one Na atom was introduced in the MoO<sub>3</sub> crystal, which leads to a further narrowing of the band gap. Furthermore, as shown in the electronic energy band of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>, defect energy levels appear between the conduction band and the valence band from (Figure 4e). In this case, the defect energy levels could facilitate the electron transfer and the valence electrons could transit upward to the defect level first and then the conduction band. Hence, it is reasonable that the MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts have negligible absorption after 600 nm since it has a large band of ≈2.592 eV. As for the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts, the broad absorption band from visible to NIR region possibly is caused its much smaller band gap and defect energy level-enabled electron transition.

The UV/Vis-NIR absorption intensity of the obtained NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts increased gradually from visible light to NIR region (especially in NIR-II region), which indicates that it has the potential to be used as a photothermal agent under a NIR laser radiation. As shown in the concentration-dependent absorption of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts (Figure S4), its mass extinction coefficient at NIR-II (1064 nm) was calculated to be 6.7 Lg<sup>−1</sup> cm<sup>−1</sup>. To promote the biocompatibility of inorganic nanomaterials, it is necessary to modify their surfaces with polymers (e.g., polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP), polyethylene glycol (PEG) or polyvinyl alcohol (PVA)) or biological macromolecules (e.g., glutathione (GSH), hyaluronic acid (HA) or BSA). Therefore, a natural biological macromolecule, i.e., BSA, was used to modify the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts to get the BSA-modified NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts (NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA). Then, the photothermal effect was studied by the continuous irradiation of DI water solution containing NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA with various concentrations using a NIR-II laser (1064 nm) for 10 min at a power density of 1.0 Wcm<sup>−2</sup>. As shown in Figure 4f, the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA displays concentration-dependent heating effect under the NIR-II laser irradiation, and the water solution is heated from 25°C to 58.3°C by adjusting its concentration in contrast to the slight temperature changes (from 25 °C to 33.8 °C) for the pure DI water, indicating that NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA could rapidly covert NIR light energy to the thermal energy. The solution of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (200 ppm) was further irradiated with various laser power densities, and the heating curves displayed the amplitude of heating increased with the increase of laser power density (Figure S5a), demonstrating the laser power-dependent photothermal properties of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA. Moreover, the good photostability of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was confirmed by the slight changes in its photothermal effect after five laser power on/off cycles (Figure S5b). The photothermal conversion efficiency of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was calculated to be 24.27% based on its heating-cooling curve (Figure S5c,d), indicating that NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA could effectively covert light energy to thermal energy.

The abundant oxygen vacancies, Mo<sup>5+</sup>, and appropriate band gap of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts makes it possible to be a nanozyme catalyst. After adding NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA into a specific concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> solution, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was dramatically decomposed in a time-dependent manner under the neutral or weak acidic condition (Figure S6). The POD-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was investigated by reducing H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to •OH that could be monitored by the traditional 3,3’,5,5’-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) substrate. NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA presented significant POD-like activity (Figure 5a) in the HAc-NaAc buffer solution (pH 6.5), whereas almost no obvious catalytic activity was observed in the typical MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelt (Figure S7) at the same conditions, which further proves that the POD-like activity of molybdenum oxide could be activated by intercalation.

When NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was irradiated by the 1064 nm NIR-II laser, its catalytic performance was improved to a certain extent in the buffer solution of pH 6.5 (Figure 5b), which might be due to the high temperature of the system to facilitate the nanozyme catalytic reaction. We further tested the temperature-dependent POD-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA at different temperature (Figure 5c), and its optimal temperature ranged from 35 to 45 °C. Moreover, the pH-dependent POD-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was also evaluated in different pH values at 37 °C, and its optimal pH ranged from 4.5 to 6.0 (Figure 5d and Figure S8a). The NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA still maintained superior POD-like activity in the presence of low concentration H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Figure 5e and Figure S8b), and the UV/Vis absorbance intensity at 652 nm (the characteristic peak for blue oxTMB) versus various concentrations (from 0 to 0.5 mM) of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> exhibited a good linearity with the limit of detection as 21.3 μM (Figure 5f) that was lower than that of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in the tumor microenvironment (TME). According to the Lineweaver–Burk equation, the Michaelis–Menten constant ($K_{m}$) and maximal reaction velocity ($V_{max}$) were calculated to evaluate the catalytic ability of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (Figure S9). The $K_{m}/V_{max}$ value of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was 2.41 mmolL<sup>−1</sup>/1.3×10<sup>−7</sup> molL<sup>−1</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> substrate with the concentration ranging from 0.5 to 10 mM, indicating the superior affinity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in comparison to that of natural horseradish peroxidase (HRP) (Table S1).<sup>[45]</sup>

Besides •OH, O<sub>2</sub> can be produced by decomposing H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> under the catalysis of biological enzyme including catalase (CAT).<sup>[46]</sup> To investigate the CAT-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA, its oxygen production capacity was measured in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> under different pH value buffer solutions. As shown in Figure 5g, the continuous oxygen can be detected after adding NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA, and the yield of oxygen in weak acidic condition (mimicking TME) is obviously higher than that in neutral physiological environment, indicating NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA can be used as a CAT-like nanozyme for decomposing intratumoral overexpressed H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to produce O<sub>2</sub>. Compared with NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA, negligible CAT-like activity was observed for MoO<sub>3</sub> even under weak acidic condition (Figure S10a), demonstrating the CAT-like activity of molybdenum oxide could also be activated by the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup>. It is worth pointing out that CAT-like performance of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA could be improved under a NIR-II laser (1064 nm) irradiation (Figure 5h), contributing to the photothermal-enhanced catalytic effect. More importantly, the generated O<sub>2</sub> was further catalyzed by NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA to form superoxide free radical (•O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>), which was proved by the electron spin resonance (ESR) assay. Only significant signal of •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> was detected for the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA-catalyzed reaction (Figure 5i), indicating that NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA had superior OXD-like activity to produce •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> in comparison to MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts (Figure S10b).<sup>[47]</sup> All the aforementioned results proved that the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA nanobelts possess the multi-enzyme-like (POD, CAT, and OXD) property, and these integrated cascade catalytic reactions could effectively enhance the production of ROS (•OH and •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>) for catalytic therapy, which can be further accelerated by the photothermal effect under 1064 nm laser irradiation.

We believed that intercalation induced structural engineering, including partial reduction of Mo<sup>6+</sup>, defect generation and interlayer spacing expansion, is attributed to the activation of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts. Previous studies have demonstrated that metal oxides with defects (e.g. oxygen vacancies) can effectively capture and activate H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to generate •OH and O<sub>2</sub>, thereby enhancing the catalytic properties of nanozymes.<sup>[37, 48]</sup> In our NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts, the rich oxygen defects were formed after co-intercalating with Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O into layered MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts (Figure 2h and 3d), which can provide more active sites for the adsorption and activation of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to yield the more •OH and O<sub>2</sub>. Subsequently, the adsorbed O<sub>2</sub> was reduced to •O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> through the charge transfer between Mo<sup>V</sup> and Mo<sup>VI</sup><sup>[49]</sup> because of the partially reducing Mo<sup>6+</sup> to Mo<sup>5+</sup> (25.5%) during the intercalation process. Meanwhile, the interlayer spacing of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts was also widened by the co-intercalation with Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O, which can reduce its band gap. The band gap narrowing endows the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts with strong absorption in the NIR-II window (Figure 4b, e), thereby enabling its good NIR-II photothermal properties. Temperature is one of the important factors affecting enzyme catalyzed reaction and the enzyme-catalyzed reaction will accelerate with the increase of temperature.<sup>[50]</sup> In our case, the increase of localized temperature induced photothermal effect will accelerate the rate of ROS generation and thus achieve highly efficient catalytic therapy. Therefore, layered MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts can be activated by intercalation, making it a highly active nanozyme for photo-enhanced catalytic therapy.

The in vitro cytotoxicity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was measured by the CCK-8 assay. Almost all the human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVEC) still survive after treating with NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA for 24 h even at the high concentration of 400 ppm (Figure 6a), demonstrating that NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA exhibited negligible cytotoxicity toward the normal cells with low H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> expression. As for the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> high expressed cervical cancer cell line (HeLa) and mouse breast cancer cells (4T1), more than 50% of cells could be killed at a concentration of 400 ppm (Figure 6b,c), suggesting that the enzyme-like catalytic activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA could be activated in the TME, which lead to the production of excess ROS and induce cell apoptosis. Meanwhile, we also investigated the photothermal effect of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA for 4T1 under the NIR-II laser irradiation (1064 nm, 0.8 Wcm<sup>−2</sup>) for 10 min. The cell viability was highly dependent on the concentration of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA after the PTT, and the cell survival rate was significantly lower than that of group without pre-irradiation by the NIR-II laser (Figure 6c), which was indicated PTT could enhance the catalytic activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA in vitro. Compared with hypoxic condition (Figure S11), the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> exhibits much higher cell inhibition rate under the normoxia, suggesting that it can be used for catalytic therapy in vitro. Moreover, the photo-enhanced enzyme catalysis using NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was further proved by co-staining with Calcein-AM and propidium iodide (PI). The living 4T1 cells (green color) and dead cells (red color) were distinguished by naked eye with the assistance of a confocal laser scanning microscopy (Figure 6d). Promisingly, most of the 4T1 cells were killed in the presence of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (200 ppm) under the irradiation of a NIR-II laser (0.8 Wcm<sup>−2</sup>) for 10 min, and this cell mortality was much higher than that of group under the dark (Figure 6d), which might be due to the more ROS production stimulated by a NIR-II laser and the photothermal activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA. To further confirm the intracellular ROS producing ability of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA, 2’,7’-dichloro-fluorescein diacetate (DCFH-DA) and Hoechst as the classical fluorescent probes were used to monitor the intracellular ROS and nuclear localization, respectively. As shown in Figure 6e, the obvious green signal (DCF channel) was observed in the cells cultured with NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (200 ppm), which was demonstrated the generation of intracellular ROS. Compared to the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA group, the fluorescence intensity of cells in NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA+H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA+H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>+laser groups displayed a gradual increase, corroborating more ROS could be activated by the addition of external H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and irradiation of NIR-II laser. These results indicated that NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA could be used as a nanozyme to produce ROS in tumor cells through an integrated cascade reaction, and kill cells.

The promising photo-enhanced enzymatic activities of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA on tumor cells make it have great potential in anti-tumor performance in vivo. 4T1 tumor-bearing BALB/c mice were assigned into four groups (six mice in each group): 1) control (PBS), 2) only laser (1064 nm, 0.8 Wcm<sup>−2</sup>, 10 min), 3) NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (dosage: 5 mgkg<sup>−1</sup>), 4) NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (dosage: 5 mgkg<sup>−1</sup>)+laser (1064 nm, 0.8 Wcm<sup>−2</sup>, 10 min). When the tumor volume is about 75 mm<sup>3</sup>, the mice were intratumoral injected with PBS or NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA solution (5 mgkg<sup>−1</sup>). After 12 h, the tumor region was irradiated by the 1064 nm laser (0.8 Wcm<sup>−2</sup>) for 10 min, and its temperature changes were monitored by an IR thermal imager (Figure 7a). The temperature of tumor site in group 4 was dramatically increased to 53.3 °C after treating for 10 min (Figure 7b), while the temperature only increased to 35.4°C for the group 2, demonstrating that NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA possessed the good photothermal activity in vivo. In addition, the photo-enhanced catalytic cancer therapy of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was explored by checking the changes of tumor volume, tumor weight, and body weight in different groups. The digital photos of mice in the four groups were taken at different period (Figure S12), and the tumors were effectively eliminated for the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA+laser group and the leaving black scar disappeared at 15<sup>th</sup> day. The tumor volumes and weight of mice in NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA group were much lower than those of control and only laser groups (Figure 7c–e), indicating that NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA had a certain enzyme inhibitory effect on tumors without laser. Upon irradiation by a 1064 nm laser, the tumor volumes and weight of mice decreased rapidly until almost disappeared in NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA+laser group, proving that photothermal could enhance the anti-tumor properties of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA. The swelling of mouse spleen was significantly alleviated in group 4 (Figure 7f), which was further indicated the tumors of mice were effectively eliminated in NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA+laser group. The body weight monitoring curves of mice in all the groups were very similar (Figure 7g), and no significant changes were observed, suggesting that NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA had low toxicity in vivo. Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) and Ki-67 staining of tumor tissues were also recorded and proved the effective suppressed cell proliferation treated with NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA plus NIR-II laser group, while almost no obvious therapeutic effects were observed in the control, only laser and NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA groups (Figure 7h and i).

To further evaluate the long-term biosafety of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA, its hemolysis experiment was first investigated. As shown in Figure S13, the hemolysis rate of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was comparable to that of PBS control even at high concentration (300 ppm), proving it had good biosafety in blood. Secondly, blood samples of mice after different treatments at 19<sup>th</sup> day were utilized for routine blood test analysis (Figure S14) and blood biochemical assay (Figure S15). Most of the cell count parameters were normal in all four groups except for the abnormal increasing number of neutrophils (Figure S14a), monocytes (Figure S14b), lymphocytes (Figure S14c), and leukocytes (Figure S14d) in group 1, 2, and 3, which was probably attributed to the inflammation caused by the tumor. However, the above abnormal parameters returned to normal values after eliminating the tumors in the group 4. Importantly, the amount of alkaline phosphatase (ALP) in the mouse blood was also significantly improved after the treatment of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA+laser (Figure S15f). Thirdly, the tissue and organ sections of mice for H&E staining and the distribution of molybdenum ions were investigated in detail. No obvious damage was observed in the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA+laser group in comparison with the control group (Figure S16). More importantly, inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) measurement (Figure S17) indicated that the accumulation of molybdenum ions in all organs of mice in NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA+laser group was extremely low, which was close to that of the PBS control group, demonstrating the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA could be degraded in mice and excreted from the body with negligible biotoxicity in vivo.

## Conclusion

In summary, we have reported the activation of layered MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts through aqueous Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sub>2</sub>O co-intercalation used as highly active nanozymes for photo-enhanced catalytic therapy. Our results have shown that the intercalation can endow the inert MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts with excellent enzyme-mimicking catalytic activity towards the enhanced generation of vast ROS under mimic TME. Meanwhile, the co-intercalation can also activate the absorption of layered MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts in the NIR region, thus enabling its good photothermal effect under a NIR-II laser irradiation. Importantly, the enzyme-mimicking catalytic activity of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts can be further enhanced by the NIR-laser-induced photothermal effect. Both the in vitro and in vivo have proven that the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> nanobelts could serve as a highly active nanozyme for selectively killing cancer cells. Importantly, the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA nanobelts presented excellent biodegradability and could be easy to be excreted from normal organs with negligible in vivo toxicity. Our study has revealed that aqueous intercalation is a simple and effective strategy to activate layered MoO<sub>3</sub> for highly efficient photo-enhanced catalytic therapy. We believe that this intercalation strategy could also be an effective way to activate/optimize layered metal oxides for other biomedical applications, such as chemodynamic, photodynamic and sonodynamic therapy. In addition, such an intercalation strategy is expected to be also a powerful approach for activating/tuning other layered materials, such as metal chalcogenides, Mxenes, and layered double hydroxides, as highly active nanozymes towards catalytic therapy.

### Chemicals.
Bovine serum albumin (BSA) was purchased from Beijing Puyihua Technology Co., Ltd. 5,5-Dimethyl-1-pyrroline N-oxide (DMPO, 98%), 5, 5’-dithio bis-(2-nitrobenzoic acid) (DTNB), sodium molybdate dihydrate (99.0%), sodium dithionite (85%) and sodium chloride (99.5%) were purchased from Aladdin Reagent Company (China). Nitric acid (70%), hydrochloric acid (37%), hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) and sodium hydroxide (96%) were purchased by Beijing Chemical Reagent Company. Dulbeco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM) was supplied by the HyClone Company. Hoechst 33342, 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB), 2′,7′-dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate (DCFH-DA) and calmodulin AM-propidium iodide (CA-PI) fetal bovine serum (FBS) were obtained from Beyotime Biotechnology.

### Preparation of α-MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts.
The α-MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelts was synthesized by sodium chloride-assisted hydrothermal method reported in the previous literature.<sup>[1]</sup> Na<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O (15 mmol) and NaCl (0.002 eq, 0.03 mmol) were dissolved in DI water (120 mL), then 12 mL of hydrochloric acid aqueous solution (3 M) was added into the above solution slowly. After pouring into a 250 mL of hydrothermal reactor, the solution was heated to 180 °C and kept for 24 h. After that, the reactor was cooled down to room temperature naturally. Finally, the whit precipitate was washed with DI water for several times until the supernatant was neutral.

### Preparation of co-intercalated molybdenum oxide nanobelts (denoted as NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>).
2 grams of α-MoO<sub>3</sub> nanobelt powder and appropriate number of steel balls were added into a stainless steel ball milling tank, and sealed by ball milling (40 Hz) for 0.5 h and stop for 0.5 h with 4 cycles to obtain the ball milled α-MoO<sub>3</sub> (MoO<sub>3</sub>-BM). The above MoO<sub>3</sub>-BM (2 g) was added into 100 mL of DI water, and the mixture was transferred into a round bottom flask sealed with a rubber plug. After deaerating with nitrogen gas for 1 h, Na<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O (24 g) and sodium dithionite (0.8 g) were poured into the solution at the same time, and stirred at room temperature for another 3 h under nitrogen protection. The precipitate was washed with DI water thoroughly until the supernatant is colorless, and the blue-color molybdenum oxide nanobelts co-intercalated with Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sub>2</sub>O molecules (denoted as NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> for short) was obtained by drying in air at 90 °C for overnight.

### Surface modification of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> with BSA.
BSA (80 mg) was added into the aqueous solution of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> (80 mL, 1 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) at room temperature. After stirring for 4h, the BSA-modified NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub> (denoted as NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA) was obtained by high-speed centrifugation (12000 rpm), and followed by washing with DI water for several times. Finally, the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was stored at 4 °C for further usage.

### Computational details.
Crystal structures optimization, electronic properties and optical properties were calculated by the CASTEP code.<sup>[2]</sup> We chose the Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) of generalized gradient approximation (GGA) and the ultrasoft pseudopotential. The ultrasoft pseudopotential of Mo, O, Na treated 4s<sup>2</sup>4p<sup>6</sup>4d<sup>5</sup>5s<sup>1</sup>, 1s<sup>2</sup>2s<sup>2</sup>2p<sup>4</sup> and 2s<sup>2</sup>2p<sup>6</sup>3s<sup>1</sup> as valence electrons. The plane wave cutoff energy of 630 eV and adequate Monkhorst-Pack k meshes were adopted to ensure the enthalpy converges to less than 1 meV/atom. The supercell consisting of 32 atoms of 2\*1\*1 for α-MoO<sub>3</sub> (Pbnm, a = 3.963Å, b = 13.855Å, c = 3.696Å).

### Characterizations.
A transmission electron microscope (JEOL JEM-2100F) was used to record TEM images. C<sub>s</sub>-corrected transmission electron microscopes FEI TEAM1 and JEOL ARM200F were used to take atomic-resolution STEM images. The SEM images were obtained by a scanning electron microscope (ZEISS Sigma 500, USA). Powder XRD patterns were measured on a Bruker D8 diffractometer (German), in which a Cu Kα (λ=1.54178 Å) is used as the X-ray source. XPS spectra were recorded on a Thermo Scientific K-Alpha+ instrument and calibrated by using the C 1 s peak as the reference. A PerkinElmer Lambda 950 UV/VIS/NIR spectrometer was used to measure the absorption spectra. A planetary ball mill (QM-3SP04) was used for the ball milling treatment. ### Photothermal effect and photothermal conversion efficiency.
1 mL of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA aqueous solutions with different concentrations were irradiated by a NIR-II laser (1064nm, 1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for 10 min, and a thermal camera was used to record the temperature changes. As for the test of thermal stability, 1 mL of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA solution (200 ppm) was irradiated by a 1064 nm laser (1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for 10 min, and the solution was then cooled to room temperature. This process was repeated with five cycles. The photothermal effect of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (200 ppm) under various laser power density irradiations (0.4, 0.6, 0.8 and 1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) were also measured in the same way.
To study the photothermal conversion effect, the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (200 ppm) was irradiated using a NIR-II laser (1064 nm, 1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for 10 min, and followed by shutting off the NIR laser. Meanwhile, thermal imager was exploited to detect the temperature increasing and decreasing.
The photothermal conversion efficiency (η) of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA can be calculated according to Equation 1:

$$\eta = \frac{hs(T_{max} - T_{surr}) - Q_{Dis}}{I(1 - 10^{-A_{1064}})} \quad (1)$$

The T<sub>max</sub> represents the highest equilibrium temperature; T<sub>surr</sub> is environment. The Q<sub>dis</sub> represents heat loss due to absorption of light by the vessel. I is the laser power. A<sub>1064</sub> is the absorbance intensity of solution at 1064 nm. Where h (W cm<sup>−2</sup> K<sup>−1</sup>) is heat transfer coefficient, S (cm<sup>2</sup>) is the surface area of the vessel, the hS was received based on Equation 2:

$$\tau_{s} = \frac{m_{D}c_{D}}{hs} \quad (2)$$

The τ<sub>s</sub> is the sample system time constant, m<sub>D</sub> and C<sub>D</sub> are the mass and heat capacity of solution, respectively. According to calculation, the η of the NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA nanobelts is calculated to 24.7%.

### Peroxidase-like activity and kinetic assay.
Peroxidase (POD)-catalysed oxidation of colourless 3,3',5,5'- tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) can form blue oxidized TMB (oxTMB) with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the electron acceptor. The POD-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was detected using TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as substrates in HAc-NaAc buffer solution (0.1 M). A microplate reader was used to monitor the absorbance of the color solution, oxTMB with the maximum absorption peak at 652 nm and a certain reaction time.
To prove whether the POD-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA can catalyze and decompose the low concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> that was near the level in the tumor microenvironment (TME). Various concentrations of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (from 0 to 2 mM) were added into the HAc-NaAc buffer solution (pH = 5.0) containing NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (50 ppm) and TMB (1 mM) at 37 °C. The temperature-dependent POD-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was evaluated at different temperature (from 20 to 65 °C) in the HAc-NaAc buffer solution (pH = 5.0) containing H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 mM) and TMB (1 mM). In addition, the POD-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (50 ppm) was further explored in the different pH values of HAc-NaAc buffer solution containing H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 mM) and TMB (1 mM).
For each H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration or TMB concentration, the initial reaction rates ($V_{0}$) were calculated from the absorbance changes by Beer-Lambert Law (Equation 3) (ε =39000 M<sup>−1</sup> cm<sup>−1</sup> for oxTMB). The rates were plotted versus H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration and then fitted by the Michaelis-Menten curves (Equation 4). Furthermore, Lineweaver-Burk plot (Equation 5) was used for measuring the $K_{m}$ and $V_{max}$. Among them, the $K_{m}$ value is called the Michaelis constant, $V_{max}$ is the reaction rate when the enzyme is saturated with the substrate, and [S] is the concentration of the substrate.

$$A = \epsilon bc \quad (3)$$

$$v_{0} = \frac{V_{max} \cdot [S]}{K_{m} + [S]} \quad (4)$$

$$\frac{1}{v_{0}} = \frac{K_{m}}{V_{max}} \cdot \frac{1}{[S]} + \frac{1}{V_{max}} \quad (5)$$

### Measurement of catalase-like activity.
The catalase (CAT)-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was evaluated at 25 °C by measuring the oxygen production with a dissolved oxygen instrument (JPSJ-605F). Specifically, 0.5 mL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (10 mM) was added into a HAc-NaAc buffer (8.5 mL, pH = 6.5 / 7.4), followed by adding 1 mL of MoO<sub>3</sub> (50 ppm) or NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (50 ppm) solution. The oxygen concentration was immediately detected and the data was continuously recorded for 20 min. Moreover, we further evaluated the effect of the irradiation by a 1064 nm laser on the oxidase (OXD)-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA.

### Measurement of oxidase-like activity.
Oxidase (OXD-like) activity of nanomaterials can promote the convertion of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> into ion radicals (•O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>). The OXD-like activity of MoO<sub>3</sub>/ NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was then detected by ESR spectroscopy using DMPO as a spin trap. 2 μL of DMPO was added into DMSO control (1 mL), DMSO (1 mL) + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 μL, 30%), DMSO (1 mL) + MoO<sub>3</sub> / NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (1 mg), and DMSO (1 mL) + MoO<sub>3</sub> / NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (1 mg) + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 μL, 30%), respectively, and measured immediately by ESR at room temperature.

### H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> consumption with NH-MoO<sub>3</sub>@BSA.
The Ti(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub> assay was performed to evaluate the POD- and OXD-like activity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA by monitoring the changes of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration. Typically, 25 μL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (2 mM) and 25 μL NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (400 ppm) were added into HAc-NaAc buffer (pH = 6.5 or 7.4) at 37 °C. The mixture was added to Ti(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub> solution (100 μL) that was obtained by adding 24 % Ti(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub> (1.33 mL) and H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (8.33 mL) into the DI water (50 mL) and maintained for 30 min. The product (Ti-O<sub>2</sub>) was detected by the record of the UV absorbance at 405 nm.

### Biocompatibility of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA.
The in vitro cytotoxicity of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA was measured using CCK-8 assay in mouse breast cancer cells (4T1 cells) and cervical cancer cell line (HeLa cells) as well as H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> low expressed cells Human Umbilical Vein Endothelial Cells (HUVEC). The cells were incubated in 96-well plates (6000 cells per well, 100 μL of DMEM medium containing 10% FBS and 1% penicillin-streptomycin solution) at 37 °C in a humid atmosphere of 5% CO<sub>2</sub> for 24 h. The NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA at serial concentrations (0, 3.12, 6.25, 12.5, 25, 50, 100, 200, and 400 ppm) were added to the above medium. As for the photothermal toxicity of NH-MoO<sub>3</sub>@BSA, various concentrations of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA were calculated with 4T1 cells under a NIR-II laser (1064 nm, 0.8 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for 10 min. After incubation for 1 day, 10 μL of CCK-8 solution in serum-free was added into every well. After incubating for further 1h, the relative intensities of all wells at 450 nm were monitored using a microplate reader with the 6 repeats in each group.

### Killing of tumor cells.
4T1 cells (3 × 10<sup>4</sup> cells / well) were pre-calculated in 24-well plates for 1 day. And these cells were divided into four groups: (i) control, (ii) Laser, (iii) NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA, (iv) NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA + Laser. Thereafter, NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (200 ppm) was added into groups (iii) and (iv), and the fresh medium was used as control. After culturing for 6h, the medium placed under a NIR-II laser (1064 nm, 0.8 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for 10 min in the laser groups. Subsequently, the medium was carefully removed and washed with cell culture PBS for several times, and further calculated with CA-PI for another 15 min. Finally, the fluorescent images of cells were recorded by a fluorescence microscope (Olympus X73).

### In vitro cytotoxicity assays under hypoxic condition.
The 4T1 cells were seeded in 96-well plates at a cell density of 5×10<sup>3</sup>/per well and incubated under hypoxic condition for 24 h. Hypoxic condition was achieved by incubating cells in an anoxic environment (N<sub>2</sub>/CO<sub>2</sub>/O<sub>2</sub> = 94/5/1 in volume ratio) in an incubator. Different concentrations of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (0, 25, 50, 100, 200, 400 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>) were then added to the 96-well plates and incubated for another 6 h. Then the plates were irradiated with 1064 nm laser (0.8 W cm<sup>−2</sup>, 10 min per well). Finally, the cytotoxicity was tested with CCK-8 reagent after incubating for 18 h.

### ROS detection in cells.
We used the typical probe DCFH-DA to evaluate the intracellular ROS. Simply, 4T1 cells (2 × 10<sup>4</sup> each well) were seeded in confocal dish and calculated with acidified DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS. After pre-culturing for 24 h, the cells were further incubated with 200 ppm of NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA (with or without 100 μM of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, with or without irradiation by a 1064 nm laser at the power density of 0.8 W cm<sup>−2</sup> for 10 min) for another 12 h. Then, the medium was removed and the cells were washed with PBS for several times. The serum-free DMEM containing DCFH-DA (10 μM) and 10 μL Hoechst 33342 (400 ppm) was added into the dish and cultured for another 20 min at 37 °C in dark. After removing medium and washing with PBS for three times, the fluorescence imaging of 4T1 cells was recorded by a confocal microscopy laser scanning (CLSM, Nikon A1).

### In vivo anti-tumor effect.
The 4T1 tumor model was established by hypodermic injection with 4T1 cells into the right hind legs of Female BALB/c mice (six weeks old). All procedures used in this experiment were compliant with the Chinese Association for Laboratory Animal Sciences and the protocols approved by the Key Laboratory for Biomedical Effects of Nanomaterials and Nanosafety, Institute of High Energy Physics. 4T1 tumor-bearing BALB/c mice were assigned into four groups (six mice each): (1) control; (2) only laser (1064 nm, 0.8W cm<sup>−2</sup>, 10 min); (3) NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA; (4) NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA + laser (1064 nm, 0.8W cm<sup>−2</sup>, 10 min). The mice can be carried out for in vivo experiments when its tumor size reaches around 75 mm<sup>3</sup>. Tumor volume = (tumor length) × (tumor width)<sup>2</sup>/2. Relative tumor volumes were calculated as V<sub>t</sub>/V<sub>0</sub> (V<sub>t</sub> is the tumor volume monitored after starting treatment, V<sub>0</sub> is the tumor volume when the treatment was initiated). The mice were intratumoral injected with PBS or NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA solution (5 mg/kg). After 12 h, the tumor region was irradiated by 1064 nm laser. The tumor volumes and weight of mice were recorded every day. These mice were executed at day 19 to obtain the major organs and tumors for histological examination. Main organ section (heart, liver, spleen, lung and kidney) and tumors were stained with Ki-67, hematoxylin and eosin (H&E). As for the metabolism of Mo in various tissues after 19 days of treatment, the appropriate samples (major organs and tumors) in group 1 (PBS control) and group 4 (NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA + laser) were dissolved in nitric acid, and then measured on ICP-MS using the online internal standard method.

### In vivo photothermal imaging.
After injecting NH-MoO<sub>3−x</sub>@BSA or PBS for 12 h, the tumor was irradiated with a NIR-II laser (1064 nm, 0.8 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for 10 min. Meanwhile, the thermal imager was exploited to detect the temperature increasing and decreasing in tumor sites.

### Blood hematology and biochemistry analysis.
After post-treatment for 19-days, blood samples were collected and then treated with anticoagulant (Na-EDTA) for further hematology analysis. Moreover, another residual blood (500 μL) of mice in each group was refrigerated at 4 °C for 4 h, and followed by centrifugation to yield the blood plasma for biochemistry analysis. Blood routine and blood biochemistry tests indicators were evaluated at the Animal Department of Peking University Medical Laboratory. Blood index: neutrophil (GR), monocyte count (MO), lymphocyte count (LY), white blood cell count (WBC), mean corpuscular hemoglobin (MCH), mean corpuscular volume (MCV), hematocrit (HCT), thrombocytocrit (PCT), platelet distribution width (PDW), red blood cell count (RBC), red cell volume distribution width (RDW) and platelet (PLT). Blood biochemistry index: alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine (CREA) and alkaline phosphatase (ALP).
