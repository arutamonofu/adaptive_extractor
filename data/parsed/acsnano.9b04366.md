**Optimization for Antibacterial Efficacy of Noble Metal-Based Core-Shell Nanostructures and Effect of Nature Organic Matter**

Tingting Cai<sup>†#</sup>, Ge Fang<sup>†#</sup>, Xin Tian<sup>†</sup>, Jun-Jie Yin<sup>‡</sup>, Chunying Chen<sup>§</sup> and Cuicui Ge<sup>†*</sup>

**Abstract:** Noble metal-based nanomaterials made of less toxic metals have been utilized as potential antibacterial agents due to their distinctive oxidase-like activity. In this study, we fabricated core-shell structured Pd@Ir bimetallic nanomaterials with an ultrathin shell. Pd@Ir nanostructures show morphology-dependent bactericidal activity, which is Pd@Ir octahedra possessing higher oxidase-like activity exert stronger bactericidal activity than Pd@Ir cubes. Furthermore, our results reveal that the presence of nature organic matter (NOM) influences the antibacterial behaviors of nanomaterials. Upon interaction with humic acid (HA), the Pd@Ir nanostructures induce elevated level of reactive oxygen species (ROS), resulting in significantly enhanced bactericidal activity of nanostructures. Mechanism analysis show that the presence of HA efficiently enhances the oxidase-like activity of nanomaterials and promotes the cellular internalization of nanomaterials. We believe that the present study will not only demonstrate an effective strategy for improving the bactericidal activity of noble metal-based nanomaterials, but also provide an understanding on the antibacterial behavior of nanomaterials in the natural environments.

**Keywords:** bimetallic core-shell nanostructures, morphology, natural organic matter, oxidase-like activity, antibacterial efficiency

---

Owing to their unique physicochemical properties, noble metal nanomaterials have been widely applied in various areas ranging from industrial catalysis to daily necessities.<sup>1-4</sup> Especially, without the risk of drug resistance, noble metal nanomaterials have shown potential applications in bactericidal fields. Ag nanoparticles, as one of most common noble metal-based antibacterial agents, possess strong antibacterial activity but also show unacceptable toxicity to normal cells or tissue.<sup>5, 6</sup> Therefore, the exploration of noble metal based-nanomaterials with excellent antibacterial activity and high bio-safety is highly desirable.

Previous studies have revealed that noble metal nanomaterials composed of less toxic and even nontoxic metal elements including gold (Au)<sup>7-9</sup>, palladium (Pd)<sup>10</sup> and platinum (Pt)<sup>11-13</sup> could selectively kill bacterial cells without producing significant toxicity to human cells. The selective bacteria killing effect is ascribed to the distinctive oxidase- and peroxidase-like activities of noble metal nanomaterials with environmental selectivity.<sup>14</sup> However, the bactericidal activity of noble metal nanomaterials remain needs to be improved.<sup>15-17</sup> Compared with their single-component counterparts, bimetallic core-shell structured nanoparticles<sup>18-20</sup> can provide more attractive opportunities for marked enhancement in specific catalytic activity due to the synergistic effects between the core and the shell. Based on the above illustration, we focused on core-shell structured bimetallic nanomaterials and investigate how the morphology affects their specific activity, achieving further maximization of the bactericidal activity of nanostructures by tailoring the shape of the core.

It is worth noting that the optimization of nanomaterials for antibacterial activities is based on laboratory conditions. Once exposure to natural environments, nanomaterials would inevitably interact with natural organic matter (NOM), which is abundant in natural soils and ground waters with the range of a few mg/L to a few hundred mg/L. It has been reported that NOM could affect the physicochemical properties and biological activities of nanomaterials.<sup>21-24</sup> For example, the stability of nanomaterials could be enhanced in the presence of NOM through electrostatic repulsion or steric hindrance.<sup>25, 26</sup> The nanomaterials coating with NOM showed minor toxicity to bacteria through shielding reactive sites.<sup>27</sup>

The oxidase-like activity of nanomaterials has been shown to be major determinants of the antimicrobial efficiency of noble metal-based nanomaterials. However, the impact of NOM on the enzyme-like activity of nanomaterials has not yet been reported. Therefore, studies on how NOM affects the enzyme-like activity of nanomaterials and the interaction of nanomaterials with bacterial cells is important to understand the antibacterial behaviors of nanomaterials in natural environments. Our findings demonstrate how to tailor the morphology of the core-shell nanostructures to improve their bactericidal activity and provide the observation on NOM-induced changes to the bactericidal behaviors of nanostructures in natural environments. The corresponding mechanisms were thoroughly analyzed by investigating the changes of the enzyme-like activity of nanomaterials and the internalization of nanomaterials by bacterial cells.

**Results and discussion**

**Synthesis and characterization of Pd-based nanostructures.** According to transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and high-resolution TEM (HRTEM) images (Figure S1a&b), Pd cubes with an average edge length of 14 nm were successfully prepared. Subsequently, the core-shell structured Pd@Ir cubes were fabricated using seed-mediated growth according to previous studies.<sup>28,29</sup> The core-shell structured Pd@Ir nanoparticles clearly retained their cubic shape after the growth of Ir (Figure S1c). The average edge length of Pd@Ir cubes was 1.2 nm larger than that of Pd cubes (Figure 1a), suggesting the formation of Ir shells. Next, 6 nm Pd cubes were synthesized (Figure S2a) and used as seeds to prepare Pd octahedra. The as-obtained 14 nm Pd octahedra enclosed by {111} were successfully prepared (Figure S2b&c) and then used as seeds to prepare core-shell structured Pd@Ir octahedra *via* seed-mediated growth. According to the TEM (Figure S2d) and HRTEM (Figure 1e) images, the as-prepared Pd@Ir nanoparticles retained their octahedral shape after the deposition of Ir atoms.

High-angle annular dark-field scanning TEM (HAADF-STEM) images together with energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) mapping clearly show a color difference between the core (Pd, red) and shell (Ir, green), confirming the successful construction of a core-shell structure (Figure 1b-d&1f-h). Moreover, we determined the molar ratio of Pd to Ir using inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS, Element 2, Thermo Finnigan, Germany), and the average number of Ir atomic layers was calculated to be 2. The successful creation of core-shell structured Pd@Ir nanoparticles with well-defined morphology provides an ideal platform to study the morphological effects on the specific catalytic performance of nanomaterials.

**Antibacterial activity of Pd-based nanostructures.** We evaluated the oxidase-like activity of Pd-based nanostructures using 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) as a substrate.<sup>30, 31</sup> As shown in Figure S3, compared to Pd@Ir cubes, Pd@Ir octahedra caused a significant increase in the catalytic oxidation of TMB ($p < 0.01$). We next examined the amount of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> generated during the oxidation of the substrate and found that compared to TMB alone, the addition of Pd@Ir cubes caused a significant increase in the amount of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> ($p < 0.05$), and Pd@Ir octahedra extremely significantly increased the generation of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> ($p < 0.01$) (Figure 2a). Next, we carried out steady-state kinetic analysis to quantitatively evaluate the oxidase-like activity of Pd-based nanocrystals.<sup>32, 33</sup> Typical Michaelis-Menten curves were observed within the suitable range of TMB concentration (Figure 2b) and a series of kinetic parameters were summarized in Table S1. The maximum initial velocity ($V_{max}$) value of TMB oxidation catalyzed by Pd@Ir cubes is approximately 1.7 times higher than that catalyzed by Pd cubes, whereas Pd@Ir octahedra increase the $V_{max}$ up to 4.4-fold. Overall, the octahedral nanostructure displays superior oxidase-like activity to the cubic nanostructure. Moreover, these Pd-based nanostructures exhibit excellent catalytic activity over a broad temperature range, indicating thermal stability, and weakly acidic conditions are beneficial to their oxidase-like activity (Figure S4).<sup>34-36</sup> Additionally, the peroxidase-like property of Pd-based nanostructures was evaluated through TMB oxidation in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Consistently, the Pd@Ir octahedra exhibit the most superior peroxidase-like activity among the three Pd-based nanostructures (Figure S5).

Previous studies have shown that the distinctive oxidase-like and peroxidase-like activities endow noble metal nanomaterials with potential bactericidal effects.<sup>10</sup> Therefore, plate counting method was utilized to determine the antibacterial ability of the as-fabricated Pd-based nanostructures (100 μg/mL) against both *Escherichia coli* (*E. coli*) and *Staphylococcus aureus* (*S. aureus*). As shown in Figure 2c&d, Pd cubes showed almost no killing capacity against bacteria, whereas Pd@Ir nanostructures displayed significantly enhanced bactericidal activity compared with that in the control group ($p < 0.01$). In particular, Pd@Ir octahedra were able to most effectively kill both *E. coli* and *S. aureus* ($p < 0.001$), in consistent with the order of the enzyme-like activity. A fluorescence-based live/dead assay was performed to directly assess the bactericidal activity of Pd-based nanostructures and the Pd@Ir octahedra exhibited the most powerful bactericidal activity, as evidenced by the dominant red fluorescent signal (Figure S6). Next, we used a fluorescent probe to evaluate the intracellular ROS level. As expected, the ROS level inside bacterial cells exposed to Pd@Ir nanostructures was significantly enhanced ($p < 0.01$), and especially an extremely significant increase was found upon exposure to Pd@Ir octahedra ($p < 0.001$), as evidenced by the strong fluorescence signal (Figure S7). Overproduction of ROS could induce oxidative damage to important biomolecules, and thus we determined the content of the oxidation byproducts, including malondialdehyde (MDA) and protein carbonyl groups. As shown in Figure S8, treatment with Pd@Ir nanostructures results in a significant enhancement of the oxidation byproducts ($p < 0.05$), and especially addition of Pd@Ir octahedra induces an extremely significant enhancement ($p < 0.01$).

In order to observe the morphological change in bacterial cells, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was employed. As shown in Figure 2e, Pd cubes induced a slight morphological change, whereas the bacterial membrane integrity was lost upon exposure to Pd@Ir nanostructures. In particular, the bacterial cell was severely damaged after treatment with Pd@Ir octahedra, showing a complete loss of membrane integrity. Next, the concentration of leaked proteins and DNA/RNA were monitored to study the integrity of the bacterial membrane.<sup>37, 38</sup> As shown in Figure S9, treatment with Pd@Ir nanostructures led to significant increase in the concentrations of leaked proteins ($p < 0.01$), and in particular, the Pd@Ir octahedra induced an extremely significant increase ($p < 0.001$). All these results suggest the most serious disruption of the membrane integrity was induced by Pd@Ir octahedra.

Next, in order to examine the broad spectrum of effectiveness, we tested the antibacterial activity of Pd-based nanomaterials against other bacterial strains, including *Bacillus subtilis* and *Salmonella enteritidis*. The Pd@Ir octahedra display excellent antibacterial activity in a concentration-dependent manner (Figure S10). Next, we monitored the bacterial growth kinetics to evaluate the long-term impact of Pd@Ir octahedra. The bacterial growth has been effectively hindered by nanomaterials even at a low concentration of 25 μg/mL at 12 h, and 200 μg/mL nanomaterials could completely inhibited the bacterial growth (Figure S11). All these results confirm that the Pd@Ir octahedra have wide-spectrum and high-efficiency antibacterial activity. Moreover, we examined the viability of human cells upon exposure to the as-fabricated Pd-based nanostructures. As shown in Figure S12, no obvious toxicity was observed in human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs), indicating excellent biocompatibility. Additionally, we assessed the stability of Pd-based nanostructures in biological environments. According to TEM images, no obvious morphological change could be observed for the core-shell nanostructures in or out of bacteria, indicating the well stability (Figure S13). Moreover, the dissolution experiment shows that only extremely small amounts of Pd<sup>2+</sup> or Ir<sup>3+</sup> were released from the Pd-based nanostructures (Figure S14). All these results confirm the well stability of Pd-based nanostructures in biological media.

**Antibacterial activity of Pd@Ir octahedra in the presence of NOM.** Once exposure to the natural environments, nanomaterials would inevitably interact with NOM, resulting in a change to their bactericidal behaviors.<sup>39-42</sup> To evaluate the antibacterial activity of nanostructures in the presence of NOM, Pd@Ir octahedra and HA were selected as model subjects.<sup>43-45</sup> As shown in Figure 3a, the survival rate of *E. coli* cells treated with Pd@Ir nanostructures (25 μg/mL) in the absence or presence of HA (5 mg/L) were 29.5 ± 3.6% and 42.9 ± 6.3%, respectively. There was a significant increase in the bactericidal activity between nanostructures alone and nanostructures combined with HA ($p < 0.05$). Moreover, a similar trend was observed for *S. aureus* cells (Figure 3b). After that, a fluorescent-based live/dead assay was performed, and much stronger red fluorescence was observed in the cells treated with Pd@Ir combined with HA compared to that treated with Pd@Ir alone (Figure S15). These results demonstrated that the bactericidal activity of nanostructures could be effectively enhanced in the presence of NOM.

Next, SEM was employed for directly observing the morphological change of bacterial cells. No obvious morphological change was found in the HA-treated group, both types of bacteria having smooth and intact cell walls (Figure 3c). Pd@Ir nanostructures induced more severe damage to bacterial membrane in the presence of HA, showing a complete loss of membrane integrity. In addition, the concentration of leaked proteins and DNA/RNA were monitored to study the integrity of the bacterial membrane. As shown in Figure S16a&b, the concentration of the leaked proteins of the cells treated with nanomaterials in the presence of HA was significantly higher than that of the cells treated with nanomaterials alone ($p < 0.01$). A similar trend was found in the leakage of DNA/RNA (Figure S16c&d), suggesting the most serious disruption of the bacterial membrane induced by Pd@Ir nanostructures in the presence of HA.

**Influence of NOM on the antibacterial behavior of nanomaterials.** In order to explore the possible mechanism of the potentiated bactericidal effect of nanomaterials in the presence of HA, we assessed the intracellular levels of ROS using the fluorescent probe DCFH-DA.<sup>46</sup> The untreated and HA-treated bacterial cells all showed extremely weak fluorescence (Figure 3d). Treatment with Pd@Ir nanostructures resulted in a significant increase in the fluorescence intensity. Importantly, the ROS level inside cells treated by nanostructures combined with HA was significantly enhanced, as evidenced by the strong fluorescence signal. These above results suggested that the potentiated bactericidal effect of nanomaterials in the presence of NOM is mediated by ROS.

Next, in order to explain the elevated ROS level induced by Pd@Ir nanostructures in the presence of HA, we examined the oxidase-like activity of nanostructures in the absence and presence of HA. Interestingly, the presence of HA clearly enhances the catalytic activity of the nanostructures towards TMB oxidation, as evidenced by the time-dependent increase in the characteristic absorbance (Figure 4a&b). As shown in Figure 4c, Pd@Ir nanostructures combined with HA showed a significant increase ($p < 0.05$) in the oxidation of TMB at absorbance of 652 nm compared to the nanostructures alone. Notably, compared to the group treated with nanostructures alone, a significant increase in the amount of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was generated in the group treated with nanostructures and HA ($p < 0.05$) (Figure 4d). A similar trend was found in the oxidation of other substrate, such as OPD and ABTS (Figure 4e&f). These findings suggested that HA could enhance the oxidase-like activity of nanomaterials.

Previous studies have showed NOM possibly induces changes to the cellular internalization of nanomaterials,<sup>47, 48</sup> prompting us to compare the internalization of Pd@Ir nanostructures by the two bacterial groups in the absence or presence of HA. As shown in Figure 5a&b, green and red in the element mapping were used to map iridium and palladium, respectively. It is clearly observed that much more nanomaterials were localized in the interior of the cells in the presence of HA that that in the absence of HA, suggesting that the presence of HA increase the cellular internalization of nanomaterials. To fully verify the differential endocytosis potential of Pd@Ir nanoparticles in the absence or presence of HA, we employed inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) to analyze the endocytosis level of nanoparticles. The bacterial cells were washed repeatedly to remove the noninternalized nanomaterials, and then the harvested cells were lysed for ICP-MS analysis. By calculation, about 10% nanoparticles were internalized into bacteria, whereas the content of nanomaterials internalized by bacteria reached more than 20% in the presence of HA (Figure S17), indicating the increased internalization of nanomaterials.

Therefore, we provide a proposed mechanistic explanation for the influence of HA on the bactericidal activity of nanomaterials as shown in Figure 6. The presence of HA not only enhances the oxidase-like activity of nanomaterials, but also promotes the cellular internalization of nanomaterials. As a result, elevated level of ROS is generated, which induces serious oxidative damage to biomacromolecules such as DNA, protein and lipids. Therefore, the presence of HA potentiates the bactericidal effect of Pd@Ir nanostructures.

**Conclusions**

Noble metal-based nanostructures have emerged as a promising class of antibacterial materials due to their excellent biocompatibility. This study has found that tuning the shape of the core is an effective approach for optimizing the bactericidal performance of core-shell nanostructures, providing an understanding on how the morphology of nanostructures affects their bactericidal performance. The presence of natural organic matter possibly induces a change to the physiochemical properties and even biological activities of nanomaterials. The present study provide an observation on the enhanced oxidase-like activity of nanomaterials induced by natural organic matter. Moreover, the existence of natural organic matter has an influence on the interaction of nanomaterials with bacterial cell, promoting the internalization of nanomaterials in bacterial cells. As a result, the bactericidal performance of nanomaterials is efficiently potentiated in the presence of natural organic matter. These results are valuable for elucidating the bactericidal behaviors of nanomaterials in natural environments.

**Experimental Section**

**Materials.** Sodium hexachloroiridate (III) hydrate (Na<sub>3</sub>IrCl<sub>6</sub>•xH<sub>2</sub>O), ethylene glycol (EG, 99%), sodium tetrachloropalladate (II) (Na<sub>2</sub>PdCl<sub>4</sub>, $\geq$ 99.99%), potassium bromide (KBr, 99%), formaldehyde solution (HCHO, 37%) L-ascorbic acid (AA, 99%), poly(vinylpyrrolidone) (PVP, MW=55000 kDa), 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) and 2,2′-azinobis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) diammonium salt (ABTS) were all obtained from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). O-Phenylenediamine (OPD, 98%) and Humic acid (HA) was obtained from Alfa Aesar (Beijing, China). The stock solutions (1 g/L) of HA were dissolved in deionized water and stored at 4 °C. H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (30%), potassium chloride (KCl), and other reagents were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). All aqueous solutions were prepared using deionized (18.2 MΩ) water.

**Synthesis of Pd cubes and Pd octahedra.** The Pd cubic and octahedral seeds were synthesized according to previous literature reports.<sup>49, 50</sup> (1) Synthesis of 6 nm and 14 nm Pd cubes. 105 mg of PVP, 60 mg of AA, 5 mg of KBr and 185 mg of KCl were dissolved in 8 mL of ultrapure water at room temperature, and the mixture was preheated at 80 °C for 10 min. Then, 3 mL of Na<sub>2</sub>PdCl<sub>4</sub> solution (57 mg) was added. The reaction was kept at 80 °C for 3 h and then cooled to room temperature. After that, the sample was washed with acetone once and water three times *via* centrifugation. Finally, the obtained Pd cubic seeds were stored in water. The 14 nm Pd cubes were synthesized by a similar method, except that only 300 mg of KBr was introduced. (2) Synthesis of 14 nm Pd octahedra. 8 mL of 13 mg mL<sup>-1</sup> PVP aqueous solution, 100 μL of formaldehyde and 300 μL of 6 nm Pd cubic seeds (1.8 mg mL<sup>-1</sup>) were placed in a three-necked flask and preheated at 60 °C for 10 min. Then, 3 mL of 8 mg mL<sup>-1</sup> Na<sub>2</sub>PdCl<sub>4</sub> aqueous solution was added by pipette. The reaction temperature was maintained at 60 °C for 3 h. After cooling to room temperature, the sample was washed several times with water and acetone. Finally, the as-prepared Pd octahedra were dispersed in ethylene glycol (EG) for further use.

**Synthesis of Pd@Ir core-shell cubes/octahedra.** The core-shell structured Pd@Ir nanocrystals were synthesized according to the published literature. 100 mg of PVP and 60 mg of AA were dissolved in 10 mL of EG. Then, the mixture was added to a three-necked flask, which had been preheated in an oil bath at 100 °C for one hour. Then, 0.8 mL of 14 nm Pd cubic seeds (4.0 mg mL<sup>-1</sup>) was added. Meanwhile, the reaction temperature was increased quickly, and when the temperature reached to 200 °C, 4 mL of Na<sub>3</sub>IrCl<sub>6</sub>•xH<sub>2</sub>O solution (0.5 mg mL<sup>-1</sup>, dissolved in EG) was injected into the flask at a rate of 0.5 mL h<sup>-1</sup>. The reaction was continued for 10 min after the end of the injection. The flask was transferred to an ice water bath to stop the reaction. The product was collected by centrifugation through washing several times with water. Finally, the sample was dispersed in water for storage and analysis.

**Characterization of Pd@Ir core-shell cubes/octahedra.** TEM (Tecnai G20 spirit BioTwin, FEI, USA) and HRTEM (Tecnai G2 F20, FEI, USA) were used to characterize the morphology of Pd@Ir core-shell nanostructures. EDX elemental mapping was performed using a Talos F200X microscope (FEI, USA). The contents of metal elements were determined by ICP-MS (Element 2, Thermo Finnigan, Germany).

**Oxidase-like activity of Pd-based nanostructures.** TMB, OPD and ABTS was used as substrates of catalytic oxidation to evaluate the oxidase-like activity of Pd@Ir octahedra in the presence or absence of HA. For TMB oxidation, 20 μL of Pd@Ir octahedra (5 mM, atom concentration) pre-mixed with 10 μL of HA (1 g/L) and 10 μL of TMB (50 mM) were added into 2 mL of NaAc/HAc buffer solution (0.1 M, pH = 4.0). The absorption spectra of reaction system were recorded at different times using a UV spectrometer (UV-3600, Shimadzu, Japan). Similar to the catalytic oxidation of TMB, the oxidation of OPD and ABTS by Pd@Ir octahedra produced special color with major absorbance peaks with 450 and 420 nm, respectively. The absorbance of peaks in 10 min were recorded through a Synergy NEO microplate reader (BioTek, USA), respectively. In addition, the oxidation of substrates was also employed to compared the oxidase-like activity of Pd-based nanocrystals. Experiments were carried out using 0.1 mM Pd-based nanocrystals.

**Kinetic measurements of TMB oxidation.** Firstly, 20 μL of Pd-based nanostructures suspension (5 mM) was dispersed in 200 μL of NaAc/HAc buffer. TMB at a range of concentrations was introduced into the reaction system. Then, the absorbance of the reaction solution at 652 nm was measured through a Synergy NEO microplate reader (BioTek, USA) every 30 seconds for a total of 3 min. The apparent kinetic parameters can be calculated according to the following equation:

$$v = V_{max} \times [S] / (K_m + [S])$$ (1)

$v$ represents the initial velocity, and $V_{max}$ is defined as the maximum reaction velocity. $[S]$ is the concentration of the substrate, and $K_m$ represents the Michaelis constant.<sup>49</sup>

**Determination of hydrogen peroxide.** The product of catalytic oxidation by Pd@Ir octahedra (5 μg/mL) in the presence or absence of HA (5 mg/L) was detected using a hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) assay kit (Beyotime Institute of Biotechnology, Shanghai, China). Pd@Ir octahedra were centrifuged at 14800 rpm for 15 min to prevent the influence of nanomaterials to the colorimetric reaction. The supernatant solution was incubated with the detection solution for 30 min. Then, the absorbance of the reaction solution at 560 nm was detected by a Synergy NEO microplate reader (BioTek, USA). Experiments were repeated three times. As for the oxidase-like activity of Pd-based nanocrystals, the level of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in products was also examined.

**Bacterial culture and antibacterial activity test.** Gram-positive bacteria *S. aureus* (ATCC 12600) and Gram-negative bacteria *E. coli* (ATCC 25922) were grown in tryptic soy broth (TSB) medium and lysogeny broth (LB) medium, respectively. Then, bacterial suspension (10<sup>9</sup> CFU mL<sup>-1</sup>) was incubated with Pd-based nanostructures (100 μg/mL) for 2 h. After incubation, the bacterial suspension was diluted to 10<sup>3</sup> CFU mL<sup>-1</sup>, and then 100 μL of bacterial suspension was transferred to a solid medium for growth at 37 °C overnight. Finally, the numbers of colonies were counted, and each assay was carried out in triplicate. We also investigated the survival rate of bacterial cells treated by Pd-based nanostructures (25 μg/mL) in the presence or absence of HA (5 mg/L).

**Live/dead fluorescent staining.** After treatment, the bacteria were incubated with propidium iodide (PI) and SYTO 9 dye (Invitrogen Detection Technologies, USA) in the dark for 30 min. Next, 5 μL of bacterial suspension was pipetted onto a microscope slide and then covered with a coverslip. Bacterial cells were observed using a confocal laser microscopy (FV1200. OLYMPUS, Japan).

**Preparation of bacterial samples for SEM.** After treatment, the bacterial cells were harvested and fixed with 2.5% glutaraldehyde at room temperature for 2 h. After washing with water, the bacterial cells were dehydrated with graded ethanol. Finally, the bacterial suspension was dropped on a silicon wafer and dried. The morphology of the bacterial cells was visualized using scanning electron microscope (SEM, S-4700, Hitachi, Japan).

**TEM analysis of intracellular distribution of nanomaterials.** After treatment, bacterial cells were washed by water several times and fixed with 2.5% glutaraldehyde at room temperature for 2 h. Then, the samples were washed with PBS, and postfixed with 1% osmium tetroxide. After fixation, specimens were dehydrated with 50%, 70%, 95%, 100% ethanol for 15 min each, and infiltrated with Spurr’s resin for 2 h. The resin blocks were cut into ultrathin sections by ultramicrotome. The ultrathin sections of bacterial cells were observed using TEM equipped with EDS mapping (Tecnai G2 spirit BioTwin, FEI, 120 kV).

**Evaluation of the leakage of intracellular components.** The concentration of extracellular protein of the bacteria was measured using BCA protein assay kit (Beyotime Biotechnology, Shanghai). The released DNA/RNA was estimated by measuring the absorbance at 260 nm using a spectrophotometer (uLITE, BioDrop, England).

**Determination of ROS production in bacteria.** The intracellular ROS level was measured using the fluorescence label 2′,7′-dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate (DCFH-DA, Invitrogen Detection Technologies). In brief, after incubation, the bacteria were stained with DCFH-DA dye (10 μM) for 30 min in the dark and washed 3 times with PBS. Finally, fluorescent images were observed using a confocal laser microscope (FV1200, Olympus, Japan).

**Statistical analysis.** All the numerical results were shown as mean ± standard deviation (SD). Significance was determined using student’s two-tailed t tests and denoted by an asterisk (*, set at $p < 0.05$) unless otherwise stated.

**Supplementary Methods**

**Peroxidase-like property of of Pd-based nanostructures.** For the oxidation of TMB with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, 10 μL of Pd-based nanomaterials (0.1 mM), 1 μL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (10 M) and 20 μL of TMB (50 mM) were added into 2 mL of NaAc/HAc buffer solution (0.1 M, pH = 4.0). Kinetic measurements were carried out by monitoring the absorbance at 652 nm on a UV-vis spectroscopy (UV-3600, Shimadzu, Japan).

**Bacterial culture and bacterial growth kinetics.** Gram-positive bacteria *Bacillus subtilis* (CMCC 63501) and gram-negative bacteria *Salmonella enteritidis* (CMCC 50115) were grown in tryptic soy broth (TSB) medium and lysogeny broth (LB) medium, respectively. In a typical experiment, 20 μL of different concentrations of Pd@Ir nanoparticles suspension was added into 180 μL of approximately 10<sup>6</sup> CFU/ml of bacterial suspension. The growth curves were determined through measuring OD600 using a Synergy NEO microplate reader (BioTek, Synergy NEO, USA) at 37 °C for 12 h.

**Lipid peroxidation.** The content of malondialdehyde (MDA) was quantified using a commercial kit (Beyotime Biotechnology, China). After treatments, the bacterial cells were harvested and lysed using an ultrasonicator. After centrifugation, the supernatant was collected and thiobarbituric acid (TBA) was added at 100 °C for 15 min. After being cooled down, the solution was centrifuged at 1000 g for 10 min. Finally, the absorbance of the obtained supernatant at 535 nm was determined by a Synergy NEO microplate reader (BioTek, Synergy NEO, USA).

**Oxidative damage to protein.** Protein carbonyl groups were assessed using protein carbonyl calorimetric assay (Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute, China), according to the manufacturer’s instructions. In brief, the bacterial cells were lysed and proteins were extracted using 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine. After centrifugation, the protein−hydrozone pellets were dissolved and the absorbance of solution was measured at 370 nm.

**Dissolution experiment.** To evaluate the release of Pd<sup>2+</sup> and Ir<sup>3+</sup> from Pd-based nanomaterials, 100 μg/mL of Pd-based nanomaterials were placed in dialysis bags (MWCO, 3500 Da) and dialyzed against 20 mL of H<sub>2</sub>O at room temperature with constant shaking. The dialysis solution was taken out and replaced with the same volume of fresh H<sub>2</sub>O to maintain submersed conditions at desired time intervals. Finally, the dialysis solutions were collected and analyzed using inductively-coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS, ELEMENT 2, Thermo, USA).
