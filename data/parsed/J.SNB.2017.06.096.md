# Novel biotemplated MnO<sub>2</sub> 1D nanozyme with controllable peroxidase-like activity and unique catalytic mechanism and its application for glucose sensing

## ABSTRACT

To get recyclable and highly active peroxidase mimetics, MnO<sub>2</sub> nanowires (NWs) were prepared by using genetically engineered filamentous phages as biotemplates. The morphology of NWs was tailored by controlling the synthesis temperature. The synthesis conditions could influence the peroxidase-like activity of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs. The catalytic mechanism of the prepared MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs was investigated, which is Ping-pong BiBi electron transfer mechanism, different from Fenton reaction and some other mechanisms of nanozymes reported so far. Based on the excellent catalytic activity and stability, MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs coupled with glucose oxidase were applied to the colorimetric sensing glucose, which showed the good re-utilization performance and reproducibility. The MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs are promising to be applied to the bioanalysis and industrial catalyst.

## 1. Introduction

Peroxidase, as an important enzyme, is capable of catalyzing the oxidation of organic electron donors (such as aromatic compounds) with hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) as the oxidant, which generate change of color or reduce toxicity of aromatic compounds [1]. Based on this phenomenon, peroxidase has been widely applied in glucose detection kit [2], immunoassay kit [3] and wastewater treatment [4,5]. However, as a kind of nature protein, peroxidase has many disadvantages such as tedious purification and poor stability [6–9]. With the development of nanotechnology, the enzyme-like nanomaterials (also called “nanozymes”) have been attracting more and more attention as substitutes of peroxidase, due to their unique superiorities such as simple preparation, high stability, high catalytic activity and good cost-effectiveness [7,10]. Thereinto, many metal oxides have been widely used as nanozymes and subsequently applied on the bioanalysis [11], such as Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> magnetic nanoparticles (NPs) [12–14], Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs [15] and V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>-ordered mesoporous carbons (V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>-OMC) [16]. However, the synthesis of these materials generally requires complex process, hydrothermal treatment, high-temperature calcination or introduction of strong oxidant, which might further restrict their application. To solve these issues, biotemplate synthesis method emerges as alternative approach, where the various biomaterials (such as phage, protein and leaf) are used as templates to nucleate and grow inorganic nanocrystals [17–20]. So far, there are few reports on biotemplate-based nanozymes.

It’s worth noting that the most of reported peroxidase-like metal oxides are either easy to aggregate due to the magnetism (such as Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>), or difficult to be centrifugally recycled due to the nano-size at three dimensions (such as NPs and quantum dots) [21]. Fortunately, some one-dimensional (1D) non-magnetic metal oxides can solve these problem, such as V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> nanowires (NWs) [22]. Due to the nano-size at two dimensions, the NWs are easy to centrifugally recycled [22]. Recently, the 1D nano-structured morphologies have drawn more and more attention among the different morphologies of nanomaterials, due to controlled structure for electron transfer, surface effect and size effect [23–26].

Manganese dioxide (MnO<sub>2</sub>), as a kind of transition metal oxide, has been widely studied in the field of catalysts, such as chemical catalyst [27], electrocatalyst [28,29], and photocatalyst [30], due to their low cost, high catalytic activity, non-toxicity and environmental friendliness [31]. Recently, we developed MnO<sub>2</sub> nanosheets using phage capsid protein pVIII as template, which exhibited peroxidase-like activity [32]. However, the catalytic mechanism of MnO<sub>2</sub> has not been completely clear [33,34]. To our knowledge, different kinds of nanozymes may have different catalytic mechanism. For example, peroxidase-like Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs exhibit Fenton reaction mechanism, where •OH free radicals are generated by ferrous ions on the surface of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> [35–37], while V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> NWs exhibit catalytic mechanism different from Fenton reaction, where metastable peroxo intermediate is formed at the first catalytic step [22]. For intensive study and application of nanozymes, the investigation about catalytic mechanisms of different metal oxide nanomaterials is still very necessary.

In this contribution, we prepared MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs by using genetically engineered phages as biotemplates. The morphology of NWs was tailored by controlling the synthesis temperature. Further, the influence of synthesis condition of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs on the peroxidase-like activity was investigated, and the catalytic mechanism was researched. Due to their excellent catalytic activity and stability, MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs were coupled with glucose oxidase (GOx, EC No. 1.1.3.4) for the colorimetric detection of glucose, which showed the good re-utilization performance.

## 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. Chemicals and materials

M13KO7 helper was purchased from New England BioLabs (Beijing, China). Polyethylene glycol (PEG), manganese acetate (MnAc<sub>2</sub>), hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, 30%) and glucose were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co (Shanghai, China). 2,2′-azino-bis (3-ethylbenzo-thiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) diammonium salt (ABTS) and GOx were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) was purchased from TCI (Shanghai, China). All other reagents were of the analytical grade and all aqueous solutions were prepared with Milli-Q water (18.2 MΩ cm).

### 2.2. Preparation of engineered phage M13 and M13-templated MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs

The engineered phage M13 was prepared according to the previous work [24]. Briefly, tetraglutamine (-EEEE) and tetraarginine (-RRRR) were respectively displayed on the N-terminus of the pVIII protein on M13 (producing so-called M13-E4 and M13-R4) And then, engineered M13 was amplified by superinfection of helper phage M13KO7 and purified facilely with PEG/NaCl.

Typically, the M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs were synthesized according to the previous work [24]. Briefly, NaOH solution was added into 1 mL of MnAc<sub>2</sub> aqueous solution (1 mM) containing M13-E4 (10<sup>11</sup> vir/mL) to adjust the pH to ~10. After stirring for 6–8 h at room temperature, the black deposits were filtered by 0.02 μm membrane to remove unreacted MnAc<sub>2</sub> and NaOH. In the control experiments, M13-R4 and wild type M13 (M13-WT) were used to replace M13-E4, respectively and other experimental conditions were consistent with the above.

For TEM observation, a dilute suspension was dropped on a carbon-coated copper grid, washed with distilled water, and dried. The morphology of the as-prepared NWs was observed by the transmission electron microscopy (TEM, HITACHI H-7650).

### 2.3. Standard assay for peroxidase-like activity of M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs

To investigate the peroxidase-like activity of the M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and ABTS was chosen as the substrates, where ABTS could be oxidized by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to a chromogenic product (ABTS<sup>•+</sup>) in the catalysis of the peroxidase mimetics (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + ABTS → H<sub>2</sub>O + ABTS<sup>•+</sup>) [13]. In a typical experiment, the reaction system (200 μL) contained MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs (1 μg), H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 mM) and ABTS (0.5 mM) in acetate buffer (100 mM, pH 3.5) at room temperature, and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was lastly added to start the reaction. The absorbance of ABTS<sup>•+</sup> at 416 nm ($\epsilon_{416nm} = 36\text{ mM}^{-1}\text{ cm}^{-1}$) [38] was continuously measured for 5 min using a microplate reader (Hitachi) and initial reaction rates were calculated from the linear portions of the absorbance-time curves.

### 2.4. Peroxidase-like activity characterization of M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs

For pH optimization, the catalytic activity of the M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs was measured by the above assay, except of the varying buffer pH (2.0–12.0). The pH values were controlled in the 2.0–12.0 range by using different buffers (100 mM): glycine−HCl buffer (pH 2.0), acetate buffer (pH 3.0–5.0), phosphate buffer (PB, pH 6.0–8.0), Tris–HCl buffer (pH 9.0–10.0) and NaHCO<sub>3</sub>–NaOH buffer (pH 11.0–12.0). To research the temperature-stability of the M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, NWs was incubated in acetate buffer (100 mM, pH 3.5) at different temperatures from 4 °C to 90 °C for 2 h, separately. After that, the solutions were cooled on the ice and then the remaining relative activities were determined by the above standard assay. The maximum activity in each group of experiments was defined as 100% relative activity.

### 2.5. Kinetics analysis for peroxidase-like activity

To study the kinetics parameters, the catalytic activity of the MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs was measured by the above assay, except for the fixed concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (3 mM) and various concentration of ABTS (0.001–1 mM) as well as the fixed concentration of ABTS (1 mM) and various concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0.01–3 mM). The values of $V_{max}$ and $K_m$ were calculated by fitting the data to the Michaelis-Menten equation: $v = V_{max} \times [S] / (K_m + [S])$, where $v$ is the reaction velocity, $V_{max}$ is maximal reaction velocity, $[S]$ is the concentration of substrate and $K_m$ is the Michaelis constant. For the reaction mechanism, the catalytic activity of the MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs was measured by the above assay, except that the concentrations of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0.25, 0.5 and 1 mM) and ABTS (0.05, 0.1 and 0.25 mM) were varied one at a time. Subsequently, the kinetic data were adjusted to the Lineweaver-Burk double reciprocal plot.

### 2.6. ESR spectroscopy measurements

The electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) experiments using a Bruker A300 EPR spectrometer (Bruker Instrument, Germany) operating in the X-band (9.842 GHz) at room temperature. For hydroxyl radical (•OH) detection under various conditions, 5,5-dimethyl-1-pyrroline N-oxide (DMPO) was adopted to trap •OH to form the spin adduct DMPO/•OH. The H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> solution was mixed with DMPO in acidic buffer (pH 4.0) and was detected after the addition of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs or control solutions.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. Synthesis and characterization of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs

To simply and controllably prepare one-dimensional MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, the genetically engineered M13 filamentous phages were used as biotemplates. As schematically shown in Fig. 1A, after incubation M13 with Mn<sup>2+</sup>, MnO<sub>2</sub> crystals were generated on phage scaffolds by the spontaneous oxidation of Mn<sup>2+</sup> in alkaline solution at room temperature [24]. MnO<sub>2</sub> nanocrystals were uniformly mineralized along the length of phage and the mineralized phages remained the filamentous morphology with the diameter of 30–60 nm (Fig. 1B). The proposed synthesis strategy is green, facile and efficient. The additional advantage of this strategy is that the nanostructure of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs can be manipulated by controlling the reaction conditions. However, the nucleation and growth of MnO<sub>2</sub> at 4 °C resulted in the assembly of discrete NPs on the phage scaffolds (Fig. 1C), suggesting the significance of synthesis temperature.

![Fig. 2. Typical absorption curves of reaction solutions catalyzed by the as-prepared M13-E4@MnO2 NWs and the controls after 5 min in acetate buffer (100 mM, pH 4.0) containing 0.5 mM ABTS (A) and TMB (B).](figure2.png)

### 3.2. Discovery of the peroxidase-like activity of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs

Generally, the peroxidase-like nanozymes can catalyze the chromogenic reaction where chromogenic electron donors (such as ABTS and TMB) are oxidated into chromogenic products by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> [1]. To verify the peroxidase-like activity of M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, the catalytic oxidation of ABTS by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as well as a series of control experiments were conducted and the absorbances of the reactions were detected from 380 to 500 nm (Fig. 2A). In the presence of bulk MnO<sub>2</sub>, the absorbance of reaction at 416 nm (the maximal absorbance of the oxidized ABTS, A<sub>416</sub>) was about 0.5. In the presence of bulk M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, the A<sub>416</sub> reached to about 1.0, which was 1 times higher than that of bulk MnO<sub>2</sub>, demonstrating that the small size effect, the large surface area and the homogeneous distribution of nanocrystals could improve the peroxidase-like activity of MnO<sub>2</sub> materials. To eliminate the possibility that the peroxidase-like activity resulted from M13, non-mineralized M13 were used instead of M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs. And then the absorption values did not obviously change, compared with the case in the absence of M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, indicating that M13 itself and free manganese ions did not have catalytic activity.

To further confirm the peroxidase-like activity of M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, another typical chromogenic substrate TMB was used for substitution of ABTS. After incubation with M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> for 5 min, TMB solution showed the maximum absorption peak at 652 nm (the maximal absorbance of the oxidized TMB, A<sub>652</sub>), while controls had not any spectrophotometric change (Fig. 2B). The result was similar to the case of ABTS. Therefore, M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs is capable of quickly catalyzing both TMB and ABTS, similar to natural peroxidase and other mimetics [22,39,40]. For the reaction catalyzed by peroxidase and other mimetics, both ABTS and TMB are two kind of common chromogenic substrates and are generally used for practical analytical applications [14,41,42]. In this work, the reaction solution containing ABTS showed the higher response of absorption value than the case of TMB. Hence, the subsequent experiments were carried out by using ABTS as chromogenic substrate.

### 3.3. Influence of synthesis conditions on the peroxidase-like activity of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs

To our knowledge, the morphology of nanomaterials is influenced by the synthesis conditions, and accordingly, the peroxidase-like activity is varied with the morphology of nanomaterials [21]. Therefore, the synthesis conditions could potentially influence the peroxidase-like activity. To investigate the influence of synthesis conditions on the peroxidase-like activity of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, we synthesized various morphologies of NWs by changing the surface charge and concentration of M13, and subsequently the peroxidase-like activities of as-synthesized NWs were evaluated. In our previous work, the surface property of M13 could be regulated by genetic engineering and the M13 phages from negative charge to positive charge in order were: tetraglutamate-fused phage (M13-E4), wild-type phage (M13-WT) and tetraarginine-fused phage (M13-R4), which agreed with the amount of agglomerated MnO<sub>2</sub> crystals (Figs. 3A,B and 1B) [24]. As shown in Fig. 3C, the mineralized phages from high activity to low activity in order were: M13-E4, M13-WT and M13-R4, which was consistent with uniformity of nanocrystals on phage and specific surface area. Compared with the various phage-templated MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, the template-free bulk MnO<sub>2</sub> had the lowest activity, indicating the nano-structure play a great role in peroxidase-like activity. Therefore, the higher activity implied the smaller specific surface area and less active sites. On the other hand, by changing the concentration of M13-E4, NWs with various morphologies could be obtained. The high concentration of M13-E4 led to the decreasing number of manganese ion binding with each phage and the discrete MnO<sub>2</sub> NPs on M13-E4 surfaces (Fig. 3D), while the low concentration of M13-E4 induced the decreasing amount of nucleation sites and the crosslinking of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs (Fig. 3E). Subsequently, the peroxidase-like activities of the as-synthesized nanowires were detected. NWs obtained from high concentration and medium concentration of M13-E4 showed similar activities, while NWs obtained from low concentration and medium concentration of M13-E4 showed lower activity due to the decreased surface area and activity sites (Fig. 3F). Considering that two kinds of NWs showed the similar activities, we prefer less dosage of M13-E4 phages to save the cost of precursors. Therefore, we chose the NWs obtained from medium concentration of M13-E4 for subsequent experiment. To date, there have been few reports about the influence of synthesis condition and morphology of nanomaterials on peroxidase-like activity [21]. These results would be important for insight into nanozymes.

### 3.4. Characterization of the peroxidase-like activity of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs

The effect of pH on the peroxidase-like activity of M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs was studied by varying the pH from 2 to 12 while the dosages of ABTS, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs were kept unchanged. As shown in Fig. 4A, the activity of NWs was dependent on pH, and the optimization curve had a sharp peak at pH 3.5, indicating the oxidation of ABTS catalyzed by the MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs only occurred under the weakly acidic conditions (Fig. 4A).

The temperature-dependent stability of the M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs was investigated with varying temperature from 4 °C to 90 °C (Fig. 4B). The catalytic activity of NWs retained almost unchanged after incubation at 4–70 °C for 2 h. The activity declined mildly with the further increase of temperature, and however, above 80% of the initial activity at 90 °C could still be retained, indicating that the NWs were very stable over the wide temperature range. Moreover, the peroxidase-like activity of the NWs had no obvious reduction after storage of two months. The excellent stability makes NWs superior to natural enzyme for practical applications.

### 3.5. Kinetics of catalytic reaction

To reasonably evaluate the catalytic performance of the peroxidase mimetics (M13-E4@MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs), the steady-state enzyme kinetic analysis was conducted at the optimal pH. Based on the Michaelis-Menten kinetics, the apparent kinetics parameters were obtained (Fig. 5A and B). The $K_M$ (ABTS) of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs (0.08 mM) was similar to that of V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>-OMCs (0.067 mM) [16] and lower than those values of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (0.12 mM) [43], and horse radish peroxidase (HRP) (0.270 mM) [44], suggesting that the MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs had better affinity for ABTS than natural enzymes. The $K_M$ (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) of the MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs (0.06 mM) was lower than those values of HRP (0.22 mM) [44], BSA-templated MnO<sub>2</sub> NPs (0.12 mM) [33] V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>-OMCs (0.16 mM), [16], Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs (154 mM) [12], suggesting the high affinity of the MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The $V_{max}$ (0.6 μM s<sup>−1</sup>) of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs was higher than that of V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>-OMCs (0.29 μM s<sup>−1</sup>) with the same weight (1 μg) in reaction system, indicating more catalytic active sites on the surface of the NWs.

For the investigation about the kinetics mechanism, typical Lineweaver-Burk plots were drawn and the lines were parallel with each other (Fig. 5C and D). this result was characteristic of Ping-pong BiBi mechanism, where the catalyst interacted with the first substrate and released the first product before reacted with the second substrate. Hence, an electron transfer mechanism (Fig. 5E) was proposed, where the first substrate (ABTS) gave an electron to MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs and was oxidized into chromogenic derivative, and subsequently MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs transfer the electron to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was reduced into H<sub>2</sub>O. The main difference between the above mechanism and the Fenton reaction is that the organic substrate (ABTS) is directly oxidized by catalyst (MnO<sub>2</sub>), rather than free radicals generated from H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. To verify the mechanism of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs as peroxidase mimetics, the ESR analysis was carried out. As shown in Fig. 5F, unlike Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs [36,37], MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs didn’t enhance free radicals •OH generation in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at acidic pH, indicating MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs didn’t react with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> by Fenton mechanism. In order to further confirm the as-proposed mechanism, the oxidizability of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs was researched by mixing MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs only with ABTS in the absence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. In acidic buffer purged with N<sub>2</sub> containing ABTS and MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, the chromogenic reaction happened, while the chromogenic reaction didn’t happen in the absence of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs (data not shown), suggesting ABTS could be directly oxidized by MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs. In contrast, the chromogenic substrate couldn’t oxidized by Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs, because the Fenton reaction depends on free radicals generated from H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> [45]. Therefore, the catalytic mechanism of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs is different with HRP [46], Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs [12], V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> NWs and V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>-OMCs [16].

### 3.6. Colorimetric sensing glucose

For the conventional glucose spectrophotometry detection kit, some natural peroxidases (such as horseradish peroxidase and seed coat soybean peroxidase) are generally used as catalysts of chromogenic reaction, where the intermediate product H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> can be quantificationally detected [47,48]. However, natural peroxidases suffer from many issues such as easy denaturation in complex conditions and difficulty to prepare and purify [6–9]. To overcome the drawbacks of peroxidases, we used the peroxidase mimetics (MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs) as the alternatives of peroxidases. Based on glucose oxidase (GOx) and as-prepared MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, the colorimetric detection of glucose with short analysis time, simple procedure and small sample volume could be realized in two separated and coupled steps: oxidation of glucose and detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Fig. 6A). The calibration curve is plotted based on the absorbance at 416 nm as a function of glucose concentration (Fig. 6B). The regression equation is $y = 0.6962x + 0.0663$ ($R^2 = 0.9965$) with the linear range of 0.01–2 mM and the limit of detection (LOD) of 2 μM which was lower than that of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> MNPs (30 μM) [13]. The linear range was wider than colorimetric glucose detection based on Au NPs (0.018–1.1 mM) [49] and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> MNPs (0.05–1 mM) [50] and the LOD was lower than that of V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>-OMCs (3.3 μM) [16], Au NPs (4 μM) [49] and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> MNPs (30 μM) [13].

![Fig. 6. (A) The scheme of colorimetric glucose detection based on MnO2 NWs and GOx. (B) The calibration curve for glucose by colorimetric assay.](figure6.png)

To investigate the re-utilization of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, 10 cycles of catalytic reaction were performed. After each reaction, MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs were simply centrifuged, washed and added into the fresh reaction solution. As shown in Fig. 7A, an average decay in activity of about 2% was observed and MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs remained above 80% of catalytic activity after 10th cycles, which was superior to the previous reports (about 50% after 10 cycles) [22]. The result revealed the excellent recyclable performance of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs, which highlights the excellent catalytic performance of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs and presents the prospect for the large-scale application. To further study the observable decrease in activity of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs after each cycle, MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs after the 10 reaction cycles were analyzed by TEM. As shown in Fig. 7B, the morphology of NWs remained intact and no surface erosion could be observed. However, the apparent organic layer seemingly cover on the surface of the NWs, suggesting the reaction by-products could be adsorbed on the surface of the NWs and reduce the number of active sites.

In addition, to research the reproducibility of the as-proposed strategy, 5 aliquots of glucose solutions (1 mM) were separately analyzed. The relative standard deviation was 2.3%, demonstrating the acceptable reproducibility. Moreover, no obvious change in the peroxidase-like activity of the MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs was observed after 60 days storage at room temperature, indicating the high storage stability.

## 4. Conclusions

In summary, the MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs with different morphologies were synthesized by using engineered M13 phages as biotemplates. For the first time, we found that as-prepared MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs possess high peroxidase-like activity and the activity was varied with synthesis conditions. The catalytic mechanism of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs were proved to be Ping-pong BiBi mechanism investigated by multi-disciplinary methods. Furthermore, a colorimetric method for detection of glucose was developed by using MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs and GOx, which showed acceptable sensitivity, excellent recyclable performance of MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs and good reproducibility. Due to its easy preparation, recyclable performance, good stability and high activity, MnO<sub>2</sub> NWs would open up a potential avenue in catalyst, biosensor and bioanalysis.
