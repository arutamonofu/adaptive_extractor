# Ceria Nanozymes with Preferential Renal Uptake for Acute Kidney Injury Alleviation

Dong-Yang Zhang,<sup>∥</sup> Hengke Liu,<sup>∥</sup> Chunying Li,<sup>∥</sup> Muhammad Rizwan Younis, Shan Lei, Chen Yang, Jing Lin, Zhiming Li,* and Peng Huang*

Cite This: ACS Appl. Mater. Interfaces 2020, 12, 56830−56838

**ABSTRACT:** Rhabdomyolysis-induced acute kidney injury (AKI) is closely related to abundant reactive oxygen species (ROS). Owing to the multi-enzymatic activity and broad-spectrum ROS scavenging capacity of ceria nanoparticles (ceria NPs), herein, we report ultrasmall citric acid modified ceria nanozymes (3−4 nm) as antioxidants to alleviate rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI through removing excessive ROS. The as-prepared ceria NPs exhibited multi-enzymatic properties such as peroxidase, catalase, and superoxide dismutase, offering efficient protection of renal cells against H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> stimulation in vitro. Moreover, due to their ultrasmall size, ceria NPs could efficiently accumulate in the kidneys, thus protecting renal cells against ROS in vivo. Our results present ultrasmall ceria nanozymes as antioxidants for rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI alleviation, which shows great potential in clinic.

**KEYWORDS:** rhabdomyolysis, nanozyme, ceria nanoparticles, ROS scavenging, acute kidney injury

## 1. INTRODUCTION

Owing to the leading cause of high morbidity and mortality, acute kidney injury (AKI) has attracted worldwide attention.<sup>1</sup> The clinical symptoms of AKI are rapid reduction of renal excretion, increased accumulation of nitrogen metabolism, and decreased urine output.<sup>2,3</sup> Currently, AKI treatment predominately relies on transplantation and supportive cares.<sup>4,5</sup> Being one of the common clinical AKI, rhabdomyolysis caused widespread muscular injury accompanied with the release of myoglobin in blood circulation and ultimately secondary kidney failure, which has been reported to be closely related with an abundant endogenous reactive oxygen species (ROS).<sup>6,7</sup> Although some drugs like amifostine (AMF) have been utilized to alleviate AKI mainly through free radicals of scavenging mechanism, the side effects and limited efficacy of AMF hinder its clinical applications.<sup>8,9</sup> However, the use of AMF opened a window to develop new strategies for clinical AKI treatment. Therefore, the development of highly effective ROS scavengers with low toxicity and high kidney accumulation will be competent for AKI treatment.

So far, nanozymes have attracted considerable attention and are expected to substitute traditional protein-based enzymes due to their low cost, high stability, and facile preparation.<sup>10−12</sup> A range of nanomaterials with multi-enzymatic activities, including peroxidase (POD), catalase (CAT), superoxide dismutase (SOD), oxidase, etc., have been extensively used in anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, cancer therapy, and biomarker detection.<sup>13−18</sup> Among them, ceria nanoparticles (NPs) demonstrated excellent removal capacity of free radicals by reversibly binding oxygen and shifting between the Ce<sup>4+</sup> (oxidized) and Ce<sup>3+</sup> (reduced) forms on the surface of NPs,<sup>19</sup> which can be utilized for the management of ROS-induced diseases. For example, Du et al. reported the combination of mitochondrial-targeted ceria NPs and atorvastatin for sepsis-induced AKI treatment, whereas the common rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI has not been investigatied.<sup>20</sup> In addition, previous studies suggested that ultrasmall nanomaterials with the size below the renal filtration threshold (5.5 nm) could pass through the glomerulus and deposit in the kidneys prior to their rapid excretion from the body.<sup>21−24</sup> So far, there are various nanomaterials such as DNA origami-based nanoparticles, polyoxometalates, melanin NPs, carbon quantum dots, and black phosphorus, have been reported as ROS scavengers for AKI treatment,<sup>25−29</sup> and the development of biocompatible ultrasmall ceria nanozymes with broad-spectrum ROS scavenging capacity and preferential renal uptake, which could continuously catalyze the removal of ROS for rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI therapy with limited side effects.

Herein, a cerium-based nanozyme was constructed for AKI alleviation through scavenging an excess amount of endogenous ROS (Figure 1). Ultrasmall ceria NPs with multi-enzymatic activities such as POD, CAT, and SOD, demonstrated effective kidney accumulation with profound renal protection against ROS scavenging. Moreover, the as-prepared ceria NPs showed better therapeutic efficacy than Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved small molecule drug AMF. Importantly, ceria NPs did not present systemic toxicity in vivo due to rapid renal clearance. This work offers a novel strategy for the development of ultrasmall nanozymes as powerful antioxidants for AKI management.

## 2. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### 2.1. Synthesis and Characterization of Ceria NPs.
Ceria NPs were prepared by a facile fabrication approach using citrate as a stabilizer at room temperature. The average diameter of ceria NPs is 3−4 nm (Figure 2A,B) that is measured from the transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image. Meanwhile, the result of dynamic laser scattering (DLS) measurement revealed ceria NPs possessed an average size of ~4 nm (Figure 2C), which is smaller than the kidney filtration threshold (5.5 nm) and beneficial for accumulation in the kidney. As shown in Figure 2D, the fluorite cubic structure of ceria NPs was verified by the X-ray diffraction (XRD) spectrum (JCPDS no. 34-0394). Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectra revealed a C=O peak at 1750 cm<sup>−1</sup>, indicating that ceria NPs are successfully functionalized with citric acid (Figure 2E). X-ray photoelectron (XPS) characterization revealed the tri-valence and tetra-valence states of cerium (Figure 2F and Figure S1), which is beneficial to the catalytic reaction of ceria NPs.

### 2.2. ROS Scavenging Activity of Ceria NPs.
An excess amount of free radicals in physiological environments could cause progressive oxidative damage, which ultimately leads to cell death. Therefore, we evaluate multi-enzymatic activities of ceria NPs to remove ROS. Impressively, ceria NPs exhibited high H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> scavenging activity in a concentration-dependent manner (Figure 3A). About 40% of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could be decomposed at 400 μg/mL of ceria NPs. The mechanistic investigation of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> scavenging activity of ceria NPs was performed by XPS. After treated with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, the XPS spectrum belong to Ce 3d of ceria NPs exhibited more Ce<sup>4+</sup> as compared to pretreated NPs, which confirmed that ceria NPs can scavenge H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> by reversibly binding oxygen and shifting between Ce<sup>4+</sup> (oxidized) and Ce<sup>3+</sup> (reduced) forms (Figure 3B). The POD-mimic activity of ceria NPs was investigated through 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) oxidation assay. As shown in Figure 3C, a concentration-dependent TMB oxidation was noticed at 650 nm, suggesting that ceria NPs possess the POD-like activity. Furthermore, kinetic analysis indicated that the experimental findings fully satisfy the Michaelis−Menten equation (Figure 3D,E and Figure S2), as the reaction constant $K_m = 308$ and 0.02 mM were calculated for TMB and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, respectively.

Next, we tested the hydroxyl radical (·OH) scavenging ability of ceria NPs through a ·OH detection kit. Ceria NPs exhibited a concentration-dependent pattern toward ·OH scavenging (Figure 3F). Similar results were observed for 2,2′-azinobis(3-ethyl-2,3-dihydro-6-benzothiazolesulfonicacidiammonium) (ABTS) cationic radical, ·ABTS<sup>+</sup>, which is produced by mixing ABTS with potassium persulfate. About 70% of ·ABTS<sup>+</sup> were scavenged by ceria NPs at 200 μg/mL (Figure 3G). Finally, a SOD assay kit was used to evaluate the superoxide radical (·O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>) scavenging activity of ceria NPs. As presented in Figure 3H, the scavenging behavior of ceria NPs toward ·O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> also had a concentration-dependent property, as 39.5 ± 1.4% ·O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> were scavenged by ceria NPs at 200 μg/mL. To investigate the catalytic mechanism of ceria NPs, an active reactant cytochrome c (Cyt c) was utilized to evaluate the accepting electron ability. As presented in Figure S3, the absorbance peaks of Cyt c at 550 and 520 nm were disappeared, while a new peak was observed at 530 nm after treated with ceria NPs, suggesting an electron transfer from Cyt c to ceria NPs. Moreover, ceria NPs can accept electrons from oxygen-independent Cyt c. All of results confirmed that ceria NPs hold multi-enzymatic activities against various ROS (Figure 3I).

### 2.3. Ceria NPs-Mediated Protection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-Stimulated Cells.
With CAT-, POD-, and SOD-like activities, ultrasmall ceria NPs could perform an efficient cellular protection against H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-induced oxidative damage. For in vitro investigation, human embryonic kidney 293 T (HEK293T) cells were used as a model. First, we evaluated the cytotoxicity and biocompatibility of ceria NPs by using 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay and hemolysis assay, respectively. About 90% of cell survival and <2% of hemolysis rate were observed at the given concentration, suggesting ceria NPs had good biocompatibility (Figure 4A and Figure S4). Then, we used 2′,7′-dichlorofluorescein diacetate (DCF) fluorescent dye to detect the level of ROS in cells. As shown in Figure 4B, lower ROS level was observed in the cells pre-incubated with ceria NPs than H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> alone. The quantitative results further showed that the intensities of DCF in ceria NPs plus the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-treated group were lower than the only H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-treated group (Figure 4C). Mitochondria, the factory of cellular energy metabolism, could be easily damaged by ROS, thus leading to cell death.<sup>30,31</sup> As shown in Figure 4D, compared to the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-stimulated cells, the mitochondrial integrity is preserved in cells pretreated with ceria NPs. More than 75% cellular death was recorded after 500 μM H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> treatment for 24 h (Figure 4E). However, the cells pre-incubated with varied concentrations of ceria NPs can enhance the cell viability. Moreover, ceria NPs also presented concentration-dependent reduction in the ratios of apoptotic and necrotic cells from ROS-induced damage (Figure 4F,G).

### 2.4. Biodistribution and in vivo AKI Treatment of Ceria NPs.
Considering the good biocompatibility and high ROS scavenging capacity, the in vivo AKI therapeutic effects of ceria NPs were studied. Rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI mouse model was constructed in Figure 5A. We found effective accumulation of ultrasmall ceria NPs in the renal tissues of both AKI and normal mice by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS). Ceria NPs were rapidly enriched in the renal tissues within 2 h after intravenous (i.v.) injection in Figure S5. Compared to healthy mice, ceria NPs stayed for longer time periods in the renal tissues of AKI mice, which is highly beneficial for AKI treatment (Figure 5B).

We further used AMF as a positive drug for AKI treatment. The blood biochemical analysis revealed an obvious reduction in the level of creatinine (CRE) and blood urea nitrogen (BUN) in ceria NPs-treated AKI mice as compared to PBS- or AMF-treated mice (Figure 5C,D),<sup>32,33</sup> suggesting the restoration of renal functions by ceria NPs. Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining of renal tissues was also performed. Figure 5E displays that a large of formation of casts and damaged tubules (marked as asterisks and arrows) were found in the renal sections of AKI mice. However, no particular damage was found after treatment with ceria NPs, which is in accordance with the results of blood analysis. Similarly, a marked enhancement was recorded in the number of apoptotic cells in PBS-treated AKI mice; however, these alterations were significantly attenuated in ceria NPs-treated AKI mice (Figure 5F). Notably, the therapeutic effect of AMF on AKI mice was not satisfactory. Importantly, i.v. injection of ceria NPs significantly increased the weight and survival of AKI mice over 15 days compared with the PBS-treated mice (Figure S6A,B). Furthermore, Figure S6C displays profound inhibition of the renal damage in ceria NPs-treated AKI mice than the PBS group.

### 2.5. Detection of Biomarkers in AKI Mice after Different Treatments.
The levels of ROS in the renal tissues were monitored to investigate the therapeutic effects of ceria NPs. The content of ·O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> in ceria NPs-treated AKI mice significantly decreased than that of PBS-treated mice, presenting a similar level as of normal mice (Figure 6A). To further investigate the therapeutic effects, the levels of SOD were detected in kidney homogenates, which is a key enzyme for catalyzing the removal of ·O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>.<sup>34</sup> The SOD levels in mice treated with ceria NPs were close to normal mice (Figure 6B), while an excess ROS levels depleted SOD in PBS- or AMF-treated groups, suggesting that ceria NPs as nanozymes efficiently scavenge abundant ROS, restored the level of SOD in kidneys, and offered renal protection of AKI mice. The expression of two key biomarkers of kidney injury in renal tissues, heme oxygenase-1 (HO-1) and kidney injury molecule-1 (KIM-1) were further analyzed.<sup>35,36</sup> As shown in Figure 6C,D, ceria NPs inhibited HO-1 and KIM-1 expression in AKI mice as compared to PBS. Next, we determined whether ceria NPs can inhibit lipid peroxidation and DNA damage. Significant reduction of lipid peroxidation in AKI mice treated with ceria NPs was observed over PBS-treated AKI mice (Figure S7). Similar results were obtained for DNA damage. 8-Hydroxy-2′-deoxyguanosine (8-OHdG), which is a biomarker of quantified DNA damage, in PBS-treated AKI mice exhibited an increase than ceria NPs-treated mice (Figure 6E). Importantly, the therapeutic effects of ceria NPs were much better than AMF. All of these results provide a satisfactory explanation of the potential of ceria NPs to alleviate AKI.

### 2.6. In vivo Toxicity Assessment.
Finally, the in vivo toxicity of ceria NPs was assessed after i.v. injection of ceria NPs (50 mg/kg). The body weight changes were recorded regularly. The primary organs and blood samples were harvested at 21 days post-injection. There was no significant difference in the body weight between control and NPs-treated mice (Figure S8). Additionally, H&E staining images of primary organs demonstrated no side effects after ceria NPs treatment (Figure 7A). Meanwhile, the analysis of blood biochemical indicators such as BUN, CRE, alanine transaminase (ALT), and aspartate transaminase (AST) suggested liver and kidneys kept their normal function (Figure 7B−E). All of results confirmed the negligible toxicity and good biocompatibility of ceria NPs in vivo, facilitating for AKI treatment.

## 3. CONCLUSIONS

We successfully constructed ultrasmall ceria nanozymes as ROS scavengers for rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI therapy. The ceria NPs with uniform size distribution, and good dispersibility were synthesized using a facile solution-based fabrication approach. The multi-enzymatic activities of ceria NPs including CAT, POD, and SOD, promise effective elimination of various ROS e.g., H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, ·OH, ·ABTS<sup>+</sup>, and ·O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>. Due to their good biocompatibility and excellent antioxidant properties, ceria NPs demonstrated an impressive protection of HEK293T cells against H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. In addition, owing to their ultrasmall size, ceria NPs rapidly accumulated in the kidneys and efficiently alleviated rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI in vivo with negligible toxicity. Importantly, ceria NPs exhibited better therapeutic performance than an FDA-approved drug AMF. To conclude, ultrasmall ceria nanozyme is a promising nanoantioxidant for the alleviation of rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI.

## 4. METHODS

### 4.1. Synthesis of Ceria NPs.
To prepare ceria NPs, citric acid (0.48 g) and cerium nitrate pentahydrate (0.52 g) were dissolved in water (30 mL) and rapidly poured into 100 mL of 3 M ammonia solution. The mixed solution was stirred overnight at 25 °C, centrifuged, and washed with water. The obtained pellet was dispersed in PBS for further use.

### 4.2. Cell Cytotoxicity.
HEK293T cells were kept in 96-well plates until plating efficiency reached ~60−70%, and then incubated with various concentrations of ceria NPs (0−200 μg/mL). After 24 or 48 h, the celluar viability was calculated by the MTT assay.

### 4.3. Hemolysis Assays.
Red blood cells in serum were separated by centrifugation and diluted with PBS. Next, red blood cells suspension (0.3 mL) was added with 1.2 mL of ceria NPs (100−2000 μg/mL) in PBS. After standing for 3 h, the UV−vis absorbance value (541 nm) of the supernatant was determined.

### 4.4. Protection of Cells with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> Stimulation by Ceria NPs.
HEK293T cells were seeded at a concentration of $1 \times 10^4$ cells/well and incubated for 24 h. Then, the ceria NPs dispersions with various concentrations (0−200 μg/mL) were treated with cells for 4 h. Next, the cells were stimulated by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (500 μM) for 24 h in the dark. Finally, the cell survival was detected by the MTT assay.

HEK293T cells were seeded into confocal dishes and incubated with a medium containing 200 μg/mL of ceria NPs and incubated for 4 h. Then, the cells were washed with PBS to remove free ceria NPs and incubated with 10 μM of DCF or 2 μM of Mito-tracker Green (Mito-tracker) in DMEM media without FBS. Next, the HEK293T cells were treated with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 mM) for 10 min. Finally, the cells were imaged by confocal fluorescence microscopy (emission wavelength: 520 ± 20 nm, excitation wavelength: 488 nm).

To quantify the intracellular reactive oxygen content, HEK293T cells were seeded into 96-well cell culture plates and incubated for 24 h. The cells were then mixed with a medium containing a dispersion of ceria NPs (0−200 μg/mL) and incubated for 4 h. Next, the cells were washed with PBS to remove free ceria NPs and incubated with 10 μM of DCF in DMEM media without FBS for 15 min in the dark. The cells were treated with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1 mM) for 10 min. After washing with PBS, the levels of ROS in cells were monitored by using a microplate reader (emission wavelength: 520 ± 20 nm, excitation wavelength: 488 nm).

HEK293T cells were seeded into 6-well plates and incubated for 24 h. Then, the DMEM containing ceria NPs dispersion (0−100 μg/mL) was incubated with cells for further 4 h. Next, the cells were treated with 2 mM H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> for 10 min. Finally, propidium iodide/annexin V-FITC was used to stain the cells, and the fluorescence was recorded by flow cytometry (BD, FACSCalibur).

### 4.5. In vivo Distribution of Ceria NPs.
Healthy or AKI mice received an intravenous injection of ceria NPs (2 mg ceria NPs in 150 μL PBS). Mice were euthanatized to collect primary organs at indicated time points. The primary organs were digested by aqua regia and further detected by ICP-MS to calculate the percentage of injected dose per gram of tissue (% ID/g).

### 4.6. In vivo Therapeutic Outcome of Ceria NPs on AKI Mice.
Different groups were assigned on healthy and AKI model mice (n = 5). Healthy mice: (1) healthy mice group (injected with 150 μL PBS), (2) ceria NPs group (injected with 2 mg ceria NPs in 150 μL PBS); AKI mice: (1) AKI group (injected with PBS), (2) AMF group (injected with 2 mg AMF in 150 μL PBS), (3) ceria NPs group (injected with 2 mg ceria NPs in 150 μL PBS). The mice were i.v. injected with active substances 2 h after im injection of glycerol. The body weight and survival curve of AKI mice were recorded for 15 days. According to animal welfare guidelines, mice that lost higher than 15% of their body weight were considered dead.

### 4.7. Kidney Function Evaluation.
After 24 h i.v. post-injection (2 mg ceria NPs in 150 μL PBS), the mice were euthanatized to collect kidneys and blood samples. The obtained kidneys were sectioned for H&E staining, and the levels of BUN and CRE in blood samples were tested.

### 4.8. Detection of Biomarkers.
Kidneys of mice collected from each group were cryopreserved in a refrigerator at −80 °C, and the renal homogenates were obtained for different experiments. A SOD assay kit (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) was utilized to evaluate the level of SOD. The KIM-1 and HO-1 ELISA kit (Abcam, USA) were utilized to determine the expression of HO-1 and KIM-1 in kidney tissues. The DNA damage competitive ELISA kit (Invitrogen, USA) was used to assess the degree of DNA damage. A TBARS assay kit (Cayman Chemical, USA) was utilized to measure the level of lipid peroxidation in kidney tissues.

### 4.9. In vivo Biocompatibility Evaluation of Ceria NPs.
The ceria NPs (50 mg/kg in 150 μL PBS) or PBS (150 μL) were i.v. injected into healthy mice were used as the ceria NPs group and control group, respectively (n = 3). All mice were euthanatized 21 days after injection, and blood samples were acquired for hematological analysis. The primary organs (kidney, lung, spleen, liver, and heart) were harvested from mice, and H&E staining was used for pathological observations.
