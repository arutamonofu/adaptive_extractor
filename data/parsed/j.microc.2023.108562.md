**ABSTRACT**

Modulation of surface oxygen vacancies (O<sub>v</sub>) by defect engineering is an attractive strategy for designing high performance transition metal oxide nanozymes. In this work, we reported the fabrication of the CoMoO<sub>4</sub> oxidase like nanozyme, i.e. CoMo-300r, by first pyrolyzing the CoMo layered double hydroxide precursor in an air atmosphere at 300 °C and subsequent NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction strategy. Various characterization outcomes confirm the mesoporous structures of both CoMo-300r and no NaBH<sub>4</sub> treated CoMo-300, while the specific surface area of CoMo-300 is significantly increased from 103.54 m<sup>2</sup>⋅g<sup>−1</sup> to 165.24 m<sup>2</sup>⋅g<sup>−1</sup> after NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction. Also, NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction treatment leads to the formation of O<sub>v</sub> rich CoMoO<sub>4</sub> and reduced metal ion species on the surface, promoting the redox reaction kinetics. The CoMo-300r prepared at a molar ratio of Co to Mo of 1:2, pyrolysis temperature of 300 °C and 10 mM NaBH<sub>4</sub> exhibited the best oxidase-like activity. The oxidase-like activity of CoMo-300r is 2.6-fold times that of no NaBH<sub>4</sub> treated optimal CoMo-300. The CoMo-300r can effectively promote TMB oxidation to produce blue color and has the Michaelis-Menten constant of 0.0428 mM, which was lower than most cobalt-based oxide nanozymes and indicated a higher affinity for TMB. In terms of inhibition of SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> on the CoMo-300r/TMB system, a sensitive SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> colorimetric sensing platform was established. The linear range and limit of detection for sulfite determination were 0.2–40 μM and 0.09 μM, respectively. The relative standard deviation for the determination of 10 μM sulfite was 1.41% (n = 11). The method was successfully applied to the detection of sulfite in white wine. This work provides a new strategy to fabricate high activity oxidase mimetic for food analysis.

**1. Introduction**

Sulfur compounds are widely present in various samples and play divers function [1]. Sulfite (SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>) plays an important role as preservatives and antioxidants in the modern food industry because a suitable amount of SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> function as additives to maintain the freshness and flavor of food [2,3]. However, excessive addition of SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> in food can lead to adverse toxic effects, such as asthma, allergic reactions, and neurotoxicity [4,5]. Moreover, frequent exposure to excessive sulfite could induce harmful effects on human health, including cardiovascular diseases, neurological disorders and cancer [6]. For health reasons, efficient determination of sulfite in food is the basis for food quality assurance and control. Considering the above-mentioned facts, the development of sensitive and selective analytical methods for the detection of SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> has attracted great interest in recent years [7]. Various methods, i.e. titration [8], chromatography [9], electrochemistry [10,11], fluorescence [12,13] and colorimetry [14], have been developed to detect sulfite in food and water samples. Among which, there is a growing interest in developing colorimetric assay for sulfite because it is simple to operate by using low cost instrument.

Recently, the nanozyme-based colorimetric methods are widely used in biosensing, environmental protection and food sample detection [15,16]. Nanozymes are nanomaterials with enzyme-like characteristics [16]. Compared with natural enzymes, they have the merits of high stability, low cost and tunable structure [17]. Since magnetic Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles were reported to have peroxidase-like activity [18], other transition metal oxides, such as CeO<sub>2</sub> [19], MnO<sub>2</sub> [20], Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> [21] and MoO<sub>3</sub> [22], were found to possess the oxidase-like activity. However, these single transition metal oxides suffer from relatively low oxidase activity. Thanks to synergistic effect, binary transition metal oxides (BTMOs) have higher electron conduction capacity, fast redox reaction speed and stronger catalytic activity compared to monometallic oxides, and therefore hold great promise for the construction of highly active oxidase mimics. Various BTMOs, such as Fe-Co oxides [23], Mn-Co oxides [24] and Fe-Cu oxides [25], have been developed as stable and efficient oxidase mimics. In particular, the Co-based oxides are often applied in the construction of mimetic enzymes [26]. Also, as an n-type semiconductor material, molybdenum-based oxides are one of the most attractive nanozymes due to their different valence states, low toxicity and superior electron transfer capability [27,28]. Thus, it is anticipated that an efficient activity nanozyme can be constructed via Co-Mo composting to form bimetal oxides CoMoO<sub>4</sub> because the redox behavior of Co atoms and the good electrical conductivity of Mo atoms make CoMoO<sub>4</sub> have good electron transfer ability [29,30]. In fact, cobalt molybdate is one of promising oxide catalysts in electrocatalysis [31] and oxidative desulfurization [32]. A few studies have reported the peroxidase-like activity of CoMoO<sub>4</sub> [33,34] and its peroxidase-like activity improvement via metal doping [35], while no works are focused on exploring its oxidase-like activity by defect construction strategy.

In recent years, defect engineering, in particular oxygen vacancies (O<sub>v</sub>) construction, has been used to modulate activity of nanozymes [28,36–39]. The controlled introduction of O<sub>v</sub> into transition metal based nanocatalysts can effectively increase their electrical conductivity and create large amount of active sites [40–43]. For metal oxide nanozyme, O<sub>v</sub> is rich in active sites and has high surface energy [44]. Thus, increasing the content of O<sub>v</sub> is an efficient strategy to enhance the enzyme-like activity of metal oxides. For example, by reducing porous CeO<sub>2</sub> microspheres, the content of O<sub>v</sub> can be increased, facilitating the switching of surface electronic charge between the Ce(IV) and Ce(III) species. The obtained porous CeO<sub>2</sub> microspheres exhibited a higher substrate consumption rate and a superior substrate affinity compared to other nanozymes [38]. In another work, Liu’s group found that the F<sup>−</sup>-capped CeO<sub>2</sub> exhibited high oxidase-like activity because the introduction of F<sup>−</sup> caused increase in both Ce<sup>3+</sup> concentration and O<sub>v</sub> level [45]. More recently, via controlling oxygen partial pressure during the pyrolysis process of Mn<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> precursor in air media, Zhan’s group reported the fabrication O<sub>v</sub>-rich Mn<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> oxidase-mimicking nanozyme [39]. The hydrothermal, high-temperature pyrolysis and NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction methods have been commonly used to fabricate oxygen vacancy containing nanomaterials [37,46–48]. Among which, using NaBH<sub>4</sub> as a reducing agent can easily produce partially reduced metal oxide surfaces and interiors at room temperature to enhance oxygen vacancy content, increasing the electron donor and accelerating redox reaction kinetics of the resultant product [48].

Herein, we reported a simple strategy to fabricate the CoMoO<sub>4</sub> oxidase-like nanozyme, i.e. CoMo-300r, by first pyrolyzing the CoMo layered double hydroxide (LDH) precursor in an air atmosphere and subsequent NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction strategy (Scheme 1). Various characterization outcomes confirm the mesoporous structures of both CoMo-300 and CoMo-300r, while the specific surface area of CoMo-300 is significantly increased from 103.54 m<sup>2</sup>⋅g<sup>−1</sup> to 165.24 m<sup>2</sup>⋅g<sup>−1</sup> after NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction. Also, NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction treatment leads to the formation of O<sub>v</sub> rich CoMoO<sub>4</sub>. Interestingly, the oxidase-like activity of CoMo-300r is 2.6-fold times that of no NaBH<sub>4</sub> treated optimized CoMo-300. The CoMo-300r can effectively promote TMB oxidation to produce blue color. Notably, the addition of sulfite fades TMB color reaction in the presence of CoMo-300r. Based on above finding, a colorimetric method for detecting SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> was constructed. The method was successfully used for the determination of SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> in white wine and has potential applications in sensing and food safety.

**2. Experimental**

**2.1. Chemicals and materials**

Sodium molybdate dihydrate (Na<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub>⋅2H<sub>2</sub>O) was purchased from Macklin Biochemical Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Cobalt acetate tetrahydrate (C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>6</sub>CoO<sub>4</sub>⋅4H<sub>2</sub>O), p-benzoquinone (p-BQ) and thiourea (TH) were purchased from Aladdin Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China). 3,3′,5,5′-Tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) was from Sigma-Aldrich (Shanghai, China). o-Phenylenediamine (OPD), 2,2′-azobis(3-ethyl benzothiazoline-6-sulphonic acid) diammonium salt (ABTS), glucose, fructose and maltose were purchased from Sinopharm Holdings Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Sodium borohydride (NaBH<sub>4</sub>), acetic acid (HAc) and sodium acetate (NaAc) were provided by Kelong Chemical Reagent Factory (Chengdu, China). Hexamethyltetramine (HMT), arginine, histidine, lysine, cysteine, glutathione, ethanol and NaN<sub>3</sub> were purchased from Chongqing Taixin Chemical Co. Ltd. (Chongqing, China).

**2.2. Synthesis of CoMo LDH precursor and CoMo-300r**

The CoMo LDH precursor was obtained by homogeneous precipitation method. Typically, 0.75 mmol C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>6</sub>CoO<sub>4</sub>⋅4H<sub>2</sub>O, 1.5 mmol Na<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub>⋅2H<sub>2</sub>O, and 1.5 mmol HMT were added into 30 mL deionized water under vigorously stirred for 20 min. The obtained solution was transferred to a 100 mL Teflon-lined autoclave, heated to 120 °C and maintained for 12 h. After the pinkish solids were obtained via centrifugation, then cleaned by H<sub>2</sub>O and ethanol three times in turn, and dried at 60 °C in vacuum for 8 h.

To prepare the CoMo-300 product, the CoMo LDH precursor was heated in an air atmosphere at 300 °C for 3 h. To prepare the CoMo-300r, 20 mL NaBH<sub>4</sub> (10 mM) and 30 mg CoMo-300 were mixed and soaked for 1 h. Then, the solid was filtrated immediately, and washed three times using deionized water and ethanol, and dried at 60 °C in vacuum.

**2.3. Enzyme-like activity assessment**

The oxidase-like performance of the prepared catalyst was evaluated via TMB color reaction. In short, the solid catalysts were first dispersed in water by ultrasonication for 8 min. Then, 0.25 mL of 0.3 g/L catalyst suspension, 0.25 mL of 2 mM TMB solution were mixed, then 0.2 M acetate buffer was added to make the final volume of 5 mL. The UV–vis measurements were carried out after 20 min reaction at 25 °C.

**2.4. Kinetic study**

The kinetic trial was performed under optimal conditions. In brief, the UV–visible spectrophotometer was used to record the absorption spectra of the mixture of 15 mg/L CoMo-300r, TMB (0.025 mM to 1 mM) in 0.2 M acetate buffer (5 mL total volume) after incubation at 25 °C for 20 min. The absorbance at 652 nm (A<sub>652</sub>) was recorded during early 5 min. The details for obtaining Michaelis–Menten equation K<sub>m</sub> and the maximal reaction velocity V<sub>max</sub> were provided in Supplementary data.

**2.5. Procedure for the colorimetric detection of sulfite**

For the colorimetric detection of sulfite, TMB (with final concentration of 0.1 mM), CoMo-300r (with final concentration of 15 mg/L) and sulfite with varied concentrations (0 – 60 μM) or 0.2 mL of the treated wine sample were mixed together, and then diluted to 5 mL with 0.2 M NaAc–HAc buffer (pH 3.5). UV–vis absorption spectra were recorded after the mixture was incubated at 25 °C for 20 min to obtain a dose of sulfite concentration-dependent responsive curves. To evaluate the selectivity of the proposed oxidase-like based colorimetric assay for sulfite, the effects of 100 μM of K<sup>+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup>, Al<sup>3+</sup>, NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>, PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup>, Cl<sup>−</sup>, S<sup>2−</sup>, SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>, Ag<sup>+</sup>, Zn<sup>2+</sup>, Ni<sup>2+</sup>, Mn<sup>2+</sup>, Cu<sup>2+</sup>, Fe<sup>3+</sup>, Fe<sup>2+</sup>, Co<sup>2+</sup>, Pb<sup>2+</sup> and 100 μM of other potential interferences, i.e. lysine (Lys), arginine (Arg), cysteine (Cys), glutathione (GSH), histidine (His), tyrosine (Tyr), tryptophan (Trp), alanine (Ala), serine (Ser), glucose, maltose, fructose, starch and tartaric acid, on the reaction system were studied.

To demonstrate the application of the method, the content of sulfite in white wine samples was measured by the method. White wine was purchased from a local supermarket. First, the wine samples were pretreated based on previous work [49]. In detail, 50 mL of white wine samples were heated to about 8 mL in a water bath, and the supernatant was taken after standing and settling, and used for the determination of sulfite and recovery trials.

**3. Results and discussion**

**3.1. Characterization**

The crystal structure of CoMo LDH precursor was examined using X-ray diffraction (XRD). Fig. S1a shows the XRD pattern of CoMo sample. The diffraction peaks at 2θ of 26.6°, 33.5° and 59.5° correspond to the (006), (012) and (110) planes of typical LDH structure [50]. In addition, the diffraction peaks at 24.1°, 33.8°, 36.5°, 39.5°, 62.2°, and 65.7° correspond to the (111), (221), (301), (231), (450), and (023) crystal planes of Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH)⋅0.11H<sub>2</sub>O [51]. The low crystallinity LDH phases appeared in the as-prepared samples may be due to the low synthesis temperature and adsorption of anions into the hydroxide layers [52]. The formation of Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH)⋅0.11H<sub>2</sub>O is attributed to the introduction of excessive HMT that can generate NH<sub>3</sub> via hydrolysis to form OH<sup>−</sup> ions [53]. This makes the hydrothermal reaction system shift to higher pH relative to the pure LDH phase, making the precursor present a mixture of two phases [53].

The morphology and structure of the materials were characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). As shown in Fig. S1b, the CoMo LDH precursor exhibited three-dimensional flower-like microsphere. After calcination at 300 °C, the obtained CoMo-300 inherited the morphology of CoMo LDH precursor (Fig. 1a), and its surface consists of a porous laminar structure with the particle size remaining essentially constant. When further treating CoMo-300 with NaBH<sub>4</sub>, the final product CoMo-300r kept flower-like microsphere morphology of CoMo-300, with a size of about 4 μm (Fig. 1b). The flower-like microsphere structure was confirmed by the TEM image (Fig. 1c), where the nanosheet layer structure can be clearly seen. In contrast to the calcined sample, the NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction treatment resulted in the collapse of the surface layer to form rough folds and the appearance of smaller nanoparticles scattered around the edges of the floral layer. The HRTEM images in Fig. 1d and e show four distinct lattice stripes in the CoMo-300r. The 0.245 nm and 0.289 nm are the (021) and (−111) crystal planes of CoMoO<sub>4</sub>, while 0.24 nm and 0.207 nm are the (311) and (400) crystal planes of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, respectively. The element mapping images show that the Co, Mo, and O elements are uniformly distributed throughout the material (Fig. 1f–h).

Fig. 2a shows the XRD patterns of the CoMo-300 before and after reduction, and both of their crystal structures are Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>/CoMoO<sub>4</sub>. For the CoMo-300, the diffraction peaks at 19.1°, 30.9° and 36.7° correspond to the (100), (−111) and (021) crystal planes of CoMoO<sub>4</sub> (JCPDS#16-0309), while those at 44.8°, 59.3° and 65.2° correspond to the (400), (511) and (440) crystal faces of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (JCPDS# 43-1003). Relative to standard ones of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and CoMoO<sub>4</sub>, the diffraction peaks of both phases are weaker and broader (Fig. 2a). This implies that compositing of two components leads to the decrease in the crystallinity of final composite. This leads to rich defects, which is beneficial to improve its catalytic activity [54].

The structural information of the samples was further analyzed by Raman characterization. As shown in Fig. 2b, the Raman spectra of CoMo-300 and CoMo-300r show almost no shift in the peak positions. The peaks at 181, 472, 523 and 665 cm<sup>−1</sup> correspond to the F<sub>2g</sub>, E<sub>g</sub>, F<sub>2g</sub><sup>1</sup> and A<sub>2g</sub><sup>1</sup> modes of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> [55], while those at 895, 801 and 360 cm<sup>−1</sup> correspond to the contraction vibration of Mo–O–Co bond in CoMoO<sub>4</sub> [55], respectively.

In order to investigate the changes in the microscopic pore structure as well as the specific surface area of the materials, the N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption trials were carried out for the CoMo-300 and CoMo-300r. As displayed in Fig. S2a, both CoMo-300 and CoMo-300r exhibit the characteristics of type IV adsorption equilibrium isotherms, indicating rich mesoporous structures of both materials. The specific surface area of CoMo-300 is 103.54 m<sup>2</sup>⋅g<sup>−1</sup>, while it increases to 165.24 m<sup>2</sup>⋅g<sup>−1</sup> after NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction. Both materials mainly exhibit a mesoporous structure. However, the pore size distribution range of CoMo-300r is wider than that of CoMo-300 (Fig. S2b), which helps to provide more transport channels for reactants and products and can expose more accessible active sites. Above outcomes indicate that NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction significantly increases the specific surface area of the composite and more mesoporous structures. This leads to exposing more active sites, facilitating the participation of the adsorbed reactants in the enzymatic catalytic reaction, and finally boosting catalytic activity.

The full XPS spectra of CoMo-300 and CoMo-300r are shown in Fig. 3a, both materials contain C, N, O, Co and Mo elements. Fig. 3b shows the XPS high-resolution Co 2p spectra before and after reduction treatment. The Co 2p of the CoMo-300 can be divided into two pairs of peaks, i.e. Co<sup>3+</sup> at 780.25 eV and 795.65 eV, and Co<sup>2+</sup> at 782.05 eV, 797.84 eV. Also, there are two satellite peaks at 787.32 eV and 803.11 eV for the Co 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Co 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, respectively. For the reduced material CoMo-300r, two peaks at 779.99 eV and 795.23 eV correspond to Co 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Co 2p<sub>1/2</sub> of Co<sup>3+</sup>, while those at 781.46 eV and 797.19 eV are characteristic Co 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Co 2p<sub>1/2</sub> of Co<sup>2+</sup> [56]. The Co<sup>2+</sup>/Co<sup>3+</sup> ratio in the CoMo-300 and CoMo-300r are 1.13 to 1.63 (Table S1), respectively. This implies increase in Co<sup>2+</sup>/Co<sup>3+</sup> ratio after NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction treatment of CoMo-300. It also indicates that some Co<sup>3+</sup> ions on the surface of the CoMo-300 were reduced to Co<sup>2+</sup> ions to generate surface O<sub>v</sub> [57], which was confirmed by solid electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) characterization (Fig. S3). As shown in Fig. 3c, the peak positions of Mo 3d spectra in the materials before and after NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction treatment (Fig. 3c) remained basically the same. After reduction, the material has two binding energies at 231.78 eV and 234.88 eV, belonging to the Mo 3d<sub>5/2</sub> peak and Mo 3d<sub>3/2</sub> peak, respectively. In addition, the spin–orbit splitting energy between Mo 3d<sub>5/2</sub> and Mo 3d<sub>3/2</sub> is 3.1 eV, demonstrating the Mo(VI) oxidation state in CoMoO<sub>4</sub> [58]. Moreover, the signal peak at 529.71 eV in the O 1s spectrum may be related to the lattice oxygen bonding (O<sub>L</sub>) of the metal oxide, and that at 532.37 eV belongs to hydroxyl oxygen or surface adsorbed oxygen (O<sub>A</sub>). The significant decrease in O<sub>L</sub> content is due to the creation of O<sub>v</sub> near 531 eV, where electron conservation is balanced [59]. The O<sub>v</sub>/O<sub>L</sub> ratio increases significantly from 0.37 to 0.87 after NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction (Table S1). It can be inferred from the above analysis that, during the NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction course, partial Co<sup>3+</sup> reduction occurs on the material surface. This leads to rise of Co<sup>2+</sup> content and the formation of additional surface O<sub>v</sub>, which is conducive to the improvement of the catalytic performance of the composite.

**3.2. Oxidase-like property of the composite material**

The oxidase-like activity of the composite was assessed by its catalytically oxidizing TMB via detecting the variance in A<sub>652</sub> in the UV–vis spectrum of ox-TMB. As shown in Fig. 4a, relative to the CoMo-300, the CoMo-300r caused significant increase in A<sub>652</sub>. This indicates that, after the reduction treatment with NaBH<sub>4</sub>, the oxidase-like activity of CoMo-300 was greatly improved. Specifically, the oxidase-like activity of CoMo-300r was 2.6-fold times that of CoMo-300. This is probably because the CoMo-300r sample had a petal-like lamellar structure with significantly increased surface area (Table S2) and more Co<sup>2+</sup> species (Fig. 3b). In addition, the enhancement of CoMo-300r oxidase-like activity after reduction could be attributed to the significant increase of O<sub>v</sub> content on the material surface in terms of O 1s XPS outcome (Fig. 3d), which facilitates the process of surface adsorption and electron transfer. As displayed in Fig. S4, after reduction treatment by NaBH<sub>4</sub>, the arc radius of CoMo-300r catalyst is lower than that of CoMo-300. This implies that, relative to the CoMo-300, the CoMo-300r has lower charge transfer resistance and quicker interface charge-transfer ability.

Next, in order to optimize the oxidase-like activity of the Co-Mo hybrid oxide nanozymes, first, the preparation conditions, i.e. calcination temperature, molar ratio of Co to Mo and NaBH<sub>4</sub> solution concentration were investigated in this work. As can be seen from Fig. 4b, at calcination temperatures of 200–600 °C range, the catalytic activity of the obtained products was low at 200 °C, while it increased considerably when the temperature was increased to 300 °C. At a further increase in temperature above 400 °C, it showed a significant decrease. In this work, 300 °C was selected as the optimized pyrolysis temperature for the preparation of Co-Mo hybrid metal oxide nanozymes. The effect of the molar ratio of Co to Mo on the oxidase-like activity of the obtained catalysts is shown in Fig. 4c, where the material exhibits higher oxidase activity at higher Mo contents. The best oxidase-like activity was acquired at a molar ratio of Co to Mo of 1:2, which was selected in the following trials. Fig. 4d shows the effect of NaBH<sub>4</sub> concentration on the catalytic activity of the obtained nanozyme. The oxidase-like activity of the obtained catalyst increases with NaBH<sub>4</sub> concentration to 10 mM, above which it declines. Thus, 10 mM NaBH<sub>4</sub> was chosen for subsequent experiments.

Second, the effects of catalyst concentration, solution pH, and reaction temperature on the oxidase-like activity of the CoMo-300r material were investigated. Fig. S5a shows that the oxidase-like activity increased sharply with increasing catalyst concentration to 15 mg/L, above which it increased slowly. Thus, 15 mg/L of CoMo-300r was chosen for subsequent trials. Fig. S5b and c show that the best catalytic activities were achieved at a pH of 3.5 and a reaction temperature of 25 °C, respectively.

**3.3. Steady-state kinetics and oxidase-like catalytic mechanism**

To study the catalytic process, the apparent steady-state kinetic factors for the TMB/CoMo-300r reaction system were obtained via changing TMB concentration from 0.025 to 1.0 mM and keeping the CoMo-300r concentration at 15 mg/L. The Michaelis-Menten and Lineweaver-Burk curves were obtained and shown in Fig. 5. The steady-state kinetic parameters of the CoMo-300r oxidase nanozyme, i.e. Michaelis–Menten constant K<sub>m</sub> and V<sub>max</sub>, were determined. They are 0.0428 mM and 8.72 × 10<sup>−8</sup> M s<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. As shown in Table S3, the K<sub>m</sub> value for CoMo-300r is smaller than those of most other Mo-based materials. Compared with horseradish peroxidase, the lower K<sub>m</sub> value indicates a higher affinity of the CoMo-300r to TMB. This is because the mesoporous structure of CoMo-300r can expose more catalytic sites and can also act as a free channel for reactants and products, which is beneficial for improving catalytic activity. In addition, the CoMo-300r also promote the color reaction of other substrates, i.e. ABTS and OPD (Fig. S5d). This additionally proves the oxidase mimics characteristic of CoMo-300r.

The catalytic mechanism of oxidase-like enzymes is related to the conversion of dissolved oxygen to reactive oxygen species (ROS) such as •OH, O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> and <sup>1</sup>O<sub>2</sub>, which in turn oxidizes TMB. TMB oxidation trials were carried out under different atmospheric conditions, i.e. O<sub>2</sub>, air and N<sub>2</sub>. The relative activity of the CoMo-300r nanozyme increased by 26.3% under O<sub>2</sub>-saturated condition, while it decreased by 30% under N<sub>2</sub>-saturated condition (Fig. 6a). This implies that O<sub>2</sub> is involved in the oxidation of TMB and plays an important role in this catalytic process. Different radical scavengers, i.e. NaN<sub>3</sub>, p-benzoquinone (p-BQ) and thiourea (TH), were then introduced into the TMB/CoMo-300r system to examine the formation of ROS during TMB oxidation. As seen in Fig. 6b, the overall oxidase-like activity decreased progressively with increasing scavenger concentration, indicating the production of •OH, O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> and <sup>1</sup>O<sub>2</sub> in the TMB/CoMo-300r system. Above results indicate that the <sup>1</sup>O<sub>2</sub>, •OH and O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> radicals were the main ROS in the TMB/CoMo-300r catalyzed system. These ROS are generated from dissolved oxygen and oxidize TMB to generate color reaction. In addition, the CoMo-300r nanozyme had good batch reproducibility (Fig. S6a). It also maintained above 87% of original oxidase-like activity even 30-day storage in aqueous solution (Fig. S6b), indicating its good stability.

**3.4. CoMo-300r oxidase nanozyme-based colorimetric assay of sulfite**

Based on the oxidase-like property of CoMo-300r, a simple colorimetric assay was developed for the determination of sulfite. As shown in Fig. 7a, the addition of sulfite causes the decline in A<sub>652</sub> of the TMB/CoMo-300r system. A<sub>652</sub> decreased with increasing SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> concentration, the color of the solution became lighter (inset in Fig. 7a). The blue color almost completely disappeared at 60 μM sulfite. To understand inhibition effect of sulfite on TMB color reaction, the reaction kinetic was explored. Comparing curve 1 and curve 2 in Fig. 7b, an instantaneous A<sub>652</sub> decline was seen when sulfite was added to the TMB/CoMo-300r system. This implies the reduction of blue oxTMB by sulfite. In addition, a delayed TMB oxidation occurred upon the addition of sulfite into the TMB/CoMo-300r system at the beginning of the reaction (curve 3 in Fig. 7b). This indicates that ROS consumption by sulfite prevents TMB oxidation until complete depletion of sulfite. Notably, the three curves have almost identical slope. This suggests unchanged catalytic ability of CoMo-300r even in the presence of sulfite.

Based on above outcome, a sensitive colorimetric detection platform for sulfite was constructed in this work. As shown in Fig. 7c, ΔA<sub>652</sub> showed a good linear relationship with SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> concentration in the range of 0.2–40 μM, with the linear equation of ΔA = 0.01532 [SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>] + 0.0371 (R<sup>2</sup> = 0.9973, n = 9), here, the ΔA<sub>652</sub> denotes the difference of A<sub>652</sub> without and with SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>, respectively. The limit of detection (LOD, 3σ) for sulfite was 0.09 μM. The relative standard deviation for the determination of 10 μM sulfite was 1.41% (n = 11). The analytical characteristic obtained by the assay was compared with that of other methods for SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>. As displayed in Table S4, relative to other reported assays, the method has a wider linear response range or lower LOD.

Considering the potential of the proposed assay for practical applications, the effects of some potential interfering ions were explored, i.e. K<sup>+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup>, Al<sup>3+</sup>, Ag<sup>+</sup>, Zn<sup>2+</sup>, Ni<sup>2+</sup>, Mn<sup>2+</sup>, Cu<sup>2+</sup>, Fe<sup>3+</sup>, Fe<sup>2+</sup>, Co<sup>2+</sup>, Pb<sup>2+</sup>, NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>, PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup>, Cl<sup>−</sup>, S<sup>2−</sup> and SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>, and substances, i.e. lysine (Lys), arginine (Arg), cysteine (Cys), glutathione (GSH), histidine (His), tyrosine (Tyr), tryptophan (Trp), alanine (Ala), serine (Ser), glucose, maltose, fructose, starch and tartaric acid. As displayed in Fig. 8, except S<sup>2−</sup>, GSH or Cys, the addition of other substances had minor effect on ΔA<sub>652</sub> of the TMB/CoMo-300r colorimetric system. However, the effect of GSH or Cys can be efficiently masked by N-ethylmaleimide (NEM) [60]. In addition, the concentration of S<sup>2−</sup> is much lower than that of sulfite in wine [61], thus, its inference is minor when determining sulfite in wine. Above outcome indicates that the TMB/CoMo-300r colorimetric system has good selectivity for the detection of sulfite.

**3.5. Determination of SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> in white wine samples**

To demonstrate the application potential of this colorimetric method for the determination of sulfite in real sample, we determined the content of sulfite in the white wine, and the result was listed in Table S5. Also, the spiked recovery trials were carried out. The recoveries were in the 93.56% to 102.92% range, with the relative standard deviations of less than 5%. This implies that the method is accurate for determining sulfite in white wine and has good potential for practical application.

**4. Conclusion**

In conclusion, by controlling the ratio of Co to Mo and calcination temperature, the optimized three-dimensional flower spherical CoMo-300 was fabricated from the Co-Mo LDH precursor, which was synthesized by a simple hydrothermal method. Via further NaBH<sub>4</sub> reduction strategy, more surface O<sub>v</sub> and metal active sites were generated to produce the CoMo-300r nanozyme. The CoMo-300r maintains the surface lamellar structure of LDH after calcination and reduction and have a larger specific surface area and abundant pore channels, ensuring the exposure of more active sites. Relative to the CoMo-300, the CoMo-300r has lower charge-transfer resistance and quicker interface charge transfer ability, boosting 2.6-fold times higher oxidase-like activity of CoMo-300r than the CoMo-300. In terms of inhibition of SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> on TMB color reaction, a colorimetric method for the detection of SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> with high sensitivity and good selectivity was successfully developed and applied to the detection of sulfite in white wine. This work provides a new strategy to fabricate high activity oxidase mimetic for food analysis.
