RESEARCH ARTICLE

# Nanozyme with Photo-Enhanced Dual Enzyme-Like Activities for Deep Pancreatic Cancer Therapy

Shanshan Li,<sup>#[a]</sup> Lu Shang,<sup>#[b]</sup> Bolong Xu,<sup>[a]</sup> Shunhao Wang,<sup>[c]</sup> Kai Gu,<sup>[a]</sup> Qingyuan Wu,<sup>[a]</sup> Yun Sun,<sup>[a]</sup> Qinghua Zhang,<sup>[d]</sup> Hailong Yang,<sup>[a]</sup> Fengrong Zhang,<sup>[a]</sup> Lin Gu,<sup>[d]</sup> Tierui Zhang,*<sup>[b]</sup> and Huiyu Liu*<sup>[a]</sup>

**Abstract:** Compared with natural enzymes, nanozymes have attracted extensive interests due to their high stability, low cost and easy preparation, especially in the field of cancer therapy. However, the relatively low catalytic activity of nanozymes in the tumor microenvironment (TME) limited their applications. Herein, we report a novel nanozyme (PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) with dual enzyme-like activities for highly efficient tumor catalytic therapy. PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> shows the intrinsic photothermal effect as well as photo-enhanced peroxidase-like and catalase-like activities in the acidic TME, thereby effectively killing tumor cells and overcoming the tumor hypoxia. Importantly, a possible photo-enhanced synergistic catalytic mechanism of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> was first disclosed. We believe that this work will advance the development of nanozymes in tumor catalytic therapy.

## Introduction

Nanozymes are enzyme mimics with both unique properties of nanomaterials and catalytic activities of natural enzymes. In recent years, they have been rapidly developed in biosensing,<sup>[1-3]</sup> antibacterial<sup>[4,5]</sup> and immunoassay<sup>[6]</sup> due to their high stability, low cost and easy preparation.<sup>[7,8]</sup> Especially, they showed great promises in reactive oxygen species (ROS)-mediated tumor therapy.<sup>[9,10]</sup> However, the highly complex tumor microenvironment (TME) leads to an insufficient catalytic efficiency of nanozymes, and makes it difficult to achieve satisfactory therapeutic effects. Hence, it is urgent but challenging to develop an efficient nanozyme-based TME-responsive strategy for highly potent tumor catalytic therapy.

TME is generally known to feature unique characteristics such as the mildly acidic nature, hypoxia, and overproduced hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) ($50-100 \times 10^{-6}$ M).<sup>[11,12]</sup> Tumor hypoxia not only reduces therapeutic effects but also induces tumor proliferation and metastasis.<sup>[13,14]</sup> To solve this problem, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-responsive nanozymes were explored for tumor therapy.<sup>[16-19]</sup> According to current studies, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could be decomposed into highly toxic ROS by peroxidase (POD) mimics under acidic condition or H<sub>2</sub>O and O<sub>2</sub> by catalase (CAT) mimics under neutral condition.<sup>[20,21]</sup> However, nanozymes possessing both POD-like activity and CAT-like activity under tumor mildly acidic condition are rarely exploited.

Another strategy to achieve effective tumor catalytic therapy is to improve the catalytic activity of nanozymes. Hindered by the limited H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration in TME, the effect of single nanozyme-based catalytic therapy is not competent enough compared with combination therapy. Encouraged by the development of phototherapy,<sup>[22,23]</sup> the introduction of the near-infrared (NIR) laser is considered as a promising strategy to promote the catalytic activity and therapeutic effect of nanozymes. On the one hand, studies have been reported that nanocatalysts could promote ROS production under light irradiation by direct electron transfer and photo-enhanced Fenton reaction.<sup>[24]</sup> On the other hand, the ROS generation can be significantly improved by the photothermal effect of nanozymes.<sup>[25,26]</sup> Therefore, the photo-enhanced catalytic activity and photothermal effect of nanozymes are expected to achieve the balanced combination of tumor catalytic therapy and photothermal therapy (PTT).

Scheme 1. Schematic illustration of the photo-enhanced tumor catalytic therapy for PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanozyme.

Herein, in response to TME, we constructed a nanozyme with dual enzyme-like activities (PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) for highly efficient tumor catalytic therapy (Scheme 1). Serving as an H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-responsive nanozyme, PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> exhibited both intrinsic POD-like and CAT-like activities under acidic condition. Importantly, the catalytic activities of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> were significantly enhanced under the irradiation of the NIR laser. Meanwhile, for in vivo deep tumor therapy, we used the light interventional device to implement the in-situ photo-enhanced catalytic activities strategy. Combining the excellent photothermal effect, PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanozyme could effectively overcome tumor hypoxia and kill tumor cells with a tumor inhibition rate of 99.8% for deep pancreatic cancer. Moreover, to better understand the catalytic reactions of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanozyme, we for the first time revealed the electron transfer process among PtFe nanorods (NRs), Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles (NPs) and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> molecules. We believe this work will open a new horizon of exploring novel nanozyme-based strategies for tumor catalytic therapy.

## Results and Discussion

It was reported that the electron states of Pt could significantly be modulated by alloying with Fe.<sup>[27]</sup> Hence, we used PtFe alloy NRs<sup>[28]</sup> with less platinum but an improved activity for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> activation to construct the PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs. First, the PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs were synthesized by assembling multiple uniform Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs on the surface of the as-prepared PtFe NRs. Transmission electron microscopic (TEM) images showed that Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs with a diameter of mainly 5−20 nm attached to ultrafine PtFe NRs with a length of about 140 nm and width about 2 nm (Figure 1a and Figure S1-a and b). The lattice fringes with a spacing of 0.250 nm and 0.201 nm could be observed and ascribed to (311) planes<sup>[29]</sup> of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs and (111) planes of PtFe NRs, respectively, indicated by scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) (Figure 1b). Scanning transmission electron microscopy-energy dispersive X-ray (STEM-EDX) elemental mapping and STEM-EDX line scan were performed to confirm the composition of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs. The distributions of Pt is even through the whole nanorods (Figure 1c-d). And the distribution of Fe and O elements are in the same particle, which also suggested the attachment of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs on the surface of PtFe NRs. The presence peaks for Pt, Fe, O elements indicated the formation of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (Figure 1e), which was consistent with TEM and EDX results (Figure S2). Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) was performed to determine the fine structure of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs. One distinct diffraction peak assigned to (111) plane at 40.28° for PtFe NRs was observed (Figure 1f). In the case of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs, five characteristic peaks at 30.55°, 35.60°, 43.53°, 57.54°, 62.92° were also exhibited, which assigned to (220), (311), (400), (511), (440) planes of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs, respectively. According to the inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometer (ICP-MS), the mass ratio of Pt and Fe in PtFe NRs was 49:51, and the percentage of PtFe and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> in PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs were estimated to be 32 wt% and 68 wt%, respectively (Table S1).

The photothermal property of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs was further investigated. UV−vis−NIR absorbance spectra showed that PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs displayed a broad absorption from the visible to the NIR region (Figure 1g), indicating the potential of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs for photothermal conversion under NIR laser irradiation. It was noted that PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs exhibited a concentration-dependent temperature increase after exposure to NIR laser (Figure 1h). The temperature rose to 59.4 °C under the concentration of 100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>, while no significant temperature variation was observed for the PBS solution. Moreover, the infrared thermal images also showed the rapid temperature increase of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> compared to that of PBS solution (Figure S3). The excellent photothermal stability of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs was revealed via laser on-off cycles (Figure 1i). In addition, the photothermal properties of PtFe NRs and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs were also investigated (Figure S4). The elevated temperature of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs suspension (29.2 °C) was slightly lower than that of PtFe NRs (31.5 °C),<sup>[30]</sup> which may be due to the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs in PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (named as Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) blocking the light, thus reducing the photo-absorption of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs. The photothermal conversion efficiencies of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs, PtFe NRs, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs were calculated to be 7.6%, 14.9%, 3.8%, respectively.<sup>[31]</sup> (Table S2). These results demonstrated the reasonable photothermal-conversion efficiency of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs.

The POD-like activities of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs, PtFe NRs, and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs were firstly investigated by the oxidation of 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. Both PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs and PtFe NRs showed obvious POD-like activity, and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs exhibited the highest catalytic activity (Figure 2a). The optimal temperature and pH for PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanozyme ranged from 35 to 45 °C and 2.5 to 6.5, respectively (Figure S5 and S6). Moreover, the steady-state kinetic parameters including the Michaelis-Menten constant ($K_M$) and the maximum initial velocity ($V_{max}$) were acquired to assess their catalytic ability (Figure S7).<sup>[32]</sup> The $K_M$ of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs were determined to be 53.55 mM for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> substrate and 0.213 mM for TMB substrate, indicating the comparable affinity of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB substrates with that of natural horseradish peroxidase (3.70 mM for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> substrate and 0.434 mM for TMB substrate).<sup>[33]</sup> Importantly, the $V_{max}$ value of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs for H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> showed 1.3-fold increases relative to Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs (Table S3). These results demonstrated that PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs could effectively promote the decomposition of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and enhance the catalytic oxidation of TMB.

To investigate whether PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs possess CAT-like activity under acidic condition, we tested the generation of O<sub>2</sub> in the presence of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs. It is worth noting that no significant difference was observed for control groups, while PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs could produce more O<sub>2</sub> molecules (Figure 2d). Compared with Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs, the generation amount of O<sub>2</sub> by PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs showed 1.4-fold increases, demonstrating that the combination of PtFe NRs and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs effectively promoted the catalytic performance of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs.

According to the Arrhenius formula, the surface plasmon resonance (SPR) effect of noble metal could effectively convert NIR light energy to heat energy, which could promote the catalytic reaction.<sup>[34]</sup> More importantly, the SPR effect of PtFe NRs is associated with the enhancement of the electric near-field in the vicinity. Therefore, we hypothesized that the SPR effect of PtFe in PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (denoted as PtFe|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) could also inhibit the rapid recombination of photoexcited electron-hole pairs and boost the excitation of electron-hole pairs in Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>,<sup>[35]</sup> thereby H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> can accept electrons facilely.<sup>[36]</sup> The SPR effect was investigated by observing the catalytic performance of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs under different light wavelength (Figure S8).<sup>[37]</sup> Based on this, we first tested if the 808 nm laser could enhance the POD-like activity of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs. After exposing to 808 nm laser, the POD-like activity of both the PtFe NRs and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs significantly promoted while no enhanced catalytic activity was detected for Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs (Figure 2b). And the POD-like activity of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs exhibited a time-dependent irradiation manner under 808 nm laser (Figure 2c). The effect of SPR and thermal on the catalytic process were also compared. The result showed that both the SPR and the thermal effect could accelerate the catalytic reaction, while the effect of temperature on the catalytic process was much higher. (Figure S9). In addition, the photo-enhanced CAT-like activity was also observed (Figure 2e). After exposing to 808 nm laser, the generation amount of O<sub>2</sub> by PtFe NRs and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs showed 1.5-fold and 1.2-fold increases, respectively, compared with Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs. These results indicated that PtFe NRs played a vital role in the photo-enhanced catalytic system.

To elucidate the catalytic mechanism of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs, we first determined the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> decomposition efficiency during the catalytic process by using ammonium molybdate. The result indicated that the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> significantly decreased after incubation with PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs under 808 nm laser irradiation, suggesting the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could be decomposed by PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (Figure 2f). To further identify the generated ROS, electron spin resonance (ESR) spectra were used as direct evidence for the generated ROS. 5,5-Dimethyl-1-pyrroline-N-oxide (DMPO) was considered as a trapping probe for hydroxyl radical (•OH) and superoxide anion radical (O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup>). After the 1.5 min and 3 min 808 nm laser irradiation, these ESR intensities were markedly increased (Figure 2g and Figure S10). Therefore, PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs indeed have the potential to decompose H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and exhibit the photo-enhanced generation of •OH and O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup>, showing their high catalytic activities.

We then investigated the chemical composition and chemical states of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs to explore the internal catalytic mechanism by using X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements. Two peaks at the binding energy of 75.1 eV and 71.8 eV could be assigned to Pt 4f<sub>5/2</sub> and Pt 4f<sub>7/2</sub>, respectively (Figure 2h).<sup>[38,39]</sup> Compared with single Pt NPs, the binding energy of Pt 4f in PtFe NRs and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs showed noticeable negative shifts (~0.6 eV and ~0.8 eV, respectively), which revealed that the electrons were transferred from Fe atoms to Pt atoms,<sup>[40]</sup> resulting in an electron-rich state of Pt. The surface Pt in PtFe NRs and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs were proven to be mainly in the metallic state. In addition, the XPS characteristics for Fe 2p peak (Figure 2i and Figure S11) showed two distinct curves, corresponding to Fe<sup>3+</sup> and Fe<sup>2+</sup>, respectively, which revealed that Fe atoms in PtFe NRs and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs were mainly in the oxidized state. These results suggested that the reduction reaction process had more chances to happen on the surface of PtFe|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and the oxidation reaction happened on the Fe|PtFe and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>.

Based on the above experimental results, a possible catalytic mechanism was proposed. During the catalytic process of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanozyme, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> acts as an electronic pump that draws electrons from H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub><sup>[41]</sup> and constantly transports electrons to Pt|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. Then, the electron-rich state of Pt|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> was formed, thus H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> can easily obtain electrons from the surface Pt|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and generate •OH under acidic condition, referring to Equation (1). Meanwhile, electron flowed from Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> to PtFe|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and the exposed Fe<sup>3+</sup> promotes the second reaction to occur effectively, as described in Equation (2). Then, Fe<sup>3+</sup> reacts with O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> to produce O<sub>2</sub> (3).<sup>[17]</sup>

$$H_2O_2 + e^− + H^+ \xrightarrow{\text{PtFe NRs}} •OH + H_2O \quad (1)$$
$$Fe^{3+} + H_2O_2 \xrightarrow{\text{PtFe NRs/Fe}_3\text{O}_4 \text{ NPs}} Fe^{2+} + 2H^+ + O_2^{•−} \quad (2)$$
$$Fe^{3+} + O_2^{•−} \xrightarrow{\text{PtFe NRs/Fe}_3\text{O}_4 \text{ NPs}} Fe^{2+} + O_2 \quad (3)$$

In addition, under the NIR laser irradiation, the photothermal-augmented effect could accelerate the above catalytic reactions of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanozyme. Moreover, the NIR laser could promote the generation of high-energy electrons in PtFe|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, and accelerate the excitation of electron-hole in Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>,<sup>[42]</sup> which leads to the observed photo-enhanced POD and CAT-like activities of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanozyme.

To ensure that PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs could be effectively uptaken by cancer cells, the endocytosis efficiency of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs was firstly investigated by Bio-TEM and ICP-MS (Figure S12 and S13). Results showed that PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs could be efficiently internalized with increased incubation time. Then, the in vitro cytotoxicity and biosafety of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs were evaluated. After incubation with PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs for 24 h and 48 h, concentration-dependent cytotoxicity could be observed and the cell viability for the 24 h group of the human pancreatic cancer cell line SW1990 was below 65% under the concentration of 100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup> (Figure 3a). This result indicated that PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs could produce a large amount of •OH at the cellular level, which can increase lipid peroxidation of membranes and inhibit the function of proteins and even destroy nucleic acids and chromosomes under acidic condition.<sup>[43]</sup> Meanwhile, the cell toxicity effect of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs on normal human pancreatic duct epithelial cell line HPDE6-C7 was further investigated. The cell viability remained at 77% even at the dose of 200 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>, which revealed the proper biosafety of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs for normal human pancreatic duct epithelial cells (Figure S14).

The hypoxic condition results in the increased invasiveness and metastasis of tumors and the enhancement of antioxidant capacity.<sup>[13]</sup> Thus, improving the hypoxic condition could reduce the antioxidative ability of tumor cells and enhance the oxidative damage. As shown in Figure 3b, the cells treated with normoxia (65.8%) showed much higher inhibition rate than that treated under hypoxia (33.8%) at the concentration of 100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>. After exposing to 808 nm laser, the photo-induced killing efficiency of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs at normoxic condition reached to 80.2% at the concentration of 100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>. These results indicated that PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs possessed an excellent in vitro catalytic therapeutic effect.

To visually observe the living and dead cells, SW1990 cells were stained with Calcein-AM and propidium iodide (PI) after incubation with PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>). The red fluorescence (dead cells) and green fluorescence (living cells) could be distinguished by confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) (Figure 3c). The dead cells in PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs-NIR group were much more than those in PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs group, which demonstrated the significant photo-enhanced therapy efficiency. In addition, significant damage of cell membrane and nucleus could be seen in PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs-NIR group due to the photothermal effet (Figure 3d and Figure S15). To investigate the intracellular nanozyme properties, the intracellular ROS level was detected. As shown in Figure 3e, bright green fluorescence signals could be observed in PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs-NIR groups. In contrast, merely no green fluorescence signal could be found in other groups. The results of flow cytometry analysis of the intracellular ROS (Figure S16-17) were consistent with CLSM images. For specific detection of •OH at the cellular level, hydroxyphenyl fluorescein (HPF) was used (Figure S18). More •OH were detected under the stimulation of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs and NIR laser.<sup>[44,45]</sup> These results indicated that PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs could effectively exert POD-like activity in tumor cells and inhibit cell growth.

Considering the mode of intravenous administration, the effect of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> on red blood cells was first investigated. The hemolysis rate of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanozyme was < 5% even at the concentration of 200 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>, suggesting the good biosafety of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (Figure S19). To evaluate the in vivo potential therapeutic effects of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs for pancreatic cancer, an in-situ pancreatic cancer mouse model was established. And the aggregation of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs in the tumor sites was first evaluated by photoacoustic (PA) imaging and X-ray computed tomography (CT) imaging. After intravenous injection of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs both CT and PA signal intensities were significantly improved in the tumor sites and reached the maximum at 1 h (Figure 4a-d and Figure S20-22). The reason for the rapid accumulation of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs at the tumor site may be induced by the unique linear structure and ultra-small size of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs. These results revealed the potential of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs as a dual PA / CT imaging contrast agents. More importantly, the tumor hypoxic condition was improved after intravenous injection (Figure 4e-h). To further explore the improvement of hypoxic condition of the tumor site, hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF)-1α staining assay was employed.<sup>[46]</sup> Compared with PBS and PBS-NIR groups, the HIF-1α expression in PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR groups were significantly reduced, which revealed the improved hypoxic condition (Figure 4i). The generated O<sub>2</sub> in the tumor site could reduce the antioxidant capacity, which may induce tumor cells to be more sensitive to free radicals, thus enhancing the therapeutic effect.

Then, the in vivo therapy efficacy of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs was investigated. Firstly, the in vivo biocompatibility of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> was investigated. The results suggested that PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> has no long-term in vivo toxicity (Figure S23). SW1990 tumor-bearing BALB/c nude mice were randomly divided into four groups (n=8): PBS group, PBS-NIR group, PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> group and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR group. An NIR laser fiber with a custom hollow percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography needle penetrated deeply into the abdominal cavity of mice and irradiated the in situ pancreatic tumor. The PTT was implemented by using an infrared thermal mapping apparatus. A rapid rise of temperature could be observed in the PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR group (53 °C). While, the PBS-NIR group only rose to 42 °C (Figure 5a-b). Bioluminescence imaging (BLI) was utilized to monitor the growth of the tumor (Figure 5c-d). No significant enlargement of the tumor area was observed in the PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> group. Quantitative measurement data indicated that the tumor volume of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs-NIR group were significantly lower than those of other groups, and the tumor inhibition rate for the four groups (PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR, PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, PBS-NIR, PBS) are 99.8%, 70.7%, 11.1%, and 0%, respectively. Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining was also performed. In both NIR groups, significant cellular damage in cancerous tissues could be observed (Figure 5f), and the area of dead cells in PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR group was much larger than that of PBS-NIR group. Moreover, there was no obvious damage in normal tissues, except for an occasional liver metastasis in the PBS-NIR group. Two mice in the PBS-NIR group developed liver metastasis after treatment and no liver metastases in other groups. (Figure S24).

## Conclusion

In summary, we successfully designed a novel nanozyme with photo-enhanced dual enzyme-like activities in response to TME. Combining with the photothermal effect, it achieved efficient deep pancreatic cancer therapy. More importantly, the possible catalytic mechanisms for the enhanced POD-like activity and CAT-like activity were revealed. The synthesis method of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs effectively realized the redistribution of electrons in two different parts (PtFe NRs and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs), resulting in the electron-rich state of Pt atoms. And the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> acted as an electronic pump that drew electrons from H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> molecules and constantly transported electrons to Pt|PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, which led to the enhancement of catalytic activities. In addition, with a wide range of absorption wavelength, PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs could serve as a bimodal contrast agent for CT and PA imaging, which could provide a multimodal imaging-guided diagnosis and treatment platform. The photo-enhanced enzyme-like activities could effectively overcome tumor hypoxia and inhibit tumor growth, resulting in high-efficiency therapeutic effect for deep pancreatic cancer. We believe that these kind of metal / metal oxide nanomaterials with excellent enzyme-like activities can provide a new perspective for the design of nanomedicines.

## Experimental Procedures

**Materials:** Pt(acac)<sub>2</sub> (99%) and Fe(CO)<sub>5</sub> (99%) were purchased from Alfa Aesar. Oleylamine, Octadecene (ODE), oleic acid (OA), cyclohexane, ethylacetate were purchased from Beijing Chemical Reagent Company, Beijing, China. PEG–vitamin E (PEG–VE) (1 kD) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. TMB liquid substrate system was purchased from Aladdin. Ammonium molybdate and DMPO spin trapping adduct were purchased from Shanghai Macklin Biochemical Co., Ltd. 3-(4,5-Dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay, acetoxymethylester of calcein (Calcein-AM), propidium iodide (PI) and 2, 7-dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate (DCFH-DA) were purchased from Beijing Solarbio Science & Technology Co., Ltd., Beijing, China. Hoechst 33342 and 1,1’-Dioctadecyl-3,3,3’,3’-Tetramethylindodicarbocyanine Perchlorate (DiD) were purchased from Beyotime Biotechnology Co. Shanghai, China. Hydroxyphenyl fluorescein (HPF) probe was purchased from Shanghai Maokang Biotechnology Co., Ltd. All reagents were analytical reagents and used without any further purification.

**Synthesis of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs:** 110 mL of ODE, 10 mL of oleylamine and 10 mL of OA were added to a 250 mL three-neck flask and heated to 100 °C under Ar flow. Fe(CO)<sub>5</sub> (1.2 mL) was injected and the resulting solution heated to 260 °C under Ar flow, After 30 min, the solution was allowed to cool naturally to room temperature. Then the Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs were washed with cyclohexane and ethylacetate twice and modified with PEG–VE.

**Synthesis of PtFe NRs:** PtFe NRs were prepared by following a reported process.<sup>[45]</sup> Typically, 200 mg of Pt(acac)<sub>2</sub>, 10 mL of oleylamine and 10 mL of ODE were loaded to a 250 mL three-neck flask and heated to 100 °C under Ar flow. Thereafter, 0.15 mL of Fe(CO)<sub>5</sub> was injected, and the resulting solution was heated to 160 °C under Ar flow. After 30 min, the solution cooled naturally to room temperature. Then, the as-prepared PtFe NRs were washed with cyclohexane and ethylacetate twice and dispersed in 10 mL of cyclohexane. Finally, PtFe NRs were modified by mixing with PEG–VE solution (30 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) followed by centrifugation and washing with deionized water twice.

**Synthesis of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs:** PtFe NRs were prepared by following a reported process.<sup>[1]</sup> Typically, 200 mg of Pt(acac)<sub>2</sub>, 10 mL of oleylamine and 10 mL of ODE were loaded to a 250 mL three-neck flask and heated to 100 °C under Ar flow. Thereafter, 0.15 mL of Fe(CO)<sub>5</sub> was injected, and the resulting solution was heated to 160 °C under Ar flow. After 30 min, the solution cooled naturally to room temperature. Then, 100 mL of ODE and 10 mL of OA were added and heated to 100 °C under Ar flow. Fe(CO)<sub>5</sub> (1.2 mL) was then added and the resulting solution was heated to 260 °C under Ar flow. After 30 min, the solution cooled naturally to room temperature. Then, the as-prepared PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs were washed with cyclohexane and ethylacetate twice and dispersed in 10 mL of cyclohexane and modified with PEG–VE.

**Characterizations of photothermal property:** The photothermal property of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs was evaluated under 808 nm laser irradiation. PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs were dispersed in PBS to obtain PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> dispersion with different concentrations (0, 6.25, 12.5, 25, 50, 100, 200 µg mL<sup>−1</sup>). The PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> dispersion were exposed to 808 nm laser irradiation for 10 min in a glass vial (2 mL), PT-3S thermos-detector was used to detect the temperature changes of the solutions. The PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs dispersion with a concentration of 100 µg mL<sup>−1</sup> were used for further photothermal property characterization. Temperature changes (△T) of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs dispersion (100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>) under 808 nm laser irradiation for 30 min, then following by naturally cooling. Real-time temperature measurement of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs solution (100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>, 1 mL) was performed under 808 nm laser irradiation for four cycles. Each cycle consisted of 5 min irradiation followed by a 5 min cooling phase. Similarly, the photothermal property of PtFe NRs and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs were also evaluated.

**POD-like activity of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs:** The POD-like activity was assessed by UV-vis spectrophotometer (Shimadzu UV-2600) after 10 min’s reaction. 2 μL of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs, PtFe NRs or Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs (PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>: 100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>, PtFe: 32 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>: 68 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>) was added into 500 μL of NaAc buffer (pH=4.5) involved 2 μL of TMB (20 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) and 2 μL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (30%). The photo-enhanced POD-like activity was carried out by exposing to 808 nm laser irradiation for 3 min during the 10 min’s reaction procedure. The irradiation time-dependent photo-enhanced POD-like activity of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs was measured by Lambda 650 S UV-vis spectrophotometer. 1 μL PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs was added into 100 μL TMB liquid substrate system. In the beginning of the reaction, the above mixture was exposed to 808 nm laser (1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for different times (5, 10, 20, 30, 40 and 50 s) (n=3). Then the absorbance value at 652 nm was monitored during the 5 min’s reaction procedure.
The steady-state kinetic assays were performed at 0.1 mL NaAc reaction buffer solution with 10 μL PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs solution (0.1 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>), H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and TMB. The kinetic assays of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as the substrate were performed by adding 10 μL TMB (20 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> in DMSO) and different amounts of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (final working concentrations: 0.6, 0.3, 0.15, 0.075, 0.0375, 0.01875 and 0 M). The kinetic assays of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs with TMB as the substrate were performed by adding 6 μL H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (30%) and different amounts of TMB (final working concentrations: 832.2, 665.76, 499.32, 332.88, 166.44, 83.22, 41.61 and 0 μM). The absorbance of all reactions were monitored at different reaction times, and the Michaelis–Menten constant was calculated according to the Michaelis–Menten saturation curve by GraphPad Prism 7.0 (GraphPad Software).

**CAT-like activity of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs:** Oxygen electrode on Multi-Parameter Analyzer (JPSJ-606L, Leici China) was employed to detect the O<sub>2</sub> generation. 2 mL nanoparticles solution (PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>: 100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>, PtFe: 32 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>: 68 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>) was added into 10 mL NaAc buffer containing 200 μL 30% H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The generation of O<sub>2</sub> was measured at different time points. The photo-enhanced CAT-like activity of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs was carried out by adding 808 nm laser irradiation (1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) throughout the measurement process.

**•OH and O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> detection by electron spin resonance spectroscopy:** 5,5-Dimethyl-1-pyrroline-N-oxide (DMPO) spin-trapping adduct was used to detect •OH and O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> generation. Specifically, 2 μL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, 10 μL of DMPO, 5 μL of NaAc buffer and 2 μL of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs with a concentration of 1 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> were added into 31 μL of DMSO solution for O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> detection. 2 μL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, 10 μL of DMPO and 2 μL of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs were added into 36 μL of NaAc buffer for •OH detection. All the samples were reacted for 3min. For the NIR groups, 808 nm laser irradiation (1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>, 3 min) was employed.

**Detection of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> consumption:** Ammonium molybdate can form a yellow complex with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, thus the residual H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in solution can be detected. 400 μL of ammonium molybdate (100 mM) was incubated with 300 μL of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (25 mM) and 50 μL of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>) for 5 min reaction. NIR laser irradiation was employed for NIR group (3 min) during the 5 min’s reaction.

**Cell culture:** SW1990 cells were cultured in complete Dulbecco’s modified Eagle medium (DMEM) containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), 1% penicillin, and 2 mM L-glutamine. The cell lines were incubated at constant temperature of 37 °C and 5% CO<sub>2</sub> in the incubator, subcultured by adding 1 mL 0.25% trypsin and complete DMEM.

**In vitro cytotoxicity assays of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs:** SW1990 cells suspension with a concentration of 10<sup>4</sup> cells per well were seeded in 96-well plates and incubated for 24 h, then medium was abandoned and different concentration of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (0, 12.5, 25, 50, 100, 200 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>) dispersed in DMEM were added into 96-well plates with SW1990 cells, and incubated for another 24 h, then the MTT assay was used to test the cytotoxicity. After 24 h incubation with PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs, SW1990 cells were exposed to 808 nm laser (1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>, 3 min per well) and the MTT assay was used to detect the photo-induced cytotoxicity.

**In vitro cytotoxicity assays for SW1990 cells under hypoxic and normoxic conditions:** SW1990 cells suspension with a concentration of 10<sup>4</sup> cells per well were seeded in 96-well plates and incubated for 24 h, then medium was abandoned and different concentration of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (0, 12.5, 25, 50, 100, 200 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>) dispersed in DMEM were added into 96-well plates with SW1990 cells, and incubated for another 6 h. Then, the plates were placed in a transparent box with air for normoxic condition and N<sub>2</sub> for hypoxic condition for 2 h, followed by 808 nm laser irradiation (1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>, 3 min per well). Then the MTT assay was used to test the cytotoxicity.

**Co-staining of SW1990 cells with Calcein-AM and PI:** The detection of living and dead cells was carried out by CLSM (Leica SP8, Germany). SW1990 cells were seeded in CLSM-exclusive culture disk (35 mm) and incubated for 24 h. PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs with a concentration of 100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup> dispersed in DMEM was incubated with cells for another 24 h, then washed with PBS twice before adding Calcein-AM and PI into wells. After incubation with Calcein-AM and PI for 30 min, cells were washed with PBS twice. PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR group was exposed to 808 nm laser irradiation (808 nm, 1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for 3 min. The green and red fluorescence were observed by CLSM.

**The generation of ROS in SW1990 cells:** The ROS generation in SW1990 cells was observed on CLSM. SW1990 cells were seeded in CLSM-exclusive culture disk (35 mm) and incubated for 24 h. PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>) dispersed in DMEM was added into SW1990 cells and incubated for another 24 h. Then the cells were washed with PBS twice, and DCFH-DA was added and incubated with cells for 30 min. Cells were then washed with PBS twice. PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR group was exposed to 808 nm laser irradiation (808 nm, 1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for 3 min. Finally, the ROS level was determined by detecting the fluorescence value of 2,7-dichloro-fluorescein (DCF) which was obtained by the oxidation of DCFH. Then DCF fluorescence signal was captured by CLSM.

**The specific detection of •OH in SW1990 cells:** SW1990 cells were seeded in CLSM-exclusive culture disk (35 mm) and incubated for 24 h. PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>) dispersed in DMEM was added into SW1990 cells and incubated for another 12 h. Then the cells were washed with PBS twice, and HPF probe was added and incubated for 60 min. Cells were then washed with PBS twice. PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR group was exposed to 808 nm laser irradiation (808 nm, 1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for 3 min. Finally, the •OH level was captured by CLSM.

**Flow cytometry analysis:** SW1990 cells were seeded in 12-well plates with a concentration of $8 \times 10^4$ and incubated for 24 h. After removing the DMEM, PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup>) was added and incubated for another 24 h. Then the cells were washed with PBS twice, and incubated with DCFH-DA for 30 min. For NIR groups, cells were exposed to 808 nm laser irradiation for 3 min (1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) and incubated for another 30 min. Finally, above cells were collected and washed with PBS for further analysing by flow cytometry (MoFlo XDP, Beckman, CA, USA).

**Co-staining of cell membrane and nucleus:** SW1990 cells were seeded in CLSM-exclusive culture disk (35 mm) and incubated for 24 h. PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs with a concentration of 100 μg mL<sup>−1</sup> dispersed in DMEM was incubated with cells for another 24 h, then washed with PBS twice. PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR group was exposed to 808 nm laser irradiation (808 nm, 1.0 W cm<sup>−2</sup>) for 3 min. Then replaced the PBS with 4% formaldehyde and incubated for 20 min, washed with PBS twice. 500 μL DiD (2 μM) was added and incubated with cells for 20 min washed with PBS twice then Hoechst 33342 was used to dye the nucleus for 30 min. finaly, the cells were washed with PBS twice and observed by CLSM.

**The in vitro hemolysis percentages of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanozyme:** mice blood was first centrifugated for 5 min (3000 rpm), then washed with PBS for 3 times. The collected RBCs were diluted 50-fold. 0.5 mL of RBC were mixed with 0.5 mL PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs and incubated for 2 hours at 37 °C, then centrifugated for 10 min (10000 rpm). PBS and D.I. water were used as the negative control and positive control, respectively. The absorption values were determined at 570 nm. The percentage of hemolysis was calculated following: hemolysis %=(sample absorbance−negative control absorbance)/(positive control absorbance−negative control absorbance)×100.

**In vivo PA / CT imaging:** SW1990 tumor-bearing BALB/c nude mice were injected with PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs (20 mg kg<sup>−1</sup>) dispersed in PBS solution via tail vein and followed by PA imaging (MSOT inVision 128, iThera medical, Germany) at different time point. For in vivo CT imaging, the images were obtained before and after intravenous injection of PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs. All animal experimentation protocols were approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of the Research Center for Eco-Environmental Sciences, Chinese Academy of Sciences.

**In vivo antitumor therapy:** SW1990 tumor-bearing BALB/c nude mice (8 weeks) were randomly divided into 4 groups (8 mice for each group): PBS, PBS-NIR, PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR. For PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR groups, PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NRs with dosage of 20 mg kg<sup>−1</sup> were injected into SW1990 tumor-bearing BALB/c nude mice via tail vein. Then the PBS-NIR and PtFe@Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>-NIR group mice were exposed to 808 nm laser irradiation for 5 min in the abdominal pancreas region by using a custom hollow percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography needle (Zhuhai Hokai Biomedical Electronics Co., Ltd., China) and an NIR laser fiber (0.8 mm, Banglei Optoelectronic Technology Co., Ltd, Guangdong, China). The infrared thermal images and temperature changes in tumor region were also obtained during the procedure. Bioluminescence images, tumor volume and body weight were employed to monitor the tumors changes and physical condition of each mice during the 15 days of treatment. Tumor volume, body weight and bioluminescence images were measured at 3 days interval.
